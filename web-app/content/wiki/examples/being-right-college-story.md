# Being Right: The College Expulsion Story

## The Story

Joe had a 3.95 GPA and got expelled from his first college. He was in rebellion mode, his identity from childhood. The college had introduced moral education rules he disagreed with. He tore down signs blocking his dorm windows. He fought every authority figure who confronted him.

The ombudsman told him: just say you're sorry and you'll get a slap on the wrist. Joe refused. He mounted a full defense: RAs testified for him, a petition of 300 students, professors speaking to his contributions. Every move just made the administration angrier. He was not on a learning journey with them. He was proving he was right.

The outcome: suspended for a year, expelled from the dorms. A history professor told him, "How can I expect you to go out into the world and have healthy children if this is the way you're going to behave?"

Joe threatened to sue. The dean of students reduced it to one quarter suspension. Joe switched schools.

## The Point It Illustrates

Being right is useless. Having the best case, the most evidence, the most logical position does not produce connection, learning, or progress for anyone.

"I can't look back and say that I was wrong. I can't look back and even say that they were wrong. I think we were both ignorant because we were both in some level of war with ourselves and each other. But none of us got any closer to understanding ourselves and each other in the process." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder

The root was an identity built on being right, formed in a family of origin where losing an argument meant danger. Joe identified with fairness and fought for it. The cost: no one learned anything. The dorm was not better. The administration was not wiser. Joe was not freer.

## When Joe Reaches For This

When someone is locked in being right. When wonder has collapsed into knowing. When the person's identity is wrapped around their position and they cannot see how that rigidity is creating the very outcome they are fighting against.

## What It Leads To

Joe's suspension led him to a job caring for developmentally disabled residents. There he discovered that connection has nothing to do with being right. People who could not follow a complex argument could be in beautiful, deep relationship. The lesson: being right has nothing to do with anything that matters.
