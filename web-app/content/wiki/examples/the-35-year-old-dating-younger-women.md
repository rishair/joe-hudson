# The 35-Year-Old Who Kept Dating Significantly Younger Women

## The Story

Joe is in conversation with a man who is 35 years old and has not found a long-term partner. The man's framing of why is the wrong-person deception in clean form. He believes he has the capacity for love — he's had two good relationships — so the missing piece must be the people, not him.

"I'm talking to a guy and he is saying how he is 35 years old, hasn't found the love of his life. But he said, 'I know that I can get it, you know, because I've had two relationships that have been really good. And so I know that I can get it. So I just think it's about finding the right person.'" -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The framing is fully formed. The conscious story is: I have evidence I can love. I just haven't found the right one yet. The implication: keep looking, the right person will eventually arrive, the absence of partnership is bad luck or bad timing.

## The Move Joe Makes

Joe does not argue with the conscious story. He asks a specific question that surfaces the structural pattern underneath the story:

"And I was like, 'Okay, cool. So what happened to those two people?'"

The question routes around the conscious story. It does not ask "are you sure you're not pushing love away?" — that would just produce more conscious story. It asks for the data. The two relationships that the man cited as evidence of his capacity become the material for examining what selection mechanism actually produced them.

## What the Data Showed

The two good relationships the man cited were both with women significantly younger than him, both in their early 20s, both at a life stage that did not include serious commitment to partnership.

"And what we discovered was that the two people that he was dating were both significantly younger than him and were at a place in their life where there's not really a capacity to commit. You know, in your early 20s, you're not gonna jump in very often and go, especially in his particular socioeconomic area where like people are just getting their careers started and they've just graduated from college, it's not a typical time for people to get married." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The pattern across the two relationships is unmistakable once the data is named. The man's "good relationships" were good in the sense that they did not produce the configuration he said he wanted (long-term partnership). The choice of partners who were structurally unavailable for that configuration was reliable enough across the two relationships that the choice was the selection mechanism at work.

The body had been choosing partners who would not produce commitment. The conscious mind had been calling those relationships "good" and concluding from them that the man was ready for commitment with the right person. The selection of partners who would not produce commitment was itself the push-away in operation.

## The Diagnostic Question Joe Asks

After surfacing the pattern, Joe asks the question that converts the diagnostic into recognition:

"How much are you actually just attracted to people who are unavailable, that you know on some level are unavailable, and then you have a good relationship but you know it's never gonna actually pan out?"

The question contains the answer the man needs to hear. It is phrased as a question because the man has to be the one to recognize it; if Joe asserts it, the man can argue with it. As a question that surfaces what the man already knows on some level, it produces the recognition without producing defense.

"And he's like, oh crap, I totally see how I'm doing that." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The "oh crap" is the subconscious grin moment described in [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]]. The body recognizes that the conscious story has been pierced. The structural pattern is now visible. The work that follows is the work on whatever is producing the selection — usually [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]], usually some specific imprint from [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]] about what love costs.

## The Point It Illustrates

**The "right person hasn't shown up" frame is reliably a cover for the body's selection mechanism.** The body is not failing to encounter the right person. The body is reliably selecting people who guarantee the right configuration will not appear. The selection is the work being done. The "wrong people" the person keeps encountering are the work of the selection succeeding.

**Past "good relationships" can be evidence against love-capacity, not for it.** The man cited the two relationships as proof he could love. The relationships were proof of what kind of love he could engage with — the kind that would predictably end. The cited evidence was the diagnostic data. See [[convincing-ourselves-were-not-pushing-love-away]] for the broader treatment of this self-deception.

**The diagnostic question is structural, not behavioral.** "How much are you attracted to people who are unavailable?" does not ask the man to change a behavior. It asks him to see what his body has been doing. The seeing is the move. The behavior change follows from the seeing, not from being instructed to do something different.

**The "I just haven't found the right person yet" frame can be sustained indefinitely.** Without the structural question, the man could continue dating significantly younger women in his 40s, his 50s, his 60s. Each relationship would have its own specific reason for not panning out. Each reason would be technically true. The structural pattern would remain invisible. The wrong-person deception is what allows the push-away pattern to persist across decades.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When a client is presenting the wrong-person deception in the "I just haven't met the right one" form
- When a client cites past relationships as evidence they have love-capacity without examining what those relationships had in common
- When a client is in [[i-cant-find-love]] or [[i-keep-chasing-emotionally-unavailable-people]] and the structural question is the next move
- When the coaching needs an illustration that "right person hasn't arrived" is reliably a selection problem, not a circumstance problem
- When the client is choosing partners who differ in surface ways but share a structural unavailability (significantly younger / significantly older / already partnered / in another city / not over the ex / not ready to commit)

## The Compressed Form

The diagnostic move this example demonstrates compresses to two steps:

1. Ask for the data: "What happened with the past people?" / "What did all of your past partners have in common?"
2. Ask the structural question: "How much are you actually attracted to people who are unavailable?"

The two questions together convert the wrong-person frame into the structural recognition. The client does the rest. The "oh crap" is the work happening.

## What This Example Does Not Cover

The example shows the diagnostic move that breaks the wrong-person deception. It does not show the work that follows the recognition. The man has recognized the pattern; now the question is what was driving the selection. The likely next moves involve [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]] (what made unavailable partners feel right?), [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]] (what would have to be faced if a committable partner were chosen?), and the [[three-ways-we-push-love-away]] reading (is this primarily a chase-the-unavailable form? an avoid-the-available form? something else?).

The transcript does not follow the conversation past the moment of recognition. The example's value is the diagnostic move itself.
