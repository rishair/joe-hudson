# Two CEOs at 80-90 Hours a Week

## The Example

Joe has watched many high-capacity CEOs over the years. Two distinct types show up at the same work load.

Two CEOs working 80-90 hours a week. Both passionate about what they do. Same load on paper. One is constantly stressed and exhausted. The other has infinite energy. Same hours; opposite experiences.

The difference is not in the work. The difference is in how the two are talking to themselves about the work.

"I have watched folks who are super high-capacity CEOs, and some of them are entirely stressed out. And some of them are not stressed out. Both of them working 80, 90 hours a week, both of them super passionate about what they do, but one of them seems to have infinite energy and one of them doesn't." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

## What The Stressed CEO Does

The stressed CEO is constantly telling themselves everything they should be doing that they are not doing. The voice in their head produces a continuous list of unmet items. The to-do list is always longer than the day. The mind is in the past about what was missed and in the future about what is still owed.

The body is reading the continuous attack of the shoulds as continuous threat. The result is the dysregulation -- the exhaustion, the depletion, the anxiety -- that the CEO then attributes to the workload.

The CEO believes the workload is the cause. The actual cause is the self-talk.

## What The Non-Stressed CEO Does

The non-stressed CEO takes a different posture toward the shoulds. Two versions of the posture appear:

**Version 1: Take action on the should as it surfaces.** Every time the voice says "I should be doing X," the CEO takes the action. The voice's request is acted on rather than held as a continuous internal load.

**Version 2: Reorder the priority explicitly.** Every time the voice says "I should be doing X," the CEO says "that's important, but it's not as important as this, so I'm going to do this." The reordering ends the loop. The voice does not continue running the unresolved should because the should has been resolved -- not by doing it, but by explicitly de-prioritizing it.

"The difference is one of them is constantly telling themselves everything that they should be doing that they're not doing, and the other one is taking action every time one of those things happens. Or they're saying, 'That's important, but it's not as important as this, so I'm gonna do this.'" -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The non-stressed CEO has either acted or chosen. Either way, the should is no longer sitting unresolved in the system. The continuous attack stops because there is nothing left for the voice to attack about.

## The Pattern Across Many Cases

This is not a one-off contrast. Joe has seen the same pattern hundreds of times:

"It's why some of the most successful CEOs that I've ever met are always focused on one or two things. They know that here's the one piece of leverage that if I push it, everything else will fall into place, everything else will be easier, or everything else will be irrelevant. And so they're solely focused on these one or two places where they know that they can make a significant difference for everything else that they're doing. And when they know that, when they've done that thought process, they're a lot less stressed as compared to somebody who feels like they should be doing everything and there's no way they can do everything and they've got to do everything. That doesn't work." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The most successful CEOs run [[focus-on-the-first-domino]] continuously. They identify the one or two things that, if done right, make everything else easier or irrelevant. They direct their attention there. The everything-else-needs-to-be-done frame is replaced by a explicit one-or-two-things frame.

The frame is what stops the should-attack. The body knows what is being prioritized and what is being de-prioritized. There is no internal disagreement about what is important. The voice has nothing to scream about.

## The Counterintuitive Punchline

"It's just interesting to watch how one of these CEOs is just getting more and more tired and exhausted and the other one is not overwhelmed, is not tired, not exhausted. And they're running a bigger company and they have more decisions to make." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The non-stressed CEO is often running the bigger company. They have more decisions to make. The load is structurally larger. And they are less stressed.

The example destroys the load-based theory of stress. If the load were the cause, the bigger-company CEO should be more stressed, not less. The fact that the opposite is observed is the evidence for the actual cause: the self-talk, not the load.

## What This Example Illustrates

The example is the load-bearing illustration for the principle that anxiety is downstream of self-talk, not downstream of load. See [[four-causes-of-stress]] -- the first cause is the way you talk to yourself. The example makes the principle concrete in a domain (high-capacity leadership) where the load-based theory has the strongest grip.

The example is also the illustration for [[focus-on-the-first-domino]] and [[focus-on-leverage-principles-not-everything]]. The non-stressed CEO is running both. The principles look like productivity techniques but the actual mechanism they operate on is the stress-self-talk loop. They are anxiety interventions disguised as decision frameworks.

## When To Use This Example in Coaching

- When a leader presents stress and attributes it to the workload
- When a CEO is in the should-loop and has not noticed that the loop is the cause of the stress
- When a person is convinced that more work will fix the stress (the "if I just push through this stretch" frame)
- When the [[four-causes-of-stress]] framework needs a concrete illustration of the thought-layer cause
- When [[focus-on-the-first-domino]] needs the felt-cost demonstration of why the focus matters

The example also works in reverse. When a leader reports they have infinite energy despite a heavy load, the read is that they are running one of the non-stressed CEO's postures. Ask which one. The answer often reveals the structure they have built without naming it.

## What The Example Does Not Address

The example focuses on the thought-layer cause of anxiety -- the self-talk loop. It does not address the other layers in [[four-causes-of-stress]]. A CEO whose self-talk is healthy can still be dysregulated at the nervous-system level, can still have unfelt emotions, can still be defending a rigid identity. Those layers need their own interventions.

The example also does not address the case where the load itself is genuinely beyond capacity. There is a load level beyond which no self-talk posture is sufficient. The principle is that for most CEOs at 80-90 hours, the load is not the operating variable. For someone working 120 hours through a real crisis, the load may actually need to be reduced.
