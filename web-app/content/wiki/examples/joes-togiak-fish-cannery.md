# Joe's Togiak Fish Cannery

## The Story

Joe was just out of college. He had just read Black Elk Speaks, the autobiography of the Lakota medicine man. The book describes the Sun Ritual: the endurance of pain as the practice through which one comes to appreciate life. The teaching landed in Joe as a thing he wanted to test on his own body.

A friend was hitchhiking up to Alaska to butcher fish at a cannery. Joe joined him. He needed some money, but the actual draw was the chance to put himself through something that would teach him the Sun Ritual teaching by force. They hitchhiked the whole way, accumulating stories the entire route.

They flew into Togiak, Alaska — on Bristol Bay, just before the Aleutian Islands. The friend warned him before they landed: "This work is like digging a ditch 16 hours a day, butchering these fish." Joe said okay.

They landed. The first thing the manager told them: "The septic is gone, you're digging ditches." So for the first stretch, they literally dug ditches sixteen hours a day. Only later did Joe and his friend find out the cannery had a backhoe the whole time. The ditches were a warm-up. The crew was being conditioned for the actual work.

The actual work was harder than the ditches.

## "Kicking You Down the Steps of the Temple"

Brett's framing for the warm-up ditches: it's like kicking you down the steps of the temple. Make the candidate suffer before the real test so they will know how to handle the real test when it arrives. Joe agreed. Only you find out that the work was a lot harder than the ditches.

They slept maybe 6 hours a night during the cannery work. When the job finally ended, they slept for 36 hours straight.

Joe got injured at one point during the work and went to a hospital. He came back, had gotten a little bit of sleep, and remembered the level of fatigue clearly because of one specific image. He was looking at his friend who was talking to another friend. One friend was drooling. The other friend wasn't noticing.

That was the level of fatigue.

## Gut Tag

The crew invented parlor tricks to pass the time. The one Joe remembers: gut tag. How many fish guts could you pile into somebody else's slickers without them noticing.

## Why Joe Stopped Eating Fish

The combination of being inside a fish cannery for that long, doing what they were doing to fish for that long, and playing gut tag with fish guts produced a durable somatic aversion. After Togiak, Joe didn't like fish. He didn't really eat fish at all for years.

The aversion lasted until Tara introduced him to sushi much later. From there, he gradually returned to fish, and eventually could eat most cooked fish too. But the line of the change runs cleanly from Togiak: he was a person who ate fish until that summer, and a person who didn't after.

## The Point

The point of telling the story is not the fish. The point is the orientation that brought him there. Joe describes the orientation directly: in his twenties, he was trying to collect as many experiences as he could collect, especially the ones outside the range a person from his background would normally enter.

Black Elk Speaks gave him a frame: endurance of pain produces appreciation for life. The fish cannery was Joe's test of the frame on his own body. The frame did something. The aversion to fish was the side effect; the change in his relationship to pain-endurance was what he was after. This is a wisdom-tradition framing from Joe's pre-path period — and one Joe later complicates in his own teaching. The mature framing distinguishes endurance (which holds the pain in place) from welcoming (which lets the pain transform). See [[endurance-blocks-transformation]] for the mature contrast. The Togiak story is the young version that came before the distinction was drawn.

## The Larger Pattern This Story Is a Specific Instance Of

This is one node in a broader biographical pattern: Joe in his twenties went looking for the most obscure experiences he could find. Hanging out with homeless people. Hanging out with billionaires. Driving with hitchhikers to random middle-of-nowhere cowboy bars (see [[joes-cold-beer-friday-cowboys]]). Sailing in Tahiti. Living with tribes — at least 6 or 7 in America and 2 or 3 outside America. Spending time in Iran, Saudi Arabia, Wales, England, Switzerland.

The orientation he names in retrospect: "I was constantly finding the most obscure people, places that I could and understanding them. I was trying to understand humanity in any way that I could."

The fish cannery is one experience-collection story among many. It is the one with the strongest somatic signature — pain-endurance, sleep deprivation, the lasting fish aversion. The structure is the same as the others: Joe putting himself into a context his background would not have produced, staying in it long enough to be changed by it, and walking out with a piece of humanity he could not have learned otherwise. See [[joes-experience-gathering-orientation]] for the developed pattern this story belongs to.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When the point is being made that endurance of pain teaches appreciation
- When the orientation of "collect any experience you can get into" needs a concrete instance
- When the story being told is about a young person putting themselves through deliberate difficulty for the sake of being changed by it
- When the contrast is being made between the curated, low-friction life of someone wealthy and the unconcerted, high-friction life of someone who is letting the world rough them up on purpose
- When someone is asking how Joe got to where he is and the answer involves the willingness, in his twenties, to be deeply uncomfortable on purpose

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, with the amusement of looking back at the young man who decided this was a reasonable summer job. Joe tells it as a story, not as a teaching. The teaching is embedded.
