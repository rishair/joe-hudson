# Joe's Cold Beer Friday Cowboys

## The Story

Joe is driving home from LA in his twenties. Bowl cut. T-shirt and pants — dressed neutrally enough to fit into the corners of any room, whether a business meeting or an artist hangout. He sees a guy with dreadlocks hitchhiking. He picks him up. The guy is from Uruguay. They talk about Uruguay.

The guy says he is going to San Francisco. Joe says, "Cool, but we're not gonna go the direct way. We're just gonna — this is a big valley, the Central Valley, so we don't even — we can just take any road. Look, I'll show you."

The hitchhiker gets a little scared. They drive. They see a trailer that says Cold Beer Friday, Saturday, Sunday. They pull over. They walk in.

The bar is literally inside a mobile home trailer. It is full of cowboys. There is music playing. The music — and Joe is precise about this — literally stops.

The cowboys look at the dreadlocked hitchhiker. They look at bowl-cut Joe. Back in those days, cowboys and hippies were not coexisting peacefully in California bars. They were enemies. Joe, in his neutral clothes, is okay.

Joe — used to putting himself into these kinds of rooms — opens the conversation. "Hey, I was just driving up from LA and this guy was hitchhiking. He's from Uruguay. Do you all know where Uruguay is?"

Next thing they know, they are drinking beers in the trailer with the cowboys. Joe ends up in a long conversation with one cowboy who literally only owns a car and a saddle. The cowboy tells Joe about being out in the open with cows for weeks at a time, coming back to stay with a woman (his girlfriend or whoever was around), sleeping on the side of the road, drinking some beer, and then off he goes again. They talk about how much whiskey the ranch owner gives him per day.

## What the Story Demonstrates

Three things layer in this story. Each is part of why Joe tells it.

**1. The willingness to enter rooms his background would not have put him in.** A young man with a bowl cut and a hitchhiker from Uruguay walking into a cowboy bar that has literally just stopped playing music when they entered. This is the orientation in its specific form: most people would have turned around. Joe stayed.

**2. The bowl-cut neutral-collector outfit as a deliberate strategy.** Joe describes his clothes in this period as engineered to let him fit into the corners of any room. Not flashy enough to provoke an artist scene, not square enough to provoke a business room. The clothes were instruments. They let him walk into spaces that would have closed to a more visible self-presentation. The clothes were one of the tools that made the experience-collection orientation operational.

**3. The opening move into a tense room.** "Hey, I was just driving up from LA and this guy was hitchhiking. He's from Uruguay. Do you all know where Uruguay is?" The move is not posturing or apology. It is named-presence with a small handle (the geography question) that gives the room something to engage with. The move is what turned a music-stopped bar into a drinking room. He had practiced this in many rooms by the time he made it here.

## The Larger Pattern

This story is one of Joe's many specific instances of an orientation he had through most of his twenties: collect any experience you can get into, especially the ones outside the range your background would normally produce. Hitchhikers, billionaires, homeless people, tribes (at least 6 or 7 in America and 2 or 3 outside America), the fish cannery in Togiak, sailing in Tahiti, the obscure middle-of-nowhere bars. The cowboys in the trailer are one of the most picturesque, but they were one of many.

The orientation he names in retrospect: "I was constantly finding the most obscure people, places that I could and understanding them. Like, hanging out with homeless people or hanging out with billionaires. I would find these little niches. I was trying to understand humanity in any way that I could."

He gives one other compressed example in the same conversation: "There's one time when we were digging holes to understand wetlands in Yuma, Arizona, and within one day, I hung out with a homeless person named Lucky, who had been living and jumping on rails and living homeless for 15 years. And the next day, I'm hanging out with one of the Waltons. And like hanging out with like from Walmart and hanging out with billionaires. It was like, that was my reality."

## What Joe Was Following

The orientation came before the path. Joe is explicit that he did not have a self-development agenda yet. He had not yet had the 8-second oneness experience that turned everything into "I am on a path." He was just following his interests. He was following his wants — before the wants had been overlaid with shoulds.

"Yeah, it's following my interests. I was following my wants, right? Before I had like a way that I had to be." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The interest, in this period, was humanity. What is human under the cultural costumes. What is shared across every kind of person. What it is to be a person.

The retroactive read he gives — "I don't know if I was born with that. I was told it at a young age. I remember being told at a really young age, oh, you'll be a psychologist when you grow up. Just because I always had this interest in humanity" — is also tied to the survival skill of reading his alcoholic father. The child who learns to read a volatile parent very quickly becomes the adult fascinated by reading humans in general. The fascination outlasts the original danger and becomes a vocation.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When the orientation of collecting experiences across class, culture, and context needs a concrete instance
- When the conversation is about how a person actually builds the substrate for later coaching work — by being inside as much of humanity as they can get inside of, for years, before they have any framework for what they are doing
- When the contrast is being drawn between the curated, comfortable life and the friction-rich, cross-class life that produces a different kind of person
- When someone asks how Joe ended up with the range of contexts he can coach inside of (CEOs, artists, blue-collar workers, billionaires, addicts)
- When the point is being made that wants come before shoulds, developmentally, and that a person who follows their wants in their twenties is laying the substrate for a different kind of adulthood

## Tone

Affectionate, slightly amused at his younger self, alive in the telling. Joe tells the story as a story, with the rhythm of someone who has told it before and still enjoys it. The bowl-cut self-mockery sits next to a clear-eyed recognition that the orientation that produced these stories was the orientation that built the rest of his life.
