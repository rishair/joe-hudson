# The Rubbermaid Dish Solution

## The Story

A group of friends lived together and none of them wanted to do the dishes. The standard household conflict cycle began: resentment, passive-aggressive notes, attempts to set chore schedules, escalating frustration.

Instead of trying to force each other to do the dishes, they sat down and asked what each person actually wanted. Out of that conversation came an unconventional solution:

- They bought two large Rubbermaid trash cans
- Filled them with water and a small amount of bleach
- Went to Goodwill and bought every cheap dish they could find
- Used dishes were dropped into the trash cans outside, all day, every day
- Once a month, they used a sprayer and soap to wash everything in the trash cans on the driveway together
- One monthly chore replaced the daily friction

The solution was, in Joe's words, "kind of gross, but also awesome."

"This was their solution to the problem after sitting down and everybody saying what they wanted. They went and got two huge Rubbermaid trash cans and they filled them with water and a little bit of bleach. And they went to the like Goodwill and they bought all the cheap dishes they could buy. And every time they were done with their dishes, they just put them in these two Rubbermaid trash cans outside with all the bleach. And like once a month they would like put soap in and like wash it with like a sprayer. And like they all did dishes once a month on the driveway. Kind of gross, but also awesome." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## The Point It Illustrates

The story is the canonical illustration of a principle that runs underneath the boundary teaching: most relational conflicts that produce demands and ultimatums are actually solvable with creativity, but the creativity only becomes available when both parties stop trying to control each other and start naming what they actually want.

The conventional response to the dish problem is to escalate the demand: "you have to do your dishes." This is the [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums|ultimatum disguised as boundary]] move. It does not solve the problem; it amplifies the resentment.

The Rubbermaid solution emerges from a different question: not "how do I get them to do the dishes?" but "what do we actually want here, and how do we get it?"

"There's that many solutions to the dish problem that is like haunting half of the marriages in the United States. You know what I mean? And instead we sit there and try to fucking control each other. It's kind of silly." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When a couple or household is stuck in a chore-distribution fight
- When someone is about to draw a boundary that is really a demand, and the actual problem could be solved by creativity instead
- When the conversation is about the gap between trying to change someone's behavior and asking what each person wants
- When the topic is creative problem-solving inside relationships
- When someone is so focused on the specific behavior they want changed that they cannot see the larger solution space

## Why the Story Works

Three features make this example land:

1. **The solution is absurd enough to be memorable.** Trash cans on the driveway. Bleach. Goodwill dishes. The absurdity makes the broader point harder to forget.
2. **It works.** The household actually solved the problem. The example is not hypothetical.
3. **It universalizes.** Joe explicitly extends it to "half of the marriages in the United States." The dish fight is everywhere. The dish solution is therefore universally relevant.

## The Broader Principle

The story illustrates several principles at once:

- **Wants over demands:** name what you want, not what they should do. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].
- **Creativity unlocked by stopping the control:** the cleverness was always available; the controlling dynamic was hiding it.
- **The actual want underneath the surface fight:** none of them wanted to do dishes daily. The fight was about who would. The solution honored the actual want of all parties.
- **Solving for everyone instead of solving for me at their expense:** the unconventional solution served everyone equally. The original demand-fight would have served at most one party.

## What This Is Not

This is not an instruction to avoid all conflict or to find creative solutions to every problem. Sometimes the problem does not have a clever solution. Sometimes the right move is the boundary or the separation.

This is also not an endorsement of the specific solution. The Rubbermaid setup is gross. The point is not the solution; the point is the question that produced it.
