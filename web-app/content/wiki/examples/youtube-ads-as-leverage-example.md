# YouTube Ads as Leverage Example

## The Story

Joe is shooting an episode. His producer is doing research by watching YouTube videos. Joe notices that the producer is listening to ads on each video.

> "Today, we're shooting this video and my producer is watching YouTube videos to do some research. And I listen and I hear, oh wait, I hear an ad. What are you doing? Like, what makes you listen to an ad? If you're researching 20 videos, you're going to be listening to 20 ads. Those 20 ads are going to be a minute apiece. That's 20 minutes that you're wasting just on this video session. What's the one thing you can do to make everything easier? Well, the one thing to do is to pay to not take ads on your videos. And then how much does that cost? Well, that costs a lot less than your hourly, you know, for a month. So let's do that." -- Joe Hudson, Escape Time Anxiety

The arithmetic is sharp because it is obvious. Twenty videos times one minute of ads per video is twenty minutes of pure waste per research session. YouTube Premium costs less per month than the producer's hourly rate. The leverage task is one purchase. The recurring drain disappears for every future research session.

## What This Example Illustrates

The example is a worked case of [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]] operating at the smallest possible scale. The principle scales up to "which three things this quarter would make most of next quarter's work irrelevant." The principle also scales down to "what is the one purchase or one decision today that eliminates a small recurring drain."

The downward scaling is often where the principle is easiest to land. People hear the principle at the strategic level and nod. The same principle at the YouTube-Premium level is unmissable. The leverage logic is identical. The scale is just smaller.

The structural shape:
- **The recurring waste:** ads, every video, every research session, forever (until someone pays for Premium).
- **The leverage move:** pay $14/month for YouTube Premium.
- **The result:** the recurring waste stops existing. All future sessions save the 20 minutes.
- **The comparison:** $14/month is much less than the hourly cost of the producer's time, even at a small number of research sessions per month.

The principle does not require math beyond grade school. It requires noticing that the recurring cost is recurring and that one decision can eliminate it.

## Why This Example Lands

Three reasons the example works as a coaching reference:

**1. The waste is invisible until named.** The producer was doing the work. The work involved watching the ads. The ads were part of how the work happened. The waste was not the producer's fault. The waste was the invisible cost of not having pulled back to ask whether the work should be happening this way at all. The example shows that the leverage-blindness is universal and not personal.

**2. The fix is trivially small.** One purchase. One decision. No new system. No new process. No re-org. The size of the fix is the point. People often assume leverage moves require large, scary changes. Many leverage moves are tiny. The size of the fix is the inverse of the size of the recurring waste.

**3. The math is undeniable.** $14 < hourly rate × any reasonable number of sessions per month. The cost-benefit calculation is one line. The conclusion is automatic once the calculation is done. The work was not to find the move. The work was to look at the situation and notice that a move existed.

## The Underlying Pattern

The structure of the example generalizes. Wherever there is recurring waste in any workflow, there is usually a small leverage move that eliminates it.

Examples of the same shape:
- A team holds the same status meeting every week. The meeting could be a shared document.
- A person re-types the same email response a hundred times a year. The response could be a saved template.
- A workflow requires the user to log in to three systems. Single sign-on eliminates that.
- A recurring report takes four hours and is read by no one. The report could be cancelled.
- A founder personally answers every support ticket. One hire or one well-written knowledge base eliminates 80% of the tickets.

In every case, the structure is: there is a recurring cost, the cost has been invisible because it is recurring, and a small one-time decision eliminates the recurring cost. The example is a template for noticing this pattern wherever it is operating.

## What This Example Is Not

- It is not an endorsement of YouTube Premium specifically. The product is the example, not the teaching.
- It is not a productivity hack. The principle is structural. The hack is one application of the principle.
- It is not a permission slip to buy every subscription that promises to save time. The discipline of the principle requires the math actually checking out. Most subscriptions do not pass the math. This one does, in this specific case, given the producer's hourly rate and research frequency.
- It is not the only kind of leverage move. The example is small and operational. Larger leverage moves (re-architecting how the company ships product, deciding what the company should not be doing at all) operate at the same logic at a different scale.

## When to Use This Example

- When a person is hearing the leverage principle at the strategic level and not landing it operationally.
- When a person dismisses leverage thinking as "for CEOs" or "for big strategic decisions."
- When a coachee describes a workflow with recurring waste that they have stopped noticing.
- When a team has been doing something the same way for so long that the cost has gone invisible.
- When the [[fourteen-day-time-experiment]] is being introduced and the leverage technique needs a small, vivid worked case to make the move feel doable.
- When a person is overthinking the leverage move and needs an example that demonstrates how small a leverage move can be.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly bemused. Joe tells the story with the energy of someone catching a friend doing something obviously unnecessary. The tone is not judgmental of the producer; the producer was doing the work as instructed. The tone is "did you see this?" not "you should have seen this." The bemused quality is what makes the example accessible -- the listener can picture themselves doing the same thing without feeling indicted.
