# The Tennis Quarter

## The Story

Joe's college girlfriend Kate was a strong tennis player. With her coach, she practiced serving and trying to hit one of those wire baskets used to pick up balls. She would land the ball in the basket 2 or 3 times out of 5.

One day the coach removed the basket and placed a quarter on the court in the exact spot where the basket had been sitting. He told her: "Serve and hit the quarter."

She did not hit the quarter once. But every ball she served landed within the area the basket had covered. She would have hit the basket every single time if it had still been there.

"My girlfriend in college, her name was Kate, and she would serve. She was a really good tennis player, and she would, with her coach, she would serve and try to hit like a basket, one of those baskets that you pick up the balls with. And she would hit it like she was good. So she hit it 2 or 3 out of 5 times. And then one day the coach took the basket off the ground and put a quarter right in the middle of where that basket was sitting and said, 'Serve and hit the quarter.' And she didn't hit the quarter once, but she would have hit the basket every single time if it was still sitting on top of that quarter." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## The Point It Illustrates

Aiming past the target produces more precision than aiming at the target. Focusing through the goal makes the goal more likely to be reached.

When Kate aimed at the basket, her serves landed 40-60% of the time. When she aimed at the quarter (a much smaller target inside the basket), her serves landed within the basket area 100% of the time. The smaller, more demanding aim produced consistently better results on the original goal.

This is the operational principle of [[goal-beyond-the-goal]]. The mind that has only the goal as its target uses the goal as its ceiling. The mind that has a larger orientation uses the goal as a waypoint, which makes the goal easier to hit.

## When Joe Reaches for This

When someone is gripping a numerical goal and producing diminishing returns. The story shows directly that the gripping itself reduces accuracy.

When someone resists the idea of having a goal "beyond the goal" because it seems impractical or fluffy. The tennis quarter is measurable, simple, and decisive.

When teaching the [[intention-vs-goal]] distinction. The story shows that the lighter, larger orientation is not less rigorous than the focused goal. It is more rigorous.

When someone is building a business and the only target is revenue. The story makes the case that adding a larger orientation (quality, service, beauty) will improve the revenue, not detract from it.

## The Broader Pattern

The same dynamic appears across domains. The civil rights leader who aims at justice (a target larger than any specific legislation) ends up moving more specific legislation. The CEO who aims at building something meaningful ends up building a more profitable company than the one who aims only at profit. The writer who aims at truth ends up producing more sellable work than the one who aims only at sales.

The mechanism: the larger aim organizes everything around it. Attention, choices, and effort become coherent. The specific goal gets hit as a byproduct of the coherent organization.
