# The CIA Sabotage Manual for Dysfunctional Organizations

## The Story

In the 1940s or 1950s, the CIA produced a document describing how to corrupt an organization from within. The document was a field manual for operatives planted inside enemy organizations. The instructions: defer decision-making to committees, make sure the committees have more than six people, take away the autonomy of people who know the work, route every choice through the slowest possible process.

"How to go into an organization and make it dysfunctional because they were trying to plant people inside of key organizations of what was considered an enemy at the time and make it dysfunctional. It's, it's a great document because when you see it, you see so many organizations are run the way that they tell you to corrupt an organization. So really cool stuff." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The historical reference is to the OSS Simple Sabotage Field Manual (1944). The OSS, predecessor to the CIA, produced it for use by ordinary citizens in occupied territories who could not engage in armed resistance.

## What the Manual Recommended

- Defer decision-making to groups
- Make sure the groups are over six people
- Route everything through committees
- Take away the autonomy of people who know the work

"Defer decision-making, defer decision-making to groups, make sure that the groups that you're deferring the decision-making to are over 6. Always try to get everything in the hands of a committee. Take away the autonomy of people who know the work, stuff like that." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Point Joe Uses It For

The instructions for sabotage and the practices of normally-functioning bureaucracies are indistinguishable. Many organizations operate exactly as the OSS manual recommends and are not aware they are running a sabotage protocol on themselves.

"You see so many organizations are run the way that they tell you to corrupt an organization." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The example does a particular kind of work in Joe's teaching: it interrupts the assumption that dysfunctional organizational practices arose for good reasons. The opposite is closer to true. They arose because they look like care (consulting more people, distributing the decision) and feel like safety (no one person is on the hook) while producing the opposite of what they appear to produce.

## The Direct-Reports Application

Joe uses a related diagnostic when assessing executives:

"When I'm working with an executive for the first time, one of the first questions I'll ask them is how many direct reports you have. And if they have over 5, it's a red flag. If they have over 8, I just know there's no functionality happening, or not a great deal of functionality happening in that organization. To have that many direct reports is just, you can't make a decision, or incredibly hard to make a decision." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

Same principle as the committee sizing in the OSS manual. Once the number gets above a threshold (5-6 for direct reports, 6 for committees), decisions become structurally hard to make.

## Where The Pattern Shows Up

- Nonprofits and government agencies where decision-making by committee is built into the structure
- For-profit companies that have grown to a size where decisions used to be made by individuals are now made by groups
- Family businesses where every decision is consensus among siblings
- Boards that have grown past the size where a real decision can be made
- Any organization that has confused "more input" with "better decision"

"That happens in a lot of governments and nonprofits, exactly. But also happens in way more for-profit companies than you can imagine." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team
