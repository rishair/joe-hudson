# The Valve Handbook (Desks on Wheels)

## The Story

Valve, the video-game company, published a handbook describing how they organize work. Two structural choices stand out:

**They choose people very carefully.** The handbook devotes serious attention to how they hire. The selection of who gets to be in the company is treated as the primary management lever.

**Once hired, employees have desks on wheels.** Where the employee pushes their desk is the project they are working on. There is no central authority assigning people to initiatives. The initiatives that exist are the ones people have rolled their desks toward.

"They figured out how to choose really good people. They have a whole thing about that. And then when you get to Valve, you have a desk, it's on wheels, and where you push your desk is what projects they do. So there's not even somebody saying, okay, these are the projects we're going to do, here's our big initiatives. They literally just have people roll their desk to what they want to do, and those are the initiatives that get done in the company." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

## The Point It Illustrates

The combination is the move. Valve solved the management problem at the hiring layer rather than the operational layer. Once the right people are in the room, the management overhead drops to near zero because the people themselves carry the judgment that would otherwise have to be supplied from above.

This is the extreme version of [[give-parameters-of-winning]]. The parameter is the room the desk can roll in. The winning condition is whether a project gets enough rolled-up desks to become real. The method is entirely the employees'.

The example demonstrates that the line between "management is necessary" and "management is unnecessary" is not fixed. It moves whenever someone designs a system that makes the management redundant. See [[enjoy-over-manage]] on the Hyatt vs. Airbnb, SK Telecom vs. Apple comparisons -- the same logic at industry scale.

## What Valve Did Not Do

They did not fire all the managers and hope. They did not declare a flat organization while keeping all the hidden hierarchies. They did not adopt this structure to be trendy.

They built two specific structural conditions: a hiring process strict enough to produce a workforce capable of self-direction, and a physical/organizational layout (rolling desks) that made self-direction the path of least resistance.

Without the hiring strictness, the desks-on-wheels would produce chaos. Without the desks-on-wheels, the hiring strictness would produce people who could self-direct but were not allowed to. Both conditions had to be in place.

## The Boundary Condition

Joe is explicit that this approach has a domain. Not every company can or should run this way. The point is not that everyone should adopt the Valve structure. The point is that wherever management feels necessary, there is probably a structural design that would make it less necessary. The question to ask is not "how do I manage better?" It is "what structure would make this management unnecessary?"

"Is there really a need for management? Is there really a need for that level of centralization? And there may be in certain circumstances, but guaranteed there is a more efficient system out there. And when somebody finds it, they will be the winner of that business and their life will be more enjoyable." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

## The Larger Pattern

Valve is one of several examples Joe uses to make the same structural point:

- **Airbnb vs. Hyatt:** Hyatt manages properties; Airbnb sets standards and lets hosts run their own. Different management requirement, similar function.
- **Apple App Store vs. SK Telecom:** SK Telecom managed app development directly; Apple set minimum requirements and let anyone publish. Apple won.
- **The US economy:** roughly four tools (interest rates, legal constraints, monetary supply) and no central manager. Still the largest economy in the world.
- **Buurtzorg nursing:** see [[buurtzorg-nursing]]. The company that abandoned the standard efficiency metric outperformed competitors on efficiency.

Each example shows the same structural insight: less centralized management, more carefully-designed conditions, better results. The "less management" is not the cause; the design that makes less management possible is the cause.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When a leader is convinced that their team requires the management they are providing and is looking for evidence otherwise.
- When the conversation about [[enjoy-over-manage]] needs a corporate example, not just a personal one.
- When a leader is asking "how could a real company actually work this way?" and needs a concrete answer.
- When [[hire-by-dream-job]] is being discussed and the structural payoff of strict hiring needs an illustration.
- When [[give-parameters-of-winning]] is being discussed and the extreme version (parameters only, no specified path) needs an example.
- When a coachee is trying to imagine the alternative to micromanagement and cannot picture it.

## What This Example Preempts

The objection: "but you have to tell people what to work on, otherwise nothing gets done." Valve is a counter-example. People who chose to be at Valve, doing work they wanted to do, choose what to work on by rolling their desks. The work that gets done is the work that the workers actually want to do. The output is not less; it is differently composed.

The deeper objection: "but my company has to do specific things, we can't just let people decide." This is often true. The Valve model is not universally applicable. But the same logic applies in degrees. Even in a company that needs central planning, there are layers where the rolling-desks logic could operate. The question is which layers, not whether.

## Tone

Joe describes Valve as a curiosity, slightly amused. The story functions as evidence that the extreme version of his principle has been tested in production. The amusement signals he is not prescribing Valve as the template, just pointing at the fact that the experiment has been run and the results are interesting.
