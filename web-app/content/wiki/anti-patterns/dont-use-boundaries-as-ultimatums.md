# Don't Confuse Boundaries with Ultimatums

## The Anti-Pattern

Stating what someone else has to do and calling it a boundary. Phrasing a demand in boundary language to make it socially acceptable. Telling another person they must change while preserving the appearance of just stating your truth.

The mechanics of stating a boundary and stating an ultimatum can sound nearly identical. The difference is internal: the ultimatum is operating from fear and trying to control the other person; the boundary is operating from clarity and describing what you will do.

"When our boundaries sound like ultimatums, it means that we're really scared, that we're operating out of the fear because we're trying to control the other person in one way or another." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## Why It Fails

The ultimatum is a control move dressed as a self-statement. The other person feels controlled and responds either by complying (resentfully), rebelling, or leaving. None of these is what the speaker actually wants. The speaker wanted the relationship to work. The ultimatum makes the relationship less workable.

The ultimatum also undermines the speaker. Each time a stated "boundary" is really a demand, the speaker reinforces the disempowered stance that requires them to control others to feel safe. The pattern compounds. The speaker becomes more boundary-y, less effective, more lonely.

The third failure: ultimatums teach the listener to stop trusting your boundary language. Once "this is my boundary" has been used as a power move several times, the next time you draw a real boundary, the listener reads it as another move in the power game. The word loses its meaning.

## The Two Tests

A statement is a boundary, not an ultimatum, if it passes both tests:

1. **You are saying what you will do, not what they must do.** "If you keep yelling I am going to leave the room for 30 minutes" is a boundary. "Stop yelling" is a demand. "Stop yelling or I am leaving" looks like a boundary but is functionally an ultimatum if the underlying intent is to stop them from yelling.

2. **Stating it opens your heart to the person rather than closing it.** See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]. If thinking about saying it makes you feel more love for the other person, it is a boundary. If it makes you feel more righteous, more separate, more defended, it is an ultimatum. See [[does-saying-it-increase-your-love]].

## The Constricted Tell

The body knows. The ultimatum has a different somatic signature than the boundary. The ultimatum tightens; the boundary widens.

"If I come at people with constriction around my wants, it doesn't land well." -- Alexa Anderson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The constriction is the diagnostic. When the chest is closed, when the jaw is set, when the voice is hard, the statement coming out is more likely an ultimatum even if it has all the right words. The same words from an open body, a soft voice, an undefended posture, will land as a boundary.

## When the Situation Genuinely Requires a Hard Line

Sometimes the right response really is "leave or I am leaving." The embezzlement example: "I don't want to do business with anybody who stole from me. There's just like, leave or I'm leaving."

This looks like an ultimatum but is not, structurally. The speaker is not trying to control the other person. The speaker is naming the limit of their own willingness to participate. The other person is fully free; the speaker is also fully free.

"I'm not kind of trying to control them. I'm just saying this isn't a relationship I want to be a part of." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The test still holds: are you trying to change them, or are you stating what you will do? When the stakes are high enough that the boundary leaves no room for continuation, that does not make it an ultimatum; it makes it a clean refusal.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is internal first, behavioral second. Locate the fear that is producing the constricted boundary. The fear is usually one of two things:

1. **Fear that the boundary will not be respected.** This makes the speaker firm up the language to compensate. The firming up is the giveaway. Real confidence in a boundary makes it softer, not harder, because the speaker is no longer trying to enforce compliance through tone.
2. **Fear of the actual consequences if the other person does not comply.** This makes the speaker pre-load the demand with consequences hoping to scare the other person into compliance. The pre-loading is the manipulation.

In both cases, the work is on the fear, not on the language. See [[empowered-vs-power]]: the empowered stance does not need to control because it is already willing to accept the consequences of being honest.

Once the underlying fear is felt, the boundary often does not need to be stated at all, or it gets stated in a form so simple and undefended that it stops looking like a boundary.

"It almost seems like once you really feel empowered and you really trust yourself, that you can state a boundary and it doesn't even land in anyone as a boundary." -- Alexa Anderson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

Joe confirms this is true and adds the warning: do not use this as an excuse to never draw the boundary while waiting for the perfect internal state. The internal state and the boundary-drawing develop together. Drawing the imperfect boundary is part of how the internal state arrives. See [[apologize-for-trying-to-control]] for the move when you catch yourself overshooting.
