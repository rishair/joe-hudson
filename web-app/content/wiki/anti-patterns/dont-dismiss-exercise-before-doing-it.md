# Don't Dismiss an Exercise as a "Nothing Burger" Before Doing It

## The Anti-Pattern

Someone is offered an exercise. Before doing it, they evaluate it intellectually and conclude it will not produce anything useful. "We've done something like this before." "This seems too simple." "This is a nothing burger." The dismissal arrives in the form of a reasonable-sounding assessment. The person is not refusing to participate; they are explaining why participation would be a waste of time.

The dismissal looks like discernment. It is actually a defense mechanism. The intellect has identified the exercise as something that might produce contact with material the system is not yet ready to feel, and is generating a preemptive reason not to engage.

"There's so many times that someone's just like, 'well, we've done something like this before, this isn't going to be useful.' And then I'm like, just, just do it. And then they have that moment of like, 'oh.' It's one of our defense mechanisms." -- Joe Hudson, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

The signature is the predicted verdict on an experiment that has not yet been run. The verdict cannot be supported by the data because there is no data. The verdict is being produced from somewhere else.

## Why It Fails

An exercise designed to produce a felt shift cannot be evaluated from the intellect alone. See [[exercises-need-body-voice-emotion]]. The exercise's mechanism is to recruit the body and the emotions alongside the intellect; reading the exercise without doing it engages only the intellect, which is exactly the channel the exercise is designed to bypass-by-including.

The intellect's prediction about the exercise is therefore structurally uninformative. It is predicting what would happen if the exercise were run on the intellect alone. That is not what the exercise does. The prediction's confidence has no basis.

The deeper failure: the dismissal protects whatever was about to be felt. The exercise was likely going to produce contact with something the person has been avoiding. The dismissal lets them stay above the contact. Avoiding the contact preserves the avoidance pattern that the work is for.

## The Two Forms of Pre-Dismissal

**The "I've done this before" form.** The exercise is dismissed by being matched to a prior exercise that did not work. The match is usually superficial. The person did a different exercise with surface similarities and is generalizing from the prior outcome. The current exercise has not been given the chance to do what it does.

**The "this seems too simple" form.** The exercise is dismissed for not looking intellectually substantial. The expectation is that real work has to look hard, complex, or unfamiliar. A short, simple exercise cannot possibly produce a shift, because the apparent difficulty does not match the expected difficulty of transformation. This is its own limiting belief. See [[exercises-need-body-voice-emotion]] for why simplicity of design is often a feature, not a deficit.

Both forms produce the same outcome: the exercise is not done, and the dismissal is reinforced by the absence of evidence against it.

## What Joe Says Instead

"Just do it." The instruction does not engage the dismissal. Joe does not argue against the prediction. He does not try to convince the person the exercise will work. He invites the person to bracket the prediction long enough to run the experiment.

The "oh" moment that follows is the data the dismissal could not have produced. The person discovers the exercise does something the intellectual prediction did not anticipate. The dismissal is revealed as the defense it was.

The move connects to [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]]: bracket the skepticism for the duration of the experiment, then apply it to the results. The pre-dismissal is skepticism applied before the experiment, which contaminates the experiment.

## Why Naming It as a Defense Mechanism Matters

Joe explicitly calls it "one of our defense mechanisms." The naming is important for the AI coach. The dismissal is not a content claim about the exercise; it is a structural move the psyche makes when it senses an exercise might produce unwanted contact. Treating the dismissal as a content claim and arguing back about the exercise's merits misses the point. The argument plays the dismissal's game.

The right response treats the dismissal as data about the person's relationship to the work, not as data about the exercise. The data: something in this person is preparing to avoid contact. The exercise is more likely to produce a real shift, not less, when this defense activates. The defense is rising because the system senses what the exercise is about to do.

## Joe's Smile

Joe describes himself "smiling so hard right now" recounting this pattern. The smile is not contempt; it is recognition. The pattern is universal enough that he has seen it many times across many people. The smile is also confidence: he has seen the "oh" moment that follows often enough to know it is coming.

The tone matters for the AI coach. The dismissal is not a moral failure to be corrected sternly. It is a familiar move that almost everyone does at some point. The right response is to invite the person past it with warmth, not to call them out for resisting.

## The Diagnostic

The diagnostic for whether a dismissal is pre-dismissal or actual discernment: was the exercise run? If the answer is no, the verdict is not discernment, regardless of how the verdict is phrased. Real discernment about an experiential exercise requires the experience.

The person who has actually run the exercise and reports it did not produce contact is giving real data. The person who has read the exercise and predicted it will not produce contact is performing dismissal. The two reports look similar in words and are completely different in basis.

## The Connection to Half-Assing

This anti-pattern is upstream of [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]]. The pre-dismissal often becomes the half-ass: the person who decided in advance the exercise was useless then participates without engagement, producing the absence of contact they predicted. The prediction creates the conditions for its own confirmation.

The two anti-patterns reinforce each other. Catching the pre-dismissal before it produces the half-ass protects the experiment from both failures.

## What This Means for the AI Coach

When a person dismisses an exercise the AI coach has offered, the move is not to defend the exercise. The move is to invite them to do it anyway. "I hear that you've done something like this before. Try this version once and tell me what happens." The invitation is short, warm, and does not engage the prediction.

If the person still refuses, the right response is [[meet-resistance-with-okay]]: the exercise is not for them right now, and that is fine. Pushing harder does not produce sincere engagement. The AI coach honors the refusal and stays in relationship.

The AI coach also has to watch for the version where the person performs the exercise as if to confirm the prediction. This is the half-ass move and requires the response in [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]].

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- A coachee is offered an exercise and immediately produces a reason it will not work for them
- A team member dismisses a proposed practice as "the same as something we already tried"
- A reader of a transformation methodology evaluates the exercises intellectually without doing them and concludes the methodology is shallow
- A skeptic of a particular practice points to its apparent simplicity as evidence of its uselessness
- A person who has done a lot of personal work assumes new exercises will not have anything to offer them

The last form is particularly common in experienced practitioners. The accumulated experience creates a confidence that no new exercise has anything to teach. This confidence is itself a defense. The exercises that produce the biggest shifts in experienced practitioners are often the ones they assumed they had outgrown.
