# Don't use wonder as a strategy

## The Anti-Pattern

Using questions to achieve a hidden goal rather than to be genuinely curious. This takes two common forms:

1. **Questions as avoidance.** Always asking about the other person to deflect attention from yourself. The questions create a semblance of connection while keeping the questioner safely hidden. This is wonder deployed as a defense against [[vulnerability]].

2. **Questions as manipulation.** Asking open-ended questions because you read that it works in sales or coaching, while your actual interest is in steering toward a specific outcome. The person on the receiving end feels probed, investigated, or handled.

Both feel creepy. The other person senses the incongruence between the open question and the closed intention.

## Why It Fails

Wonder that is strategic is not wonder. It is impartiality that has been counterfeited. The moment there is a hidden agenda behind the question, the questioner is partial, not impartial, and the question carries that partiality in tone, timing, and follow-up.

"If wonder gets a little tilted, it can become a strategy, and as soon as it becomes a strategy, it starts to feel creepy." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder

People detect strategic questions quickly. Mirror neurons register the mismatch between the open words and the closed state.

## What To Do Instead

Have wonder for the sake of wonder, for the sensations it creates in the body, for the awe it opens. If you notice the other person resisting your questions, turn wonder on that resistance: "What's making you feel creeped out right now?" If you discover an ulterior motive in yourself, name it. That naming is [[vulnerability]], and it shifts the state.

"Don't use this as a strategy. Have wonder for the sake of wonder, for the gifts that it brings, for the feelings and sensations in your body that it creates." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder

If questions are being used to avoid your own vulnerability, the fix is not better questions. The fix is to share something about yourself.

The simple invitation when caught: "Well, what do you want to ask me?" You can also apologize for the probing energy. "I'm sorry, I was kind of asking you questions as a way to avoid myself." Naming the strategy ends the strategy and opens a real exchange. This is the same principle as the rest of [[view]]: state of mind, not technique. The technique dissolves the moment it is named as technique.
