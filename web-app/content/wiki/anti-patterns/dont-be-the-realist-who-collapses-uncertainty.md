# Don't Be the Realist Who Artificially Collapses the Uncertainty

## The Anti-Pattern

The realist is the most dangerous of the three artificial collapses of uncertainty because the realist appears to be doing what is being recommended: facing reality. The realist is not facing reality. The realist is collapsing the genuine uncertainty into a fixed (usually bad) outcome and presenting the collapse as level-headed maturity.

"There's like a kind of silent third one that sort of masquerades sometimes as facing the uncertainty. And this is the realist, the person who says, 'I'm not an optimist or a pessimist, I'm a realist.' And yet they're still collapsing reality to a certain outcome that they end up recreating. It's not the outcome that they want." -- Brett Kistler, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The realist has the same structural problem as the optimist and the pessimist: a fixed prediction of the future treated as known. The realist's prediction is just better dressed than the other two.

## The Flying-Through-The-Crash Diagnostic

The cleanest test for whether someone is in genuine ownership or in disguised pessimism wearing a realist costume is the aviation analogy. Brett:

"In aviation, if your plane is crashing, you're going to just continue to assume you're going to find somewhere to land, or if you have no way to bail out, you're just going to fly all the way through the crash. And sometimes you'll be surprised that you're still alive at the end of it. Whereas you're like, 'oh, I'm a realist. I'm out of engines. I don't have a landing area, runway. I might as well just nose in.'" -- Brett Kistler, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The realist who says "I might as well just nose in" has performed the crash in advance. The genuine ownership stance keeps flying. The two stances see the same data — no engines, no runway, plane is going down. The realist collapses the situation to its predicted endpoint and acts accordingly. The owner keeps flying, because flying is still the available action and the outcome is still not actually known until it is.

The realist always crashes. The owner sometimes crashes, sometimes does not, and either way is the kind of pilot they wanted to be on the way down.

## Why It Fails

**Realism is pessimism with a better costume.** The neutrality is theater. The realist has fixed the outcome to bad and is calling it acceptance. Acceptance does not require fixing the outcome. Acceptance can hold genuine uncertainty.

**The realist's prediction has the same cortisol cost as the pessimist's.** The body cannot tell the difference between "we're fucked" and "I'm just being realistic, this is not going to work." Both run the system on cortisol. Both shut down executive function. Both produce the bad outcome through dysregulation. See [[dont-be-pessimistic-in-uncertainty]] for the mechanism.

**The realist refuses the opportunity that the situation contains.** The same chaos that contains threat contains opportunity. The realist has decided in advance which one is operative. See [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]] for the principle the realist is refusing.

**The realist's identity is invested in being right about the bad outcome.** This is the most subtle problem. The realist who has predicted the bad outcome has staked their self-image on being correct. Any movement toward the good outcome threatens the identity. The realist may, at the margin, actually contribute to the bad outcome to preserve the rightness of the prediction. This is invisible to the realist.

## How to Recognize the Realist Stance in a Coaching Moment

The realist stance often sounds like:

- "I'm just being realistic, this isn't going to work."
- "Let's not kid ourselves."
- "I'm just calling it like I see it."
- "I'm not an optimist or a pessimist; I'm a realist."
- "We just have to accept that this is how it's going to go."
- "I'm just being practical."

The diagnostic question: does the stance leave room for the situation to be other than predicted? If yes, it might be ownership. If no, it is the realist stance, regardless of how mature it sounds.

Another diagnostic: ask "and what would it look like to keep flying anyway?" The realist will treat the question as naive. The owner will engage with the question.

## The Subtlety: Some Realism Is Real

Not all use of the word "realistic" is the realist anti-pattern. A clear-eyed assessment of probabilities that still leaves room for the situation to be other than predicted is fine. The anti-pattern is specifically: a fixed prediction of the bad outcome dressed in the language of neutrality.

The test is in the body and in the action. Genuine realism produces clarity and engagement. The realist anti-pattern produces flatness and disengagement.

## What to Do Instead

The intervention has three parts.

**Name the prediction.** Make the implicit fixed-outcome explicit. "It sounds like you've already decided how this is going to go." Often this is the first time the person has noticed that they have made a prediction; they were experiencing themselves as just looking at the facts.

**Test the prediction's tightness.** "What would it look like if it went differently?" If the person can engage with the alternative, the prediction is loosening and the realist stance is dissolving. If they cannot, the prediction is identity-level and more work is needed.

**Bring back ownership.** Once the prediction has loosened, the question becomes "and how do you want to be in this, given that the outcome is actually still open?" See [[take-the-adventure-stance]] and [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]].

## Joe's Words

"There is a, you're seeing reality for what it is, but there's also an ownership of, 'oh, this is how I'm going to be in it.'" -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

"The reality in it is you have to face what's true. So you can not face what's true with negativity: 'oh my God, we're fucked, this is never gonna go well.' That's not true. 'This is gonna be great, it's gonna be awesome, we're gonna do fantastic.' That's not true." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

Both "we're fucked" and "we're going to be great" are not-true because both fix the outcome. The realist who says "this is just not going to work" is in the same not-true space as the optimist who says "this is going to work out." The not-true-ness is the fixing of the outcome, regardless of which direction it is fixed in.

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- A CEO who has decided the company will fail and is "just being a realist" about it
- A person in a relationship who has decided it will end and is "facing facts"
- A person in a difficult creative project who has decided the work will not be good enough
- A leader who is providing "honest assessments" that are actually fixed predictions
- A team that has "given up hope" in the language of maturity
- A person who has stopped trying because "you have to be realistic"
