# Don't Should Yourself Into Welcoming Others' Anger

## The Anti-Pattern

Hearing the principle "love your own anger and you can be with others' anger," a person concludes: "I should be good with other people's anger." They override their actual current capacity and try to be present with anger they cannot yet hold. The nervous system goes into freeze. They respond in ways they did not want to respond. They reinforce the very pattern they were trying to break.

"I think it's most dangerous if someone's like, okay, I should be good with other people's anger. You shouldn't." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

## Why It Fails

The capacity to welcome others' anger develops as a byproduct of loving your own anger. It is not a skill to be applied through will. Trying to apply it through will puts the person in a position where their system cannot meet what is in front of it, and then bad things happen.

"You either are or you're not. And if you're not, take care of yourself. And if you are, great, lean in." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The capacity is binary in the moment: either present, in which case lean in; or not present, in which case take care of yourself. Trying to bridge the gap with should is the failure mode.

The mechanism of the failure:

"If your system goes into freeze, if you are unable to maintain love, draw a boundary, remove yourself, and learn. And learn to love your own anger, and it'll all work out." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

When the system goes into freeze, the welcoming is performed but not real. The other person feels the inauthenticity. The freeze accumulates as held charge. The person who shoulded themselves into the position ends up worse than if they had simply left.

## The Specific Risk

The risk Brett names and Joe confirms:

"The shoulding oneself to be okay with someone else's anger could easily put you in a position where you're putting yourself in a position to receive more anger than your nervous system can handle. Which will then make you respond in ways that you didn't want to respond." -- Brett Kistler, The Wisdom of Anger

"Correct. That's right." -- Joe Hudson

The pattern: should -> override of capacity -> nervous system overwhelm -> reactive response -> regret. The reactive response is exactly what the person was trying to avoid by being good with the anger.

## What Joe Says Instead

The instruction is single-pointed: focus on loving your own anger. The capacity to be with others' anger arrives as the byproduct.

"Just worry about loving your own anger and all the rest of it will take care of itself." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The reframe is critical: the person's job is not to love other people's anger. The person's job is to love themselves. From there, the response to others' anger emerges naturally.

"It's not your job to love anybody's anger. It's your job to love yourself, all the aspects of you." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

And when capacity is not present, the response is to draw a boundary and remove yourself. Without shame about not having the capacity. Without performing capacity that is not real.

## How to Tell If You Are Doing It

The signal is the should. Any time the thought "I should be okay with this anger" appears, the person has already left the territory of natural capacity and entered the territory of forced performance. The should itself is the diagnostic.

Other signals:
- Bracing while staying in the room.
- The body wanting to leave but the mind overriding it.
- Reasoning about how being present is the right thing to do, rather than actually being present.
- A subtle internal accounting of what a good person would do.

When these are present, the move is to leave. The lesson is not about the other person; the lesson is about loving your own anger so that next time the capacity is actually present.

## What Joe's Words Are Actually Saying

The phrase "love your own anger and all the rest of it will take care of itself" can be misread as a prescription with results. It is closer to a description of how the system works. The capacity to be with others' anger is not produced by trying. It is produced by the underlying state.

This is the same structure as [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]: the work is not to improve the capacity; the work is to know yourself, and the capacity follows.

## In Coaching

When a person asks "how do I get better at being with my partner's anger?" the path is not to teach techniques for receiving anger. The path is to ask whether they are loving their own anger. The latter, sustained, produces the former.

When a person describes putting themselves in situations where they are repeatedly overwhelmed by another's anger, the question is what should is keeping them there. The cleanest answer is usually a boundary and time away from the relationship to do their own anger work.

When a person reports failed attempts to be present with anger, validate the failure. The capacity was not there. The attempt was a should. Next time: leave, do the work on your own, return when capacity has actually grown.

## Tone

Firm and gentle. Joe is protective of the person who might try this. The danger is real and he names it as the most dangerous misuse of the teaching. The protection is from the listener themselves.
