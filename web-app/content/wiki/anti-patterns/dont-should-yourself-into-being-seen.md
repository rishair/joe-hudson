# Don't Should-Yourself Into Being Seen

## The Anti-Pattern

The person recognizes their fear-of-being-seen pattern and concludes the fix is to push harder. They start telling themselves: *I should really be seen. I should really go have the hard conversations. I should really be more vulnerable. I should really show up more.*

The force-frame is the anti-pattern. The shoulds are the same mechanism that produced the original shame, now aimed at the very behavior that was supposed to dissolve the shame. The person is using shame to address shame. The system gets louder, not quieter.

The person may successfully push themselves into being seen once or twice on the force of the shoulds. The pattern returns, because the shaming voice that did the pushing is the same voice that produces the freeze. The voice has not gone away; it has just temporarily been pointed at "be vulnerable now" instead of "stay invisible now."

## Why It Fails

Three reasons the should-frame fails for this specific work:

**Shame perpetuates the behavior shame targets.** Shame is the engine that holds bad habits in place. See [[shame-drives-behavior]]. When the shame is aimed at the freezing/hiding, it adds to the shame layer that produced the freezing. The freezing intensifies because its fuel has been added to, not subtracted from.

**The internal critic is the [[the-eye-of-sauron]].** The fear of being seen is partly the fear of an external critical eye. The internal should-frame is the same critical eye now operating from inside. Adding the internal critic to the situation does not reduce the felt sense of being watched and judged; it amplifies it. The person now has the imagined external critic *and* the actual internal critic running simultaneously.

**Vulnerability done from should is not vulnerability.** True vulnerability is being in your truth even when scared. See [[vulnerability]]. Being-seen behaviors performed from should are not being-in-your-truth; they are performing being-seen as a strategy to escape the shame about not being seen enough. The strategy has the wrong source. The other person can usually feel the difference even if they cannot name it. The performed vulnerability lands as managed, and the contact the person was hoping for does not arrive.

"They're like — if you beat yourself up, then you are like the Eye of Sauron turned on yourself. So any way in which you are putting that same criticism on yourself — to undo that is going to be incredibly useful for this pattern. Because that self-criticism is the shame." — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen*

## What Joe Says Instead

Joe's correction is to refuse the should-frame entirely and replace it with the love-frame. The same direction (showing up more, being more seen, having more hard conversations) coming from a different source.

"What I would say is I would point back to the beginning, which is: what do you need to take care of yourself? What do you need to open to your heart, to yourself and to other people? Because that framing prevents it from being — I should do this, I should do that, I should do this other thing. Because there's a self-care, there's a love, there's an expansion in that feeling. There's an opening up to yourself and to the other person. That can't coexist with: you should do this, what's wrong with you, da da da da." — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen*

The reframe shifts the question from "what behavior should I perform" to "what do I need to take care of myself." The shift produces:

- A felt-sense direction rather than a performance instruction
- Behaviors that emerge from openness rather than from force
- The capacity to also acknowledge what the original hiding-pattern gave (safety, comfort) instead of attacking it
- Movement that lasts because it does not require continuous self-attack to maintain

The companion move is to acknowledge what the hiding-pattern was *for*. The person did not hide for no reason. The hiding gave them safety, comfort, protection in environments where being seen would have been costly. Honoring that — even thanking the pattern for what it gave — is part of the work. See [[honor-what-the-coping-strategy-gave-you]] for the related principle.

"Addressing this pattern isn't about: I've been doing it wrong all this time, I've been hiding, and I should be getting out there and getting seen." — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen*

## The Diagnostic for the Anti-Pattern

The AI coach should watch for this anti-pattern when the person:

- Uses the word "should" in describing what they want to change
- Reports they have been trying to force themselves to speak up and it works for a week then collapses
- Beats themselves up for freezing in a specific past situation and now reports being more frozen, not less
- Asks for advice on how to "just be vulnerable" or "just speak up"
- Reports they have made multiple lists of vulnerable behaviors to perform
- Talks about vulnerability as a discipline rather than as a state

When any of these signals are present, the coaching path needs to refuse the should-frame before any actual move can be offered. Otherwise, even the right move (open the heart, do the exposure, ask the proud-question) becomes another should and contributes to the problem.

## What the Anti-Pattern Is Not

This anti-pattern is not the same as healthy commitment. A person can decide they want to practice being seen more and structure their week around exposures. The difference is the source: a commitment from openness ("I want this for myself") is different from a should from shame ("I'm pathetic for hiding and I have to fix it now"). The behaviors may look identical from outside. The internal source is what matters for whether the practice produces shift or collapse.

This anti-pattern is also not a recommendation against effort. Doing the work takes effort. The effort is not the issue. The shaming engine driving the effort is the issue. Effort sourced in love does not produce the same wear as effort sourced in shame.

This is also not a prohibition against ever noticing one's own behavior. The person can absolutely notice they froze, notice they hid, notice they did not say the thing. Notice-and-release is different from notice-and-attack. See [[notice-where-you-shame-yourself-be-gentle]] for the related practice.
