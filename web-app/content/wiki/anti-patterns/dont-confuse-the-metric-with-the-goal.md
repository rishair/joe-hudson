# Don't Confuse the Metric With the Goal

## The Anti-Pattern

A goal is what you want. A metric is one instrument that tells you whether you're getting there. The two are different. The anti-pattern is letting the metric become the goal -- pursuing the metric as if hitting it is the actual point.

The metric, designed as a proxy for the real outcome, was a partial signal. When pursued as the goal, the proxy gets optimized while the real outcome stays untouched. The metric goes up. The thing the metric was supposed to be measuring does not.

This anti-pattern operates at every scale: in personal life, in relationships, in business, in spiritual practice. The form changes; the structure stays the same. Wherever there is a real want and a measurable proxy for it, the proxy can be confused for the want, and the work can drift into proxy-optimization.

## The Examples Joe Names

**Love and Instagram followers.** "The goal is I want to be loved. The metric is how many people are my friends on Instagram. And the metric becomes a goal, and then you're not loved."

The friend count rises. The followers grow. The likes accumulate. None of it is love. The thing being tracked is not the thing being wanted. Optimizing the tracked thing does not produce the wanted thing. The person feels increasingly seen by strangers and increasingly unloved by anyone close.

**Being a good husband and the moment-to-moment mood of the wife.** "Or is my wife happy with me right now? ... Every moment. ... Yeah, that's the metric. The metrics don't tell me if I've hit the goal."

The goal is being a good husband, a partner in a thriving marriage, a person his wife trusts with her depth. The metric -- "is my wife happy with me right now?" -- is a proxy that fires every moment. When the proxy becomes the goal, the husband starts managing his wife's mood. The mood improves intermittently. The marriage doesn't.

The actual goal often requires actions that temporarily worsen the metric: saying the hard thing, refusing to caretake, being honest in a moment when honesty disrupts the mood. The husband optimizing the metric will not do these things. The husband who knows the metric is just a metric can.

## Why It Fails

The metric was designed to be a proxy. The designer (the coach, the founder, the parent, the self) chose the metric because it was measurable and because it was correlated with the real outcome. The correlation was always partial. The metric was useful precisely because it was simpler than the outcome.

When the metric becomes the goal, three things happen:

1. **The work optimizes for the metric.** Effort flows where measurement is. Things that move the metric get done. Things that don't move the metric get dropped.

2. **The proxy decouples from the outcome.** The aspects of the metric that were correlated with the outcome get exhausted; the aspects that were not correlated (or were anti-correlated) get amplified. The metric continues to rise; the outcome stagnates or reverses.

3. **The internal experience hollows out.** The person feels the gap between the metric reading and the actual experience of the outcome. Instagram followers rise; loneliness deepens. The wife smiles more often; the husband knows the marriage is empty.

This is the structural form of Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Joe's framing is less academic and more direct: the metric reports on the driving, the metric is not the destination, and treating it as the destination produces the predictable hollowing-out.

## What This Looks Like in Common Domains

**Money.** Goal: security, freedom, impact. Metric: dollars in the account. When the dollars become the goal, the person earns more and feels less secure. See [[money-as-surrogate]] and [[same-scarcity-story-regardless-of-wealth]].

**Recognition.** Goal: belonging, being seen, mattering. Metric: followers, likes, awards, titles. When the metric becomes the goal, the person accumulates recognition and feels less seen.

**Body.** Goal: health, vitality, feeling at home in the body. Metric: weight on the scale. When the metric becomes the goal, the person loses weight and feels worse in their body.

**Productivity.** Goal: meaningful work that matters. Metric: hours worked, items shipped, tasks completed. When the metric becomes the goal, the person produces more and feels less meaningful.

**Relationship.** Goal: depth, intimacy, mutual gratitude. Metric: arguments avoided, partner's surface mood. When the metric becomes the goal, conflict drops and intimacy drops with it.

**Spirituality.** Goal: presence, awareness, love. Metric: hours of meditation, books read, retreats attended. When the metric becomes the goal, the practice becomes another scorecard. See [[dont-use-measurement-to-beat-yourself-up]].

## What Joe Says Instead

The metric belongs on the dashboard. The dashboard tells you how you're driving. The dashboard does not tell you where to drive. The destination is the goal. The goal is what the metric was a proxy for in the first place.

The corrective move when metric-as-goal is detected:

1. Name what the actual goal is. Not the metric. The thing the metric was supposed to be measuring. ("I want to be loved." "I want a deeply intimate marriage." "I want to be at home in my body.")

2. Notice that the metric was a proxy for that goal. The proxy was useful as an instrument and is dangerous as a target.

3. Ask whether the metric is still well-correlated with the goal. Often the proxy has decoupled. Sometimes the metric needs to be replaced with one that tracks the actual goal better.

4. Hold the metric as the dashboard reading. Look at it for information. Do not optimize it. Optimize the driving.

See [[principles-goals-metrics]] for the framework that organizes the three layers cleanly. See [[track-process-metrics-not-outcomes]] for the related principle about which metrics to design when designing them deliberately.

## When This Anti-Pattern Is Most Active

- Whenever a goal is set with a quantitative number ("$10M revenue," "lose 20 pounds," "100,000 followers"). The number is almost certainly a metric, not the goal. The goal is what the number is for.
- Whenever a goal has been "achieved" but the felt outcome did not arrive. The metric was hit; the goal wasn't, because the goal was something other than the metric.
- Whenever the work has become about managing the measurement. The optimization is happening at the metric layer instead of at the work layer.
- Whenever the person reports feeling worse the more they hit the target. The metric is rising and the actual goal is moving away.
