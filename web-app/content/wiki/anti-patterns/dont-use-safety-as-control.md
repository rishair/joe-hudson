# Don't Use Safety as a Form of Control

## The Anti-Pattern

Saying "I don't feel safe" with the implicit or explicit demand that others change their behavior. The structure: name your felt state in a way that places responsibility on others to fix it, rather than naming what you would want or owning what you would need to address it.

The word "safety" carries enough moral weight in current discourse that this demand often goes unchallenged. The mechanism is manipulation in the costume of vulnerability.

"People, especially in this, in the modern culture, they use safety as a form of control. So they say, I don't feel safe, and then that means other people need to change their behavior. So that's a whole nother level of dysfunction that can happen within that word of safety." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## Why It Fails

Safety used as control is a category violation of what the word is for. Safety is meant to name a state. When it is used to control, it becomes a demand. Demands disguised as states are unanswerable because the speaker is asking for behavior change while claiming to only be reporting an experience.

The team that operates under this dynamic learns to read the safety claim as a power move. Trust corrodes. The speaker stops being heard. Genuine safety concerns lose their weight because the word has been worn out as a control mechanism.

This is the parallel pattern to [[vulnerability-as-control]]. Both take a real, important phenomenon and weaponize it. Both produce the opposite of what they appear to want.

## The Mechanism Underneath: The Abused Becomes the Abuser

Why does someone use safety as a control move? Usually because the move pattern they know best is control. Safety-as-control is a specific instance of a larger pattern: the abused, in moving toward power, become the abusers of those around them.

"The abused becomes the abuser. It seems like it's a natural evolution for people to move out of an abuse cycle. You'll see it in countries, countries that got deeply abused then start abusing. And you'll see it in kids who were abused often abuse their kids, or kids that are bullied at home bully at school. As somebody is feeling disempowered, they think the way to get to empowerment is to control other people. And then they use what I would call it topping from the bottom. They use their victimhood as a way to dominate other people." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

Safety language is a particularly effective vehicle for this because it carries enough cultural weight to make the demand morally unanswerable. The disempowered person, looking for a way to obtain power, finds in the word "safety" a tool that cannot easily be refused without making the speaker look unsafe themselves.

This does not mean every claim of unsafety is manipulation. It means that the manipulation, when it happens, tends to find this particular language because the language has been culturally pre-loaded to win arguments. See [[aggressive-victim-stance]] for the broader concept.

The real exit is not more control of the other but [[empowered-vs-power|empowerment]]: being yourself despite the consequences. The cycle ends when one party stops trying to win the power game and starts being empowered.

## What Joe Says Instead

Use [[trust-vs-safety|trust]] as the organizing word. Trust resists weaponization in a way safety does not. You cannot demand trust the way you can demand safety; you can only become more trustworthy or articulate what would make you more trusting.

When the actual underlying request is for behavior change, name it directly. "I want X" or "I need Y" is harder to say than "I don't feel safe," but it is also harder to weaponize and easier to engage with. See [[wants-vs-partiality]].

When the actual underlying experience is the body registering threat, address it with specificity. What specifically would help? What is the structural element that needs to change? Specific requests are answerable; diffuse safety claims are not.

## Joe's Words

"It's very hard to control somebody out of trust. You need to be more trustworthy by X, Y, and Z usually is a pretty solid, like, yeah, you've stolen from me and I can't trust you. So this is gonna end the relationship, or you need to figure out how to convince me to trust you again. So it's harder to manipulate other people with trust." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The implied contrast: it is easy to manipulate other people with safety. Use the word that resists manipulation.

## Boundary Conditions

This anti-pattern is not "never say you don't feel safe." Sometimes you do not feel safe. Sometimes the right response is to name it and ask for what would help. The anti-pattern is the specific use of the word as a demand that places the responsibility on others while preserving the appearance of just reporting an experience.

A useful test: would the same content land differently if you said "I want" or "I need" instead of "I don't feel safe"? If yes, you are using safety as control. If no, you are reporting a state and the listener can engage with it as a state.
