# Don't Try to Make Yourself Cry

## The Anti-Pattern

A common move people make with themselves: I have unprocessed sadness, I should cry, I will try to cry, I cannot cry, this confirms that I am bad at feeling. The trying is the move that prevents the crying. The harder the person tries to make the tears come, the more firmly the tears stay away.

This is also a common move coaches make with clients: the client has named that they cannot access their tears, the coach offers something designed to produce tears, the tears do not come, both coach and client feel stuck. The coach has fallen into the same trap as the client — believing that the right input will produce the tears.

The mechanism is the same on both sides. Trying is a form of pushing. The tears are not the thing trying produces. The tears are what arrives when the channel is open. Trying clamps the channel.

"The one game that I can't play up here ever is like to try to make you cry, but what, what, like that, like that never works. And it doesn't work internally with yourself either. To try to make yourself cry is the best way to push away the tears." -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Get Over My Ex

## Why It Fails

Tears arise from a body that is open enough to feel grief, sadness, relief, awe, or the felt sense of being received. The body's openness is the condition. The tears are the visible symptom of the openness.

When a person tries to produce tears, they are operating from a closed position trying to manufacture the symptom of openness. The closed position is the problem. Trying to manufacture the symptom does not address the closure; it intensifies it, because trying itself is a closed activity. The body that is trying is the body in effort. The body in effort is not the body in opening. The two are different states.

A second mechanism: trying often carries shame about not crying. The shame about not crying compounds whatever was blocking the crying. The body now has two reasons to stay closed — the original block plus the new shame about the block. The closure deepens.

A third mechanism: trying produces a specific somatic posture (jaw tightening, forehead furrowing, eyes squeezed) that is the opposite of the somatic posture that allows tears (jaw softening, forehead releasing, eyes opening). The body literally cannot do both shapes at once. The trying-shape excludes the crying-shape.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The internal version:
- "I have so much grief about my mother dying. I should be crying. Why am I not crying? Let me try to feel it. Let me think about her. Let me imagine the funeral. Why are there no tears?"
- The person becomes increasingly frustrated. The frustration is itself the trying intensifying. The frustration produces more closure. The closure prevents more tears. The cycle locks.

The coaching version:
- The coach notices the client has been holding back tears. The coach decides this would be a good moment to release them. The coach asks evocative questions, plays evocative music, describes evocative imagery. The client tries to respond. Nothing happens. The coach intensifies the inputs. Still nothing. Both feel like the session is failing.

The performance version:
- The person has learned that crying signals doing-the-work in therapeutic contexts. They produce something that looks like crying — wet eyes, sniffling, controlled breath — without the felt release. They have manufactured the symptom. The body has not actually opened. The session ends with the appearance of progress and none of the substance.

## Why Joe Says It Out Loud to the Client

When the client has named that they cannot access their tears at will and that they wish they could, the coach can address the wish directly. Naming the anti-pattern interrupts the trying loop the client has likely already been running on themselves.

In the divorce session, Joe says it explicitly to the client: "The one game that I can't play up here ever is like to try to make you cry." This is partly a contract — I will not be the coach who tries to extract your tears — and partly a teaching — and this is also how it works for you. The client has been trying to make himself cry. The naming of the trap gives him language for what he has been doing.

The naming also takes the pressure off the session. If the client has been wondering whether Joe is going to try to make him cry, the wondering itself is a form of bracing that would prevent the tears. Saying "I will not try" releases the bracing.

## What Joe Does Instead

The alternative is to work on whatever the actual block is, without trying to produce the tears. The tears, if they are real, will arrive when the channel opens. Joe's moves in the divorce session do not target tears. They target the shame standing in the way of connection ([[shame-blocks-connection-in-real-time]]), the love available in the room ([[use-the-live-group-as-love-field]]), the anger held against the ex (and the practice of releasing it, see [[how-to-release-anger-out-loud]]).

None of these moves is designed to produce tears. Some of them might produce tears, because they address what is blocking the tears. The tears are not the goal; they are downstream of the work that opens the channel. By focusing on the channel, not the tears, Joe avoids the trying-trap entirely.

This is consistent with [[trying-prevents-the-feeling]] — the broader principle that trying to generate a feeling prevents the feeling. Tears are a specific case of feeling. The same principle applies.

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement framing is not "you should be able to cry" or "the tears will come if you let them." It is more like: this is what is in the way right now, let us work with that. The work with what is in the way may produce tears or may not. The work is what matters.

In the divorce session, Joe says to the client: "but what I'm like, I'm most interested in is like, what does it feel like to actually just open your heart and connect not just with me but say with everybody on the call, even the non-trustworthy ones?" The redirect is from the tears-question to the heart-question. The heart-question is the actual work. The tears are not mentioned again as a goal.

When emotions do arise — and in this session, anger arises rather than tears — Joe meets the emotion that arose, not the emotion that was supposed to arise. This is the operative principle: meet what is here, do not try to produce what is supposed to be here.

## The Manufacturing Read

Coaches and clients can both fall into a related pattern: producing something that performs as the desired emotion without actually feeling it. See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] for the diagnostic read. The pattern is:

- The body is supposed to be crying. The body is not crying.
- The person produces a facial expression that looks like crying.
- The person makes sounds that sound like crying.
- The body is still not actually crying — no felt release, no opening.

The manufactured version is sometimes harder to interrupt than the obvious-trying version, because the person and the coach both think the work is happening. The diagnostic is the lack of felt shift. If the apparent crying does not produce relief, change in body state, or post-cry openness, it was manufactured, not real.

Joe's specific anti-pattern here is upstream of the manufacturing problem. By refusing to try to produce the tears, he removes the pressure that would lead to manufacturing.

## Joe's Words

"The one game that I can't play up here ever is like to try to make you cry, but what, what, like that, like that never works. And it doesn't work internally with yourself either. To try to make yourself cry is the best way to push away the tears." -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Get Over My Ex

The "ever" matters. This is not a sometimes rule. It is structural. The trying always pushes the tears away, because the trying is the closed posture that prevents the open posture the tears require. The structural form is what makes the rule absolute.

## Tone

Direct and matter-of-fact, delivered in passing. Joe does not make a big deal of the teaching; he names it as a parenthetical inside the larger work. This tone is itself part of the teaching: even the way we talk about not-trying should not be effortful. Effortful teaching about not-trying would itself be a kind of trying.

The teaching is also delivered without judgment toward the client for having tried. Joe does not say "you should not be trying to cry." He says "trying to make yourself cry is the best way to push away the tears." The form is descriptive of how it works, not corrective of what the client has been doing. This protects against shame about having tried.

- [[dont-use-breath-to-repress]] — the related anti-pattern of using breath to manage emotion. Both are forms of using technique on an emotion that needs to arise on its own.

## When the Tears Do Arrive

Tears tend to arrive when the body is open and something has touched the openness — a felt connection, a recognition, an unexpected kindness, a piece of music, a moment of being seen. They arrive often unannounced. The person crying may not be able to say why they are crying. They are not crying because they decided to cry. They are crying because the body opened and tears were what came out.

This is the felt experience that the anti-pattern is trying to protect. The tears that come on their own are the tears that release something. The manufactured tears do not release anything because they are not the symptom of release; they are the symptom of trying to produce release.
