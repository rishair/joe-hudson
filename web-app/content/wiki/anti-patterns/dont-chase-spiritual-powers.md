# Don't Chase the Powers That Arise on the Path

## The Anti-Pattern

The work of self-knowing produces side effects that look like powers. Reading people. Knowing what they are feeling. Sensing what is happening in their body before they name it. Picking up the room without needing it explained. Anticipating what someone will say. These capacities are real. They arise as byproducts of sustained self-inquiry.

The anti-pattern is to make these capacities the target. To start practices oriented toward acquiring them. To value them. To use them to distinguish oneself. To pursue them as a route to a more sophisticated form of being someone special.

"My experience is that if you go after trying to figure those skills out, it can be incredibly destructive. It can be like chasing money. It can be like chasing sex. It, it can really destroy somebody. It can create a much more solid form of ego." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

## Why It Fails

Three structural reasons.

**First: the capacities arose as byproducts of self-knowing, not as outcomes of practices targeted at them.** Joe is explicit that his ability to look at someone's body and read what emotions they are holding and how they were raised was not learned by trying to learn it. It came as a byproduct of going through all that emotional material in himself. The capacity is a downstream consequence of a particular kind of inner work. Pursuing the capacity directly skips the inner work, which means the capacity does not actually arise -- or if some surface version of it does, it is brittle and unreliable.

"I did this form of breathwork for like, uh, 7 years or so... when I was 2, 3 years into it, I could look at somebody's body and know what emotions they were holding and like how they were raised. Not because anybody said, this arc means critical parent and this means like, I'm not playing the available mother... It was because I actually just went through all that stuff in myself." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

**Second: the chasing of the capacity reinforces the very ego the work was meant to dissolve.** The chaser wants to be the one who has the power. The wanting requires an I that has it. The I that has it grows more solid the more powers it accumulates. The trajectory is the opposite of the trajectory the work was meant to be on. The person becomes more of a someone, not less.

**Third: powers acquired without the self-knowing that should accompany them are dangerous.** A person who can read others without the self-knowing to handle what they read will use the reading for manipulation, status, control, or seduction. The capacity is real; what is missing is the person responsible enough to wield it. Joe and Dr. K both speak to this directly in the conversation. The moment you have something really powerful, life can trap you in a vice-like grip you did not anticipate. "Mo money, mo problems," in Dr. K's compression -- the same recognition applied to spiritual power.

## The Zen Parable Joe Cites

Joe references a Zen story to make the point. A young monk walks down the path with an old monk. The young monk reports: "Oh, I can start to know what people are thinking." The old monk responds: "What does that have to do with awakening?" The young monk continues: "Oh, I can read people's thoughts." Same response: "What does that have to do with awakening?" The young monk keeps reporting capacities; the old monk keeps deflecting them. The parable lands the structural point: these capacities are not awakening. They can be effects of it. They can be effects of practices not oriented toward awakening at all. They are distinct from the thing.

The parable's deflection ("what does that have to do with awakening?") is the operational corrective. When the capacity arises, do not make it the thing. Notice it. Let it be what it is. Keep doing the actual work.

## Dr. K's Patanjali Reference

In the same conversation, Dr. K cites Patanjali's Yoga Sutras. The third of the four chapters is the list of siddhis -- the powers that arise on the path. Telepathy, time travel, knowing the moment of your death, taking the size of an atom or the universe. Patanjali simply lists them: by the way, if you do these practices, you will develop these capacities. He neither glorifies them nor warns against them. He names them as predictable consequences of the practices.

The bare listing is the appropriate stance. The capacities arise. They are not the goal. They are not the proof of arrival. They are not evidence of specialness. They are just what happens when certain things have been done for long enough. The chaser of the capacities has missed what the practices were actually for.

Patanjali's third chapter is also short. The other three chapters (on what an asana is, what meditation is, the broader practice) are much longer. The structure of the text itself reflects the relative weight: the powers get one short chapter; the actual work gets the rest.

## What to Do When the Capacities Arise

The recommendation is positive, not just negative. When the capacities arise, the right relationship is to let them arise naturally as byproducts of the deepening self-knowing. Notice them. Do not deny them. Do not amplify them. Do not market them. Do not build them into an identity.

Joe's framing of his own work clarifies what the right use looks like. When he sits with someone and reads what they are feeling in their body, he is using the capacity, but not as a power he is wielding. He is using it as a continuation of his own self-knowing, which now includes deep empathic access to others as a natural consequence. The use is not separable from the inner work. The inner work continues; the capacity is part of how the inner work shows up in service.

"That's the way I recommend doing it because that's the thing that actually gets you to the freedom that you're really looking for beyond the power of being able to do it." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

The freedom is the actual thing. The power is incidental.

## What Joe Says Instead

The instruction is not "deny that the capacities exist" or "avoid contact with anyone who has them." The instruction is:

- Do the self-knowing work for its own sake.
- Let whatever arises arise.
- Notice without amplifying.
- Use the capacities in service when they are useful; do not build them into who you are.
- Be suspicious of any practice or teacher whose primary appeal is the acquisition of powers.
- Be suspicious of any felt sense in yourself that the powers prove anything about you.

## The Specific Trap for the Coach

The anti-pattern has a particular form for someone who is becoming a coach or facilitator. The empathic capacity to read clients is real, useful, and dangerous in the same proportions. A coach who has acquired some surface version of the capacity without the underlying self-knowing will use it to impress, to differentiate, to build authority that the client cannot verify. The client experiences being seen; the coach experiences being skilled; what is actually happening is the coach using the capacity to construct their own role.

The corrective for the coach-in-training: keep the focus on the inner work. Do not pursue practices that target the capacity directly. Notice when the capacity arises and let it serve the client without being narrated. If the temptation to narrate it ("I just sensed that," "I picked up something") is strong, that is the signal that the capacity is being used to build identity rather than to be in service.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is excited about the powers they are developing through practice.
- When a teacher or program is foregrounding the acquisition of intuitive or empathic abilities.
- When someone is looking for a shortcut to specialness via spiritual practice.
- When the AI coach is in conversation with someone whose inner work is being structured around the acquisition of capacities.
- When [[spiritual-narcissism]] is the operative risk.
