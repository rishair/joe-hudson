# Don't Use Other People's Anger as the Signal That You've Done Wrong

## The Anti-Pattern

The speaker uses another person's anger as the primary signal that they have done something wrong. When the other person is angry, the speaker concludes that they did something to cause the anger and that the something was wrong. The reaction has become the verdict. The speaker has outsourced their internal compass to the other person's emotional state.

The pattern operates in real time. Partner gets angry → the speaker's body immediately initiates the "what did I do wrong?" loop → the speaker cycles through possibilities until they land on something they could be responsible for → the something becomes the explanation for the partner's anger → the speaker accepts the explanation and the responsibility. The whole sequence is automatic and often invisible to the speaker.

"Somehow in your mind, the way you know if you've done something wrong is if somebody else is angry." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Her 2-year Relationship Paralysis Dissipate

The pattern is so automatic that the speaker treats it as accurate perception rather than as a measurement system. Asked how they know they did something wrong, they would point to the partner's anger as obvious evidence. The question of whether the anger was caused by what they did, or by the partner's own state, or by the configuration the speaker did not create, has been collapsed into the binary: they are angry, therefore I did wrong.

## Why It Fails

The pattern fails because the other person's anger is not, in fact, reliable evidence of the speaker's wrongdoing. Anger arises in another person for many reasons unrelated to the speaker:

- The other person is having an old emotional response to something that is not actually about the present situation. See [[trauma-is-present-experienced-as-past]].
- The other person is moving an emotion they have been carrying that has nothing to do with the speaker.
- The other person is angry about a real grievance, but the grievance is about the configuration of the relationship rather than about anything the speaker did wrong.
- The other person is angry as a strategy — sometimes conscious, sometimes not — to extract a behavior from the speaker.
- The other person is angry because they are scared of an emotion they cannot feel. See [[triggers-dissolve-when-you-can-feel-the-emotion-others-fear]].

In all of these cases, treating the anger as a signal of the speaker's wrongdoing produces wrong conclusions. The speaker accepts responsibility for things they did not do, fails to surface the actual dynamic, and feeds the pattern that produced the anger in the first place. The measurement system is broken because the signal it relies on is not actually what it claims to measure.

The pattern also produces a structural disempowerment of the speaker. The speaker's sense of their own moral standing is now downstream of the other person's mood. If the other person is happy, the speaker is okay; if the other person is angry, the speaker is bad. The speaker no longer has direct access to their own assessment of their behavior. Their moral life is being lived by proxy.

There is also a corruption of the relationship. When the speaker treats partner-anger as evidence of speaker-wrongdoing, the partner is implicitly being given a tool that can produce any speaker-behavior the partner wants. Get angry, the speaker will adjust. This is sometimes used consciously (then it crosses into manipulation, see [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]]), but more often it operates unconsciously on both sides — the partner notices that anger produces behavior change, the speaker notices that compliance reduces anger, and the configuration locks in. Neither person is actually living their own moral life inside the configuration.

## Where the Pattern Comes From

The pattern is usually childhood-installed. A child whose caregiver had unpredictable reactions — often related to alcoholism, mental illness, mood disorder, untreated trauma, or just inconsistent presence — learns that the caregiver's state is the most consequential variable in their environment. The child also learns that the caregiver's state is sometimes about something the child did and sometimes about something the child did not do, but the child cannot tell the difference in the moment.

The competent move under these conditions is to treat all caregiver-anger as if it were about the child. This produces some false positives (the child takes responsibility for caregiver-anger that was not theirs), but it minimizes the false negatives (the child fails to adjust when adjustment was needed). The false-positive cost is lower in the moment than the false-negative cost. The child learns to default to the false-positive. Over time, the default hardens into an automatic measurement system: caregiver-anger means I did something wrong.

The system is also installed in less extreme conditions. A parent whose love was conditional on the child performing in a certain way. A family where one member's mood set the temperature of the household. A school environment where being correctly read by a teacher determined the safety of the next year. Any setting in which the child's well-being was substantially dependent on managing another person's emotional state can install the pattern. See [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]] and [[what-did-i-do-wrong-response-to-aggression]] for the related developmental mechanisms.

## The Logical Necessity of the Pattern (Why It Has to Be Installed)

The pattern looks irrational from outside ("why would you treat their anger as your wrongness?") but is structurally necessary for the child. The deeper layer is the alternative the child cannot afford to entertain:

"As a kid, if your parents got angry at you, the assumption is, I've done something wrong. 'Cause the alternative is that my parents are fucking crazy and I have no one to take care of me. So as a kid, you can't think that. So you think I've done something wrong." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

The child has two ways to interpret caregiver-anger:
- **Option A**: I did something wrong.
- **Option B**: My caregiver is unsafe, irrational, or out of control, and I am alone in a world where the person responsible for me cannot be trusted to be okay.

Option B is intolerable for the child. It collapses the entire structure of safety the child depends on. Even when option B is the more accurate reading, the child's nervous system rejects it because option B has no survivable form. There is no operational move available to the child who concludes "my caregiver is crazy and I have no one."

Option A, by contrast, is operationally tractable. If the child concludes "I did something wrong," the child has a move: figure out what they did, fix it, prevent it next time. The conclusion preserves agency, preserves the relationship with the caregiver, and preserves the child's working model of the world as a place where adjustment is possible.

The wrongness assumption is therefore not a cognitive error. It is a survival adaptation. The cost is the child's accurate self-perception. The benefit is the preserved ability to function in the household.

By adulthood, the original conditions have usually passed. The other people in the speaker's life no longer have the power the original caregiver had. But the measurement system still runs. The body does not know the conditions have changed. Any time another person is angry, the system fires.

The implication for the corrective: it is not enough to tell the adult that their wrongness reading is inaccurate. The adult has to be brought into contact with the option-B reality that the child could not afford. The corrective work is partly about making it safe in adulthood to perceive what could not be perceived in childhood — that other people are sometimes irrational, that their anger is sometimes about their own state, that the receiver is not the cause of the caregiver's weather. This recognition was withheld from the child for protective reasons. In adulthood, the withholding is no longer protective; it is the source of the symptom.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is twofold. First, name the measurement system itself as a measurement system. The speaker has been treating the other person's anger as direct perception of their own wrongness; the naming reveals that it is actually a learned mechanism that converts one piece of information (other-anger) into another (self-wrongness). Once the conversion is visible, it can be disrupted.

Second, install a different cognitive move for the in-the-moment situation: when the other person is angry, the speaker's automatic thought becomes *this is about their trauma, not about me*. The substitution is not a denial of any actual contribution the speaker made. It is a refusal to treat the other-anger as direct evidence of self-wrongdoing. The speaker can still consider whether they did something, but the consideration is now a deliberate inquiry rather than an automatic conclusion.

In the source session, Joe installs the cognitive substitution via a live role-play:

"You're going to literally have the thought process that is, oh, this is about his trauma. This isn't about me. That's all you're going to think. Okay? Yeah, okay. Why can't you just decide if you're going to be with me or not? Why are you laughing and smiling? This is serious. I'm hurting over here. Why can't you decide to be with me or not? Yeah, so there is the real thing."

The role-play installs the new measurement system by giving the speaker the live experience of using it. The body learns that it can stay in itself while another person is angry, applying the trauma-lens thought as the cognitive anchor. See [[apply-trauma-lens-during-role-play]].

The deeper move is to install the speaker's own internal compass as the primary measurement system. The speaker checks whether they behaved with integrity by checking with themselves, not by reading the other person's reaction. This is the corrective for the underlying outsourcing. See [[chase-your-own-approval]] for the broader pattern of relocating the measurement system inside.

## Joe's Words

"What I want is for you to just — what I want is for you to look at me and you're going to think, and I'm going to say it, right? But you're going to look at, it's going to come from me, not from the boyfriend. You're going to look at me and you're going to literally have the thought process that is, oh, this is about his trauma. This isn't about me. That's all you're going to think." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Her 2-year Relationship Paralysis Dissipate

"Somehow in your mind, the way you know if you've done something wrong is if somebody else is angry... Why do we use that as a guide to what you fucking up? Let's do — let's make it real life for a second." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Her 2-year Relationship Paralysis Dissipate

The "what you fucking up" framing — bluntly phrased — names the function the system has been serving. The speaker has been using other-anger as their guide to where they are fucking up. The system has a job; the corrective replaces the system without leaving the job unfilled. The new measurement system is the speaker's own internal compass plus the trauma-lens default for interpreting other-anger.

## What This Anti-Pattern Is Not

This is not [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] in reverse. That anti-pattern concerns the doer-of-anger; this concerns the receiver-of-anger. Both are part of the larger configuration around anger in relationships, but they apply to different roles.

This is not a license to ignore feedback from others. The corrective is not "their anger is always about them, never about you." The corrective is to stop treating their anger as *automatic* evidence of your wrongdoing. There may be times when you did something the other person is rightly angry about; the corrective preserves the capacity to assess that, by removing the automaticity that prevents assessment.

This is not the same as denying that the speaker contributes to the partner's pain. The speaker may be doing things that hurt the partner. The corrective does not erase that. It separates the question "am I contributing to the pain?" from the signal "the partner is angry." The two questions get conflated in the broken measurement system; the corrective separates them so each can be answered on its own terms.

This is also not [[gaslighting]]. The pattern is not the partner manipulating the speaker; it is the speaker manipulating their own perception via the inherited measurement system. The partner may or may not be doing anything that contributes. The pattern is in the speaker's apparatus.
