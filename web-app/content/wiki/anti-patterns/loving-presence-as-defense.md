# Don't Use 'Loving Presence' as Defense or Disappearance

## The Anti-Pattern

A partner has learned that loving presence is the right response to the other partner's anger or hurt. They have learned the language ("I'm just here with you," "I'm holding space," "I'm loving you in this") and the posture (calm body, soft eyes, gentle voice). In the middle of a fight, they perform the form. Internally, they are absent.

The performing partner is not in their body, not feeling what is happening, not bringing themselves to the encounter. They are doing a curated presence that looks like loving presence. The other partner experiences this not as love but as the most maddening kind of non-meeting.

"I've had experiences in fights where I'm like, okay, I'm going to be a loving presence and my partner will be like angry or express something and I'll just be like, yeah, I'm here with you in it. But there's some way that I'm actually not. There's some way that I'm actually like, in that place, I'm sort of a vacuous, amorphous, non-entity, not meeting the person with my, with my feelings, with my truth. And I'm just, I'm here to hold space. And it can be like the most frustrating thing." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight Well

The anti-pattern is the substitution of the spiritual-looking posture for the embodied being-there. The partner who is in this anti-pattern sometimes does not know they are. They believe they are doing the loving presence move. The body and the partner's response tell a different story.

## Why It Fails

Loving presence is a state, not a posture. The state is embodied: present in your own body, feeling what is happening between you, not managing your reactions, not curating your output. The posture (soft voice, calm body) is a downstream marker of the state. The posture without the state is the anti-pattern.

The receiving partner detects the difference somatically. Genuine loving presence produces a sense of being met. Performed loving presence produces a sense of being alone in the room with someone pretending to be with you. The second is, in some ways, worse than open conflict, because it removes even the friction that would tell you the other person is engaged.

"It can be maddening to be in a relationship with somebody and really be showing up vulnerably, which is what anger really can be, and feel unmet in it when someone says, yeah, I'm just here and I'm loving you. And there's a difference between when that actually is real." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight Well

The receiving partner often cannot articulate what is wrong. They know the form is right. They are being told "I am loving you." But the loving is not landing. The escalation that follows is the body's protest at the non-meeting. The performing partner reads the escalation as confirmation that they should be more loving, and the cycle compounds.

## The Tell: What's Happening in the Body

The diagnostic is internal, not external. The performing partner can ask:

- Am I in my body, or above it?
- Am I feeling what is happening, or noticing it from a distance?
- Are my muscles tense in the holding of the "calm" posture?
- Am I smiling while the inside of me is bracing?
- Am I fully allowing the heartbreak of what is happening, or am I bypassing it?

Joe accepts Brett's framing and amplifies the distinction:

"To love somebody is a very embodied thing. It's literally to feel. We've all had that moment with our wife or our husband or our boyfriend and girlfriend where you look across the table and you're just like, oh, whoa, just fucking love you. That's how it feels. That's how it feels when they're raging. It is an embodied feeling. It is a pleasurable feeling that's happening and you can't force yourself into it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The reference state is the spontaneous I-fucking-love-you that arises across the table. That state cannot be manufactured. It can be set up for (by tending the soil, by doing the trauma work, by learning to fight well), but it cannot be produced on demand. The performed loving presence is the attempt to produce it on demand. The attempt fails because the state does not respond to that kind of production.

## The Test Joe Offers: Are You Deciding If It's Loving, or Is Your Partner?

A specific guardrail for the performing partner who is unsure:

"If you're in loving presence, it's you to decide if you're in loving presence, or if it's, it's not them to decide it. It's, you know, do you feel like you're in your body or not? And that's the difference. Are you in your body or have you left your body?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The carve-out is important. The partner cannot definitively rule on whether you are in loving presence -- partners sometimes resist genuine loving presence (see "The Real Loving Presence Provokes Testing" below). But the test of whether you yourself are in loving presence is bodily: are you in your body, or have you left it?

If you are in your body, what you are doing is loving presence even if the partner is rejecting it. If you have left your body, what you are doing is performing the posture even if the partner is being soothed by it. The internal test trumps the external response.

## The Real Loving Presence Provokes Testing

Joe adds an important nuance. Genuine loving presence often provokes the partner to test it. This is not a sign that the loving presence is failing.

"There is a natural response to loving presences to bite it, to get like, to test it. Are you really gonna fucking love me? Fuck you. You're not gonna fucking love me. Fuck you. Like, they say something different, but that's really what's happening. They're poking and testing to see if that love's real, if they're really gonna be loved." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

This is the partner doing trauma work. The partner is checking whether this is going to turn out like mom and dad, like the others, like every previous experience where loving presence eventually failed. The testing is the trauma's due diligence. It is part of the work.

The performing partner can read the testing as evidence that they are not loving well enough and double down on the performance. The genuinely loving partner can recognize the testing for what it is and stay present through it.

The diagnostic on which one is happening is, again, internal. If the partner who is "loving" is in their body and the response is testing, the testing is normal trauma material. If the partner who is "loving" has left their body, the response is the body's protest at the non-meeting, regardless of how it sounds.

The two situations can look identical from the outside. Inside the performing partner, they are different. This is why the internal test (am I in my body?) is the only reliable diagnostic.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is not "stop trying to be loving." It is to do the work that lets the loving be real.

"What you can do is go into your trauma, respect each other, learn how to fight. And then some trust builds. Something happens where you realize, oh, this is trauma. This isn't us. This isn't personal. And then it's a blessing, it's a gift when that happens. It's not something you can particularly control. You can just set the conditions for it. You can just tend your soil." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The tending-the-soil framing is key. The genuine loving presence is the harvest. The work is the tending. The performing partner is trying to harvest before the soil has been tended. The corrective is to go back to the soil work: face your own trauma, learn to fight, build the trust between you, and let the loving presence arise as the byproduct.

In the meantime, when the loving presence is not arising spontaneously, the move is to bring what is actually present rather than perform what is not. Sometimes that means bringing your own truth, your own anger, your own fear, your own difficulty. The principle from [[hold-back-from-relationship-is-what-needs-healing]] applies: what you are holding back is what needs to come, even if what is coming is not the curated loving presence.

See [[view-can-show-up-with-anger]] for the specific case where what looks like fighting is actually genuine VIEW. The clean state is not always quiet.

## What Looks Like This But Is Not

Genuine quiet presence in a fight, when the body is in the room and the heart is open, is not this anti-pattern. The signal is the embodiment, not the volume. A quiet presence that is felt by the partner is doing the work even if it looks similar from the outside.

A partner choosing to stay regulated rather than escalate is not this anti-pattern. The regulation is a choice from the body's actual state, not a performance over a different state.

A partner using a brief reflection or naming move to slow the fight ("I'm here. Tell me what's happening for you.") is not this anti-pattern. The naming-and-asking move is engaged contact, even if it sounds calm.

The anti-pattern is specifically the curated calm-loving posture that substitutes for actual being-there. The marker is the partner's experience of non-meeting, paired with the performing partner's internal sense of having left their body.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The partner who is "holding space" reports feeling exhausted, dissociated, or absent during fights
- The partner being "held" reports feeling more alone in the fight, not less
- The holding partner cannot tell what they are feeling, only what they are doing
- The fight escalates after the loving presence is performed, not before
- The partner being "held" says something like "I want you to actually be here with me"
- The holding partner notices they are smiling or speaking softly while their body is tight or absent
- The holding partner uses VIEW language as the standard response to any difficulty regardless of internal state
