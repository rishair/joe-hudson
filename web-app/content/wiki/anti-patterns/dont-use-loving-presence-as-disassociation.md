# Don't Use 'Loving Presence' as a Way to Leave Your Body

## The Anti-Pattern

Someone has learned that the right move during a partner's difficult emotion is to be a loving presence. They take the instruction literally and produce the behavior: stay calm, hold space, say "I'm here with you," do not match the emotion. The behavior is performed correctly. What is actually happening: the partner has left their body. They are not present. They are managing the situation through the performance of presence.

Brett names the pattern precisely in describing his own experience of doing this with his partner:

"I'll just be like, 'Yeah, I'm here with you in it.' But there's some way that I'm actually not, there's some way that I'm actually like, in that place, I'm sort of vacuous, amorphous, non-entity, not meeting the person with my, with my feelings, with my truth. And I'm just, I'm here to hold space. And it can be like the most frustrating thing." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

The partner who is angry, hurt, or vulnerable is meeting a wall dressed as presence. The wall does not register as presence to them. It registers as abandonment in a more elaborate costume.

## Why It Fails

The body does not lie. The body of the "loving presence" performer is tense, withdrawn, slightly checked out. The partner reads the body, not the words. The mismatch between "I'm here" and the actual felt absence is what produces the maddening quality of the experience.

"There's a difference between when that actually is real. It seems like there's like a difference between when someone's like, 'I'm going to practice being a loving presence,' and my muscles are all tense and I'm holding them really tightly and I'm not feeling what I'm feeling right now, and I'm smiling versus like fully allowing the heartbreak." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

The maddening quality is structural. The partner cannot point at what is wrong. The other partner is doing the thing they were supposed to do. But the meeting that the angry or hurt partner needed is not happening. Anger, especially, is often vulnerability looking for someone real to land on. The vacuous "I'm here" gives the anger nothing to make contact with.

This is the same failure mode as [[view-as-technique]] but in a specific form: the technique is "being loving and present" and the missing state is actual embodied feeling. Without the embodiment, the technique is a sophisticated form of leaving the room while staying in it.

## The Diagnostic: Are You In Your Body or Out of It?

Joe gives the precise marker. The diagnostic is not whether the other person believes you are present. The diagnostic is your own body.

"It's you to decide if you're in loving presence or if it's, it's not them to decide it. Do you feel like you're in your body or not? And that's the difference. Are you in your body or have you left your body? Are you fully in your head saying I'm loving presence? Are you tranced out? And then you're being present in a way, but not embodied. And so it's a very love -- to love somebody is a very embodied thing. It's literally to feel." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The four signals that you have left the body:

1. **You are saying "I'm being a loving presence" in your head.** The internal narration is the marker that the activity is being managed rather than being lived.
2. **Your muscles are tense.** Loving presence in the body is open, soft, breathing. Tight muscles are the body holding a defended position while the mouth says otherwise.
3. **You are smiling but not feeling.** The smile is the cover for the absence of the felt state.
4. **You are tranced out.** A subtle dissociation that registers internally as "calm" but is actually distance.

If any of these are present, the "loving presence" is the disassociation in costume. The work is not to perform the loving presence better. The work is to come back into the body and find the actual feeling.

## What Real Loving Presence Looks Like

The state being pointed at is not the absence of feeling. It is the presence of a specific feeling: the felt-sense recognition of love for the other person, alive in the body, even while difficult emotion is also present.

"To love somebody is a very embodied thing. It's literally to feel. We've all had that moment with our wife or husband or boyfriend, girlfriend, where you look across the table and you're just like, 'Oh, whoa, just fucking love you.' That's how it feels. That's how it feels when they're raging. That's how it's like, it is an embodied feeling. It is a pleasurable feeling that's happening and you can't force yourself into it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The reference state is the spontaneous flood of love that arises sometimes -- the "oh, I just fucking love you" moment that arrives unbidden. That is the embodied state. Real loving presence during a partner's anger is the same state, sustained while the partner is in difficulty. It is not a technique. It is a state that becomes available through practice but cannot be performed in its absence.

The implication: when the state is not present, the move is not to fake it. The move is to recognize that the state is not present, do whatever you need to do to come back to the body, and let the real meeting happen even if it is messier than the loving-presence performance would be.

## The Counter-Move: Meeting Anger With Your Own Felt State

Brett describes the counter-experience: a moment when he stopped trying to be the calm container and got in his partner's face with his own anger. Not anger at her. Anger as expression. Boundaried, measured, real.

"I got like right back in her face with my anger. And it was like, it was measured, it wasn't at her, it was actually just like being in my anger. And she felt met in it. And a whole lot changed in the relationship." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

Joe affirms the move with care. The risk of the example being misread as "yell at your partner" is acknowledged. The actual move was something else: the [[view]] state (the felt-sense unconditional love) showed up alongside the anger. The anger was the form. The view was the substance. The partner felt met because the substance was real, not because the form matched.

"There's your whole body, but the unconditional love that is view showed up with the anger. And it's, it's not -- I've seen that. It's rare, but I've seen it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This is the deeper teaching the anti-pattern points to. What the partner is looking for is real contact. Sometimes real contact looks like quiet loving presence. Sometimes it looks like getting in their face with your own anger. The form is secondary; the embodied-felt-love is what makes either form land.

## What Joe Recommends Instead

The recommendation has two parts.

**Part one: stop trying to perform loving presence when the state is not there.** The performance produces the vacuous experience that is worse than honest absence. If you cannot meet the partner from love right now, naming that is more functional than faking it.

**Part two: do the work that produces the actual state.** Loving presence is the result of a specific kind of practice -- the practice of bringing your body, your feelings, and your unconditional love into the room with the difficulty. This is not a technique you apply in the moment. It is a capacity built over time.

"What you can do is go into your trauma, respect each other, learn how to fight, and then some trust builds. Something happens where you realize, oh, this is trauma. This isn't us. This isn't personal. This is just weeding a garden and I can enjoy weeding a garden or I can fucking hate it. It's my choice. And then, some, once it, it's, it's a blessing. It's a gift. When that happens, it's not something you can particularly control. You can just set the conditions for it. You can just tend your soil." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The state arises in conditions you can set up but cannot force. The disassociation-disguised-as-presence is what happens when you try to produce the state directly. Setting the conditions and letting the state emerge is the actual move.

## The Carve-Out: Tests of the Loving Presence

Joe adds a complication. Sometimes the partner who is supposedly being a loving presence is actually present, and the angry partner is testing whether the love is real. The anger is not asking for matched anger; it is poking to see if the loving presence is what it claims to be.

"There is a natural response to loving presences to bite it, to test it. Are you really gonna fucking love me? Fuck you. You're not gonna fucking love me. Fuck you. Like they say something different, but that's really what's happening there. They're poking and testing to see if that love's real." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This means the partner reading "you're not really here" cannot always be trusted as the diagnostic. The diagnostic is internal: are you actually in your body? If yes, the partner is testing the love and the move is to keep being it. If no, the partner is correctly reading the absence and the move is to come back.
