# Don't Manage Your Kids Into Moving Their Anger

## The Anti-Pattern

A parent who has learned the value of emotional movement watches their child not moving an emotion and decides to help. The decision contains the contamination. The wanting-them-to-move-it is itself an agenda. The agenda is what the kid is responding to, and the kid will resist it.

The behavior looks reasonable on the outside: "I want to teach my kid to move their anger." The actual structure underneath: "I have an agenda for my kid's emotional state." The kid feels the agenda. The kid does what kids do with parental agendas — they refuse.

When the parent who asked this question (Vlada) submitted "my kids are not interested in getting anger out, so how do I help them move it?", Joe's immediate response named the trap before answering the question:

"Oh, I love your agenda." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

The "I love your agenda" is doing several things at once. It is naming that the question has an agenda buried inside it. It is meeting the agenda with warmth (no shame, no correction). And it is signaling that the agenda is the part of the question Joe wants to address first.

## Why It Fails

Two reasons.

**1. Kids respond to felt state, not content.** A parent saying "I want you to move your anger" while the felt state is "I need you to do this so I can feel like a good parent" sends a mixed signal. The words are an invitation. The state is a demand. The kid reads the state. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]].

The kid is not stupid. The kid is sophisticated about parental energy because their nervous system has been calibrated by it since birth. They feel the wanting-them-to underneath the giving-them-permission-to.

**2. The contradictory teaching when the anger is inconvenient.** Many parents who want their kids to move anger are happy to have that happen in a designated context (the pillow, the punching bag, a play-therapy framing). They are not happy to have it happen when the kid is angry at them or at a boundary they are drawing. The lesson the kid actually learns is: "Anger is acceptable when it does not impact my parents, and not acceptable when it does."

"It's like, I want you to go move your anger. But when you're, like, angry because this thing isn't, you're not getting this thing or this thing you don't want, that I want you to stifle. So it's like, yeah, don't get angry at me now, but hey, let's go over here and then go beat the heck out of something. And which is really telling the kid it's not okay to get angry." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

Brett's sharpening of the same dynamic:

"Or it's okay to get angry, but you can't let it transform you and create a different situation for you because my way is still the highway." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3

The teaching the kid receives is not "move your anger." It is "move your anger in ways that don't change the parent's power." That teaching is correctly identified as a managed version of the same prohibition the parent grew up with, and is resisted on the same grounds.

## What Joe Says Instead

Three moves replace the management.

**1. Model it.** The parent moves their own emotions where the kid can witness. Not performatively — actually. The kid sees what it looks like for an adult to be angry and not be destroyed, to be angry and come back to love. The witnessing is the teaching. See [[model-emotional-movement-for-your-kids]].

"The first one. Yeah, great. You, you see the agenda, which is lovely. All people want to move, like it's natural for us to want to move emotions. There's two things you can do. The first one is show them what it's like, like just ask them to witness you as you're moving your anger." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

**2. Draw boundaries so the anger arises naturally.** When the parent draws a boundary the kid does not like, the kid will become angry. This is the field condition the anger needs. The parent's job is then to celebrate the anger that arrives. "Yeah, fucking right on. I love your anger." See [[draw-boundaries-with-kids-to-let-anger-arise]].

"The second thing that is useful is to draw boundaries with them. If you tell them they can't have something, they will get angry, and then you're like, oh great, that's anger, that's fantastic." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

"With our kids, like there was a big learning that when they were angry because of a boundary that was being drawn... that was the time to go like, yeah, fucking right on. I love your anger. That was the time to really be excited about it." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

**3. With older kids, teach explicitly through conversation, not management.** Once a kid is around 10, the management approach guarantees resistance. The alternative is to have an explicit conversation about what the parent has learned and to invite the kid into exploration together.

"At some age, like once the kids get to about 10 years old, then I would be having a conversation with them that says something to the effect of, hey, it's really — this is what I've learned about getting angry. It's really important to me. My job is to teach you this as a parent. And so how do we explore this in a way that you get to learn? And I feel like I've done a good job and you've gotten to learn everything from me, like what works for you, which is a very different thing than trying to manage them into an experience, which they will resist." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The shift from management to invitation matters. The management treats the kid as a project to be moved through. The invitation treats them as a person being asked to explore something the parent cares about.

## What Joe Says When the Kid Insists They're Not Angry

A specific moment Joe describes: the kid is clearly angry, the parent is celebrating it ("I love your anger"), and the kid responds "I'm not angry." The parent's move:

"Yeah, you're not angry. Stop saying that." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 (in his daughters' voice, mocking the dynamic)

The light, non-attached agreement. Not arguing that the kid is angry. Not pushing. Just letting the kid have the position they need to have while the underlying anger continues to be welcomed in the field. The point is not to win the argument about whether they are angry. The point is that the parent's love for the anger does not depend on the kid's acknowledgment.

## Joe's Words

"Oh, I love your agenda." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 (the opening response that names the trap before answering)

"All people want to move emotions. It's natural for us to want to move emotions... If nobody's been trained by society, you know, you look at little kids who aren't trained, they want their emotions moving. That's just our nature." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

"The bigger part that you're saying there, and that is in the question, is that there's a management to it. And that management, kids resist it. No matter what you're doing with your kid, the more you're trying to manage it, the more you resist it." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

## What This Is a Specific Case Of

This is the parenting version of [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]]: the moment you make an emotional skill into a should, into a project, into a thing to be enforced, the practice converts into its opposite. Anger management has the same shape whether the manager is the self or the parent: trying to install the practice from the outside undoes the practice.

It is also a specific case of [[teach-teenagers-to-be-their-own-authority]]: with kids past a certain age, the parent shifts from authority to coach. The management role is structurally over. The invitation role replaces it.

## Tone

Joe delivers the "I love your agenda" response with warmth and humor. There is no shaming of the parent for having the agenda — having it is normal. The shift is to make the agenda visible so it stops running the show. The tone throughout is "this is great information, here's where it gets in the way, here's what works better."

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a parent asks how to get their kid to move an emotion.
- When a parent reports their kid is resistant to emotion-work invitations.
- When the family has a designated "emotion zone" (the pillow, the punching bag) but anger at the parents is treated as disrespect.
- When the parent has learned the practice and now wants to install it in the kid.

## What Joe Does Instead

- Models the practice. The parent does their own emotional work where the kid can witness it.
- Welcomes the anger that arises naturally when boundaries are drawn.
- For older kids, has explicit conversations as equals rather than managing.
- Drops the agenda and trusts the kid's nature.
