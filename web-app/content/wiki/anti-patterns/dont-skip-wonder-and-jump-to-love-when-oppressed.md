# Don't Skip Wonder and Jump to Love When You Feel Oppressed

## The Anti-Pattern

A person hears the teaching: love changes things. Love the stress, love the parent, love the feeling. Their internalization: "Okay, I heard you. I'm going to skip the wonder and the experiments and the gradual approach. I'm just going to whack — straight to love."

It does not work. Love refuses to land on something you feel oppressed by, when the force is willed rather than allowed.

"Okay, Joe, I heard you say blah, blah, blah, blah. I'm just gonna skip all this wonder and curiosity thing and I'm gonna whack, I'm gonna just go to love stress. But love doesn't work like that." -- Joe Hudson, Your Brain Has A Stress Reset Button

This is the second of two backwaters Joe names in the [[separation-wonder-reality-experiments-love]] arc. The first backwater (see [[dont-love-stress-to-make-it-go-away]]) happens once the practice is working. This second backwater happens before it has started — the attempt to bypass the arc entirely.

## Why It Fails

Love comes easily to babies because there is no oppression in the relationship. You can immediately love a baby. There is nothing to resist, nothing pressing on you, nothing to defend against. The love simply arrives.

"Love works like that with babies, thank goodness. You just immediately love a baby. But if you feel oppressed by something, you — it's very, very, very difficult to love. You can't just force yourself into loving it." -- Joe Hudson, Your Brain Has A Stress Reset Button

For something you feel oppressed by, the love that would be deployed in the bypass is not love. It is the simulation of love — a willed posture that the body and the heart refuse to back.

The body's refusal is not a failure of will or virtue. It is information. The body knows that this thing is, right now, something it feels oppressed by. The will-of-love asks the body to override that. The body cannot override it through willing.

## The Path Instead

The path is the prior stages. You have to be mad at it for a while. You have to resist it. You have to allow wonder to open up. Then experiments. Then the love arrives on its own.

"You have to be mad at it for a while. You have to resist it, like I said a little while ago, and then you have to allow that wonder to open up." -- Joe Hudson, Your Brain Has A Stress Reset Button

The mad and the resistance are not failures. They are stages. They are part of what makes the love that eventually arrives a felt thing rather than a performed thing. Skipping them produces no love — only the performance of love over an unaddressed substrate of resistance.

See [[separation-wonder-reality-experiments-love]] for the full arc.

## The Same Pattern in Anger Work

The structural parallel is [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]]. In anger work, the bypass is: "they had their reasons; I should be compassionate." In the love-the-thing work, the bypass is: "love changes things; I'll just love it." Both bypasses operate by deploying the destination-state before the felt material has moved.

The order matters in both cases:
- Anger work: anger → release → clarity → real compassion arrives.
- Love-the-thing work: separation → wonder → reality → experiments → real love arrives.

In both, skipping to the destination produces a brittle version that does not produce the shift the person came for. See [[negative-emotions-precede-positive]] for the principle that organizes both cases.

## How to Catch It

The signature:

- The "love" is being talked about rather than felt.
- The person is using love-language ("I just need to love it") but the body is tight.
- The "loving" is producing strain, not opening.
- There is a sense of forcing or willing the love into place.
- The person reports doing the work but nothing is shifting.
- When asked what they actually feel about the thing right now, the answer is some flavor of "I'm trying to love it" rather than any actual felt-thing.

When this signature is present, the move is not to push harder on the love. The move is to back up to the stage they are actually at. Probably wonder. Possibly mad-at-it. Sometimes separation. The work is to be where they actually are, not where they think they should be.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is not to abandon love as the eventual destination. The corrective is to honor the arc.

For someone trying to skip-to-love with their stress:
- Start with the separation. Get enough sleep, take the vacation, create distance.
- Then wonder. Where is the stress in the body? What thoughts amplify it? What is its purpose?
- Then reality. How did this come to be? What is it actually?
- Then experiments. Try welcoming it. Try resisting it. Notice what each produces.
- The love arrives.

For someone trying to skip-to-love with a parent who hurt them:
- Honor the anger and the separation.
- Allow wonder when it shows up — about the parent's own childhood, about how this came to be.
- Find out what was actually real, beyond the preconceived notions.
- Try different ways of being with the parent. Boundaries are part of the experiments.
- The love that eventually arrives is not the love you tried to force. It is a different thing.

See [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]] for the parallel forgiveness arc.

## What Looks Like This But Is Not

A person who has done the arc and reaches genuine love is not skipping. They are arriving.

A person who has done some of the arc and reports felt-love appearing in flashes is also not skipping. They are in the experiments stage and the love is starting to show up as part of what is being noticed.

A person who has done significant work and now uses the word "love" to describe their actual state with the thing is also not skipping — even if the word arrives early in a conversation. The test is whether the word maps to a felt-thing or to a willed posture.

The bypass is specifically the move where the person has not done the stages and is using "love" as a way to skip them.

## Joe's Words

"It's really hard to just force the love, especially if you feel oppressed." -- Joe Hudson, Your Brain Has A Stress Reset Button

"In general, the main component of loving something is to just let it unfold through wonder and exploration and experimentation, and just really try to understand what's happening. And then that slowly turns into love through experimentation." -- Joe Hudson, Your Brain Has A Stress Reset Button

The teaching is not "don't love" — the teaching is "let love arrive through the arc that produces it."

## When Someone Experiences This

When a person reports that they have "tried to love their stress" and nothing happened.

When a person reports "I want to forgive my mother but I can't" — and they are trying to forgive without having done the prior anger and wonder stages.

When a person hears a teaching about loving an emotion and immediately tries to deploy it without the descent.

When the work has stalled and the stall is at the point of forcing love that the body refuses.
