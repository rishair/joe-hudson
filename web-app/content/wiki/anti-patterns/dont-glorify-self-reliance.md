# Don't Glorify Self-Reliance

## The Anti-Pattern

Treating self-reliance as a virtue, a culturally aspirational quality, or a developmental achievement reinforces the very pattern that produces the harm. The pedestal makes the wall sticky. The person who has built a self-reliant identity is now defended by both the original survival need and by the social reward for having that identity. The combination is much harder to unwind than the original pattern alone would be.

"So is the way that we put them in society, often put self-reliance on a pedestal. Look how self-reliant this person is." -- Brett Kistler, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

## Why It Fails

The teaching has two layers. The first is structural: per [[self-reliance-is-an-illusion]], self-reliance is not even a coherent thing to admire. The person being praised for being self-reliant is in fact relying on thousands of inputs they cannot see. The admiration mistakes hidden interdependence for independence and praises the hiding rather than the hidden truth.

The second layer is therapeutic. The person who has come to self-reliance through abandonment, neglect, or having to grow up too early carries a wound underneath the pattern. The pattern protected them. The protection was real and necessary. But the protection was also costly — see [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] for the cost catalogue. Praising the protection makes it harder for the protected person to let it go, because letting it go now means losing both the original function (which is fading anyway) and the social reward (which is current).

The leader who is admired for being able to hold it all has a strong incentive to keep holding it. The friend who is praised for never needing anything has a strong incentive to never need anything. The CEO who is celebrated for being the one who can do it has a strong incentive to keep being the one who does it. Each instance of admiration cements the wall.

The further damage: the people who admire the self-reliance also receive the implicit teaching that this is what they should aspire to. The pattern propagates by social transmission. The new generation absorbs the message that independence is virtue and dependence is weakness, and installs the same configuration in themselves.

## The Burning Man Example

The pedestaling of self-reliance is so culturally common that even communities organized around its opposite reproduce it. Brett offers the example:

"Even like a Burning Man principle is radical self-reliance, and yet also it's show up naked with no water and find that the playa provides, like somehow it's both of those things." -- Brett Kistler, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The contradiction is the diagnostic. The event teaches both "you must be radically self-reliant" and "the community will provide for you." The two cannot both be true; the second is closer to the actual experience; the first is the cultural pedestal that has been smuggled in. Even a community that lives out interdependence in practice cannot quite name interdependence as the value, because the cultural pedestal of self-reliance is too strong.

The same pattern shows up in many contexts. The startup ecosystem praises the founder who built it themselves while papering over the investors, employees, and infrastructure that made the building possible. The bootstrapping narrative valorizes the lone success while ignoring the support network. The cultural language is unable to articulate the actual structure because the pedestal has held the language in a particular shape.

## What Joe Says Instead

The repair is to name what the self-reliance is actually doing rather than to praise it as a virtue.

When the self-reliant person is in front of you:

- See the scared person, not the strong one. The self-reliance is the surface; the fear of helplessness is what built it. See [[self-reliance-pushes-people-away]] for the dynamic of being treated as a self-reliant figure and the cost that produces.
- Recognize the wound the pattern was built to protect. The wound deserves attention, not the protection.
- Notice that they are, in fact, not self-reliant. Trace one of the things they think they did alone and watch the web become visible.

When self-reliance is being praised in the culture:

- Name the underlying interdependence that the praise is missing.
- Notice that the praise is often coming from people who would benefit if the self-reliant person kept holding everything (the team that does not have to step up; the partner who does not have to show up; the society that does not have to provide a real safety net).
- Recognize that praising self-reliance is one of the ways a culture passes on the wound rather than healing it.

The corollary: when the work is done with a self-reliant person, the new identity that emerges is not "more self-reliant." It is more receiving, more interdependent, more willing to ask. The cultural language of growth has often valorized the wrong direction. The actual direction of growth for a self-reliant person is toward needing more, not toward needing less.

## The Misreading to Watch For

The anti-pattern is not "do not appreciate competence" or "do not value resilience." Resilience, resourcefulness, grit — all of these are real capacities and are worth recognizing. See [[self-reliance-pushes-people-away]] for the catalogue of what the pattern produced that is genuinely valuable.

The anti-pattern is the specific celebration of *not needing anyone* as the achievement. The capacity to do is one thing; the configuration of needing no one is another. The first is worth honoring. The second is the wall.

The distinction in practice: praising someone for their work is fine. Praising someone for not needing help with their work is the anti-pattern. The former honors what they did. The latter reinforces the protection against being helped, which is the configuration that needs to dissolve.

## Joe's Words

"That is part of what makes self-reliant people self-reliant, right? People look at that self-reliant person and they make them bad guys. Instead of seeing that they're scared." -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The same dynamic operates in reverse with praise. Looking at the self-reliant person and making them heroes also misses that they are scared. The hero treatment, like the villain treatment, prevents the underlying fear from being seen and met. Both projections protect the projector from having to see the person.

"You always know who the leader in the room is, like who the CEO is, because they're the ones that's handing out the compliments. Nobody looks to the CEO and says, wow, that was a really good job, Bob." -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The leader's self-reliance is sustained in part by being structurally exempt from receiving recognition. The team's failure to give the leader real acknowledgment is one of the ways the pedestal operates in daily practice. The repair: look at the leader, notice what they actually did, and tell them.
