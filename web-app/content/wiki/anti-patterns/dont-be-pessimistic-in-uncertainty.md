# Don't Be Pessimistic in Uncertain Times

## The Anti-Pattern

Faced with an uncertain situation, the person reaches for pessimism. "We're fucked." "This is never going to work." "It's all going to shit." "The world is falling apart." The pessimism feels like facing reality — clear-eyed, no-illusions, mature. It is not facing reality. It is a prediction of a fixed bad outcome dressed up as honesty.

The pessimism is felt as a small relief: at least nothing worse can happen than what is already being expected. The relief is purchased at the cost of producing — through the person's own state — much of the bad outcome the pessimism was predicting.

## Why It Fails

**Pessimism is its own mechanism.** The pessimistic stance runs the nervous system on cortisol. Cortisol shuts down the executive function that would have noticed the opportunity in the situation, would have made the small adjustment that changes the outcome, would have spotted the conversation that opens the door. The pessimist is not predicting the bad outcome. They are producing significant portions of it through their own dysregulation.

"I see people in the world today and they're just like, 'oh, with this and this, we're all fucked.' And I'm just like, yeah, you are. You are creating that reality." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The pessimist sees the bad outcome arrive and counts it as confirmation. The pessimist was, in part, the cause.

**Pessimism cannot be argued with.** Any contrary evidence is dismissed as naive. Any small win is reframed as a delay before the inevitable. The pessimism has built itself into a fortress that any evidence to the contrary actually strengthens.

**Pessimism produces the same problem as optimism.** Both pessimism and optimism collapse the uncertainty into a fixed outcome. Both refuse to face what is actually true (which is not-knowing). Both prevent the person from responding to the actual situation. The two stances appear opposite; they have the same structural flaw. See [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]].

**Pessimism is a way to not feel.** The doomsdaying often functions as a way to not feel the fear, grief, or helplessness that is actually here. Once the bad outcome has been "decided," the person does not have to keep feeling the uncertainty. The pessimism caps the unbearable feeling. The cap is the problem.

## Catastrophizing as the Body-Held Version

Pessimism in its sustained body-held form is catastrophizing. The mind produces the bad-outcome scene at the level of frame and language, the body braces, the emotion that would otherwise move through is held back. The loop runs because the body is asking the mind to do something the mind cannot do.

See [[catastrophizing-vs-visualizing]] for the full mechanism. The principle there is the operational counterpart of this anti-pattern. The intervention for pessimism is the same as the intervention for catastrophizing: bring the body in, let the underlying emotion move, stop running the head-only loop.

## What the Pessimism Is Defending Against

The pessimism is almost always a defense against an emotion that is here and that the person does not want to feel.

"There's almost guaranteed an emotional experience that you are resisting in this. So there's a, 'I don't want to be so out of control or uncertainty so much that I have to feel that grief or that fear or that anger.' So there's also that component of it typically that's at play. So that level that often can create depression is oftentimes the anger that you're not allowing yourself to feel." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The cure for pessimism is therefore not better thinking. It is the feeling of what the pessimism was preventing. See [[apply-view-to-fear]], [[emotional-inquiry]], and [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]].

## The Long-Term Pattern Check

A specific intervention for chronic pessimism: notice how long the catastrophic prediction has been running and how little of it has actually come true.

"You might want to notice that things have not gone horribly wrong, or the only thing that's gone horribly wrong has been generated by you thinking things are going to be horrible and go horribly wrong." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The pessimist has often been predicting catastrophe for years. The catastrophe has not arrived in the form predicted. What has arrived, often, is the smaller version of the bad outcome that the pessimism itself produced through dysregulation. The recognition of this gap is one of the moves that can break the loop.

This is the first move in the [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty]] practice.

## What to Do Instead

The intervention is not the opposite of pessimism (optimism — see [[dont-be-optimistic-in-uncertainty]]). It is the dropping of the prediction altogether.

- Notice the prediction has been made and notice the cost of the prediction (the cortisol, the contraction, the loss of agency).
- Notice what the prediction has been costing in lived life over months and years.
- Feel what the pessimism has been defending against — usually grief, fear, helplessness, or anger.
- Find the certainty that is actually here (see [[the-certainty-that-is-actually-here]]).
- Take the adventure stance (see [[take-the-adventure-stance]]).
- Ask "how do I want to be in this?" (see [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]]).

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- A person who doomscrolls compulsively and reports the world is falling apart
- A person who responds to every piece of news with "this is going to be a disaster"
- A person whose body language is permanently braced
- A person who has been predicting catastrophe in their work, relationship, or family for years
- A CEO who is in collapse-mode about market conditions
- A team that is being managed by fear
- A person who sounds like they are facing reality but is actually performing it
