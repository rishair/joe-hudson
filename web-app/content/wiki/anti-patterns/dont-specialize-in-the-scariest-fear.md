# Don't Specialize in the Scariest Fear (And Skip the Subtle Ones)

## The Anti-Pattern

A person who has worked extensively with one category of high-intensity fear -- BASE jumping, combat, public speaking to large audiences, surgery under pressure, betting the company -- can build an identity around being the person who faces the scariest things. The identity is honest: they really do face those things. But it becomes a cover for not facing the smaller fears that are actually shaping their daily life.

The structural move is: by repeatedly facing the most extreme version of a fear, the person becomes convinced they have faced fear in general. The smaller fears (the awkward conversation, the request that might be refused, the change of routine, the subtle relational tension) get systematically ignored. The person can even argue, correctly, that the small fears are objectively trivial compared to the ones they have made peace with. The argument is what protects the avoidance.

## How Brett Names It

Brett Kistler, who is a BASE jumper and has written extensively about welcoming fear in extreme sports, names this trap explicitly about himself:

"There's also a way that we can build an identity, me speaking as a BASE jumper here, of diving into the thing that's most scary. And then that can be something that becomes less free where, you know, I see a bunch of things in my life and one of the things is the most scary. And then I'm like, well, that's the one that I gotta dive into. And there can be a way that I become attached to being the identity that dives into certain kinds of fears. And then I'm actually ignoring a whole bunch of the rest of the fears." -- Brett Kistler, Life After Murder

The diagnostic Brett gives: "Why do I keep recreating all these worst case scenarios when I've done all this work on myself to accept the worst possible outcome. And it's like, oh wait, I've actually done all the work to accept that worst outcome. And I haven't accepted the subtle small fears of actually taking any different path than going into that one."

The recreation of worst-case scenarios is the signal that the extreme-fear practice has become its own avoidance. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] and [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]] for the related dynamics.

## Why It Looks Like Inner Work But Isn't

The extreme-fear practice has many of the surface markers of authentic inner work. The person:

- Demonstrably faces situations that most people would not
- Has clearly developed real capacities for handling fear under pressure
- Often has language for what they have done that matches the language of inner work
- Can describe the felt experience of facing fear with accuracy
- Has external evidence (jumps survived, deals closed, surgeries performed) that the work has been done

What is missing: the practice has not transferred to the parts of life where the fear is not dramatic. The subtle fears continue to shape behavior unmodified. The relational patterns continue. The avoidances of ordinary intimacy, ordinary asks, ordinary changes of plan continue. The drama of the extreme practice obscures the persistence of the subtle avoidances.

This is a domain-specific version of [[im-not-an-anxious-person]]. There, the denial is "I am not afraid." Here, the denial is "I have already done the fear work because look at what I do." Both prevent the actual work.

## How the Identity Reinforces Itself

Once the identity is in place, every confirmation strengthens it. The next jump, the next high-stakes situation, the next moment of acute fear-faced becomes evidence that the work is being done. The absence of evidence about the subtle fears does not register as absence. The person concludes from the strength of the dramatic evidence that the inner work in general is being done.

This is why the trap is so hard to see from inside. The person is not lying to themselves about facing extreme fears. They are mistaking that domain of work for the totality of fear work.

## The Specific Mechanism

Joe and Brett's diagnostic in the same conversation: "First of all, like the human brain and the body, like, we are, we are like some brilliant creatures and there are ways in which we can fool ourselves even by doing the right thing. Because it's like, I'm doing the right thing, right? But there's ways that we can focus so deep on that that we can ignore all the things that we're hiding from." -- Emile DeWeaver, agreeing with Brett

The mechanism: depth in one domain becomes the alibi for absence in another. The same body that has been brilliantly engineered to survive will engineer brilliant avoidances dressed as practice. The avoidance does not have to look like avoidance. It can look like exemplary commitment to growth.

## How to Diagnose It

Three questions that often surface the trap:

1. **What is the smallest, most ordinary thing in your life right now that has a slight charge of fear around it?** If the person cannot answer, or routes the answer to a domain that is not actually ordinary (e.g., "the next big deal"), the subtle-fear access is closed.

2. **When was the last time you asked for something interpersonally that felt vulnerable and small?** Not a large reveal. A small ask. The hand on the partner's shoulder. The honest "I noticed I felt off when you said that." If the answer is "I don't really do those," the practice has not transferred.

3. **What in your life are you avoiding that, if seen by an outside observer, would look like a fairly normal thing to do?** Often the answer is the subtle fear the extreme-fear practice has been masking.

## What Joe Says Instead

The work is to extend the felt skill of meeting fear to the domain where the fear is small enough that the person does not consider it fear. The skill is the same; the application is what changes. Not jumping off a bigger cliff. Saying the small awkward thing. Making the small ordinary ask. Noticing the small avoidance and turning toward it.

This often feels boring or beneath the person. The boredom is data. The boredom is the dragon of the subtle fear, dressed as preference. The actual practice is in the boring domain, not in the dramatic one.

See [[two-words-for-fear]] for the distinction between threat-fear (which the extreme-fear specialist has mastered) and growth-fear (which is what is being avoided in the subtle domain). The mastery of one does not produce the mastery of the other.

## Joe's Direct Words

This anti-pattern is named through the conversation between Brett and Emile in agreement, with Joe not contradicting and the framework Joe operates from supporting it. Joe's broader principle is the relevant authority:

"There are ways in which we can fool ourselves even by doing the right thing... we can focus so deep on that that we can ignore all the things that we're hiding from." -- (Emile DeWeaver, in agreement with Brett's framing, in dialogue with Joe)

The anti-pattern is named by Brett and corroborated by Emile within a conversation Joe is hosting and affirming. It is not from a Joe-only teaching, but it lives within Joe's framework.

## Related Anti-Patterns

This is distinct from [[dont-confuse-embracing-fear-with-inviting-danger]], which is about manufacturing scary situations to feel fear. Here, the situations are not manufactured -- they are real, often dangerous in a meaningful way. The trap is that the real practice in the dramatic domain becomes the cover for the absent practice in the subtle domain.

It is related to but distinct from [[im-not-an-anxious-person]]. There, the denial is total. Here, the denial is partial -- the person admits to fear in the extreme domain and denies its presence in the subtle one. The work to address it is similar in shape (find the actual unprocessed fear) but different in starting point (the person already speaks the language of fear-work; the trap is the assumption that the language guarantees the work).

It connects to [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] in Brett's specific diagnostic: by accepting the worst-case outcome, he was recreating it. The avoided subtle fears were the route by which the worst case kept coming back.

## Tone

The anti-pattern is named without scorn. The extreme-fear practice was real and produced real capacity. The trap is structural, not characterological. The person who falls into it is doing better work than most, and is one step away from the work that would actually open the rest.
