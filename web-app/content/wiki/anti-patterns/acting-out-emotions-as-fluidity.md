# Don't Confuse Acting Out Emotions with Emotional Fluidity

## The Anti-Pattern

A specific misreading of [[emotional-fluidity]]: the person hears that fluidity means feeling all their emotions and concludes this licenses any behavior the emotion seems to call for. The result is somebody who yells when angry, cries at people on demand, throws temper tantrums, or holds the people around them hostage with their emotional state -- and frames the whole pattern as "I'm being emotionally fluid" or "I'm in touch with my emotions" or "I'm just being authentic."

This is not fluidity. It is the opposite of fluidity. The emotions in this pattern are constricted, shame-ridden, and being deployed as instruments to control other people. The fluid form of the same emotions would look completely different.

"What could be happening in some people's minds when I say emotional fluidity, they might think like, oh, that means that I easily get angry at anybody and start yelling at them, or I easily cry at people, or I'm like wildly out of control of my anger... or I have temper tantrums or whatever. I hold people hostage with my emotional experience. That is not at all what I'm speaking about when I say emotional fluidity. In those cases, the emotions are very constricted." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Fluidity

## Why It Fails

Three reasons the acting-out pattern is the opposite of fluidity:

**1. The emotions are constricted, not flowing.** A person yelling at their partner is delivering a kinked version of anger. The kinked version is loud, performative, and aimed -- which is what happens when the underlying anger has been held back long enough to produce pressure. Fluid anger does not need a target. It moves through the body and resolves into clarity. See [[kinked-emotions]].

**2. Shame is layered around the emotions.** People who act out emotions are usually deeply ashamed of having those emotions. The acting-out is partially a discharge of the shame. The shame is what makes the discharge messy and aimed at others. A person who actually welcomed their anger would not need to dump it on someone else.

"In those cases, the emotions are very constricted. There's a lot of shame around having the emotions. There's a lot of trying to control other people with your emotions. None of that is welcoming your emotional experience." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Fluidity

**3. The emotions are being used to control other people.** The yelling, the crying-at, the tantrum -- each is operating as a behavior modification tool. The targeted person is supposed to back off, apologize, comply, or take care of the emoter. This is [[dont-do-emotion-at-people|emotion at people]], which is emotional abuse regardless of how authentic-feeling the emoter believes they are being. The "fluidity" framing makes the abuse harder to confront because it has been dressed in growth-work language.

## How the Misreading Happens

The misreading is enabled by a partial reading of [[you-dont-control-emotions-they-dont-control-you]]. The person hears the first half ("you do not get to choose what emotion you have") and concludes that whatever the emotion seems to want them to do is what they should do. They drop the second half ("emotions do not control you"). The result is full capture by each emotion as it arrives, dressed as authenticity.

The corrective is to restore the second half. Feeling the emotion does not require performing the action the emotion seems to demand. The fully felt anger of Gandhi did not require yelling at the British. The fluidity is in the feeling; the action is a separate operation, made from a more responsive place.

## What Welcoming Looks Like Instead

A person actually welcoming their anger:

- Feels the anger fully, in their own body
- Does not aim it at the person who triggered it without that person's consent
- Often releases the anger somewhere private first (the car, the woods, a pillow, a trusted friend who knows what is happening) -- see [[get-angry-in-the-car]]
- Returns to the conversation with the clarity that the released anger produced, without the dump
- Notices the emotion has moved, rather than the emotion has been performed

A person actually welcoming their sadness:

- Feels the sadness fully, in their own body
- Does not deploy it as a guilt-producing pressure on someone nearby
- Lets the body do what it needs to do: tears, slowness, quiet, curling up
- Receives whatever support is freely offered, without extracting it through performance

The difference between the welcoming version and the acting-out version is invisible from a particular angle: both involve the emotion being expressed. The visible distinguishing feature is who the expression is aimed at and what it is for. Welcoming aims at the body's release. Acting out aims at the other person's change.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is twofold. First, name that the acting-out is not fluidity but the opposite: constricted, shame-driven, control-seeking. Second, point at the actual fluidity work: feel the emotion all the way through, alone or with someone who has consented, and let the action that follows arise from the clarity rather than from the pressure.

The two are not even on a spectrum. They are different operations. The acting-out keeps the emotion in place (because it serves a function -- usually trying to make the other person respond a certain way -- and the emotion will keep getting recreated as long as the function is in play). The welcoming moves the emotion through (because the emotion's job is done; nothing keeps it in place).

See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] for the corrective practice. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for the bright line that distinguishes welcomed emotion from aimed emotion.

## The Distinguishing Question

The diagnostic question to apply: is the expression of this emotion aimed at the other person changing, or aimed at the body releasing? If the answer is "aimed at the other person changing," the practice has become control, not fluidity, no matter what the emoter calls it.

A second question: would you do this exact behavior alone in a room with no one present? If the volume, the words, or the gestures only happen with an audience, the practice is targeted, not fluid. Fluid release happens because the body needs it, not because the audience needs to see it.

## Tone

Firm. Joe is direct in correcting this misreading because it produces real harm when it is allowed to stand. The language of growth work can disguise emotional abuse, which makes the abuse harder for both the abuser and the target to confront. Naming the misreading is part of preventing the harm.
