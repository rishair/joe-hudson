# Don't Try to Live a Life Everyone Will Approve Of

## The Anti-Pattern

The person organizes their life around the question "will other people think I'm doing this right?" They pursue the goals that get approved of. They take the jobs that get approved of. They date the kinds of people who get approved of. The strategy is to build a life that will produce universal validation.

The strategy is structurally impossible. No one is universally visible. No one can produce universal approval. The chase is mathematically unwinnable. And the chase itself prevents the very seeing the person is chasing — because they are presenting a curated front rather than themselves.

## Why It Fails — Three Structural Reasons

### 1. No one can be universally seen

The first impossibility is perceptual. No human being can see another human being completely, at all times, across all situations. Not strangers, not friends, not partners, not the person themselves.

"Nobody anywhere is gonna think that you're living the perfect life at all times, let alone everybody thinking that you're living a life at all times that's perfect, because nobody can always see you. You can't even always see yourself, right?" -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The strategy presupposes a kind of comprehensive visibility that does not exist. The presupposition is a category error about how perception works. There is no observer in the universe who can see your entire life and rate it. The very idea of the rating requires an observer position that does not exist.

Even the people closest to you cannot do it. Joe names his own marriage of 26 years as evidence: he cannot always get his wife's approval. He never got the full approval of his parents.

"Having been married for 26 years, I can't even always get the approval of my wife. And I've definitely never gotten all the approval of my father or my mother." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

If 26 years of marriage cannot produce universal approval from the partner, then no amount of effort can produce universal approval from the world. The strategy is asking for what the universe does not have to give.

### 2. Disapproval has nothing to do with you

The second impossibility is causal. People disapprove of things for reasons that are about them, not about you. Their blood sugar. Their lack of coffee. Their last fight with their partner. Their childhood. Their paranoia about the day.

"There's always going to be somebody out there who's not going to be happy with what you're doing. And if that's the case, and you're looking at them to tell you, okay, you're good enough, then you're always going to feel unseen in that." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

"They criticize you for many reasons. Maybe their blood sugar is low. Maybe they haven't had enough coffee. Maybe they didn't exercise that day." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The person trying to get approval is trying to control variables they have no access to. The variables are inside the other person's nervous system, their day, their history. Even the perfectly-lived life will encounter disapproval from someone whose disapproval was determined by what they had for breakfast.

The strategy requires controlling for these variables, which is impossible, which means the strategy produces guaranteed failure regardless of how well it is executed.

### 3. The strategy hides you, which prevents being seen

The third impossibility is the deepest. The strategy of pursuing approval requires presenting what is acceptable rather than what is true. The person curates a front. The front is what gets seen — whether approved of or not. The actual person stays hidden behind the front.

Even when the front succeeds at producing approval, the approval lands on the front, not the person. The person, who never showed up, did not get seen. The felt experience is unseen-ness, even amid approval.

"What we do typically when we are in that position of not feeling seen is that we're not being ourselves. So we can't be seen. We're being somebody else to please people, which never works." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

"I can feel that I'm always presenting something, I'm always fronting so that people can't actually see what I am. They're just seeing my front." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

This is the deepest reason the strategy produces the very state it was trying to escape. The pursuit of approval requires the very behavior (hiding) that prevents the seeing the pursuit was supposed to produce. Even success on the strategy's own terms produces the felt failure the strategy was supposed to prevent.

See [[false-front]] for the developed concept article on the curated self. See [[you-cant-win-the-see-me-wont-be-seen-game]] for the developed naming of the structural unwinnability.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is to invert the central question. Instead of "do they approve?", "do I approve?" See [[chase-your-own-approval]] for the developed practice.

"It can only be found in those moments of, oh, for this moment, I am surprised by how well somebody sees me or how well I see myself. And it only happens when you're living a life where you're thinking, 'Oh, is this something that I approve of? Am I acting right now in a way that feels great to me?' And if I'm not, then what do I do to act in a way right now that feels great to me despite what the people who haven't had enough coffee or the people who've had bad upbringings or the people who are paranoid think?" -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

"If you want to feel seen, you have to be yourself. If you're not yourself, you can't be seen. Only some make-believe part of you can be seen." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The inversion does the work the original strategy could not. It puts the question where it can actually be answered (inside the person, by the person). It removes the dependence on variables (others' blood sugar, others' history) that cannot be controlled. It allows the person to show up rather than the curated front, which is the prerequisite for being seen at all.

The inversion is also the only honest move once the structural impossibility is recognized. The original strategy was always going to fail. Continuing it after recognizing this is a kind of self-deception. The inversion is what becomes available when the self-deception is dropped.

## The Common Refusal of the Argument

When this anti-pattern is named for a person operating from inside it, the common refusal is some version of: "But if I don't do what they want, I'll lose [the job / the relationship / the family / the status]." The refusal treats the externalized approval-seeking as functional. The argument's response is: the strategy is failing anyway. The job, the relationship, the family, the status — even when secured by the strategy — are not producing the felt experience the strategy was supposed to deliver. The strategy is securing the externals at the cost of the felt life. Continuing the strategy means continuing to pay that cost.

The argument is not a demand for risk. It is a clarification of what is actually being paid. The person can choose to keep paying. The argument just makes the cost visible.

Some refusals are more sophisticated: "I'm not chasing approval; I'm being responsible." The refinement preserves the strategy with a different name. The diagnostic question: when you make a decision, are you asking "do I approve?" or "will they approve?" If the second, the strategy is operating regardless of what it is being called.

## How This Anti-Pattern Connects to the Alone-in-Room Concern

This anti-pattern is dynamic 1 of the [[three-dynamics-of-feeling-alone-in-the-room]] framework. The high-functioning person who feels alone in rooms despite having built the right life is, in the dynamic 1 form, running this anti-pattern. They built the life that gets approved of. The approval did not produce the seeing. The strategy succeeded at its surface goal (the boxes are checked) and failed at the underlying goal (being seen).

The naming of this anti-pattern is often the first move when the alone-in-room concern is presented. The structural impossibility of the strategy has to be visible before any other work can land. Until the person sees that the strategy was always going to fail, they will treat their unseen-ness as evidence of incomplete execution of the strategy — they will conclude that they need to be more approved-of, which intensifies the chase.

The recognition that the strategy is structurally unwinnable is what releases the chase. Once released, the inversion ([[chase-your-own-approval]]) can begin.

## What This Anti-Pattern Is Not

- **Not "don't care what anyone thinks."** Caring about feedback from people you trust is not the same as organizing your life around external approval. The first is information. The second is the strategy. See [[chase-your-own-approval]] for the distinction.
- **Not "make decisions that hurt others to assert your independence."** The anti-pattern is the strategy of universal approval-seeking. The corrective is not the opposite strategy (universal disregard). The corrective is the inversion — asking what you approve of and acting from there, which usually includes considering impact on others.
- **Not a rejection of social bonds.** The person who drops this anti-pattern does not become anti-social. They become more available for actual relationships, because the front that was preventing real contact has dropped. See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]] for the developed mechanism.
- **Not unique to high-achievers.** The anti-pattern is common across the population. The high-achiever variant is just the most visible — the person who built an externally-impressive life and is now feeling the cost. The same anti-pattern operates in less-impressive lives that were similarly organized around external approval.

## When to Name This Anti-Pattern

The naming is most useful when:

- The person presents [[i-feel-alone-in-rooms-despite-success]] and the approval-seeking dynamic is visible.
- The person is presenting accomplishments while also reporting felt aloneness, and the contradiction needs the structural account.
- The person is in [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] and the foundational argument for why the strategy is impossible needs to be laid before the inversion can begin.
- The person is in [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] and the dynamic-1 structural account is needed.
- The person is sophisticated enough that they will resist the inversion ([[chase-your-own-approval]]) unless they have first seen the structural impossibility of the alternative.

## Tone

The naming of this anti-pattern is best done with structural confidence rather than personal critique. The person did not choose to install this strategy — they inherited it from a culture that valorized external approval. The work is not to shame them for having pursued it. The work is to make the mathematics visible.

The mathematical framing helps. "No one can be universally seen. No one is universally available as an approver. The strategy was pursuing a mathematical impossibility." The person is being treated as someone who can recognize a structural argument when it is presented clearly, not as someone who needs to be talked out of a moral failing.

The closing register tends toward relief. The mathematical impossibility is, paradoxically, freeing. The person did not fail at the strategy because they did the strategy wrong. The strategy was always going to fail. The recognition releases the self-blame for not having executed it better.
