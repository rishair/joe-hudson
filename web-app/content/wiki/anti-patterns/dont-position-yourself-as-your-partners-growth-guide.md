# Don't Position Yourself as Your Partner's Growth Guide

## The Anti-Pattern

In a romantic partnership, the position of being the one who knows what your partner needs for their growth -- the one who can see their patterns more clearly than they can, the one who is more equipped to facilitate their development, the one who has access to the tools that would help them. The position can feel like loving help, sophisticated awareness, or generous service. The position kills what it touches.

The pattern shows up as:
- "I can see exactly what they need to work on."
- "I have the tools that would help them."
- "I just want to guide them through this part."
- Offering interpretations of their behavior to them as if you are their coach
- Reflecting their patterns back to them with the implicit expectation that they should now address those patterns
- Curating books, podcasts, retreats for them based on what you have decided they need
- Feeling more advanced than them and treating the partnership as a teaching opportunity

The anti-pattern is distinct from [[dont-try-to-change-your-partner]] in that it is dressed up in growth-and-development language. The partner is not being asked to be different. They are being treated as a student of yours.

## Joe's Naming

Brett describes the pattern in his own experience: one partner feels responsible for the other's growth, or feels more equipped to facilitate it.

"I've seen and I've experienced this before in my relationships where each partner, one partner feels like they are responsible for the other's growth. Or in somehow, in some manner guiding it, or in some manner more equipped to facilitate it, and that just stifles things immediately." -- Brett Kistler, How Relationships Reveal Us

Joe's response is unequivocal:

"Yeah, that's hell. That's absolute hell. Yeah, Tara and I definitely went through that phase. It was brutal. Yeah, and full of arrogance and hubris and distraction from your own shit." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

Three diagnostic words: arrogance, hubris, distraction. The arrogance is the assumption that you know what they need. The hubris is the position that your role in their development is privileged. The distraction is the way the project of facilitating them replaces the project of your own work.

## Why It Stifles

Brett's word -- "stifles" -- points at the immediate result. The receiving partner cannot grow under the gaze of the facilitating partner because:

- The facilitating partner has already decided what the growth looks like. Anything the receiving partner does that does not match the prescription will be read as not-growth.
- The receiving partner can sense the implicit grade. Their internal movement gets entangled with the partner's evaluation of it. Movement that is being evaluated does not move.
- The receiving partner's autonomy in their own development is foreclosed. They cannot be the author of their own growth when someone else has assigned the curriculum.
- The relationship has lost peer-equality. The facilitating partner is operating from above. The receiving partner is operating from below. Growth that requires meeting your own ground cannot happen from the bottom of a hierarchy.

The principle [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] applies: the facilitating partner is robbing the receiving partner of their own pain (by interpreting it for them) and their own gold (by claiming to know what the gold looks like).

## Why It Is Distraction From Your Own Shit

Joe's specific accusation is that facilitating the partner's growth is a distraction from your own work. The mechanism:

- The energy required to facilitate someone else's growth is large.
- That energy is finite. What goes to them does not go to you.
- The position of facilitator carries the implicit claim that you have already done the work they need to do. The claim insulates you from noticing what work you have not done.
- Watching someone else's patterns is easier than watching your own. Watching theirs while in the position of teacher is the most insulated form of looking-at-someone-else's-patterns there is.

The anti-pattern is therefore self-sustaining. The more you facilitate them, the less attention you have for yourself. The less attention you have for yourself, the more obvious the partner's patterns appear by contrast, the more reasonable the facilitation feels.

Tara and Joe's "phase" of this -- which Joe names as brutal -- is the worked example. It ended. The relationship survived. The naming is offered as a warning to others who are in the same configuration.

## What This Anti-Pattern Often Conceals

- A reluctance to be in [[vulnerability]] with the partner, because facilitator is a position of strength
- An unhealed wound that is being addressed indirectly by treating the partner as the patient
- A way of staying in the relationship without being fully in it
- The need to be valuable that [[worthlessness-freedom]] addresses -- the position generates importance
- An unconsented power dynamic in which one party has been positioned as the more developed one

The position is also often gendered. The "I have access to the work and they don't" framing maps onto whichever partner discovered the path first, and tends to be unconscious about how the framing reproduces hierarchy.

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement is direct: do your own work. Let them do theirs. Trust them with their own development.

The principle holds even when their development is going slower than yours, even when their development looks different from yours, even when they are not visibly working at all. Their development is theirs. Your job is to be a partner, not a coach.

If you have insight about them that you want to share, the test is: are you sharing it because you want to be in honest contact with them, or because you want them to do something with it? If the former, share it as your truth about your experience. If the latter, do not share it.

The principle of [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] applies to partners as well. The wisdom about how someone heals is theirs, not yours -- even if you are very developed, even if you can see what they cannot.

## The Honest Carve-Outs

The anti-pattern is not:
- Sharing what you are learning when they ask
- Offering a resource when they invite it
- Practicing together when both of you are choosing to
- Reflecting your own experience and letting them take what they take from it
- Asking them how their work is going and listening to what they say

The anti-pattern is the unconsented project of producing growth in them according to your specifications. The marker is whether the position of facilitator has any expectation attached to it. If yes, anti-pattern. If no, ordinary partnership.
