# Don't Think the Awakening Makes You Done

## The Anti-Pattern

A specific failure mode in coaches and would-be coaches: believing that the awakening experience, the training program, the certification, the breakthrough, or the years of practice have completed the work. The person concludes that they are equipped. The conclusion that they are equipped is itself the disqualification. The role of coach asks for continuous work, continuous triggering, continuous reflection -- and the person who thinks they are done has structurally stepped out of the orientation the role requires.

The specific marker: the person becomes resistant to the things that would still teach them. Feedback lands as misunderstanding rather than as data. Supervision feels unnecessary. Triggers in coaching feel like client problems rather than coach material. The person's own development has stopped because no information is now being allowed in.

"If you think that you have had an awakening experience, if you think that you're fully enlightened, you are not fucking done." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The bluntness is intentional. The conventional spiritual framing around awakening tends to confer status. The status is the trap. The principle interrupts the status by naming the trap directly: claiming the awakening as completion is structurally how teachers go off the rails.

## Why It Fails

The structural problem with believing the awakening completed the work:

- **The role re-activates material.** Being a coach puts the practitioner in front of intense relational fields, projections, transferences, and triggers. New material surfaces constantly. The practitioner who thinks they are done has no framework for that new material -- it must be the client's problem because the coach is "complete."
- **The status becomes an identity.** "I have awakened" becomes the identity. The identity must be defended. Defense costs the coach the openness that allowed the awakening in the first place. See [[dont-identify-as-awakened]] for the identity-level anti-pattern.
- **Supervision becomes unwelcome.** A coach who is done does not need supervision. The supervision that would catch drift before it became distortion is rejected as unnecessary. The drift goes uncorrected. The distortion compounds.
- **The peer field flattens.** A coach who has "arrived" has no peers above them. The recognition that one is still growing requires acknowledging that others might see what one cannot. The done coach refuses that asymmetry. Their peer relationships flatten into mutual admiration without mutual challenge.
- **The student-teacher dynamic distorts.** When the teacher is "done," the students cannot be peers in any direction; they are receivers of completed wisdom. This is structurally the setup for [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]].

## The Triggering-In-Coaching Reframe

A specific corrective: the triggers that arise in coaching are not problems. They are exactly what the coach is supposed to be working with. The role activates material that would not have surfaced otherwise. That activation is the curriculum.

"Being in conversation, being in partnership with a client, will trigger you. And that's a good thing if you know what you're doing, if you know how to respond to it." -- Jerry Colonna, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The triggering is information about what is still alive in the coach. The "if you know how to respond to it" is the operative phrase. The skill is not the absence of triggering; it is the capacity to register the trigger as data and to do something useful with it -- including taking it to supervision, working with it in personal practice, naming it within the session if appropriate, letting it become the next thing the coach learns about themselves.

A coach who is triggered and metabolizes the trigger as their own work is in the right orientation. A coach who is triggered and frames the trigger as evidence of the client's problem has slipped out of the orientation. The frame matters. The slipping is structural; it happens to everyone; the question is whether the orientation is sustained or whether it drifts.

## The Supervisor Requirement

A direct structural recommendation: coaches need supervisors, the same way therapists do by law in most jurisdictions.

"There's a reason why therapists by law must have a supervisor, and I would argue that coaches should have that as well." -- Jerry Colonna, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

Joe affirms: "Absolutely."

The structural reason supervision exists in therapy is exactly the reason it should exist in coaching. The role amplifies material. The amplified material is hard to see from inside. Someone outside the coach-client dyad can see what is happening in the field. That outside view is necessary protection against the drift that the role makes structurally probable.

Without supervision, a coach is asking their own judgment to be the only check on their work. The same judgment that has all the same blind spots that the work is supposed to help clients see. The coach without supervision is structurally vulnerable to whatever blind spots their own development has not yet illuminated. The supervisor is the external reference that catches those.

The form of supervision can vary. Formal supervision with a more senior coach. Peer supervision groups. Community contexts where the coach's patterns can be named back to them by people who know them well. Long-term colleagues who can say "this feels off." All of these are versions of the same structural protection.

What is not supervision: the coach asking their own clients to confirm they are doing well. The coach surrounding themselves with people who admire them. The coach holding the "I am done" frame so firmly that no feedback can reach them. These are anti-supervision configurations dressed up as feedback systems.

## "I'm Still Growing Up at 61"

Jerry's modeling of the principle in his own life:

"Even as at 61 I'm still growing up, man. I am still a wreck. And that's a good thing." -- Jerry Colonna, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The acknowledgment that one is still working out being human, still surfacing material, still being a wreck in places -- this is the orientation the principle requires. The orientation is not depressive or self-flagellating. Jerry frames it as a good thing. The continued growing-up is what keeps the coach alive in the work. The coach who has stopped growing up has stopped being able to coach the way the work requires.

The "wreck" framing is also useful. It is not the polite version. It is the honest version. The coach is not a finished, perfectly integrated being who graciously offers their completeness to clients. The coach is a fellow wreck who has been doing the work long enough to be helpful and who continues to do the work because the work does not end.

This is the orientation that makes the role workable. It is the orientation that the "I have awakened" frame structurally destroys.

## The Two Awakenings (Permission to Be Imperfect, and the Reminder That the Work Continues)

The principle holds two truths simultaneously:

1. **Permission to be imperfect.** You do not have to be a fully completed being to coach. Jerry says explicitly: "I don't think you have to be perfect." Coaching does not require enlightenment; it requires the ongoing orientation of working on yourself.

2. **Reminder that the work continues.** Whatever progress has been made does not mean you are done. The role itself will surface more material. The orientation has to be sustained as that material arrives.

These two truths together: it is okay to be a wreck (you do not have to be perfect to coach), and it is not okay to claim you are done (the wreck-ness is your continued curriculum). The orientation is permission paired with honesty. The failure modes either drop the permission (becoming punishing about every imperfection) or drop the honesty (claiming completion).

## The Detection Form

How to detect this anti-pattern in yourself or someone else who coaches:

- They speak about their awakening or breakthrough as a credential rather than as data
- They have stopped seeking supervision or feedback
- They get defensive when a peer or supervisor reflects something back
- They frame triggers in coaching sessions as client problems
- Their language about coaching has shifted from "I am still learning" to "I help people who are stuck"
- They have stopped doing their own work, citing that they have already done it
- Their clients depend on them in increasing measure rather than developing independence
- They have built an audience that confirms their wisdom rather than a community that challenges them
- They speak of other coaches in evaluative terms ("they haven't done the work yet")
- They have a vocabulary for everyone else's patterns and no current vocabulary for their own

Any one of these is information. Multiple of them together is the configuration.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is to sustain the orientation continuously. The work does not end. The role makes the work both more intense and more necessary. The structures that protect the coach from drift -- supervision, peer accountability, community, ongoing personal practice -- are not optional add-ons. They are constitutive of being able to hold the role.

The orientation that produces a coach who can hold the role for the long term: "I am still finding out about myself. The clients are still teaching me. The triggers are still material. The work I am doing on myself is the work I am offering. None of it is done."

This orientation is freeing, not weighty. It removes the pressure to perform completion. It locates the practitioner in an ongoing practice rather than in a completed status. The role becomes sustainable because it does not have to be defended; there is no completed self to defend.

See [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]] for the broader principle this anti-pattern is the failure mode of. See [[dont-identify-as-awakened]] for the identity-level version of the same trap.

- A coach is framing client triggers as client problems rather than coach material
