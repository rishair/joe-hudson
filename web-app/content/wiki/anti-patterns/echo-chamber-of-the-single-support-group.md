# Don't Try to Get All Your Support From One Group

## The Anti-Pattern

The pattern: a person has one support group, and treats it as their full support system. The group does what it is designed to do — say, business support — and does it well. What it does not do is challenge the person on dimensions outside its purpose. The person grows in one direction and stagnates everywhere else, often without noticing.

A business support group will not challenge you on the time-and-energy balance you keep with your family. A men's group will not necessarily challenge you on your career stagnation. A meditation sangha may not challenge you on your marriage. Each group is doing its job. None of them is doing the others' job. The person who has only one is operating with structural blindspots they cannot see from inside the group, because the group is structured not to see them either.

"You can have a support group that's an echo chamber. You know, you might have a business support group, and no one in there is going to challenge you on your, like, balance of the time and energy you're spending with your family, for example. Maybe that's what's not happening in your support group." -- Brett Kistler, How To Build A Great Support System

## Why It Fails

A [[support-system]] organized around a [[shared-purpose-not-shared-proximity|shared purpose]] is designed to challenge members on that purpose. The same design feature that gives it focus also limits its scope. A group focused on building businesses is not going to spontaneously develop the capacity to challenge a member on their parenting, because parenting is not what brought the members together and not what they have agreed to engage on.

This is not a defect of the group. It is the necessary trade. A group that tries to challenge members on everything ends up challenging them effectively on nothing.

The failure mode is in the member's expectations, not the group's structure. The member treats the group as if it were their full support system, when it is in fact one support system serving one purpose. The unsupported axes of their life sit quietly, getting no challenge, no witness, no growth.

Joe's example of this in action: a group of entrepreneurs who supported each other on tech, on leadership, on building the business — but were not supporting each other on the anxiety driving their decisions. The anxiety stayed invisible inside the group, because the group's frame did not include it.

"They were all there supporting each other on many, many things, supporting each other on the tech, supporting each other on leadership, but they were not supporting each other on dealing with the anxiety. And it was amazing because as soon as it got brought up, you just saw everybody, and, and, and as soon as they got that support, it was like water on a dry sponge." -- Joe Hudson

The anxiety became visible only because it got named. Inside the original group structure, it was not named because it was not the purpose of the group.

## The Specific Echo-Chamber Risk

Beyond unaddressed axes, there is a related risk: the single support group reinforces a narrow set of values, language, and assumptions. The member becomes more articulate in the group's frame, more entrenched in its norms, more identified with its identity. The growth is real but narrow. Worse, the narrow growth feels like growth-in-general, because there is no other frame in the person's life to register what is not happening.

A business support group will reinforce the business frame. A meditation community will reinforce the contemplative frame. A men's group will reinforce a particular men's-group frame. Each can become an echo chamber if it is the only voice in the person's life.

The remedy is not to abandon the single group; it is to add others with different purposes. The diversity across support systems is what prevents the echo-chamber lock-in inside any one of them. See [[shared-purpose-not-shared-proximity]] for why each system needs a clear purpose and why the diversity is across systems, not within.

## What Joe Recommends Instead

Multiple support systems with different purposes, run in parallel. Each one does its specific work. Together they cover the dimensions of a whole life.

"That might not mean that that entire support group needs to change. Let's say you have a council of leaders, but you might also have, like you said, a men's group. I have a men's group. You might have a couples group. You can have more than one support group. Each of them having a purpose that is important in your life." -- Brett Kistler, with Joe's agreement

Joe's own configuration, as of the conversation:
- A couples group focused on being couples and parents
- A men's group / Friday night hike
- A 21-person content-creator group focused on business as service
- The Council, his leadership cohort

Each has a different purpose. Each does work the others cannot do. The whole life is covered because the systems together cover it, not because any one of them does.

This is not a recommendation to be in many groups for the sake of being in many groups. It is the recognition that the dimensions of a whole life (work, partnership, parenting, friendship, personal practice) cannot all be supported by a single container. Pretending that one can is the failure mode.

## How to Notice You're In This Pattern

A few diagnostic questions:

- What are the major dimensions of your life right now? (Work, partnership, parenting, health, personal practice, creative work, civic life, friendships.)
- Which of those does your support group challenge you on? Which does it not?
- For the dimensions it does not challenge you on: who or what is challenging you on those? If the honest answer is "nobody," that dimension is currently unsupported.
- Have you been in a phase where you have grown rapidly in one dimension and not at all in others? That asymmetry is often a sign of single-support-group reliance.
- If your one support group disappeared, what would happen? If everything you have been growing in would stall, the single point of failure is real. If much of your life would continue, your support is more distributed than you realized.

## What This Is Not

This is not an argument for having so many support groups that you cannot show up well to any of them. The point is coverage of the major axes, not maximization. For most people, two or three support systems is enough; some make do with one because they have other forms of structural support (a strong marriage, a deep friendship, a religious community) doing the work the additional systems would do.

This is also not an argument for having a support system for every difficulty. Some difficulties are private and individual. Some are addressed by a partner, a therapist, a single trusted friend. The principle is about the major recurring dimensions of life, not every individual challenge.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports being supported on one axis and stuck on another, without seeing the connection
- When someone is in a high-functioning group and noticing that some part of their life is quietly degrading
- When someone is treating a workplace, a sangha, or a friend group as their full support and feeling something missing
- When someone is asking "what's wrong with my support group?" when the better question is "what is my support group not built to do?"
- When a member of a high-performance cohort is showing symptoms (anxiety, burnout, neglected relationship) that the cohort is not equipped to address
