# Don't Claim Solitude as Cover for Avoiding the Shame of Loneliness

## The Anti-Pattern

A specific failure mode of loneliness work: the lonely person reframes their loneliness as "chosen solitude" without doing the underlying work. The reframe protects them from having to feel the shame at the core of the loneliness. The shame stays intact, the avoidance is now defended as preference, and the loneliness continues underneath the polished new framing.

"Some folks can do who are feeling that loneliness, who are feeling that shame, can say, 'No, no, I'm just choosing to be alone. I can choose to be alone.' And they haven't actually dealt with the underlying shame, so it's not gonna be a win for them. It's gonna be avoidance." -- Joe Hudson, Dear Lonely Person

The behavior pattern:

- "I just prefer my own company."
- "I'm an introvert. People drain me."
- "I'm focusing on my work / my craft / my practice. I don't have time for relationships right now."
- "I went on retreat / to a monastery to find myself." (When the actual function is avoiding the conditions where the shame would surface.)
- The person reads books about the benefits of solitude and quotes them as a defense of their avoidance.
- The person frames any invitation to connect as an imposition or an evaluation.

The specific form Joe and Brett name:

"Somebody could choose the monastic life to say, 'I'm gonna go find myself in the solitude,' when in reality they're avoiding intimacy. Not the case for everybody that's doing that, but that could be a path." -- Joe Hudson, Dear Lonely Person

"Or avoiding the shame that comes up around other people or the anxiety that comes up around other people." -- Guest 1, Dear Lonely Person

## Why It Fails

The reframe addresses the wrong end of the problem. Loneliness is built on shame. Solitude that does not engage with the shame is loneliness in nicer clothes. The shame stays in place. The lonely person continues to suffer; they just now have a story that prevents them from looking at what they're suffering from.

The real problem with the reframe is that it works socially — people accept "I just prefer my own company" without challenge — and so the avoidance becomes durable. There's no felt friction to push against. The person can live decades inside the reframe with the shame growing quietly underneath.

The diagnostic test: invite the person into a low-stakes connection. If the body reads it as threat (tightening, irritation, immediate refusal), the "solitude" was avoidance. If the body reads it as a pleasant option that can be accepted or declined freely, the solitude is genuine.

A second diagnostic: the genuine solitude practitioner returns from solitude with more capacity for contact, more presence, more ease around others. The avoidance-as-solitude practitioner returns with the same or worsening difficulty around others. Solitude builds capacity for connection; avoidance erodes it.

## What Joe Says Instead

The work is to distinguish genuine solitude from avoidance-disguised-as-solitude. See [[loneliness-vs-solitude]]. Once distinguished, the work for the avoidance form is the same work that loneliness always requires: address the shame, build the capacity to be seen, do the [[vulnerability]] practice.

For people who are genuinely in the avoidance form, the most useful thing a coach can do is gently surface the avoidance without shaming the person for it. The reframe was a protective move; the protective move makes sense. The work is not to attack the reframe but to notice what it's been protecting. Once the underlying shame is in view, the reframe loses its function and falls away naturally.

The direction: not "you should be more social." The direction is: "what would it be like to bring yourself into one small connection this week, and notice what happens in your body?" The small experiment surfaces the actual condition. The actual condition can then be worked with.

See [[16-experiments-for-connection]] for the practice form.

## How to Spot It in Coaching

- The person defends solitude before anyone has challenged it.
- The person's solitude is structurally avoidant rather than nourishing — phone use, scrolling, screen consumption, rather than presence and practice.
- The person speaks about other people in dismissive or evaluative terms — "people are exhausting," "I don't have anything in common with anyone" — rather than from neutrality.
- The person is in significant solitude but not generating the benefits the research associates with it (creativity, self-esteem, resilience).
- The person feels relief at the idea of not having to connect rather than feeling rich in their own company.
- The person reports their solitude with shame underneath — a slight defensiveness, a slight need for the coach to validate it.

The last cue is the cleanest: genuine solitude has no shame to defend. The person enjoying their own company can say "I love my solitude" without any need for the listener to agree. The person using solitude as cover needs the listener to confirm the cover.

## When This Anti-Pattern Is Most Live

- A person who has been single and isolated for many years and has built an identity around it.
- A person in spiritual or contemplative traditions where solitude is valorized.
- A high-functioning professional whose work consumes their life and who frames the absence of close relationships as a choice.
- A person who left a community, religion, or family system and never replaced the container, who now frames the absence as preference.
- An introvert (in the genuine sense) who has, in addition to introversion, also been avoiding connection due to shame — the introversion provides cover for the avoidance.

The pattern is not about introversion per se. Genuine introverts can have rich close relationships and significant solitude both. The anti-pattern is using the introversion frame to defend against connection that the person actually wants and is also afraid of.

## What Not to Do

- Do not attack the reframe. The reframe was protective. Attacking it activates the shame the reframe was hiding, and the person retreats further.
- Do not try to drag the person into social contact. The avoidance is structural; forcing contact will produce more avoidance.
- Do not flatter the reframe by agreeing that solitude is wonderful. If you collude with the cover, the work cannot happen.
- Do not skip the [[loneliness-vs-solitude]] distinction. The person needs the framework to apply themselves; once they have it, they can usually identify which one they're in.
