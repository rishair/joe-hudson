# Don't Reflect Your Behavior Patterns As Company Culture

## The Anti-Pattern

Designing company culture by default rather than by design, so that the culture becomes a reflection of the leader's personal behavior patterns rather than what is actually most effective for the team or the work. The leader is not making explicit choices about culture; the leader is just being themselves at scale, and the company organizes itself around the leader's preferences, comfort, and unconscious patterns.

"CEOs who will measure frickin' everything, but they don't measure culture. Or they measure culture, but they're not aware that basically the culture they want isn't the most effective culture. It's just the culture of their behavior patterns. They're just reflecting their behavior patterns into the company. I can't tell you how many people I've spoken to are just like, the company is run by trying to make the CEO happy. Which is wholly ineffective." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## Why It Fails

The leader's behavior patterns were optimized for the leader's life, not for the company's mission. The leader's comfort with conflict, communication style, decision-making rhythm, work hours, emotional tone, and so on, are products of the leader's history. Making these the de facto company culture imports the leader's neurosis into the organization at scale.

The company that has organized itself around making the CEO happy is producing a specific kind of work: not work that serves the customer, not work that serves the mission, but work that minimizes friction with the CEO. The product is downstream of this. The strategy is downstream of this. The talent is downstream of this. Everything in the company is shaped by the central optimization, and the central optimization is the wrong thing to optimize for.

The leader experiences this as the company working well. The leader is, in fact, having their preferences met. The hidden cost is the gap between what the company is producing and what it could produce if the culture were designed for the mission rather than for the leader.

## Why the Leader Cannot See It

The leader is the central node in their own experience. The patterns that have organized the leader's life are invisible to the leader because they are the water the leader swims in. Other people in the company see the patterns and adjust to them. The leader sees only that things are running smoothly.

The diagnostic moments are usually inadvertent. A direct report quits and gives unusually candid exit feedback. A consultant comes in and names the dynamic. A spouse comments on a behavior that the leader had not noticed. The leader's own coaching reveals a pattern that turns out to be the pattern the whole company has organized around.

Without an external mirror, the leader will not see this. The company is structured to not be the mirror.

## The Borrowed Culture Variant

A subtler form: the leader has chosen the culture from a book or another company, but the choice was made once and is being defended against feedback. The culture is still operating as default; the only change is that the default came from outside the leader rather than from inside.

"There's an assumption that this is the way that the culture should be based on some reading. So it's either the culture should be this way because of who I am as a CEO or because of some reading, instead of, oh, well, if we tweak this part of culture, what happens to performance? It's not iterative, it's not an interactive thing." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The borrowed culture often looks more sophisticated than the unconscious-personality culture. It uses the right words. It cites the right books. But the iterative quality is still missing. The culture is still being imposed rather than being designed in conversation with reality.

## What Joe Says Instead

Run the culture as an experiment. See [[team-as-scientific-method]]. Make explicit choices. Measure them. Iterate when reality reveals that the choice is not producing what you wanted.

The first move is often the hardest: distinguishing what you actually want for the company from what you personally prefer. The CEO who likes early meetings has to ask whether early meetings serve the company. The CEO who hates conflict has to ask whether the company benefits from the conflict avoidance. The CEO who loves long strategic discussions has to ask whether the team would actually be better served by shorter ones.

The pattern continues throughout the work. Every default the leader has is potentially a culture choice that has been made unconsciously. Surfacing the default is the precondition for choosing whether to keep it.

## The Linked Pattern: Hiding Wants

This anti-pattern often coexists with [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants|the failure to name wants]]. The leader who is unconsciously imposing their behavior as culture is often also hiding their explicit wants. The team is operating around two layers of unspoken signal: the leader's personality (which is being absorbed as culture) and the leader's hidden wants (which are leaking as politics).

Naming the wants without examining the patterns produces a leader who is more explicit but still imposing themselves. Examining the patterns without naming the wants produces a leader who is more self-aware but still mysterious to the team. Both pieces are required.

See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] for the wants form of the same dynamic.

## The Connection to Chameleon Leadership

The opposite failure is also possible and is captured in [[chameleon-leadership]]: the leader who has no consistent self and shape-shifts to whatever each person seems to want. That leader is also not designing culture; they are defaulting to the dynamic each interaction produces.

The fix for both is the same: explicit design choices, made consciously, measured against effectiveness, iterated.

## Tone

Joe is matter-of-fact here. The pattern is so common in Silicon Valley that he is not warning against an unusual mistake; he is naming the default state of most leadership. The diagnostic is delivered with the weariness of someone who has seen this many times.
