# Don't Use Coaching as a Surrogate for Intimacy

## The Anti-Pattern

Becoming a coach because coaching produces deep intimacy without the risk that real intimate relationships require. The coach gets the felt experience of being deeply with someone -- the close attention, the vulnerable exchange, the witness of profound material -- without having to be exposed in the way an actual partnership exposes a person. The coaching relationship is structurally one-sided. The coach can be fully present, deeply engaged, intimately connected, and walk away to their own life without the entanglement an actual intimate relationship would involve.

The behavior is coaching. The structural problem is that the coaching is meeting intimacy needs the coach is not meeting elsewhere, and the relational structure of coaching makes those needs only ever partially met, so the pattern persists without resolution.

## Why It Fails

The harm is symmetric -- one face for the coach, one for the client.

**For the coach.** The intimacy needs the person actually has -- the wanting of mutuality, of being known by someone who also lets themselves be known, of being in a relationship where they can lose themselves and be received -- are not being addressed. They are being managed through a surrogate. The surrogate is partial by design (the coaching relationship is bounded, professional, one-sided), so the underneath wanting does not get resolved. The coach accumulates more and more coaching intimacy without ever feeling actually met. The risk-avoidance becomes its own ceiling on the coach's capacity for real partnership.

This parallels [[money-as-surrogate]] and the broader principle of surrogate satisfaction: a surrogate cannot deliver what the actual thing would deliver, because the structural feature that made the surrogate appealing (lower risk) is also the structural feature that prevents satisfaction. The coach using coaching as intimacy surrogate is in the same configuration.

The pattern often co-occurs with a fear of losing oneself in love (see [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]]) or with patterns from transactional or contractual relationships (see [[transactional-relationships-as-defense]]). The coaching role offers exactly the bounded form these patterns prefer: defined start and end, defined fee, defined scope, no merger.

**For the client.** The harm to the client is more diffuse but real. A coach using the relationship for their own intimacy needs is, in a subtle way, extracting from the client. The client is the one being met, vulnerable, exposed -- and the coach is using that exposure to feel connected without exposing themselves to a comparable degree. The asymmetry is structural to coaching (the coach is not supposed to be vulnerable in the same way), but when the coach is unconsciously using the asymmetry to meet their own needs, the client is being instrumentalized for the coach's regulation. The client may feel a residual sense of being used without being able to name it.

A second harm: the coach who needs the intimacy will subtly distort the work to keep producing the intimacy. The coach may prefer clients who go deep faster, who are emotionally open, who provide more of the substance the coach needs. The coach may avoid the moves that would end the relationship sooner. The work bends to keep the intimacy available rather than to serve the client's actual development.

## How It Shows Up

- The coach feels more alive in coaching sessions than in their other relationships
- The coach's primary close relationships are with clients
- The coach finds it hard to maintain comparable depth with partners, friends, or family
- The coach has chronic resistance to ending coaching relationships even when the work is complete
- The coach feels a small grief when a client graduates beyond needing them
- The coach's social life consists disproportionately of people connected to their professional work
- The coach experiences the bounded nature of coaching (no contact outside sessions, no friendship after) as a constraint to be worked around rather than as a healthy structure
- The coach is more vulnerable with clients than with their own partner
- The coach gets in their body the same way during sessions that they would in a romantic encounter

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is to develop actual intimate relationships outside the coaching work. The coach has to meet their intimacy needs in relationships that are mutual, that risk something, and that have the entanglement that produces real partnership. Joe's articulation:

"Another reason that people come to coaching is because it's like intimacy, except for it doesn't have the risk. Like there's this deep intimacy that happens when you coach someone, when I coach someone, but there's not the risk that you have, like, say, with your marriage. And so there's a way for people to get a surrogate of intimacy and feel that connection, but they don't actually have the risk. That's also quite dangerous for a number of reasons, both for the person doing it." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The structural principle: coaching can be intimate, and that is fine, and the coach can enjoy that intimacy, but the coaching cannot be a substitute for the coach's actual intimate life. If it is functioning as a substitute, the work the coach has to do is on the avoidance of intimacy elsewhere, not on the coaching itself. Inner work on the fear of being seen, the fear of losing oneself, the fear of the risk of love -- these address the underneath. Coaching better is not the answer; doing the actual intimate-relationship work is.

## How To Detect It (In Yourself)

- You feel more known by your clients than by your partner or friends
- The thought of ending all coaching tomorrow produces a feeling of bereavement disproportionate to losing a job
- You find yourself sharing things in sessions that you do not share in your personal life
- You experience boundary structures around coaching as obstacles rather than as protections
- Your fantasy life or daydreams involve clients in ways you would not want to acknowledge
- Your existing partnerships have a quality of "less than" compared to the connection you feel in sessions
- You have not had a partnership that lasted past the point of [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]] becoming active
- You came to coaching after a major relationship ended or a major intimacy failure
- The coaching relationship is the deepest connection in your life

## The Work to Do Before Coaching

If the surrogate-intimacy pattern is active, the work is:

1. Notice the pattern honestly. The acknowledgment that the coaching is doing intimacy work is itself the first move. The pattern is invisible while it is being avoided.
2. Examine the avoidance underneath. What is being avoided in actual intimate relationships? Fear of being seen? Fear of losing oneself? Fear of conflict? Fear of being hurt? The pattern is built on top of one or more of these.
3. Do the avoided work. If the avoidance is fear of being seen, the work is to be seen. If it is fear of being hurt, the work is to risk being hurt. See [[love-means-signing-up-to-be-hurt]] and [[vulnerability]].
4. Develop intimate relationships outside of coaching. Friendships, partnerships, family relationships in which there is mutual exposure, risk, and entanglement.
5. Notice the pull toward coaching intimacy and treat it as a signal. The pull does not need to be acted against; it needs to be understood. What is it pointing at?

The work is not to stop enjoying coaching. The work is to no longer require coaching to be the place where the intimacy need gets fed.

## The Subtle Versions

The obvious version: the coach who blatantly crosses professional boundaries to extract emotional intimacy from clients.

The subtle versions:

- The coach whose boundaries are nominally intact but whose internal experience is using the client's vulnerability to regulate their own loneliness
- The coach whose marketing leans heavily on the intimacy of their work in a way that telegraphs their need for it
- The coach who is professionally appropriate but whose private inner life centers on the clients they are working with
- The coach who consistently selects training or coaching modalities that maximize the intimacy of the relational form
- The coach who has been in this work for a long time and whose actual intimate relationships have atrophied during that time without their noticing

## Why This Matters Especially for Aspiring Coaches Coming Out of Relational Loss

The aspirant who is considering coach training shortly after a major intimacy loss -- a divorce, a death of a partner, the end of a long friendship -- needs to look at whether the wanting to coach is partly about reconstituting intimate connection in a less exposed form. The wanting itself is not wrong, but if it is being acted on without examination, the coaching will be built on a surrogate-intimacy foundation, and the work will subtly bend toward keeping that intimacy available rather than toward the client's freedom.

The timing matters. The pattern is more likely to be active when the actual intimacy in the person's life is in deficit. Doing the inner work to address the deficit, and only then considering training, is the structurally healthier path.

## Boundary With Genuine Closeness in Coaching

The principle is not "coaching should be cold and distant." Coaching is, properly, intimate. The vulnerability the client brings is met by the coach's presence, which has its own quality of closeness. The coach can love their clients. The coach can be moved by the work. The coach can experience genuine connection in sessions. None of that is the anti-pattern.

The anti-pattern is the structural relationship to the intimacy. Is it a byproduct of doing the work well, with the coach's own intimacy needs met elsewhere? Or is it the actual reason the coach is in the work, with the coach's own intimacy needs unaddressed and the coaching functioning as the substitute? The first is healthy. The second is the pattern.
