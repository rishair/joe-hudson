# Don't Write a List of What's Wrong With You

## The Anti-Pattern

A specific kind of self-improvement practice: writing down everything that is wrong with you, in detail, with specificity. The list might include things like "I'm depressed because my mom hated me because I have a buck tooth," or "I have a really big forehead," or "I'm bad at relationships," or "I procrastinate." The practice is often framed as honesty, as self-awareness, as facing the truth, as the necessary first step toward change.

It is not. It is the most reliable way to install and reinforce the shame structure described in [[shame-drives-behavior]]. The specificity is what makes it especially toxic. A vague "I'm not good enough" is bad; a list of fifty specific reasons why is much worse.

The anti-pattern is delivered in Joe's satirical doomscrolling video as one of the "professional moves" for becoming an elite doomscroller:

"Write down all the things that are wrong with you. So if you write on the list and you're like, oh, I'm depressed, that's great, but you want it specific. So it's like, I'm depressed because my mom hated me because I had a buck tooth. And I still do. Or like, I have a really big forehead. That's a good — that I use that one personally. That's a great one to use. So you want something that's specific to how you are broken in at least 50 times." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

The instruction is satirical. The point is that this is exactly what not to do — and the reason it is so harmful is precisely because the specificity makes it effective at installing the shame the listener is trying to escape.

See [[satirical-anti-advice-as-teaching]] for the format and [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] for the underlying principle.

## Why It Fails

The brokenness-catalog fails for several interlocking reasons:

1. **Specificity locks shame in.** Generic shame is harder to hold than specific shame. "I'm not good enough" is vague enough that the body can sometimes let it pass. "I'm not good enough because of my forehead, my buck tooth, my procrastination, my temper, my relationship history, etc." gives the shame a structure to attach to. The body now has specific objects to feel bad about, and each one becomes its own little shame-generator.

2. **The list reinforces the unique-defectiveness premise.** The format of "here are MY specific defects" implicitly carries "these are uniquely mine." Other people's defects do not appear on the list. The list, by its structure, isolates the writer from the universal human pattern. See [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]] on the unique-to-me distortion as the engine of false shame.

3. **The list is treated as discovery when it is actually invention.** The person believes they are seeing what is already true. They are actually constructing a self-portrait organized around defects. The portrait then becomes the operating identity. Months later the person is operating from an identity that has fifty documented things wrong with it, and the identity does what identities do — it produces behavior consistent with itself.

4. **The list trains the attentional bias.** Whatever you focus on, the brain learns to find more of. A daily list of defects trains the brain to find defects. The brain becomes faster and more efficient at noticing defects. The list grows. The bias deepens.

5. **The shame produces the very behaviors the list is trying to address.** The "I procrastinate" entry on the list installs shame about procrastinating, which produces more procrastination (because the shame is the engine of the procrastination, per [[shame-drives-behavior]]). The list creates the conditions for the items on it to multiply.

6. **It collapses the distinction between awareness and self-attack.** Awareness sees what is here. Self-attack lists what is wrong with what is here. The two look identical from outside — both are descriptions of behavior or trait. The difference is the felt stance. Awareness is curious; self-attack is verdict. The list, by virtue of being a list of what is "wrong," is structurally on the self-attack side regardless of how dispassionately it is written.

## The Close Cousin That Works: The Brutal-Honesty List

There is a real practice that looks similar from outside but works on the opposite mechanism. See [[joes-26-year-old-list]]. At 26 Joe wrote down everything about himself he didn't want to admit. Six months later most of the items had changed. He attributed the change to awareness — the recognition itself was the work.
