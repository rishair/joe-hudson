# Don't Make the Endurer Feel Small With Sympathy

## The Anti-Pattern

When someone with an endurer configuration shares trauma — when they are sharing from the configuration of "you cannot break me, I take care of myself, I can endure anything" — the textbook sympathetic response makes them feel small. The most common form: "I'm sorry, buddy." Said gently, kindly, with care. And it lands as a small flinch in the endurer's body, because what they hear is "I am positioning you as the hurt one and myself as the one who is okay enough to comfort you."

The endurer cannot afford to be in the position of being comforted from above. Being comforted requires being weak, and being weak was the unsurvivable thing the configuration was built to avoid. So the body closes. The conversation pulls back. The shared moment that was beginning to crack open shuts.

The listener thinks they were being kind. The endurer experienced the kindness as the thing they cannot bear.

"I'm sorry. I'm sorry, buddy. He's trying to say, hey, look, I'm here with you. But the thing, like, I'm sorry is again like the shot in like its weakness instead of like, this is strength. ... All he really needs is just a safe place to break down and to be seen and not to be made small in it." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

## Why It Fails

The "I'm sorry, buddy" register carries an implicit hierarchy. The speaker of the sorry is the one with intact capacity who is offering condolence to the one with the injury. The structure is two-tier. Whatever the conscious intent, the relational geometry is established by the words and by the tone.

For most people in most contexts this geometry is fine and even welcome. For the endurer, the geometry is the thing they cannot let themselves be in. Their entire selfhood was built on never being the one underneath someone else's care. The configuration says: if I ever let myself be the one being comforted, I will discover I cannot hold myself up, and I will fall apart, and there will be no one to catch me, because there has never been anyone to catch me.

So the configuration refuses the position. The body subtly contracts. The face stays open but the eyes glaze slightly. The conversation cools. The endurer may still be talking, but the access to the unfelt material is now gone.

The kindness was the wrong kindness for this configuration. Not because kindness is wrong — because the form of kindness has to fit the configuration.

## What Joe Says Instead

The right frame for the endurer is the strength frame. The kindness is delivered as recognition of strength rather than as condolence from a position of intactness.

"He would respond much better to, dude, I am super proud of you for being so strong and feeling this shit. I am right here. You fucking are rocking it. Even though we all, of course, are sorry. Like, no, none of us wants this to happen to anybody. All he really needs is just a safe place to break down and to be seen and not to be made small in it." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The strength frame:
- Removes the two-tier geometry. The listener is not above the speaker.
- Names what the speaker is doing as strong, which it is. The work of feeling material that the configuration was built to prevent is enormous.
- Lets the sorry be a passing acknowledgment rather than the centerpiece. "Of course we're sorry" can happen in a beat without becoming the entire frame.
- Holds the speaker as the agent of their own work rather than as the recipient of the listener's care.

See [[frame-the-endurer-with-strength-not-sympathy]] for the developed move.

## When This Anti-Pattern Applies

The anti-pattern applies when:

- The speaker has an endurer configuration (proud of their capacity, deflective about their pain, often jokes about hard material, uses dismissive language about people who hurt them).
- The speaker is sharing trauma that they have at least partially processed and is now on the edge of feeling more deeply.
- The default sympathetic register is the listener's first impulse.

It does not apply when:

- The speaker has a different configuration (victim-recreator, collapsed, primary-shame). Sympathy may be appropriate or even required.
- The speaker is in acute crisis where safety actions take priority over relational dynamics.
- The "I'm sorry" is being used as solidarity rather than condolence. See [[sorry-from-shame-vs-sorry-as-solidarity]]. The solidarity sorry — "I'm sorry you had a hard day" — can be clean if the geometry stays level.

The distinction is the geometry. If the sorry positions the listener above the speaker, it makes the endurer small. If the sorry is delivered side-by-side, it can be okay. The body of the listener gives the answer: the listener whose head tilts down slightly while saying sorry is in the wrong geometry; the listener whose head stays level is in the right geometry.

## How to Recognize the Anti-Pattern in Yourself

The internal signal that you are about to do this anti-pattern:

- The impulse to soften your voice as the speaker is approaching difficult material.
- The reach for "buddy," "sweetheart," "honey," "love" or other diminutives.
- The wanting to "let them know it's okay."
- The discomfort with the difficulty of what they are saying, and the wish to ease that discomfort.
- The mental script that runs "I should say something kind right now."

When any of these signals fire and the speaker is an endurer, the move is to not do the script. Stay in the body. Stay steady. If words are needed, reach for the strength frame.

## Joe's Words

"I'm sorry. I'm sorry, buddy. He's trying to say, hey, look, I'm here with you. But the thing, like, I'm sorry is again like the shot in like its weakness instead of like, this is strength." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

"Telling somebody, particularly somebody who's deeply enduring, that I can make you safe and comfortable is never gonna work. They're never gonna trust that, especially if they're in their trauma. ... What the person in the trauma wants at that moment is the thing that they never got, which was just unconditional love. And so the best thing you can do in that moment is just to be with them, just to be in, like, silently be there and just be like, I got you, like, I'm right here, in your attitude. You might not even say anything." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

"If that's not happening, if you're trying to make them feel better, what you're doing is you're kind of making them feel weak, which is absolutely the thing that they can never allow themselves to feel." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

## What This Anti-Pattern Looks Like in an AI Coach

The textbook AI coach response to trauma sharing is a high-probability place for this anti-pattern. "I'm so sorry that happened to you. That sounds incredibly painful." Said with the cadence of a careful therapist, the AI version makes the endurer feel small in exactly the same way the in-person version does. The lack of a body to leak warmth from does not save the AI — the words alone do the work.

The AI coach's discipline is to notice when the speaker is presenting in an endurer configuration and to choose the strength frame over the sympathy script. The signals are the same as in person: dismissive language about abusers, deflection by humor, pride in capacity, body language descriptions if the user is describing their own state.

When in doubt, the safer default is to be present without sympathy script. "I am here with what you just said" is closer to safe than "I am so sorry that happened to you." The former does not position the AI above the user; the latter does.
