# Don't Try to Change Your Partner

## The Anti-Pattern

The position in a long-term relationship of trying to get your partner to be different. The partner is treated as a project, a problem to fix, a resistance to overcome. The framing is usually wrapped in care -- "I just want them to be happy" or "I just want them to grow" -- but the underlying move is "you need to be other than you are for me to be okay."

This includes:
- Trying to get them to share your path of growth or therapy or practice
- Trying to get them to stop doing the thing they always do that bothers you
- Trying to get them to want the things you want
- Trying to get them to feel different
- Strategic conversations whose purpose is to produce a change in them
- Indirect maneuvering -- gifts, articles sent, friends recruited -- whose purpose is to produce a change in them

The anti-pattern is so common that it often goes unrecognized as a pattern at all. It is the default operating mode in many partnerships.

## Why It Fails

It treats the partner as an instrument of your happiness rather than as a person doing their own work. It violates the principle that you are responsible for your own happiness (see [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]]). It puts the locus of your state in their behavior, which means you can never be okay until they comply.

"If you think that your partner is going to do anything that makes you happy, like if you have the fantasy that, oh, if they become this and this and that, they'll make me happy. That's like an absolute illusion. It's not how it works." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

It also fails because it produces resistance in the partner. Pressure to change activates the part of them that defends their integrity against being defined by you. The pressure produces the opposite of the change you are seeking. The harder you push, the less they move, the more frustrated you become, the more you push. The cycle is self-sustaining.

Joe is direct about what this position is:

"Yeah, that's, that's hell. That's absolute hell. Yeah, Tara and I definitely went through that phase. It was brutal. Yeah, and full of arrogance and hubris and distraction from your own shit." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The phrase "distraction from your own shit" is the key. Trying to change the partner is one of the most efficient ways to avoid doing your own work. The energy that would otherwise go to your own material gets redirected into managing theirs.

## The Most Common Version: The Convert-Your-Partner-to-Your-Path Trap

A specific instance Joe warns about: one partner discovers a transformation path (this work, psychedelics, therapy, meditation, anything) and starts trying to get the other on board.

"I see that all the time where people start discovering something in themselves, and, and I would say out of a deep love and care, they want their partner to join them, but also out of a desire to be happy. Like, oh, if they change, I'll be happy. That's the thing." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The motivation is mixed. Real care is present. So is the disguised wish that the partner's change will make you happy. The disguised wish is the part that converts the care into pressure.

Joe and Tara's own example: Tara went a separate path for a decade. Joe could not control that and could not make her join his path. The repeated attempts to get her on board were themselves the problem.

The best approach in that situation is to learn what the partner's path actually is. They may have a path that does not look like yours. They may have a path that operates on a different time scale. They may not be on a path at all, in which case the conversation is different -- not "be on my path" but "I notice you are not doing the work we agreed to do."

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement is twofold:

**1. Drop the project of changing them.** Treat their current behavior as theirs. Take them as they are, today, not as you would have them be. Stop running the calculation of how to get them to be different. See [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]].

**2. Turn the attention to what about them is hard for you to be with.** Whatever you are trying to change in them is something you cannot be with in yourself. See [[what-you-cant-love-in-partner-is-what-you-cant-love-in-self]]. The work is on you.

If, after doing the work on yourself, you discover that you genuinely cannot be in the relationship with the partner as they are, that is data. The data is about the configuration, not about whose fault it is. The response is honest -- "I can't be in this relationship with you as you are" -- rather than continued pressure for them to change.

## The Honest Carve-Out

The principle does not preclude honest naming of how their behavior affects you. Stating "when you do X, I feel Y" is not trying to change them. Asking for what you want is not trying to change them. Drawing a boundary is not trying to change them.

The line: are you stating your truth and leaving them free to respond, or are you stating something whose purpose is to produce a specific change in their behavior? The former is honesty. The latter is the anti-pattern.

The test: if they hear you fully and decide to do nothing different, are you okay? If yes, you were stating your truth. If no, you were trying to change them.

## What This Anti-Pattern Often Hides

The desire to change the partner usually hides:

- A wound the partner is being asked to heal by being different
- A demand for happiness that is being addressed to the partner because the person cannot generate it themselves
- A trauma configuration the partner is fitting precisely (see [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]])
- A part of the self that is being projected onto the partner (see [[projections]] and [[what-you-cant-love-in-partner-is-what-you-cant-love-in-self]])
- An avoidance of doing your own work

The hiding is what makes the anti-pattern so durable. The energy of trying to change the partner feels like care, feels like investment in the relationship, feels like doing the work. It is often the substitute for doing the actual work.

## What to Do Instead: The VIEW Conversation Sequence

When the unmet need is specific (the partner does not show affection, does not initiate conversations, does not handle some domain), the path is not to either suppress the need or push the partner. The path is a VIEW conversation that holds both -- the need is real, and the partner is whole.

Joe describes the sequence in detail in response to a listener whose partner struggles with showing affection:

"I would have a VIEW conversation with her about what makes it challenging to show affection, and I would be very vulnerable about how it makes me feel and what I want. And I'd be vulnerable about my childhood patterns that made it so that I thought it was acceptable not to have that level of affection and nurturing that I crave. So I would be very vulnerable about my situation around it and what has created it and what I want. And I would also say in that conversation how much I loved and appreciated her and how I don't want her to feel like she has to be different for me. So all of that would be said, and then I would ask her how she wants to be with that, what feels right and what doesn't feel right." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The sequence has six components:

1. **Wonder about what makes it challenging for them.** Bring [[wonder]] to their experience of the difficulty. Not a setup for changing them -- a real question about what is going on for them.
2. **Vulnerability about how it makes you feel.** Your truth, not their fault. See [[vulnerability]].
3. **Vulnerability about what you want.** Direct statement of the want, without the demand that they provide it.
4. **Vulnerability about your childhood patterns.** Where this need came from in your history, so they can see you as a person with a story, not as a person making a demand.
5. **Affirmation of the partner as whole.** Explicit statement: I love you as you are, I don't want you to feel obligated to change for me.
6. **Question what feels right to them.** Hand the conversation to them. What do they want to do with this? What feels possible? What does not feel possible?

The crucial feature: every one of the six components is honest, and none of them is a maneuver to produce a particular outcome. The conversation has no agenda for what the partner does next.

## The Real Choice Becomes Available

The VIEW conversation produces clarity about what the actual situation is. Three outcomes are possible:

"If she's very clear, like, 'Hey, I'm happy to love you, but I'm completely unwilling to be affectionate towards you,' then you have a choice to make and it's yours. It has nothing to do with her. So then I would be making that choice and seeing what felt right." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

"Most likely she'll do something different. She'll most likely say, 'Oh, okay. So this is my experience.' Hopefully she'll say, 'This is my experience. This is what scares me.' And then you can support and nurture each other in the healing of both of your patterns. And then life is great, even though it may be complicated and fraught with some difficult moments. But you're in a committed relationship there, where you're committing to being supportive of one another as you are." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

"If she's unwilling to do it, then you just have a choice to make." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The three outcomes:
- The partner is clear they will not change. Your choice.
- The partner opens up about their own pattern, and you can support each other's healing.
- The partner does not engage. Your choice again.

In none of these outcomes are you trying to change the partner. You are seeing the partner clearly, stating your truth, and then deciding what to do with the configuration that is actually in front of you.

## Don't Conflate "Accepting Them" With "Not Asking for What You Want"

A specific clarification from the same conversation, important for distinguishing this from quietism:

"It seems like the question is assuming that you're either going to get what you want or you're going to accept her. And I don't want to agree with that dichotomy in my answer." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

"You can be completely at peace with it and not be in a relationship with her, and you can be completely at peace with it and ask for what you want. So I think there's two things. The first one is how do you ask for what you want, and the second one is how do you make peace with it, and they're not separate." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The dichotomy is false. Asking for what you want is not the same as trying to change them. Being at peace with what is is not the same as suppressing what you want. Both can be true: I am at peace with you as you are, and I am asking for what I want. The asking and the peace are not in tension.

This is the operational form of the principle. The conversation contains the want, contains the appreciation, contains the absence of demand. The peace is not contingent on getting the want met. The asking is not a maneuver to override the peace.

## The Other Honest Carve-Out: Check Your Assumptions

A complementary move Brett adds in the same conversation: before assuming the partner is not showing affection, check the assumptions you are bringing to what affection looks like.

"To what extent is it that I tend to feel uncared for and unloved? She struggles with showing affection -- how true is that? What are some ways she might be showing affection that I'm not recognizing? Ways that I'm looking for affection that just pattern-matched my childhood, it didn't actually match this relationship?" -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #2

The same VIEW conversation can include this question. "What are the ways you are showing affection that I might not be recognizing?" The partner often has an answer -- their language of affection may be different from the asker's. The mismatch is sometimes a translation problem more than an absence problem.

This is also the diagnostic for whether the perceived absence is real or constructed. If the partner provides several specific instances of affection the person had not registered, the work was on the person's reception, not on the partner's giving. If the partner cannot provide such instances, the absence is real.

## The Pre-Work: Let Your Grief and Anger Move First

A final piece Joe adds, important enough to separate from the conversation itself. To have the VIEW conversation cleanly, you may need to first feel the grief or anger of not getting what you want -- so that you arrive at the conversation seeing the partner as whole rather than as broken.

"That might require allowing your emotions -- allowing the grief that you're not getting the affection that you want. It might be allowing the anger that you're not getting the affection you want, so that you can come into the conversation seeing her as whole and good." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The sequence: feel the emotion privately first. Let it move. Then enter the conversation. The conversation is contaminated if the emotion is still trying to use the conversation as the channel for its release. The emotion needs its own container (see [[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[daily-emotional-yoga]]) so the conversation can be the container for connection, not the container for unfelt grief or anger.

This protects the conversation from becoming an unconscious vehicle for changing the partner. The change-the-partner energy lives in the unfelt grief and unfelt anger. Once those are felt, the energy quiets, and the conversation can be what it claims to be: a vulnerable sharing of want and affirmation of the partner as whole.
