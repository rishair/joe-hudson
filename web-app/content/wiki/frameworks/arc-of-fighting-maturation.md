# The Arc of Fighting Maturation in a Relationship

## The Framework

Couples mature in their fighting along one of two starting points, each with its own developmental trajectory. The end state is the same in both: the orientation of "I can't wait to have a fight because each one is an opening." Most couples are somewhere on the arc and most do not know that an end state is available at all.

The two starting points are:

1. **Couples that start with mean, abusive fights** (modeled from family-of-origin)
2. **Couples that start by not fighting** (often paired with the Stepford-Wives surface of "we don't really fight")

The two paths converge through a middle stage where techniques get learned and often weaponized, then through a stage where trauma gets recognized as the actual fight, and finally to a state where the fights are anticipated as the medium of integration.

## Starting Point 1: Mean and Abusive Fights

The couple comes in fighting badly. This usually means [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] is present -- yelling without permission, threatening, getting angry at each other as discharge. The fights are modeled from what each partner saw growing up. The fighting feels normal because it is what they know.

The arc from here:

1. **Bad fights as the default.** The couple fights frequently, hurtfully, and without resolution. The fights recycle. Neither partner sees a problem with the form of the fighting, only with the partner's behavior in it.

2. **Tools get learned.** Therapy, books, podcasts, friends introduce the couple to techniques. Nonviolent communication, "I" statements, breathing, taking time-outs. The tools start to do something. The fights soften, sometimes.

3. **Tools get co-opted.** The same partners who fought viciously now weaponize the new tools. "I really need you to stop doing that" delivered as a demand wearing the costume of an "I statement" is not nonviolent communication. The tools become the new form of the same fight. This is the "new age fight" stage. See [[view-as-technique]] for the broader pattern.

4. **The trauma gets seen as the trauma.** The couple recognizes that the fights are being driven by old material, not by the present situation. The shift from "you are the problem" to "we are both in our trauma" begins. See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] for the in-the-moment move that becomes available here.

5. **Healing fights become possible.** The couple starts to have fights where one partner is loving while the other's trauma fires. The trauma gets met by the love it could not access the first time. The couple sees this work two or three times.

6. **Game on.** Once the trauma-healing-through-loving-witness has happened a few times, the partners become addicted to it. The fight is no longer the problem; the fight is the medium. "I can't wait to fight."

"It's game on. I can't fucking wait to fight. I can't wait to have a fight because everyone is this way of opening and becoming more of ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

## Starting Point 2: We Don't Fight

The other common starting point is the [[we-never-fight]] presentation. The couple presents as "we're so compatible." From the outside, the relationship looks easy. From the inside, the partners are managing themselves carefully to avoid the conflict that would surface what is actually there.

The arc from here:

1. **No fighting at the surface, no contact at depth.** The couple coexists smoothly. Neither knows what the other actually thinks about the harder topics. The Stepford-Wives quality is present: "very fake plastic watering plant kind of world." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

2. **The worst outcome: no-fight-no-fight-no-fight-divorce.** "Don't fight, don't fight, don't fight, divorce. Don't fight, don't fight. I don't know what happened. I thought we were totally happy and she just walked out." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win. This is the path the framework is trying to interrupt.

3. **Small fights about the wrong things.** When fighting starts, it is about dishes, coffee machines, the wrong words at the wrong time. The fights are squirmishes. The real material is still being held back.

4. **Speaking the full truth.** The held-back material starts to come out. See [[hold-back-from-relationship-is-what-needs-healing]]. The fights get harder and more real. Some of them get a little wild, but the wildness usually does not last long for this configuration.

5. **The same convergence as starting point 1.** Tools get learned. Tools sometimes get co-opted. Trauma gets recognized. Healing fights become possible. Game on.

"Then they start to learn how to speak their truth. They speak their truth. Some of those fights might get a little wild, but that usually doesn't last long for folks like that. And then they go into the, 'Oh, I can heal it.' Maybe they'll learn some skills. Maybe they'll do the same thing where the skills are weaponized as the other group. And then eventually they'll see that the trauma is healable. And then once you feel that, once you see that 3 or 4 times, you're addicted. It's just, it's the best thing ever." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

## The Common Middle and End

Both paths converge through:

**The trauma recognition.** The couple sees that the fights are being driven by old material. The partner stops being the cause and starts being the trigger.

**The healing fights.** Several fights in which one partner stays loving while the other's trauma surfaces. The trauma gets the loving witness it could not access in childhood. Both partners feel something integrate.

**The addiction to the healing.** Once this happens enough times -- Joe says three or four -- both partners want more of it. The fights become anticipated.

**Game on.** The destination orientation. The fights are the work. The work is welcomed.

## The Move That Bridges Stages: Non-Defensiveness

What allows the late stages to actually work is the capacity to drop defensiveness quickly. The fights stop escalating because both partners have built the skill of recognizing the trauma, agreeing with what is true in the partner's complaint, and moving back to contact.

"It requires each person to learn how to fall into non-defensiveness quickly. How do I go from defensive to non-defensive really quickly? And when you've done it 2, 3, 4, 5 times, it's really easy because it's so fucking cool." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The non-defensiveness is not "I will never disagree with you." It is the capacity to hear what is true in the partner's complaint without organizing around defending against it. The defense drops, the truth lands, the conversation moves forward.

"It's so good when you can move from, 'No, that's not -- ' 'Yeah, yeah, you're right. I see that.' Or, 'You're right. I see how you can see that. I see how that reality is true for you and mine is true for me. And I'm not saying yours is wrong or mine is right.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This skill is what the late stages depend on. Without it, the trauma-healing fights cannot happen because the defense prevents the truth from landing. With it, even hard fights can produce integration.

## The Late-Stage Reversion

One important wrinkle: even couples deep in the "game on" stage can hit periods of reversion to vicious fighting. This is not necessarily a regression. See [[tara-fights-that-destroyed-joes-narcissism]] for Joe's own example of a six-month period seven or eight years into his marriage with Tara, after years of healing fights, where the vicious fights came back. The retrospective recognition was that those fights produced an integration that gentler fights could not.

This pattern of late-stage reversion is described more broadly in [[feared-stage-of-relationship-deepening-can-feel-like-things-getting-worse]]. The arc is not monotonic. Deeper material can require harder surfacing. The hardness does not mean the arc has reversed; it can mean the next layer has arrived.

## The Move That Can Stall the Arc: Constant Processing

A specific stall point appears between the "tools weaponized" stage and the "trauma recognized" stage: constant processing. The couple has learned tools but not yet recognized the underlying trauma. They process everything. They are each other's therapists. Every interaction becomes a topic for examination.

Brett names this:

"There's a stage where they're like, 'Oh, there's actually, there are practices, there are tools, there are frameworks.' And then sometimes in that process, people can get kind of stuck in a sort of like a cycle of forever processing. Where, where sometimes they feel like any anytime they're interacting with a partner, they need to basically point out the most uncomfortable thing they can notice in the relationship at any time." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

Joe's reframe of the stall: the constant processing is the couple still trying to fix one another, with new tools.

"That one usually involves trying to be each other's therapist and you're still trying to fix one another. And one of the main things that you learn in Fighting Well is that you can only fix yourself. You can put zero attention on fixing the other person." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The exit from this stall is recognizing that you can only fix yourself. The partner's work is theirs. The processing-of-the-partner is itself the move that prevents the next stage.

## When This Framework Helps

- A couple is in a particular stage of fighting and wants to understand what comes next
- A couple in stage 3 (tools weaponized) wonders why the techniques have stopped working
- A couple in stage 4 (constant processing) wonders why all the work seems to produce more friction
- A coach is trying to locate a couple on the arc to understand what move is appropriate
- A person who started in "we never fight" is suddenly fighting and worries it means the relationship is failing

## Tone

Joe lays out the arc with the calm of having watched many couples walk it. The "game on" delivery has energy. The "no-fight, no-fight, no-fight, divorce" delivery has the weight of how common this trajectory is. The framework is offered as a map, not a moral hierarchy. Couples in earlier stages are not failing; they are at where they are. The point of the map is to make the next move visible, not to rank.
