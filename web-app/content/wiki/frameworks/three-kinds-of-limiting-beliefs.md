# Three Kinds of Limiting Beliefs

## The Framework

Limiting beliefs are categorized by how the mind plays the trick on you, not by their content. There are three structural types:

1. **False thought** -- believing a thought that is not factually true. The head's lie.
2. **Misinterpretation of experience** -- assigning a story to a body experience that the experience itself does not require. The emotional lie.
3. **Thinking you know the whole story** -- treating a partial view as complete and therefore refusing further information. The epistemic lie.

"There's other ways, but those are the three main categories I think about when I think about limiting beliefs." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

This is structurally different from [[seven-limiting-beliefs]], which catalogs the content of common limiting beliefs (perfectionism, improvement, shoulds, defense). The three kinds apply to any belief, regardless of content.

## 1. False Thought (The Head's Lie)

A thought you believe that is factually false or rests on a definition that does not hold. The lie is in the cognition itself.

The pattern: you take a statement to be true, organize a self-concept around it, and live as the person that statement implies. Sometimes the statement is checkable and turns out to be wrong. Sometimes the statement is true only under a definition the person never examined.

See [[joes-dad-failed-ceo]] for the canonical example. Joe's father believed he had failed as CEO. He restructured his life around that identity. Years later it turned out he had not failed at all by any standard a venture capitalist would use. He had built so much value that the parent company shut down his company to protect their main product, and the legal recovery from that shutdown returned hundreds of millions. The belief was false. The life had been organized around the false belief for fifteen to twenty years.

Even when the situation is more ambiguous, the false-thought category still applies. "Let's say he did fail. He did drive the company into the ground. So one person would believe that's a failure and one person would believe that's a learning experience. One person would believe, oh, now I'm more equipped to do it. One person would believe I'm not equipped to do it." The definition of failure is itself a place where the false thought lives. "You have to agree on the definition of failure. And then you have to think that that's you, it's not circumstantial. So all of those are potential limiting beliefs."

The intervention shape: locate the false statement, examine its definition, test it against evidence, see whether it holds.

## 2. Misinterpretation of Experience (The Emotional Lie)

The body has a sensation. The story labels it as something that does not match what the sensation actually requires. The lie is in the interpretation, not in the data.

See [[joe-ferris-wheel-ride]] for the canonical example. Joe is on a high-speed ride, frozen with terror. He hears someone else laughing. He starts laughing. The same sensation now reads as exhilaration. "Same experience, same person having the same experience, and one was an interpretation of the experience that was terror, and one was an interpretation of the experience which was, this is exhilarating."

The misinterpretation can take several forms:
- Resisting an experience that could be welcomed (the most common form).
- Welcoming an experience that should be resisted (less common but real).
- "I'll be happy when X happens" -- the misinterpretation that happiness is future-located when it is already available.

"A limiting belief can also be, I'll be happy when blank. It's the setup of future happiness instead of the recognition that the happiness is available to you right now, that you don't have to be different. You don't have to grow. You don't have to transform." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

Joe's list of common misinterpretations:
- Exercise is difficult
- I won't be consistent in my exercise
- Money comes from hard work
- I'll be happy once I learn to speak up and tell my truth
- No one wants my anger

Each one is a story that overlays an experience and tells the person what the experience means before they can find out for themselves.

The intervention shape: feel the experience without the interpretive overlay, let the body and the emotions move through it. See [[what-is-it-without-resistance]] and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

## 3. Thinking You Know the Whole Story (The Epistemic Lie)

You treat a partial view as complete. The lie is not in any specific claim but in the closure. You stop receiving new information because you have already decided.

See [[covey-subway-story]] for the canonical example. A man is irritated by a father whose three kids are acting out on the subway. He concludes the father is a bad parent. Then the father says, "I'm so sorry, they lost their mom just the other day." The whole story was different from what the partial story implied. The shift is instantaneous.

"The difference is kind of what I know, what I know I don't know, what I don't know I don't know. And that's what it is. Like this one, it's very much about just thinking that you understand it when you don't." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

The neurological frame: our sense organs cannot fully capture reality. We can distinguish two musical tones and still hear the chord. We mix two colors and cannot retrieve the originals from the third. Just at the level of sensing, our access is partial. Every viewpoint on any object is a different photograph from a different angle. See [[body-lies-as-much-as-mind]].

Iteration becomes possible only when this is admitted. "You could say, I've made my iPhone 1.0, I'm done, it was good. Or you could say, Yeah, and now I'm gonna make an iPhone 2.0 and a 3.0... So as we see new perspectives, we can iterate and we can create new things."

The intervention shape: find what you do not know. Find the angle you have not considered. Ask the person across from you what they see. Hold any view as a partial photograph rather than as the truth.

## Why This Categorization Helps

Each kind requires a different intervention. The same diagnostic moves will not surface all three types. The same dissolution practices will not dismantle all three.

- A false thought wants checking against evidence and examination of definitions. The intellect helps here.
- A misinterpretation wants the body to feel the experience directly without the labeling layer. The intellect cannot do this part.
- A whole-story belief wants new information and the willingness to receive it. Neither the intellect nor the body fixes this one alone -- it requires curiosity and the dropping of certainty.

The diagnostic of which type is at play is itself useful. See [[limiting-belief-detection-signals]].

## The Sister Trap: Knowing You Know the Whole Story About Limiting Beliefs

Brett raises this directly. If you take the framework and use it to assess everyone else's blindness ("oh, look, they have a bunch of limiting beliefs"), you have installed the framework as another whole-story belief. See [[using-limiting-belief-frame-to-judge-others]].

The framework is for noticing your own. Used on others, it becomes a position of superiority that is itself a limiting belief. The Tibetan Buddhists' [[comparative-mind]] applies directly here.
