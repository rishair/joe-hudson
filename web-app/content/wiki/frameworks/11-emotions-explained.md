# The 11 Human Emotions Explained (The One-Line Definition and Action Step for Each)

## The Framework

A compressed, public-facing teaching that walks through eleven emotions in roughly seven minutes. For each emotion, two pieces are delivered: a one-line definition that reframes what the emotion IS (so the listener stops fighting it and starts reading it), and a one-line action step that says what to do when that emotion arrives.

The eleven emotions in the order Joe teaches them:

1. **Fear**
2. **Anger**
3. **Guilt**
4. **Sadness**
5. **Shame**
6. **Jealousy**
7. **Anxiety**
8. **Disgust**
9. **Contempt**
10. **Joy**
11. **Love**

The framework presupposes [[wisdom-of-each-emotion]]: each emotion carries specific information, and the work is to read it rather than to manage it away. The framework's contribution is the compressed one-liner per emotion — a form short enough to remember and dense enough to redirect the person's relationship with the emotion.

## Why It Exists

The longer treatments — [[wisdom-of-each-emotion]], [[stages-of-emotional-development]], the dedicated articles for each emotion ([[anger]], [[fear-as-signal-of-care]], [[shame-as-disconnection]]) — assume a listener already convinced that emotions are worth working with. The 11-emotions framework assumes nothing. It is a public-facing video designed to be shared. A person who watches once should walk away with one durable instinct for each emotion that arrives in their life.

The frame is paired with the foundational premise that runs through all of Joe's emotion work: "Most of us were never taught what emotions were. We were actually actively taught or passively taught to avoid them." -- 11 Human Emotions Explained. The framework is the on-ramp that fixes that gap in seven minutes.

## The Eleven One-Liners (The Definitions)

### 1. Fear

"Fear is excitement without the breath, or it's a signal that there's something that you need to take care of or a way you need to take care of yourself." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The definition contains a fork. Fear is either an unfelt excitement (in which case the move is to breathe and let it become excitement) or a signal that something needs taking care of (in which case the move is to pause and identify what). The fork itself is the teaching: the listener stops asking "is this fear good or bad?" and starts asking "which kind of fear is this?"

See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]] and [[fear-vs-excitement]] for the full treatments.

### 2. Anger

"Anger at its core is clarity and determination. Think of it as a tube, and if that tube gets kinked or blocked, it might come out as, 'Nice dress,' or, 'You son of a da da da da da,' or it might turn out as, 'I'm not angry.' But unblocked, it's just clarity and determination." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The tube metaphor compresses the entire kinked-emotions teaching for anger. Clean anger is a small clear sentence: "this is what I will do." The passive-aggressive jab, the explosion, and the denial are all the same energy with kinks at different points in the tube.

See [[anger]] and [[kinked-emotions]] for the full treatments.

### 3. Guilt

"Guilt just means that we've stopped listening to our deepest wants." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The definition reframes guilt away from "I did something wrong" and toward "I am not listening to what I actually want." The action step follows directly: get back in touch with the want.

See [[guilt]] for the full treatment.

### 4. Sadness

"It's saying goodbye, and it's recognizing that there's something that isn't working for you anymore." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

Two pieces in one line: sadness as the close of something (saying goodbye), and sadness as the recognition that something has stopped working. Both pieces direct the work toward letting the transition happen rather than fighting it.

See [[sadness]] for the full treatment.

### 5. Shame

"Shame is a felt sense of disconnection that makes us stay inside of the tribe's rules and often freezes our emotions." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The compressed form holds three pieces: shame is a connection signal (not a verdict on character), it functions as a tribe-rule enforcer, and it freezes other emotional movement. The combination explains why shame is so durable and what to do about it.

See [[shame-as-disconnection]] and [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] for the full treatments.

### 6. Jealousy

"Jealousy is when you want something and you're pushing it away at the same time." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

Jealousy is structurally the simultaneous reach-and-recoil. The reach is the want. The recoil is the fear-of-loss (or the felt-impossibility of having it). The action step follows: feel both — the want and the fear — and the jealousy resolves itself into information.

See [[jealousy]] for the full treatment.

### 7. Anxiety

"Anxiety isn't that you have too much to do or that your life is overwhelming. It just means that there's something that you know needs doing and you're not doing it." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The definition specifically inverts the cultural reading of anxiety. The story "I have too much to do" is the symptom; the actual mechanism is "there is a specific thing I am not doing." The action step is to find and do (or feel) that specific thing.

See [[anxiety-three-definitions]] and [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]] for the full treatments.

### 8. Disgust

"Disgust happens when you're forced to swallow something that you didn't really want to take down." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The metaphor of "swallowing something toxic" generalizes from food to ideology, witnessed abuse, force-fed cultural narrative, or any input the person was made to take in against their preference. The action step is to identify the thing and remove the self or the community from contact with it.

See [[disgust]] for the full treatment.

### 9. Contempt

"Contempt is when you think you're better than somebody else, and hint, at your essence, you're not. And so what it's doing is it's showing you a blind spot." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

Contempt is a shadow signal. The trait you find contemptible in the other person is the trait you have not owned in yourself. The action step is the uncomfortable one: see how you have those same traits.

See [[contempt]] for the full treatment.

### 10. Joy

"Joy is the matriarch of a family of emotions, and she won't come into a house unless her children are welcome." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The matriarch metaphor is the compressed delivery of the entire joy-and-suppression mechanism. Joy is unavailable to the person who has shut the door on the difficult emotions, because the suppression is global, not selective. The action step: welcome all your feelings and all parts of yourself and other people.

See [[joy-as-matriarch]] for the full treatment.

### 11. Love

"Love is an emotion that allows us to feel so safe that we can play out and heal our attachment issues." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The definition gives love a specific functional role: love is the container of safety inside which the unfinished attachment work of childhood gets to surface and complete. The action step contains a setup move: make an agreement with your partner and your friends that "our shit's gonna come up here and we're gonna use it as ways to heal."

See [[love-as-deep-welcoming]] and [[love-as-container-for-attachment-healing]] for the full treatments.

## The Eleven Action Steps (Compressed)

A second compressed form of the framework: just the action step per emotion. The minimal-viable response to each emotion as it arrives.

- **Fear:** Breathe. Identify whether this is excitement or a need-for-care signal. Take the indicated action.
- **Anger:** Read the boundary. What needs a no? What needs protecting?
- **Guilt:** Ask "if I couldn't feel guilty right now, what else would I notice? What else do I want?" Or: "how do I want to do that thing differently next time?"
- **Sadness:** Bring attention to your heart. Honor whatever is there. Let it move through.
- **Shame:** Find a connection somewhere first — to your heart, to yourself, to a partner, to anyone or anything. Connection precedes everything else with shame.
- **Jealousy:** Don't buy the story that "this is terrible and I'm terrible for feeling it." Feel the longing, the want, and the fear of loss underneath.
- **Anxiety:** Identify the action step that's been avoided. It might be an external action, an internal feeling that's been suppressed, or an authentic expression that's been held in.
- **Disgust:** Ask "what have I been force-fed that's toxic for me?" Remove yourself, or the community, from contact with it.
- **Contempt:** See how you have those exact traits of the person you have contempt for. They are usually in your shadow.
- **Joy:** Feel all your feelings. Welcome all of yourself and other people. Joy follows.
- **Love:** Feel love. Lean into the fact that the attachment stuff that surfaces is the surface getting healed. Set up the agreement with your people that this is the deal.

The action steps are deliberately small. Each one is something a person can do in the moment the emotion arrives. The framework is built for in-the-moment use, not for retrospective analysis.

## The Specific Action-Step Questions for Guilt

The guilt action step deserves expansion because the two questions Joe pairs with it form a small two-move sequence that is unusually clean:

"You can ask yourself, 'If I couldn't feel guilty right now, what else would I notice? What else do I want? What else do I feel?' Or you can ask yourself, 'How do I want to do that thing differently next time?'" -- 11 Human Emotions Explained

The first question removes the guilt as a noise filter and reveals the underlying want (which is what the guilt was covering). The second question takes the guilt as a signal of a non-aligned action and routes it forward into a corrective intention rather than backward into self-recrimination. Either question alone breaks the guilt loop. See [[guilt]] for the full treatment.

## The Mechanism Across All Eleven

The eleven definitions share a common structure. In each case, the emotion is reframed from "a thing happening to me that I have to manage" to "a signal pointing at something specific that I can read and respond to." The action step is the response that turns the signal into wisdom rather than into noise.

This is the same mechanism described in [[wisdom-of-each-emotion]] and in [[emotions-as-road-signs]]: emotions point rather than push, and reading what they point at produces the right action. The 11-emotions framework is the compressed form of that mechanism, applied to each of the eleven in turn.

## What the Framework Replaces

The default cultural picture: emotions are good or bad, and the work is to have more of the good ones and fewer of the bad ones. This picture is wrong in both directions. The "bad" emotions carry information that is needed; the "good" emotions cannot be produced by chasing them.

The framework substitutes a different picture: every emotion is doing exactly what it is supposed to do. The work is to read each one and respond. The "good" emotions arrive as a consequence of the system being welcomed; the "bad" emotions resolve as a consequence of being felt and read.

See [[joy-as-matriarch]] for the specific mechanism by which chasing joy fails, and [[wisdom-of-each-emotion]] for the parallel principle for the difficult emotions.

## In Coaching

The framework is most useful as a reference for matching a presenting concern to a starting reframe. When a person reports a specific emotion they are wrestling with, the one-line definition for that emotion is often the entry point. The definition lands; the person sees the emotion differently; the work begins from a different posture.

The order of teaching is also instructive. Fear and anger come first because they are the most-misunderstood and the most-suppressed. Guilt, sadness, shame come next because they are often the deepest layers under the surface presentations. Jealousy, anxiety, disgust, contempt come fourth because they are the more specific cases. Joy and love come last because they cannot be worked on directly; they are produced by the work on the others.

When using the framework live: pick the one or two emotions the person is most stuck on. Deliver the one-line definition. Watch what shifts. Then offer the action step.

## Tone

Public-facing, compressed, slightly playful. The "Nice dress" example for kinked anger is delivered with light comic timing. The "hint, at your essence, you're not" for contempt is direct and slightly cheeky. The teaching does not assume the listener has any prior context in emotional work. The whole framework is built to land in seven minutes for someone who has never thought about their emotions as carrying information before.
