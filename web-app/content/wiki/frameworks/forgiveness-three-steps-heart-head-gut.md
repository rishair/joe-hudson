# The Three-Step Forgiveness Process (Heart, Head, Gut: Emotions, Curiosity, Boundaries)

## Definition

A three-step framework for the work of forgiveness, organized around the three centers (heart, head, gut). When the three steps are complete, forgiveness arrives as an inevitable byproduct. The framework is descriptive: this is what people who actually forgive do, not a technique to deploy.

"I do think forgiveness is like a 3-step process. There's probably other microsteps one could take. The first would be looking at the emotions under it... The second step would be more like the head step, and that would be getting curious... And then the third part would be, oh, what are the boundaries I need going forward? That would be like the gut part... And usually once you have the curiosity, the emotional processing, and those clear boundaries, forgiveness is an inevitable byproduct." -- Tara Howley, I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

## The Three Steps

### 1. Heart (Emotions)

Be with the emotions that the harm brought up. Whatever your way of processing emotions is, do that work here. The typical contents:

- Hurt at what was done
- Anger at the person who did it
- Fear of it happening again
- Resentment that has built up over time
- Sometimes grief at what was lost

"The first would be looking at the emotions under it, like, 'Oh, when so-and-so said such and such a thing, I was really hurt,' or, 'I'm really angry,' or, 'I'm scared.' And usually there's resentment, there's anger, fear of the thing happening again, which is where boundaries come in. So the first step would be being with the emotions that got brought up, processing the anger, processing the hurt, being with the fear and processing that, whatever your emotional processing patterns are, and being with that, processing it would be a first step." -- Tara Howley

This step cannot be skipped. Trying to forgive while the anger is still in the body produces the coerced-forgiveness failure mode. See [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]]. The heart has to be cleared as a heart-work first; the head and gut work cannot do its job.

### 2. Head (Curiosity)

Get curious about what made the other person do what they did. What were they carrying? What was their wound? What was their history? What was their state in the moment? The move is from the closed mind of the held-story ("they are X") to the open mind of wonder ("I wonder why they are X").

"The second step would be more like the head step, and that would be getting curious, like, 'oh, I wonder what made that person do XYZ. I wonder, like, what was going on for them? Or what — why would they say such a thing?' Or what — or even asking them what was happening for them, getting curious, coming to a state of wonder about what wounds or wisdom led them to say or do the action they took." -- Tara Howley

The function: the held-story is what keeps the heart closed. Wonder dissolves the story by making space for what is true rather than what has been concluded. The result is compassion — not as moral effort, but as the natural by-product of seeing the other person as a full human being shaped by their own history.

"Wonder is really about coming to compassion for the other, but compassion for self." -- Tara Howley

The wonder is two-directional. As you become curious about the other person, you also become curious about yourself: what wound of yours met theirs, what your part was, what you were not yet able to see. The compassion that arrives covers both.

See [[wonder]] for the underlying concept.

### 3. Gut (Boundaries)

Find the boundary you need going forward so the harm cannot happen again. What will you not allow? What will you do if a similar dynamic begins to form? Where is the line?

"And then the third part would be, oh, what are the boundaries I need going forward? That would be like the gut part. Like, oh, I don't ever wanna be alone in a room with that person again. I get that boundary. Or I'm not gonna spend a week with my, you know, my Aunt Alice. Like, that sounds awful on my vacation. I'm never gonna do that. Great. You get your boundaries." -- Tara Howley

The boundary is what allows the open heart to be safe. Without the boundary, the held resentment is doing the protection job (poorly, at high cost). With the boundary, the resentment can release because the protection is now structural rather than charge-based.

See [[boundaries]] and [[resentment-takes-the-place-of-boundaries]].

The boundary is iterative. The first boundary you try may not be the right one. The right boundary often takes several attempts to find. The deepest boundary may turn out to be larger than the specific instance — not "I won't be alone with this person" but "I get to have my boundaries, period." See [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]].

## Why the Order Matters

The steps stack. Each one prepares the ground for the next.

- The emotion-work makes the curiosity possible. A heart full of unfelt anger cannot wonder about the other person's wound; it can only confirm its own story.
- The curiosity makes the boundary findable. A mind still locked in the wrongness-frame draws the boundary as a punishment or an exile, not as care.
- The boundary makes the forgiveness safe. An open heart without the boundary is the configuration that gets re-harmed, which produces the next round of resentment.

Skipping a step does not produce forgiveness; it produces a state that looks like forgiveness but collapses under any test.

## Forgiveness as Inevitable Byproduct

The unusual feature of the framework: forgiveness is not the goal. It is what shows up when the three obstacles are cleared. Aiming at forgiveness directly is the failure mode.

"Usually once you have the curiosity, the emotional processing, and those clear boundaries, forgiveness is an inevitable byproduct." -- Tara Howley

This is what makes the framework structurally different from "try to forgive." The work is not to forgive; the work is to process the emotions, find the curiosity, and draw the boundary. The forgiveness happens by itself.

This parallels the principle in [[separation-wonder-reality-experiments-love]] that love arrives at the top of a stack rather than being achievable by direct effort. Both arcs share the architecture: do the actual preparatory work, and the desired state emerges naturally. Effort to produce the state directly produces a brittle imitation.

The compactness of this framework (3 steps vs 5) reflects its different scope. The 5-stage path applies to anything you are oppressed by; the 3-step process is specifically for restoring an open heart after harm.

## How To Know You Are Done

The diagnostic is somatic and cognitive. See [[track-forgiveness-somatically]] for the full diagnostic. In brief:

- The heart feels open when you think about them
- The mind no longer has a story (no "they are X")
- The gut is unclenched (the boundary is held, not braced)
- You can feel love or basic goodwill for them
- You do not feel obligation to be around them

If any of these are not yet true, the corresponding step is not yet complete. The diagnostic tells you which step to return to.

## Internal Forgiveness Uses the Same Framework

The framework applies to self-forgiveness too. The three steps run as:

1. Heart: be with the shame, guilt, regret about your own action
2. Head: get curious about what was operating in you when you did what you did — what wound, what fear, what un-held boundary
3. Gut: find the boundary you can now hold that you could not hold before, so the dynamic does not repeat

See [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]] for the spoken practice that can be paired with the heart-step. See [[forgiveness-as-self-care]] for the framing that makes the inward version structurally identical to the outward version.

## What Each Step Looks Like When It Is Going Wrong

**Heart step failing:** You try to skip to wonder before the anger has moved. The wonder is forced and does not produce compassion. The body is still angry; the mind is performing curiosity. The give-away is that the curiosity is intellectual ("they must have had a hard childhood") rather than alive ("I actually wonder what was happening in them").

**Head step failing:** You feel the emotions but the story does not budge. You can name what they did but cannot get curious about why. The wonder is being blocked by either insufficient emotional clearance (return to step 1) or by a structural commitment to the wrongness-frame. See [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]] for the structural work on the wrongness-frame.

**Gut step failing:** You feel the feelings and you have the curiosity but the boundary is not arriving. Often this is because the boundary you are looking for is too small. The real boundary is larger than the specific instance ("I get my boundaries, period") and arrives only when you let yourself see what you actually need. Sometimes the boundary requires giving up something you have been telling yourself you must have (the relationship, the position, the role). The fear of that loss is what is blocking the boundary from forming.

## What This Framework Replaces

- The "just forgive" demand. The framework refuses the direct ask and replaces it with the structural work.
- The "feel compassion for them" instruction. Compassion is downstream of the wonder, not produced by trying to feel it.
- The "set a boundary so you don't have to feel the feelings" technique. The boundary work is step 3, not step 1.
- The "forgive and forget" frame. The framework does not require forgetting; it requires processing.
- The "they had their reasons" jump. The reasons are seen at step 2 (head/curiosity), only after step 1 (heart/emotions) is done. See [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]].

## Tone

This framework is offered as actual instruction. Each step is named clearly; each step has its own work; the order is structural. The tone is practical and embodied, not theological. It refuses both the demanding tone of religious forgiveness and the squishy tone of self-help compassion.
