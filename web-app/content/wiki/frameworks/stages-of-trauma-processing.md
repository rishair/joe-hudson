# Stages of Trauma Processing

## The Framework

Trauma is not processed in a single act. It moves through stages, each of which is a different relationship the system can have to the original event. The stages are not strict — they overlap, they recur, a person can be at different stages with different aspects of the same trauma — but the sequence is recognizable, and naming the stage someone is at lets a coach (or AI coach) meet them where they actually are.

The stages, in approximate order:

1. **Silence.** The trauma is not talked about. The system has not yet found a way to give it any external form. The person may not have language for it at all. Often there is no awareness that it is "trauma"; it is just what happened, sealed off.

2. **Joking.** The trauma starts to find external form, but only inside the safety of humor. The person can refer to it sideways, lightly, with the distance that the joke creates. The joking is a real stage, not a failure mode — it is the first version of the material moving from sealed to mentionable. See [[joking-about-trauma-is-a-processing-stage]].

3. **Talking.** The trauma can be spoken about more directly. The person can tell the story. The body, however, often stays out of it — the narration is reportorial, the feelings are not yet arriving. This is what Joe means when he says "if they've processed it a thousand times, they'll just say it offhandedly, like, yeah, this is what happened in my childhood."

4. **Real processing (feeling the suspended emotion).** The body starts to come online. The person feels what they could not feel when the event happened. The arc shows up in session as cracking — voice changes, hands move, tears arrive, the body protectively positions itself. This is the work the rest of the framework points at. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

5. **Ancillary processing.** Most of the central event has been felt. What remains are the surrounding wounds — the people who did not see what was happening, the parent who did not save you, the teachers who did not ask the questions, the absence of help that was just as injuring as the abuse itself. See [[ancillary-trauma-needs-processing-too]].

The stages are not a hierarchy of correctness. A person at the joking stage is doing the work. A person at the talking stage is doing the work. A person at the feeling stage is doing the work. The recognition of which stage allows the response to fit.

## The Joke as Useful Stage (Not Just as Defense)

The conventional reading of joking about trauma is "deflection" or "avoidance." That reading is incomplete. Joking is also the bridge from sealed to mentionable. The person who can joke about it has done something that the person at the silence stage has not yet done.

"Probably it started with not talking about it, then it was joking about it, and now he's actually starting starting to do some of the real processing about it." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The implication for a coach (and for the person doing the work): the joking is not a stage to be embarrassed about or to bypass. It is the stage in which the material first becomes available. The person who skips it (because someone shamed them about the joking) often skips the access point and ends up not processing at all.

The joke is also a useful diagnostic for the coach. A person who is only able to joke about the trauma is showing exactly where they are. The coach does not push past the joke. The coach welcomes the joke as the doorway and waits for the next door to be reached for.

See [[joking-about-trauma-is-a-processing-stage]] for the developed principle.

## Why the Talking Stage Often Plateaus

Many people get to the talking stage and stay there for years. The story has been told a hundred times — to therapists, to partners, to friends. Telling the story does not process it. The story is the report; the processing is the feeling.

The plateau is recognizable. The person can recite the story without affect. They can answer questions about it. They can name the moves of the abuser, the dates, the locations. None of this contacts the unfelt material. The mind has built a clean account that lets it walk around the actual experience.

The plateau breaks when something pierces the report and the body comes online. Often this is not the central event — it is an ancillary detail that the mind had not prepared a defense for. The unguarded entry is the doorway. See [[what-cracks-you-open-shows-whats-processable-now]].

## The Real Processing Stage Has a Specific Texture

When someone is doing the actual feeling, the texture is recognizable:

- The voice changes. It thins, slows, catches, breaks.
- The body positions itself protectively — head covered, head turned to the side, leg raised across the body, shoulders curving in.
- The hands move differently. There is often a fluttering, a rubbing, a wringing.
- Eye contact shifts. Sometimes more, often less. The person may close their eyes.
- The narration falters. Sentences do not complete. The story stops being the priority.

These are not signs that something is going wrong. They are signs that the body is doing the work the report was preventing. The right move at this stage is not to make the person comfortable — it is to be with them in a way that lets the work continue. See [[how-to-receive-someones-trauma-sharing]].

## The Ancillary Processing Stage Is Often Underweighted

Once the central event has been felt, there is often a layer that the mind treats as secondary but the system treats as primary: the wounds around the wound. The mother who did not save you. The father who was supposed to protect you. The teachers who never asked. The siblings who knew and could not help. The whole environment of not-being-seen that wrapped around the central event and made it survivable only through the configuration that the person is now trying to undo.

Joe's specific observation in the Sean clip: after processing the violence, the fighter does not go to another event of violence — he goes to school, and to the teacher who took his desk away because he was falling asleep, with no one asking what was happening at home. The teacher-not-asking-anything story is what surfaces, not another beating story.

"He's talking about his — the massive abuse that's going on in the school, and he quickly starts talking about how he basically wasn't seen, right? No teacher came up to him and said, hey, what's going on? ... When I'm working with people through their traumas, one of the things that I notice all the time is that, one, if they were seen, something changes, but the not being seen in it causes just as much pain. And so usually when someone's processing trauma, the first thing is to talk about it. The second thing is to like deeply feel all the hurt and the pain. Then there's all the ancillary hurt and pain. Like the mom that didn't save you or the teachers that didn't come to you. All of that actually has to be processed so for you to not be living a life as if that trauma is still occurring all the time." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The ancillary stage matters because the person who has processed only the central event still has the surrounding wounds running in the background. The configuration partially relaxes; the rest of the configuration is held in place by the unprocessed surround.

See [[ancillary-trauma-needs-processing-too]] for the developed principle.

## How a Coach Uses the Stage Diagnostic

The diagnostic shapes the response.

**Silence stage.** The coach does not try to extract the story. The coach establishes the conditions where the story might one day be told — the consistent presence, the absence of judgment, the demonstrated ability to be with difficult material. This is the long game.

**Joking stage.** The coach laughs with the joke without minimizing the material. The coach does not push past the joke into the seriousness underneath. The coach lets the joke be the first form the material takes. The coach also lets there be space for the material to take a different form when the person is ready.

**Talking stage.** The coach listens to the story and notices what is and is not arriving in the body. The coach may gently invite a felt entry: "what's happening in your body right now as you tell that?" The coach does not force the body online — that is one of the failure modes that produces dissociation. The invitation is offered; the person decides.

**Real processing stage.** The coach is present in a way that allows the work to continue. Not comforting in a way that interrupts the feeling. Not interpreting in a way that pulls the person back into the mind. Just being there, available, not making the difficulty go away. See [[how-to-receive-someones-trauma-sharing]] and [[be-with-them-without-trying-to-fix-or-diagnose]].

**Ancillary processing stage.** The coach pays attention to what comes after the central event in the person's free-associated material. The teacher story, the mother story, the friend-who-didn't-do-anything story. These are not detours. They are the next layer. The coach treats them with the same gravity as the central event.

## Stages Are Not Monotonic

A person who has processed a piece of trauma to the feeling stage may still be at the joking stage with a different piece. The same person may revisit a piece they had processed years ago and find a new layer that needs new feeling. The stages are not a one-way ladder. They are the description of where a particular piece of material is at this moment.

This matters for the coach's expectation. A client who has done significant work and can talk freely about most of their material may still arrive at silence about one specific element. The silence is not regression. It is the location of the next piece of work.
