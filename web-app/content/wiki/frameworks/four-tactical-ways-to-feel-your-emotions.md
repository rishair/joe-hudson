# The Four Tactical Ways to Feel Your Emotions

## The Framework

A four-part on-ramp Joe uses in public-facing teaching to give beginners concrete, actionable entry points into feeling. Each of the four operates at a different layer of the system. Together they cover the territory a person new to this work needs to start the practice.

The four:

1. **Recognize the emotion when it is happening.**
2. **Breathe (and breathe deeply).**
3. **Feel it in the body.**
4. **Express it without becoming overwhelmed.**

The framework presupposes the foundational principle: "There is no time when you're awake when a feeling isn't happening. You might not note it, you might not register it, but there is always an emotional sensation happening in your body at all times." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

If the person believes feelings come and go like weather, they will spend their attention waiting for feelings to arrive. The framework only works once the person accepts that the feelings are already here. See [[you-cant-let-go-of-emotions-only-thoughts]] for the principle that emotion is like breath -- always currently occurring.

## Why It Exists

The longer teachings -- [[emotional-inquiry]], [[stages-of-emotional-development]], [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] -- assume a person already convinced that feeling is worth the work. The four tactical ways assume nothing. The framework is the entry point for someone who has never deliberately felt an emotion in their life, or who has been told their whole life that being emotional is weakness, or who has reverse-engineered their feelings from external clues for so long that the body itself has gone quiet.

The framework is built to be told in five minutes and remembered. It is not the practice. It is the door to the practice.

## The Stakes (Why Bother)

The argument before the framework: emotions are not optional decoration. The emotional center of the brain is what makes decisions. A person not in touch with their emotions is still making emotional decisions -- but unconsciously.

"The problem with that is that the emotional center of our brain is what makes decisions. So if we can't be aware of our emotions, we're often making decisions in a very unconscious way. The great way to think about that is how many times have you made a decision because you wanted to feel good or because you wanted to not feel like a loser or because you wanted to feel loved or you didn't wanna feel rejected or you wanted to feel like a success or you didn't wanna feel like a failure?" -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

See [[decisions-are-emotional]] for the full principle. The four tactical ways are the practical answer to the diagnostic: if your decisions are being routed around your emotional center, here is how to get the wire reconnected.

## How the Loss of Access Happened

The framework includes a brief origin story for why the person has the gap. The point is to normalize rather than pathologize: most adults lost access through the same mechanisms.

"We stop becoming aware of our emotions when those emotions have been scary to feel. So if you take a kid that was physically abused as an adult and you put a quarter in one hand and a key in the other hand, and you have them close it without telling them which is which, they often won't be able to tell you which is the quarter and which is the key because they have learned how not to feel their body because they were physically abused. If your emotions weren't safe, if you were emotionally told it wasn't okay to have an emotion, or you were blamed or bribed or beat or made fun of for having emotions, or just told to settle down, settle down, anything that told you it wasn't safe to feel your feelings, you've learned to cut them off." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The quarter-and-key example carries the load: the human system shuts off what was unsafe to access, and the shutdown survives the original conditions. See [[childhood-imprinting]] and [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] for the broader treatment. The framework's job is to begin reversing the shutdown.

## Way 1: Recognize the Emotion When It Is Happening

The first task is detection. Most people have lost the wire from body to awareness. They are having emotions; they do not register them. Detection has multiple possible entry points:

- **Through thinking patterns.** Joe's primary example: binary thinking is the cognitive signature of fear. "Every time that my thinking was binary, black or white, good or bad, buy the car or don't buy the car, I knew I was in fear. And so I could recognize my fear, not from my body, but because of the way that my thoughts were going." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions. See [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]] for the full read.

- **Through other people noticing.** "Maybe it's because your wife tells you, 'Oh, you look upset.'" The body broadcasts continuously; other people can read the signal before the person can. Their reads are usable data.

- **Through actions taken.** A person who has just made a decision they cannot explain often was making an emotional decision. The decision is the evidence the emotion was operating.

- **Through scheduled checking.** "Every 2, 3 hours, stop and say, 'What am I feeling right now?' And even if you can't put a word to it, like scared, you can say, 'Oh, I'm feeling like my tummy is excited or zippy, and I feel like my chest is constricted.' Because the thing about emotions is that they all happen in the body. So if you can just start labeling how you feel in your body, you're gonna start being able to identify your emotions." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The body-sensation entry is critical. People who cannot name emotions can often name body sensations. The body sensations are the emotions. Naming them as sensation first builds the wire to naming them as emotion later.

## Way 2: Breathe (Deeply)

Breathing relaxes muscles. Emotions live in muscles. Constricted muscles are how the body stops feeling. Loosened muscles let feeling through.

"When we breathe, and particularly when we breathe deeply, what we're doing is relaxing our muscles. And the thing about emotions is that they affect our muscles. And the more constricted we are, the less we're feeling or the less we're trying to feel." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The framework names specific muscles to target: jaw, chest, belly.

"Some particular muscles that we hold to not feel are the jaw muscles. So we chomp down on an emotion, the chest, which often makes us cave in. Our tummy is another one that will hold tight to not feel a feeling. So if we can breathe and loosen those things up, just right now you can loosen up your belly, loosen up your jaw, and you'll notice the sensations in your body become more alive. You can feel them more." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

See [[emotions-live-in-muscles]] for the full principle. The four-way framework's contribution is the explicit checklist of where to look: jaw, chest, belly. The chin-up signature ([[chin-up-as-emotion-block]]) is the dedicated read for the jaw and head position.

A live demonstration is built into the teaching: "I want you to stop feeling completely. Stop all feeling from your body. And what you've noticed by now, if you've really done it, if you've really tried to stop feeling, you'll notice that your muscles are constricting. Grr, grr, grr." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions. The demonstration is the short form of [[try-to-feel-scared-then-stop-experiment]].

The caution: breath can also be used to repress (see [[dont-use-breath-to-repress]]). Deep breathing in service of feeling is different from deep breathing in service of getting the feeling to go away. The motivation is the difference.

## Way 3: Feel It in the Body

Once the muscles are loosened, the third task is to direct attention into the body's sensations themselves. This is the practice. The previous two were the setup.

"Now, all of this accumulates to the third tactical way, which is to notice the sensations in your body. Now, there's lots of ways to do it. The one that we really like is called emotional inquiry... what you'll start noticing is that emotions have a particular sensation in the body. There's some great science on this, even done with heat maps where they're showing the heat in bodies and how many people have similar heat maps if they're feeling the same way." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The framework's named entry is [[emotional-inquiry]]. The framework's articulation of how to do it is brief but precise:

"The more you can do that with a curiosity, a sense of wonder, like a little kid who's like picking up a turtle, the more you're gonna be able to feel it. It's not just putting your attention in your body here, it's about investigating and like finding out and digging around and then letting those sensations move." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The turtle metaphor lands here in compressed form. The full treatment in [[emotional-inquiry]] expands the 3-year-old-with-the-frog calibration, the inside-out vs top-down distinction, the three-stage architecture. The framework's contribution is the on-ramp: just look at the sensation the way a kid looks at a turtle, and let it move.

## Way 4: Express It Without Becoming Overwhelmed

Expression is named as the most important of the four. Without expression, the work stays incomplete. The body needs to do what the emotion is asking it to do, and that doing is the discharge.

"I think the most important thing about learning how to feel your feelings is to express them. And this is going to feel really weird because there's a lot of people out there who've been taught their whole life that being emotional is crap. It's not good. It makes you weak or whatever the stories are. And they're all BS, right? So having your emotions and being able to feel them, not being controlled by them, but being able to feel them and express them creates a tremendous amount of clarity." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The framework offers two specific expression practices.

**The fake-it-in-the-woods method.** Joe's own story is the central case. He had not cried in fourteen years. He went into the woods and faked crying for two or three months. Then it became real. See [[joes-crying-story]] for the full story and [[daily-emotional-yoga]] for the broader practice.

"You could do it the way that I did, which is somewhat ridiculous... I took that photograph out, I looked at it, I put it on my desk to remind me that I needed to cry. Somehow I knew that that was really important, and I still didn't cry. So then I thought, oh, I'm gonna practice crying. And so I would go off into the woods, and then I would hike off trail far away from anybody, and I would fake cry. And I did this, I think, for like 2 or 3 months until finally real tears came out. And when I was finished, I felt so much relief. I felt so good." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

**The actor practicing a scene method.** A more general entry point that works for any emotion.

"Another way to do it, which I think is a great way to do it, is to pretend you're an actor whose job it is is to have a very convincing emotional scene, an anger scene or a sadness scene or a scared scene. And you are not the person, you're just the actor doing it. So it's very convincing, but it's not really you. So it's not personal. And that's a great way to allow your body to remember how to have that expression." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

See [[actor-giving-most-convincing-performance]] for the full practice. The framework's contribution is the public-facing version: pretend to be an actor whose job is to give the most convincing performance, and the body remembers how.

The "without becoming overwhelmed" qualifier matters. The expression is for the body's discharge, not for anyone else. Joe is explicit:

"I'm never saying get angry at somebody else, I'm just saying get angry in the privacy of a room where nobody's getting hurt." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] for the anti-pattern this guards against. The expression must have a contained channel, not a relational target.

## What This Framework Replaces

The default cultural picture of "managing emotions" or "controlling emotions" is exactly what produces the unfelt material that the framework is designed to release. The framework substitutes: feeling is not the loss of control; feeling is what becomes possible when the body is no longer doing the work of holding the emotion back.

"Once your body remembers it and it feels the relief and it finds out that sadness doesn't last forever, that anger doesn't destroy everything... or that fear doesn't make you incapable, it makes you more capable when you really feel it. When you find those things out, when your body feels those things, then the emotions become more and more fluid." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The body has to verify, in its own experience, that the feared consequences of feeling do not occur. The framework's expression step is the verification. Each successful expression deposits evidence in the body that the emotion was survivable. The deposit compounds. Over time the body's chronic holding releases and the system arrives at [[emotional-fluidity]].

## Overwhelm Is the Symptom of Skipping the Framework

A specific diagnostic Joe surfaces inside the framework: nearly all experiences of overwhelm are experiences of unfelt emotion that has accumulated.

"Almost every time that you're overwhelmed, what's actually happening is that you have an emotion that isn't being felt. It's not just that things are busy. It's because you go like, 'Ugh,' and you're holding and you're tightening down, not feeling an emotional experience instead of breathing in and actually allowing that emotion to move." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The implication: when overwhelm arises, the four-way framework is the live intervention. Recognize (way 1) what is being avoided. Breathe (way 2) to loosen the holding. Feel it in the body (way 3) where the emotion is currently being clamped. Express (way 4) what wants to move. The overwhelm is the signal that the four ways have been skipped. See [[overwhelm]] for the full concept.

## The Mechanism Across All Four Ways

The four ways are not four separate techniques. They are four moves in a single sequence the system is built to perform:

- The body is generating an emotional sensation (always; see way 1).
- The body's muscles are constricting around the sensation to keep it from being felt (way 2 addresses this).
- The sensation can be attended to directly once the muscles soften (way 3).
- The body needs to do what the emotion is asking it to do, and the doing is the discharge (way 4).

Skip any one and the system stays incomplete. Way 1 without way 4 is observation without integration. Way 4 without way 1 is acting out without awareness. Way 2 without way 3 is relaxation that does not become feeling. Way 3 without way 2 is attention pointed at clamped sensation that cannot be felt because the clamping is still in place.

The framework's value is the sequencing. A person doing all four in order produces a complete cycle. A person doing two in isolation produces partial results that often confuse them about whether the work is doing anything.

## Where the Framework Lands in the Larger Methodology

The four tactical ways are the on-ramp version of:

- [[emotional-inquiry]]: the live moment-by-moment practice (way 3 elaborated)
- [[daily-emotional-yoga]]: the scheduled dedicated practice (way 4 elaborated)
- [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: the core healing mechanism (what the four ways produce over time)
- [[stages-of-emotional-development]]: the longer arc (the four ways move a person from stage 2 or 4 toward stage 5)
- [[11-emotions-explained]]: the parallel public-facing framework that covers eleven specific emotions one at a time with a definition and an action step per emotion

The framework is not a substitute for these. It is the door. Once a person has been through the four ways enough times to trust the mechanism, the deeper teachings become available.

## Complement With the 11-Emotions Framework

The four-tactical-ways framework is the on-ramp to feeling any emotion (the general practice). The [[11-emotions-explained]] framework is the on-ramp to reading specific emotions (the catalog of what each one is and what to do with it). The two are designed to be used together.

A person who has the four tactical ways can feel whatever arrives. A person who has the eleven one-liners can read what arrives. Combined: feel it, read it, respond. The first framework gives the somatic practice; the second framework gives the conceptual map of what the somatic practice is making available. The AI coach can deploy either one alone or both in sequence depending on what the person is missing.

## Tone

Public-facing, slightly self-deprecating, accessible. Joe presents this as practical advice without spiritual framing. The crying-in-the-woods story is offered with explicit acknowledgement that it sounds ridiculous. The "BS" dismissal of "being emotional is crap" is direct. The teaching does not assume the listener has any prior context in emotional work, contemplative practice, or therapy.
