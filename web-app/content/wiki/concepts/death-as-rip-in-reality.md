# Death as a Little Rip in Reality

## Definition

The death of someone deeply loved produces a felt discontinuity. The standard framework of "person is alive, then person is dead" does not accurately describe what the bereaved experiences. The bereaved experiences something more like a tear in the fabric of how things are supposed to work. The person is gone in one sense and not gone in another sense. The relationship continues in a way that does not match the standard model of relationships. The body keeps feeling them. The love keeps flowing. The "us" does not collapse on the death-date the way the standard model predicts.

This is the rip. Reality has a small tear in it where the dead person used to be. The tear is not closed. It will not close in the way the bereaved keeps expecting it to close.

"It's like a little rip inside of reality." -- Joe Hudson, She Came Asking to Let Go, She Left with a Love That Doesn't End

## The Reframe

The standard interpretation of the rip is that something is wrong with the bereaved. They are not "moving on." They are stuck. They have not done enough grief work. They are clinging. They are "still in it."

The reframe: the rip is real. The bereaved is not misperceiving. The relationship really does continue. The love really does flow. The "us" really does still organize the bereaved's life. The rip is what death actually is for someone who deeply loved.

What the rip is showing is not pathology. It is information.

"It, it seems like what it's showing you is, is a love that's beyond you." -- Joe Hudson, She Came Asking to Let Go, She Left with a Love That Doesn't End

The rip is the doorway. The standard model of reality says love is between two specific people who are both alive. The rip in that model is showing the bereaved that the model was incomplete. There is a love that is beyond two-specific-people-both-alive. The rip is where that love becomes visible.

## What the Rip Reveals

The rip reveals that the love was never purely about the two people. The love was real, and it was about the two people, and it was also more than the two people. The death does not destroy the love. The death shows that the love was always larger than the framework that contained it.

The bereaved had been operating inside the standard model: this person is here, I love this person, the love is between us. After the death, the model breaks. The love does not stop. The model is wrong. The rip is the evidence.

What the love actually is becomes visible only after the rip opens. It is non-personal. It is for everything. The specific person was a particular case of it — a doorway, a container, a recipient — but the love itself was never confined to that case. See [[the-dead-as-conduit-to-non-personal-love]] for the operational form of what to do with this recognition.

## Why the Rip Is Usually Experienced as Weirdness

The standard cultural script for death does not contain the rip. The script is: person dies, you grieve, eventually you stop grieving, you move on. The script presumes the relationship ends at death and the grief is the residue of the ended relationship.

The bereaved who experiences the rip experiences something the script does not name. The relationship continues. The conversations continue. The decisions are still made with the dead person in mind. The body still reaches for them in the morning. The love is still flowing.

Without language for this, the bereaved experiences it as wrong. "I should be over this." "Why am I still talking to him?" "Why do I still feel him?" "Why does my life still feel like ours?"

In the source session, the widow said: "I still feel like it's not real. I'm still in that. It maybe it'll take years and years of my brain to —" The "not real" framing is the rip. The script tells her something is wrong with her brain. The rip teaching tells her her brain is reading the situation correctly. Reality has a tear in it. She is not delusional. She is perceiving the tear.

"Yeah, it definitely is." -- Joe Hudson, confirming her observation that death is weird

The confirmation matters. The rip is not denied. The weirdness is not explained away. The weirdness is named as real and then offered as the gift.

## The Move From Weirdness to Gift

The operational instruction Joe offers around the rip:

"There's something we can do that just shows allows that rip of reality to actually be a gift rather than a weirdness." -- Joe Hudson, She Came Asking to Let Go, She Left with a Love That Doesn't End

The move is not to close the rip. The move is to change the relationship to it. The same rip can be experienced as "something is wrong with me" or as "something is showing me." The phenomenon is the same. The interpretation differs.

The shift to "gift" requires the bereaved to:

1. Stop trying to close the rip. The rip cannot be closed. The energy spent trying to close it is the energy not available for the relationship the rip is making possible.
2. Notice what the rip is showing. The love that keeps flowing through the rip is the data. It is showing that love is bigger than the two-specific-people model. It is showing that the bereaved has a capacity that the relationship developed and that the death has not removed.
3. Use the rip as a doorway. The non-personal love that the rip is revealing has nowhere it is required to go. It can flow toward everything. See [[the-dead-as-conduit-to-non-personal-love]].

## Why the Standard Grief Vocabulary Misses This

Common grief vocabulary uses metaphors of healing, closing, processing, moving on. All of these implicitly assume the rip is a wound that should eventually close.

The rip teaching uses different metaphors: tear, doorway, gift, conduit, what-is-being-shown. These metaphors assume the rip is structural — a permanent feature of the new configuration — and the work is to relate to it well, not to close it.

The standard vocabulary is not wrong. There is healing. There is processing. The acute phase of grief does move. But the rip itself does not close. The bereaved who has done years of clean grief work will still have the rip. The relationship will still continue. The love will still flow.

The teaching is to stop expecting the rip to close. The rip is the new shape of reality. The work is to live well inside the new shape.

## When This Teaching Lands

This teaching is for the bereaved who is past the acute phase, has done significant grief work, and is now in the configuration of "I am ready to move on but I feel like something is still here." The "something still here" is the rip. The bereaved is trying to close it. The teaching invites them to stop trying to close it and notice what it is showing.

It also lands for the bereaved who is in the middle phase and reports things like:

- "I still talk to him."
- "I feel her presence."
- "It doesn't feel real."
- "Part of me is still waiting for him to come home."
- "I keep planning my life around him even though he's gone."

These reports are usually pathologized by the bereaved themselves. The rip teaching is the alternative framing. None of these reports are evidence of pathology. They are accurate perception of the rip.

It does not land for the bereaved in the acute phase. Acute grief has its own work. The rip teaching arrives after enough of that work has been done that the rip is recognizable as a continuing feature rather than as the chaos of the acute loss.

## The Limit of the Teaching

The rip teaching is not a generalization to all losses. It is specifically about deep love and physical death. Not every loss tears reality. Not every death tears reality for everyone involved. The depth of the love is what produces the depth of the rip.

It also does not apply to all bereaved in the same way. Some bereaved experience the rip clearly. Others experience the grief as more conventional. The teaching is for the configuration in which the rip is being experienced. Where it is not, the teaching is unnecessary and may even feel imposed.

The teaching is offered, not asserted. The bereaved either recognizes their experience in the description or does not. The recognition is the validation. The teaching's job is to make available a framing that the bereaved can use if it matches.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person describes their grief as "not feeling real."
- When the bereaved describes still feeling, talking to, or planning around the dead person.
- When the standard "moving on" model is not matching the bereaved's actual experience.
- When the bereaved is treating the continued presence of the dead as evidence of being stuck.
- When the bereaved is in the late phase and asking how to let go.
- When [[i-cant-get-over-this-loss]] presents and the underlying experience is the rip rather than acute unresolved grief.
