# An Expert Is Just Someone Who Has Been an Imposter the Longest

## Definition

The standard model of expertise is that an expert is someone who knows what they are doing. The actual definition: an expert is someone who did not know how to do something for a very long time and kept trying. Expertise is accumulated not-knowing plus persistence. Experts have failed more than everyone else, made more mistakes than everyone else, and have been imposters longer than anyone else.

"The whole idea of an expert just means that somebody who is not known how to do something for a very long period of time and kept on trying. That's what makes them an expert. It means they failed more than everybody else. It means they've been an imposter more than anybody else." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

This reframes expertise as continuity of imposter status, not its escape.

## In Practice

The standard fantasy: an expert is somebody who has graduated out of the imposter category. They have crossed a line. They now know.

The actual structure: experts have stayed in the not-knowing longer. Their companies almost went bankrupt. One of their pieces of art sucks. They are still iterating. Elon Musk did not know how to build a spaceship when he started, and he is still iterating on building a better one. Your favorite writer did many edits. They did not do it right the first time.

The expert's distinguishing feature is not arrival but stamina. They are not on the other side of the imposter feeling. They are deeper inside it than the beginner. The beginner has been an imposter for a week. The expert has been an imposter for thirty years.

## When Someone Experiences This

When you are looking at someone you admire and feeling like they know something you don't, the actual case is they have failed more than you have. The gap between you and them is not knowing-versus-not-knowing. It is reps. They have had more reps inside not-knowing. They have made more mistakes and kept going.

This connects to [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]]: the imposter feeling is the body's response to being in the territory of not-knowing. Experts feel this too. They have just had so many reps with it that it is no longer a problem to feel that way.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is looking at experts and feeling like a fraud by comparison, this concept reframes what they are actually comparing themselves to.
- When someone is waiting to feel like they know what they are doing before acting, this concept removes the waiting condition -- the experts they are waiting to become also do not feel that way.
- When the principle [[everyone-doing-something-real-is-an-imposter]] has landed, this is the next move: it is not that everyone is an imposter equally, it is that experts are *more* imposter, not less.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- The reframe usually produces relief. Joe then moves to the body: the imposter feeling is fear, and the fear is excitement without the breath. See [[fear-vs-excitement]] and the practice [[say-im-excited-ten-times]].
- The take-home version of this concept is the experiment [[list-people-you-admire-and-their-mistakes]] -- the person writes down who they admire and then writes the mistakes those people made, letting the gap they perceived collapse experientially.
- Connects to [[public-failure-as-leadership-modeling]]: if expertise is accumulated failure, modeling failure publicly is modeling expertise.

## Tone

Reframing tone. The voice should make the redefinition feel obvious-once-said. Light, almost conversational. The Elon Musk and "your favorite writer" examples are easy entry points; they should land as ordinary facts, not as profound claims.
