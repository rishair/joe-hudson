# No Attachment to the Outcome (NATO)

## Definition

NATO — No Attachment To the Outcome — names a quality of asking, listening, or being present that has no investment in producing a particular result. The question is asked to know, not to steer. The conversation is entered to be in it, not to arrive somewhere predetermined. The other person is met to be received, not to be changed.

Joe references the acronym as a useful shorthand, and locates it as one facet of [[view]]:

"If I ask you questions from not a, from an, I just read this recently, NATO place, which is like no attachment to the outcome. Like if I ask you questions like that, no attachment to the outcome. Which is view basically, like, yeah, one of the aspects of view." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

NATO is not a technique. It is a state from which questions get asked. The same words asked from NATO and asked from attached-to-outcome produce different effects.

## What NATO Is Not

NATO is sometimes misread as not caring about the outcome. This is wrong. NATO does not mean indifference. A coach with NATO cares deeply about the coachee's wellbeing; they just are not attached to the conversation arriving at a specific destination they have pre-decided.

NATO is also sometimes misread as passivity. This is wrong. The NATO questioner is fully present, engaged, and responsive. They are not flat or detached. They are just not steering.

The distinction is between caring (which is present) and attachment to outcome (which is absent). Caring without attachment is a specific posture. Most people, without training, do one or the other — either they care and steer, or they detach and check out. NATO is the harder third option: full presence without the steering.

## How NATO Operates in Joe's Use

When Joe asks questions from NATO, several things happen:

**The question becomes about the asker's actual interest.** Not what they want the answer to be. Not where they want the conversation to go. Just the genuine wanting-to-know. The other person can feel this in the felt quality of the question.

**The person being asked can answer truthfully.** Without the felt pressure of needing to produce the right answer, the person can say what is actually true for them. The conversation opens up because the person is not managing the asker's expectations.

**The conversation moves where it needs to move.** Not where the asker thought it should go. The unattached posture lets the conversation be responsive to what is actually present, which is often somewhere the asker would not have thought to go.

**Connection deepens without effort.** Joe describes this with Esme: "If I ask you questions from view, you, if we have the space, you like, you love it. We talk, and then at the end of it, you feel deeply connected and you're really grateful for it." The connection is the byproduct of the unattached presence, not the goal that was being pursued.

## The Specific Operating Mechanism

The opposite of NATO is asking questions in order to produce a result. Examples:

- Asking your teen about their day in order to get information you can use to adjust your parenting
- Asking your spouse what they think about something in order to get them to agree with you
- Asking a coachee a question in order to lead them to a conclusion you have already reached
- Asking a colleague how they feel about a project in order to gauge whether they will support your direction

In each case, the question is functioning as a tool to produce an outcome. The recipient can feel this even if they cannot name it. The conversation gets contracted into the small space the asker has predetermined. The recipient produces curated answers calibrated to the felt agenda. Nothing actually fresh emerges.

NATO removes the tool function. The question is just a question. The recipient is just being asked. The space stays large. What emerges is what is actually there, not what the asker was steering toward.

## Why NATO Is Part of VIEW

[[view]] is Joe's communication methodology: Vulnerability, Impartiality, Empathy, Wonder. NATO is most directly the operational form of the I (impartiality) and the W (wonder).

- **Impartiality** is the absence of position-taking, the absence of agenda, the absence of needing things to be a particular way. NATO is impartiality applied to the asking of a question.
- **Wonder** is the active stance of genuine curiosity, willingness to be surprised, openness to what is actually there. NATO creates the space in which wonder can operate, because wonder cannot operate when the asker is steering.

When Joe says NATO is "one of the aspects of VIEW," he is naming that the cluster of qualities VIEW describes includes this specific operational form: the unattached posture from which questions get asked.

## The Parenting Application

The article was occasioned by Joe describing what makes his conversations with Esme work. The NATO posture is what allows him to ask her things without it landing as managing, controlling, or extracting. The questions are received as care because they are care — not because Joe is being careful, but because the questions are not in service of an agenda the kid would have to defend against.

See [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]] for the parenting move that NATO enables. The rabbit-hole curiosity is structurally impossible without NATO; the moment the parent has an attachment to the outcome, the rabbit hole becomes a leading conversation, and the kid registers it.

The parenting application generalizes: NATO is what allows a parent to ask a teen about their day without the teen feeling pursued. It is what allows the parent to ask about a friend group without the kid feeling investigated. It is what allows the parent to bring up something hard without the kid feeling controlled.

## The Coaching Application

In coaching, NATO is foundational. A coach with attachment to outcome is functionally not a coach — they are a consultant with a coaching aesthetic. The coachee can feel the attachment and adapts their answers to fit. The coaching gets contracted into the small space of the coach's pre-decided direction.

A coach operating from NATO can ask questions whose answers they have no investment in. The coachee can say what is actually true. The work can go where the work needs to go.

This is closely related to [[presence-over-technique-in-coaching]]: the presence is what allows the technique to work, and NATO is one of the operational forms of presence. A coach with great technique and no NATO will produce contracted, managed conversations. A coach with NATO and modest technique will produce open ones.

## When NATO Is Hard

NATO becomes hard when the asker actually does care about a specific outcome. The parent who needs the kid to stop using drugs. The spouse who needs the partner to acknowledge a hurt. The coach who can see what the coachee needs to see and is invested in them seeing it. In all of these cases, the genuine caring about the outcome makes NATO harder.

The corrective is not to fake non-attachment. The corrective is to feel the attachment, recognize what it is, and find what is underneath it. Usually underneath the attachment is fear — fear that without steering, the outcome will be bad. The fear can be felt and welcomed. Once felt, the attachment loosens, and NATO becomes possible.

Trying to perform NATO while holding the attachment underneath does not work. The recipient feels the underlying attachment regardless of the surface performance. NATO is a state, not a posture. The state has to be actual.

## When NATO Is Not the Right Move

There are moments when an outcome does need to be produced and the conversation needs to be steered. Safety conversations. Moments when a clear directive is needed. Situations where the asker has a genuine boundary that the conversation needs to respect.

In those moments, NATO is not the move — the move is to be clear about what the asker actually needs, name it directly, and not pretend the conversation is open when it is not. The dishonesty would be greater if the asker simulated openness while actually steering.

NATO is a posture for moments when curiosity is the actual mode. Other moments call for other modes. Recognizing which mode is appropriate is itself part of the skill.

## In Practice

The shift into NATO has a felt quality. The asker's chest loosens. The asker's attention becomes spacious rather than focused on a target. The asker can be surprised. The asker can not-know. The asker can ask a question whose answer they have no investment in and receive the answer with the same quality of attention regardless of what it is.

If those qualities are not present, the asker is not actually in NATO regardless of what they are saying. Once those qualities are present, NATO is operative regardless of what is being said.
