# Engineering Heartbreak to Avoid Heartbreak

## Definition

A specific instance of the mechanism described in [[trauma-mapping]] and [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]: the body recreates the very experience the person is avoiding, because the avoidance is what keeps the loop incomplete. The person who is most afraid of heartbreak engineers the conditions for heartbreak, over and over, because the body is looking for the chance to finally feel what was avoided.

The word "engineering" matters. The recreation is not accidental and not deterministic. It is a kind of architecture. The person constructs the situations — chooses the partner, takes the leap, drops the commitment — that will produce the conditions of the original wound. The construction is unconscious. The shape is precise.

## In Practice

The session example: Brett's pattern of going deep into something, crashing, ending up disconnected from people he loves, and then feeling heartbreak about the disconnection. Joe traces it back to a childhood divergence (leaving his family's religion), which carried the implicit message that being himself causes pain to the people he loves. As an adult, his rabbit-hole-then-crash pattern recreates the same shape: intense personal direction, distance from people he is supposed to be there for, then the heartbreak of recognizing the distance.

"You're engineering heartbreak that you then avoid. Yeah, is what you're saying. Yeah. Right. And then you get to feel heartbreak/shame for avoiding the thing that you created to feel that you'd not using the feel." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

The double structure is important. The person is not just engineering the heartbreak. They are engineering the heartbreak and then avoiding the feeling of it. The avoidance is what makes the loop incomplete. The incompleteness is what causes the body to engineer the next iteration.

## What Actually Gets Recreated

Often what looks like heartbreak being recreated is actually shame being recreated. The person believes they are afraid of the heartbreak; on examination they are afraid of the shame about not feeling the heartbreak correctly.

Brett's clarification in the session: "I've learned to be actually very okay with heartbreak itself, but I'm not okay with shame."

The specific unfelt emotion varies by person. Heartbreak. Shame. Abandonment. Helplessness. The mechanism is the same: the body engineers situations where the avoided emotion would naturally arise, and the person continues to avoid feeling it, and the loop continues.

Joe's own version was emotional abandonment. He recreated emotionally abandoning people over and over until he fell in love with the abandonment, at which point the recreations stopped. See [[joes-abandonment-mapping]].

## Why "Engineering" Captures Something New

The trauma-mapping language ("attracting," "recreating," "mapping") can sound passive. Things happen to the person. The engineering language is more accurate. The person is actively constructing the situations. The construction is unconscious, but it is not random.

This matters for coaching. The passive framing can produce a kind of self-pity ("why does this keep happening to me?"). The engineering framing puts agency back. Not blame — the person is not doing this on purpose — but agency. The body that is doing the engineering can also choose, through feeling, to stop.

## The Shame Spiral

Once the engineering is partially seen, a new layer often appears: shame about the engineering itself. "I'm doing this to myself. I should know better. I keep doing this." The shame about the pattern then becomes part of the engine for the next iteration. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

The way out is not to fight the shame. The way out is to feel it, the same way as any other avoided emotion. The whole architecture is built out of unfelt feelings. Feeling them — including the shame about the pattern — is what dissolves the architecture.

"It's almost like it's the shame that I'm recreating." -- Brett Kistler, Allowing Yourself to Change. Joe's response: "yeah, that's beautiful. So what's wrong with shame?" The work is to feel into the shame itself, not to escape it.

## The Detection Question

When someone reports a pattern that keeps reproducing despite their best efforts to avoid it, the diagnostic question is: "what would you have to feel if this happened and you couldn't avoid feeling it?"

If the answer is the same feeling that the pattern produces over and over, the loop is identified. The pattern is engineering opportunities to feel the avoided emotion. The way out is to feel the emotion directly, not to keep avoiding it through the pattern.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A recurring relationship pattern that keeps producing the same heartbreak.
- A career pattern of building something and then sabotaging it, with the same wreckage each time.
- Cycles of intimacy and distance that feel involuntary but recur with precision.
- A long history of "this kind of thing always happens to me."
- Pride that masks the recurrence ("I'm a person who takes big risks") combined with the same emotional outcome each time.

## Tone

Joe delivers this insight as something familiar, almost obvious. "It's the same thing that we've talked about... the cell wants to get to homeostasis." He frames it as something they have discussed many times, even when it is the first time the specific person is hearing it framed this way. The framing reduces the drama. This is not a special discovery. It is what bodies do.

"It's so hard to see it. But that whole idea of we are creating the thing by the way in which we're avoiding it. It is the way in which we're avoiding the emotional experience that we're recreating it in our life." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change
