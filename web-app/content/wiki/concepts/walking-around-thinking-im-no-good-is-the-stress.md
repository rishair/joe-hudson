# Walking Around Thinking "I'm No Good" Is Itself the Chronic Stress

## Definition

Self-shame is not just a thought. It is a sustained body-state of background stress produced by the felt experience of carrying around the belief that some part of the self is no good. The body does not enjoy being told it is no good. The accumulated, all-day-long act of telling the body this is a chronic stress condition — distinct from the discrete moments of shame, and distinct from the loops of bad-habit behavior that shame produces. It is the ambient cost of running shame on the self continuously.

"Shame is basically saying there's a part of myself that's no good. Walking around thinking I'm no good is like no — there's no walk in the park. No one's like, hey, you know what? You know what would feel really good in my body right now is to think I'm no good. So multiple places that you're thinking you're no good in life, that's shame just creating this underlying stress in your system, and it doesn't solve anything." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Change

The concept names a specific cost of shame that is often missed: the body's continuous response to being told it is bad. The body responds the way it would respond to any other sustained signal of badness — with tension, depression, avoidance, the felt-sense of pressure that the person reports as background stress without knowing what it is.

## What Makes This Distinct from Other Shame Concepts

The shame literature in this compendium covers several angles on shame. This concept is one specific angle among them:

- [[shame-drives-behavior]] is about shame's mechanical effect on action: shame holds bad habits in place. The angle is causal.
- [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] is about shame's effect on emotional flow: shame freezes other emotions. The angle is intrapsychic dynamics.
- [[shame-as-disconnection]] is about shame's social and signal function: shame is the felt sense of being outside the tribe. The angle is what shame *is*.
- [[should-as-threat]] is about the verbal vehicle of self-shame: the should hits four registers as a threat. The angle is the structure of the threat.
- This concept is about the **chronic ambient stress** of carrying self-shame as a continuous body condition. The angle is the felt cost of running shame on yourself across the day.

The concepts compound. Shame freezes emotion (stagnation), holds behavior (drives), signals disconnection (felt sense), and — independently of all those — creates a continuous background stress that the body has to carry while all the above is happening. This concept names that fourth cost.

## The Dual-Stress Framing

Joe pairs this concept with a second source of stress in the same teaching: the stress of the should-loop not working. The full structure of self-shaming stress is two-fold.

**First stress: the should itself is stressful to deliver.** Saying "I should X" produces immediate body contraction. The body reads the should as a threat. See [[should-as-threat]].

**Second stress: the should doesn't work, which is itself stressful.** "It's also stressful 'cause it doesn't work. So you're sitting there telling yourself you should do something, then you don't do it, then you beat yourself up for not doing it, then you tell yourself you should do it again, and then you don't do it, and then you beat yourself up. And that's an incredibly stressful situation." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Change

The dual structure compounds. The person is bearing the stress of running the shame (this concept) plus the stress of the loop's mechanical failure to produce change (the loop structure described in [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]). Two stress sources, one mechanism.

This explains why people in long-running unsuccessful change efforts often report feeling chronically wiped out without being able to name why. The wiped-out feeling is the cost of running two compounding stresses continuously.

## The Body Test

The concept is testable in the body, in real time. Anyone can run it.

Say to yourself, internally, with attention to the body: "there's something wrong with me." Notice what happens. The body usually contracts. Breath shortens. A felt-sense of pressure or tightness appears. This is the body's response to the signal "I'm no good" in one instance.

Now imagine doing this all day, in dozens of small variations: "I shouldn't have eaten that, I shouldn't have said that, I should be further along, why am I like this, I'm so bad at this." Each one is a small contraction. The contractions accumulate. The accumulated state is the chronic background stress this concept names.

The test makes the abstract concept embodied. The person is not being told this in theory; they are running the experiment on their own body and feeling the result.

## Why the Stress Is Not Solved by the Behavior

The conventional logic suggests that the stress of self-shame would resolve once the shamed behavior changes. "Once I finally stop X, I won't have to feel bad about it anymore."

The actual structure inverts this. The stress of carrying "I'm no good" precedes the behavior failure and continues after the failure. The behavior never quite resolves the stress because the underlying belief — that the self is no good in this area — remains intact. Each new instance of the behavior reactivates the belief, even when the long-run trend is in the desired direction. The stress is owned by the belief, not by the behavior.

This is one of the reasons why "I'll stop shaming myself once I finally do X" does not work as a self-management strategy. The shame is not waiting for X. The shame is producing the failure of X, and the chronic stress of the shame is what makes the body unable to deliver the change the shame is supposedly trying to enforce.

The exit is not at the behavior layer. The exit is at the layer of the chronic belief that some part of the self is no good. See [[inherent-goodness]] for the foundational reframe.

## The Multiplier Effect of Multiple Sites

Joe specifies that the chronic stress is not from one site of self-shame but from "multiple places that you're thinking you're no good in life." The cost is additive across all the areas where the self-shame is running.

A person who has self-shame around five different areas — eating, exercise, work productivity, how they treat their partner, how they parent — is carrying five concurrent streams of "I'm no good" through the day. Each is contributing its own contraction. The body is responding to all of them simultaneously.

This explains why the [[list-things-trying-to-change-for-4-years]] practice produces such a strong felt reaction when the person sees their full list at once. They are seeing, in one view, the multiple sites of "I'm no good" they have been carrying. The felt cost becomes visible all at once. Often the moment of looking at the full list produces an embodied sense of "this is too much," which is the body finally registering the aggregate stress condition that has been ambient for years.

## What Releases the Stress

The release is not "stop having shame." Shame is a real emotion with real signal value; trying to eliminate it produces another layer of "I'm no good for having shame." See [[shame-as-disconnection]] for the reframe of shame as signal.

The release is in changing the relationship to the moments of self-shame as they arise. The in-the-moment practice [[notice-where-you-shame-yourself-be-gentle]] is the direct intervention on this concept. Each moment of noticing + gentleness reduces the contraction in that moment. The accumulated effect, across many moments and many days, is a reduction in the chronic background stress this concept names.

The deeper work involves [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] applied to the shame itself, [[emotional-inquiry]] as the structured practice for that, and the reframe at the belief level via [[inherent-goodness]]. All of these compound to release the chronic stress, not by suppressing the shame but by changing what the body does with the shame when it arises.

## When This Concept Surfaces

The concept is useful when:

- A client reports chronic stress, low-grade anxiety, or background tension without being able to name a specific cause. The self-shame loop may be the source.
- A client has done significant work on visible stressors (job, relationship, health) without the chronic stress resolving. The remaining stress is often the self-shame layer.
- A client intellectually understands shame but underestimates the felt-cost of carrying it. The concept gives them a frame for the body experience they have been having.
- A client is wondering why they feel tired all the time despite normal sleep and good habits. The stress of running multiple self-shame loops is energetically expensive.
- A client is in the [[ive-been-trying-to-change-this-for-years]] concern and the body-cost of the failed-trying needs to be named.

## Why This Concept Matters for the Coaching

Naming this concept gives the client a specific frame for an experience they have been having without a name for it. Many clients know they feel stressed and tired. Fewer know that a significant fraction of that stress is the cost of carrying self-shame thoughts continuously. Once named, the concept becomes actionable — there is a target the practices can aim at.

The concept also makes the case for the practices more visceral. The argument "you should stop shaming yourself" lands as another should. The argument "the body is carrying chronic stress from the I'm-no-good thoughts" lands as a body-cost observation. The second frame is harder to dismiss and easier to act on.
