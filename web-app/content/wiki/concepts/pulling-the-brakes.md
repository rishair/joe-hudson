# Pulling the Brakes

## Definition

The felt sense of stopping yourself just before you would have landed in your aliveness. A specific somatic experience, often described by the person themselves as "pulling a handbrake" or "pushing down a brake pedal." The braking is constant, not occasional. The person is doing it most of the time without noticing.

What gets braked: enjoyment, expression, charisma, want, openness, love, presence. The braking is non-specific in its target. It is a stop-mechanism that fires before whatever is about to come out.

When the brake releases, the person reports the opposite of effort. "It's so effortless and it's so easy to be charismatic and to just be loving and open." The release is not an addition; the loving and open thing was already there. The brake was the only thing in the way. See [[undoing-not-doing]].

## In Practice

The client metaphor that named this for Joe:

> "I'm really afraid of being judged. And it feels phenomenologically like I'm actually pulling a handbrake or pushing down a brake pedal all the time. And those moments when I release that, it's so effortless and it's so easy to be charismatic and to just be loving and open."

Joe's response distinguishes the brake from the person doing the braking: "It's easy to be you." The brake is not the person. The person is what the brake is suppressing.

The brake takes specific everyday forms:
- Rehearsing what to say before speaking. See [[rehearsing-how-to-say-it]].
- Editing tone in the middle of a sentence.
- Cutting off laughter before it lands.
- Pulling back from a hug that was about to soften.
- Stopping a feeling halfway to its expression.
- The mid-sentence "I shouldn't say this."
- The micro-pause before answering an emotional question.
- An internal voice that says "doesn't matter, stop pulling the brakes" — the very recognition of the brake is sometimes the first step toward the release.

## What the Brake Is For

The brake was installed for protection. The fear underneath is almost always one of: being judged, being seen as too much, being inconvenient, being rejected, being shamed, being a burden. In childhood, the brake prevented something the child needed to prevent. In adulthood, the brake continues to fire even when the original threat is gone. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

A specific origin: the child who learned that expressing needs or pain got them shunned. The brake then organizes against expressing anything that could be read as need. The adult brakes their want, their pain, their truth, on the assumption that expression will produce the original consequence.

> "What happened when you expressed your needs or your pain when you were a kid?"
> "Yeah, I got shunned, kind of."

The brake is the inherited protection from that shunning. It is faithfully doing its old job in a world where the job is no longer required.

## The Brake and Chronic Conditions

The brake can wire itself into the body in a way that produces physical consequences. Joe asks a specific diagnostic question of clients with chronic illness, chronic pain, or autoimmune conditions: "how does pulling the brakes relate to your chronic illness?" The framing is not causal — Joe is explicit that he is not claiming the braking causes the illness. The framing is correlational and felt: when the braking happens, what happens to the illness, and vice versa?

What clients often discover: the brake serves the illness, or the illness serves the brake. They prevent the same thing — the full expression of need, want, pain, anguish. The brake says "do not express." The chronic pain produces a state in which expressing would be "inconveniencing people." The two mechanisms reinforce each other. The body has organized around the same prohibition that the mind has organized around.

> "It's been a way to stop me from expressing my needs and wants... If you're the inconvenience for having it, then yeah, so you can't even express your needs when you're in chronic pain. Because that's an inconvenience."

This is the inversion: the very condition that most legitimately calls for expression of need produces the most shame about needing. The brake doubles up.

## The Brake and "Fixing Yourself"

A specific form the brake takes when a difficult feeling arrives: the immediate move toward intervention. The pain kicks in, and instead of being-with the pain, the person snaps into "what do I need to do to fix this?" The fixing-mode looks productive. It is the brake.

> "I get really stuck in what interventions I need to make to be free of the pain... that takes me out of the enjoying the moment."

The distinction matters: thinking about how to relieve pain and not braking are not mutually exclusive. A person can plan a treatment without dropping out of the present moment. The brake is the dropping-out, not the planning. The diagnostic is whether the person is rushing into the head as an escape from the body, or staying in the body while the head considers options.

## What Releasing the Brake Produces

- Effortless presence. The thing the person was trying to manufacture (charisma, openness, ease) was already there.
- A specific somatic softening, often described as the body "moving like grass in the wind."
- Sometimes a wave of feeling that had been held back — anguish, grief, rage. The brake was holding it. Release of the brake is release of the held feeling.
- A different relationship to the very thing that was being braked. The fear of being judged often diminishes precisely when the brake releases, because the person discovers that the imagined judgment was the brake's projection, not the world's response.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports [[i-keep-postponing-enjoyment]] and identifies that something stops them from staying in the present.
- When someone says "I'm afraid of being judged" and the fear has a somatic signature.
- When the person describes effortlessness in one specific context (with their therapist, alone, when no one is watching) and effortfulness everywhere else. See [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]].
- When chronic illness or chronic stress is present and the person reports they cannot express their needs.
- When a person uses the metaphor of "stopping themselves" or "pulling back" repeatedly.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[running-toward-yourself]]: the opposite move. Release the brake by moving toward the thing you have been braking against.
- Toward the chronic-illness connection if relevant: "how does pulling the brakes relate to your illness?"
- Toward [[shame-as-disconnection]] if shame is what the brake protects against. The shame underneath is the operative material.
- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: what the brake was holding back.
- Toward [[how-would-you-enjoy-x]]: applying enjoyment to whatever the brake is currently doing, including the braking itself.

## Tone

Curious, almost neutral. The brake is not a moral failure. It is a faithful mechanism doing the job it was installed to do. Joe names it without urgency. The release of the brake is not produced by attacking the brake; it is produced by understanding what the brake was protecting.
