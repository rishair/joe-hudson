# Running Toward Yourself

## Definition

The move of orienting toward whatever is actually happening in you, especially when the instinct is to escape it. The opposite of running away from yourself.

What "yourself" means in this phrase: whatever is true in you right now. The pain. The shame. The stress. The sadness. The desire. The anguish. The "yourself" is not a fixed identity to defend; it is the current felt reality. Running toward it means being with it, letting it have its expression, not pulling the brakes on it.

The framing comes from the client. When Joe asks what stops the person from enjoying their experience, the answer is "I'm running away from myself." Joe immediately uses the metaphor as a coaching tool: "Run towards yourself and see if more enjoyment happens. And now run away from yourself and see if more enjoyment happens or less enjoyment happens."

The move is testable in real time. The body knows the difference.

## In Practice

Running toward yourself is a stance, not a technique. The stance has specific signatures:

- The body softens rather than braces. See [[embrace-vs-brace]].
- Attention drops from the head into the chest, gut, or pelvis.
- Breath deepens rather than catches.
- The instinct to manage the experience releases.
- The voice (when speaking) slows.
- A specific allowing arrives: whatever is here gets to be here, fully.

Running away from yourself has the opposite signatures. Speed. Tension. Rushing into intervention. The internal voice asking "how do I make this stop?" instead of "what is this?" See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].

## The Stress Application

A specific extension: running toward yourself in the middle of stress. Most people assume stress is an obstacle to presence. The work flips this: you can be present *while stressed*. The presence is not the absence of stress; it is the running-toward-yourself-while-stressed.

> "Stress happens whether you're running away from yourself or not running away from yourself. There's going to be moments of like somebody saying something that's hard, or everything going wrong, or some timing issue. So how do you run towards yourself in the time of stress?"

The stress is not the variable. The relationship to the stress is the variable. A person can stay in themselves through stress, and a person can leave themselves through stress, and these produce completely different lives.

The diagnostic in the moment: when stress arrives, where does attention go? Into the head, into planning, into rehearsing what to say, into figuring out the next move? That is running away. Into the body, into what is felt, into being-with what is happening? That is running toward.

## The Victim Application

The most counterintuitive form of the move. When the person is in a victim-feeling — chronic pain, helplessness, anguish about the unfairness of a condition — the instinct is to push the victim-feeling away. Don't be a victim. Don't be a burden. Don't make this a story about how bad it is. The pushing-away is running away from yourself.

Running toward yourself in this case means letting the victim-feeling all the way in. Letting the body be in anguish. Letting the voice say "this fucking sucks." Letting the helplessness be the whole truth in this moment.

What happens next is the surprise. The victim-feeling, fully allowed, often moves into empowerment on its own. Not through an effort to become empowered. Through full allowance of the not-empowered state.

> "When you allow that feeling of victim all the way in and all the way through, it moves to enjoyment... When you ran towards yourself, which was being in pain and being in anguish, that enjoyment showed up... We're scared that if we allow the feeling of the victim to be fully there, that we'll end up being a victim forever. Yeah, but what it actually just did is empower you, right? The empowerment that you're trying to have when you don't allow the victim just becomes this very natural thing once you allow it."

This is the structural answer to the worry "if I let myself feel like a victim, I'll be a victim forever." The opposite is true. The not-letting-yourself-feel-it is what keeps the victim-feeling locked in. The full feeling is the exit. See [[feared-stories-about-emotions-sense-something-real]].

## The Live Coaching Use

The move is also a real-time coaching instrument. Joe asks the person to try both directions and report. "Run toward yourself and see what happens. Now run away from yourself and see what happens." The person discovers experientially which direction produces enjoyment, peace, charisma, ease.

The discovery is not intellectual. The instruction "run toward yourself" is meant to be executed in the body in the moment, and the consequence is meant to be noticed in the body in the moment. This is one of the rare moves where the entire teaching is contained in a few seconds of live experimentation.

After the person has felt the difference once, the move is available to them outside the session. The body knows the orientation now. It can be returned to.

## What Running Toward Yourself Is Not

- It is not catharsis. Running toward yourself does not require dramatic expression. A quiet allowing in the body is the same move.
- It is not self-indulgence. The full feeling of the helplessness is not the same as wallowing in it; wallowing is a story *about* the helplessness, often used to avoid actually feeling it.
- It is not the absence of action. You can run toward yourself and then take the next step. The two are not in conflict. Christopher in the session: "I can differentiate between just... planning out something reasonable versus just ruminating and escaping." The first is running-toward-while-acting. The second is running away.
- It is not figuring yourself out. The head wants to understand. Running toward yourself is felt, not understood. The understanding comes after, if it comes at all.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the person says they are running away from themselves and Joe takes them at their word.
- When [[pulling-the-brakes]] has been named and the question becomes "what is the opposite move?"
- When [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]] is happening and the person is approaching their own feeling as an obstacle.
- When stress, pain, or shame is present and the person is trying to escape it.
- When the person is in a victim-feeling and trying to perform their way out of it.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: running toward yourself usually produces the felt experience of whatever has been held back.
- Toward [[enjoyment-as-path]]: enjoyment of whatever is actually happening, including the difficult parts.
- Toward the discovery that the feeling, fully met, transforms. The victim-feeling becomes empowerment. The pain becomes spaciousness. The shame becomes connection. See [[emotional-fluidity]].
- Toward [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]]: what is at the bottom of the avoided territory is often peace.

## Tone

Quiet, direct, experimental. Joe offers the move as a try-this-and-see, not as a doctrine. The body's report is the authority. "See if more enjoyment happens" — the "see" is operative. The person is the one verifying.
