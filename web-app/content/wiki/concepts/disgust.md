# Disgust

## Definition

Disgust is the signal that you were forced to take in something you did not want to take in. Not just food. Anything: an idea, an ideology, an image, a witnessed act, a relational dynamic, a cultural narrative — any input that crossed the boundary of consent and got swallowed.

"Disgust happens when you're forced to swallow something that you didn't really want to take down." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The food metaphor is the structural form, not the limit of the emotion. The body learned the original meaning of disgust from literal toxic intake (rotten food, poisoned water) and generalized the response to any input that the system recognizes as something it should not be processing. The somatic signature is consistent across cases: the gag reflex, the recoil, the bodily "no" to what is being put inside.

## What This Reframes

The default cultural picture of disgust: an unpleasant reaction to be suppressed for social reasons, or an indicator that something is gross in a trivial sense. The signal-of-forced-intake picture: disgust is the body reporting that consent was violated and something toxic is now inside that needs to be removed.

The reframe takes disgust out of the trivial register (a squeamish reaction) and puts it into the boundary register (a violation of intake-consent that the system is flagging for action). Disgust deserves the same respectful reading as anger or fear. It is information about boundaries, specifically the boundaries of what the person should be taking in.

## The Diagnostic Question

The action step opens with a specific diagnostic question:

"If you're feeling disgust, you can ask yourself, what have I been force-fed that's toxic for me?" -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The question opens up the territory beyond food. The answer might be:

- A movie that was disturbing in a way the person did not want to experience.
- An ideological principle (from family, religion, peer group, culture) that the person was given as a child and now finds toxic.
- The witnessing of abuse, either direct or via media.
- A relational dynamic that the person was forced to participate in.
- A piece of cultural narrative that the person was made to swallow as truth.

"Could be a movie that wasn't the right movie, an ideological principle, having witnessed abuse or seeing other people being abused. Anything that's toxic, not just technically like a food toxic, but conceptually toxic. What did I take in that I don't wanna take in?" -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The question is not "what disgusts me about other people." The question is "what was put inside me that I did not consent to and that my body is now rejecting." The location of the toxic material is inside the self, and the work is to recognize what was put there and to clear it.

## The Removal Action Step

Once the toxic material is identified, the work is removal. Removal has two scales:

1. **Self-removal:** removing yourself from the environment that is delivering the toxic input. Leaving the relationship, the conversation, the room, the platform, the workplace, the religious community — whatever the source of the ongoing forced-intake is.

2. **Community-removal:** removing the toxic thing from the community, or removing the people around you from contact with it. This is the protective form of the action: not just protecting yourself but acting against the continued spread of the toxicity.

"Then how do I take care of myself so that I remove myself from an environment that's not healthy, or take care of my community, take care of people around me, get rid of the thing that's toxic." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The pairing of self-care and community-care inside the same action step is intentional. Disgust often arrives in contexts where the person has been protecting others or going along with something that everyone is being forced to swallow. The action step honors both the personal exit and the larger work of removing the toxicity from the field.

## Connection to Anger and Boundaries

Disgust and anger are closely related signals. Anger marks the place where a boundary has been crossed; disgust marks the place where an unwanted input has been taken in. Both are boundary signals, with different points of operation.

Anger says: "stop doing that to me / stop letting that happen." Disgust says: "get this thing out of me / get me out of this thing." The first is upstream-facing (against the source). The second is downstream-facing (against the toxic material that is now inside).

Both often co-occur. A person who has been force-fed an ideology may feel disgust (about what was put in them) and anger (about the people who put it there). Both signals are valid; both call for different action.

See [[anger]] and [[boundaries]] for the related treatments.

## The Family-Level Form

A specific application: disgust toward a parent or close family member who delivered toxic dynamics during childhood. The disgust shows up later, often years after the original intake, as the body's delayed report that what was being delivered was not okay to receive.

"I feel disgust toward my parent" is one of the entry points into the toxic-parent material. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] for the concrete presenting concern. The disgust is usually paired with guilt (about the disgust itself, about the recognition that the parent was toxic) and the work involves separating the disgust from the guilt and honoring both as separate signals.

The reframe lands particularly hard here: the disgust is not evidence that the person is a bad child for having the feeling. The disgust is the body reporting that what was force-fed during childhood was actually toxic, and the appropriate response is to remove the self from continued contact.

## The Kinked Forms

Disgust that is not allowed to be felt as the signal-it-is takes several destructive forms:

- **Internalized disgust:** turning the disgust against the self ("I am disgusting") rather than reading it as "I have been given something disgusting." The self becomes the toxic material rather than the receiver of it.
- **Displaced disgust:** redirecting the disgust onto a person or group who is not actually the source of the toxic material. This is the structural form of much prejudice and bigotry.
- **Suppressed disgust:** the person continues to consume the toxic input (the relationship, the ideology, the dynamic) and pushes the disgust signal down. The body keeps trying to surface the signal; the suppression keeps it from being read.

All three are the kinked forms. None of them lead to the removal action that resolves the disgust. All three extend the situation in worse directions.

See [[kinked-emotions]] for the broader treatment.

## What Disgust Is Not

Disgust is not the same as moral judgment. Moral judgment is a cognitive position about what is right or wrong. Disgust is a body-level signal about what was taken in. They can co-occur but they are different signals.

Disgust is not the same as preference or distaste. A person can dislike a food, a style of music, or a political opinion without disgust. Disgust specifically signals that something has been put inside that the body is trying to expel. The body is doing more than registering a preference.

Disgust is not contempt. Contempt is the felt sense of being above another person; disgust is the felt sense of having a toxic input inside. See [[contempt]]. They sometimes co-occur, but they are different emotions pointing at different material.

## In Coaching

When a person presents disgust, the first move is the reframe: this is a boundary signal about what got taken in, not a verdict on character. The diagnostic question is the entry point: what have you been force-fed?

The path then goes toward identifying the toxic material and acting on the removal step. The action is usually larger than the person initially imagined — leaving the relationship, ending the consumption of a media diet, withdrawing from a religious community, naming a family dynamic as unacceptable. The disgust is appropriate to the scale of the intake.

When disgust shows up as self-disgust ("I am disgusting"), the work is to separate the self from the toxic material. The disgust was originally about something put inside; it has gotten redirected at the container that received it. The redirect needs to be reversed. The self is not the toxic material; the self is the body that received the toxic material and is now rightly trying to expel it.

When disgust is laced with guilt (about having the disgust at all, particularly toward a parent or a community), the work is to honor both signals separately. The disgust points at the toxic intake. The guilt usually points at a want that has been ignored (often the want to be loyal, the want to belong, the want to have had a different history). Separating the two restores the information of both.

## Tone

Joe delivers the disgust definition with the same matter-of-fact respect as the other emotions. There is no recoil from the word "disgust." The metaphor (force-fed something toxic) is delivered straight, with the implicit acknowledgment that the listener has had this experience. The teaching does not moralize about what the toxic intake should or should not have been; the body has already made that judgment.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports disgust toward a person, a situation, or a memory.
- When someone is describing a relational dynamic they have been participating in that has begun to feel revolting.
- When a person has recognized an inherited ideology as toxic and the disgust is part of the recognition.
- When [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] is the presenting concern and disgust is one of the layers.
- When the disgust has been turned inward as self-disgust and needs to be redirected to its actual target.
- When the disgust has been displaced onto a person or group who is not the actual source of the toxic input.
