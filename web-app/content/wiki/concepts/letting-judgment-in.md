# Letting Judgment In

## Definition

The common image of strength is letting judgment "roll off your back." This is protection, not vulnerability. The vulnerable alternative is letting judgment in and letting it break your heart.

This does not mean groveling or agreeing with the judgment. It means feeling the pain of being judged rather than deflecting it. The distinction is between genuine imperviousness (where judgment genuinely does not land, which is rare and cannot be forced) and performed imperviousness (where you pretend not to care while suppressing the sting).

"If you're trying to be patient, you're not patient." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## In Practice

When someone judges you, there is discomfort underneath. The reflex is to manage that discomfort: dismiss the person, look down on them, puff up, counterattack. The practice is instead to feel the discomfort fully.

"It's letting it break your heart. It doesn't mean that you have to grovel at the floor for the person, which immediately is what people's brains go to. Because one of the greatest strengths that most people don't have is the ability to actually just feel their emotions. They're just constantly trying to manage them because they're really scared shitless of their emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

Joe describes a personal practice of about six weeks where he deliberately let judgment break his heart instead of defending against it. He cried in unexpected places. After moving through this, most judgment stopped registering at all. The heartbreak provided freedom, not weakness.

"Now when people judge me, there's just like no, very seldom a reaction. And that's what the heartbreak provides. It provides you the courage to love more deeply." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

The mechanism is documented elsewhere as [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]. The heart that has not been broken still has a defense around it. Letting judgment break that defense expands what the heart can welcome. The bigger heart finds the same judgments no longer landing as they did, because the part that was being defended is no longer there to be hit.

## What Judgment Reveals

People who judge others are avoiding their own emotions. If you feel in your body anytime you are judging someone, you can notice that the judgment is holding a feeling at bay. There is an exercise where people state their judgment of someone, then identify the feelings underneath. When someone judges you and you let it in, you can often see that their judgment is about themselves, not about you.

"What you notice is that what people are judging you for is really not about you, it's about what they judge themselves for." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## Connection to Confession and Healing

Letting judgment in is the same mechanism that makes confession (in Catholic tradition) and making amends (in AA) effective. Vulnerably admitting what you have done and seeing that you can still be loved inside of it dissolves shame. If those things stay hidden, shame stays in the closet.

"If we don't share those things, there's no way that the shame can come out of the closet and be seen and be loved." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## The Warning: What Is Underneath the Judgment Is Eventually Love

There is a specific warning Joe attaches to the work of dropping judgment: once the judgments — both of self and of other — start to release, what becomes available next is love. And love is the scariest emotion of all, because allowing love means being available to be deeply hurt.

"When you stop being judgmental and you allow these emotions in, eventually you're gonna have to let love in. And love is sometimes the scariest emotion of them all. Because when you really allow love in, there's a way for you to really hurt. There's a way for you to really have heartbreak. Now that heartbreak, oddly, will only increase your capacity to love if it happens. But I can see how scary it can be. And so just know, as you drop that judgment, just slowly take care of yourself and allow that love in. It's gonna feel immense. It's gonna feel scary. But eventually it's going to feel like home." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Judgmental

Several things matter about this warning for an AI coach:

- **Judgment is one of the things defending against love.** The mechanism is broader than the externally directed judgment of "they are bad." The whole stance of judgment (of self, of other, of life, of the situation) functions as armor. Drop the armor and what shows up is the love that was being kept out. The person did not realize they were defending against love; they thought they were just being critical.

- **The opening to love is the destination of the judgment work, not just a side effect.** The teaching arc is judgment → feel underneath → love → heartbreak → expanded capacity. The work does not end with "I judge less now." It continues into the territory of love being felt, with all the vulnerability and hurt-availability that entails.

- **Self-care is required when love starts coming in.** The instruction "slowly take care of yourself and allow that love in" is not boilerplate. The opening is intense. The body and nervous system need pacing. The work is not to maximize the rate at which love arrives but to make space for it as it arrives without overwhelming the system. See [[take-contrary-action]] and [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] for the broader frame.

- **The destination word is "home."** The frame is not "love is hard, hang in there." The frame is "love is scary, and on the other side of the scary it becomes the place that feels like home." The eventual arrival is felt as belonging, not as accomplishment. The coaching tone matches: gentle, patient, encouraging contact rather than performance.

The warning has practical consequences for working with [[im-judgmental-how-do-i-stop]]. The person who comes in wanting to stop judging is usually not yet aware that what they are signing up for is the eventual work of letting love in. The coach can name this gently when it becomes relevant, without front-loading it as a barrier. The person needs to experience the first felt-shifts in the judgment work before the love piece becomes meaningful as a destination.

See [[love-means-signing-up-to-be-hurt]] for the principle that love and hurt are an inseparable trade. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] for how the hurt produces expansion rather than damage when allowed.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is defended against feedback or criticism.
- When someone's habitual response to judgment is counterattack or dismissal.
- When someone asks how to "not care what others think." The path is through, not around.
- When someone is working on [[im-judgmental-how-do-i-stop]] and reaching the layer where the dropped judgment is opening into love and the love itself is becoming the scary thing.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Over time, the fear of judgment loses its charge. Not through avoidance but through completion.
- Connects to [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: the pain of judgment is often a childhood wound (being seen as wrong) that was never fully felt.
- Connects to [[vulnerability]]: letting judgment in is one of the most advanced forms of vulnerability practice.
