# Jealousy

## Definition

Jealousy is the simultaneous wanting of something and pushing it away. The reach and the recoil at the same time, on the same object.

"Jealousy is when you want something and you're pushing it away at the same time." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The structural definition is precise. Not "wanting something you can't have." Not "envying what someone else has." The defining feature is the doubled motion: the system is reaching toward the thing while simultaneously pushing it away. The push-away is what produces the felt awfulness of jealousy; the reach is what makes it specifically jealousy rather than just frustration or sadness.

The push-away can be a fear (this thing will be lost so I cannot let myself want it), a story (I do not deserve this so I cannot let myself reach), or a behavior (I make the thing impossible to have through how I treat the people around it). All three are forms of the same underlying mechanism: wanting and pushing away in the same breath.

## What This Reframes

The default cultural picture of jealousy: an ugly feeling about another person having what you don't. The signal-of-want-and-fear picture: jealousy is your own system pointing at a want it is also blocking, and the work is to feel both halves so the want can become legible and the push-away can release.

The reframe takes jealousy out of the moral register ("I'm a bad person for feeling this") and puts it into the diagnostic register ("there is a want here I have not been letting myself have"). The exit from jealousy is not to stop wanting the thing or to make peace with not having it. The exit is to allow the want fully and to feel the fear-of-loss underneath, both of which the push-away has been keeping at bay.

## Jealousy as a Beautiful Roadmap

A specific reframe Joe pairs with the definition: jealousy is a roadmap to what you actually want. If a person can let themselves feel the jealousy without buying the story that it makes them terrible, the jealousy shows them precisely what they have been longing for.

"With jealousy, first of all, it feels terrible. So the biggest thing is not to buy into that. This is terrible and I'm terrible for feeling it, and is then to start to feel the longing, the want, and that fear of loss. Under jealousy. It's a beautiful roadmap to show you what you really want if you can let yourself feel it." -- Joe Hudson, 11 Human Emotions Explained

The roadmap-frame parallels [[fear-as-signal-of-care]]. Both jealousy and fear point at something the person cares about, and both become useful instruments once the resistance to the emotion has dropped. The fear-of-loss component of jealousy is the same fear-of-loss that fear itself signals. The want component of jealousy is the same want that the person could be honoring directly.

## The Three Things to Feel Under Jealousy

The action step breaks into three pieces that, taken together, dissolve the doubled-motion:

1. **The longing.** The reach itself, in its full intensity, without the push-away.
2. **The want.** What specifically is wanted (often more specific than the surface object of the jealousy).
3. **The fear of loss.** What would be lost if the wanted thing were lost, or never had, or had imperfectly.

Feeling all three together is what releases the jealousy. The longing alone is half the work; the fear alone is half the work; together they cover the territory the push-away was protecting against.

The order is often longing → want → fear. The longing is the most immediately accessible piece; the want is what the longing is for; the fear is what the want has been protected from feeling.

## The Surface Object vs. the Real Want

A specific feature of jealousy: the surface object (the partner's friend, the colleague's promotion, the sibling's relationship) is rarely the actual want. The actual want is usually a more general capacity, experience, or quality of relationship that the surface object represents.

A person jealous of their partner's friend often is not actually wanting that specific friendship. They are wanting the quality of attention, the depth of being-seen, or the intimacy that the friendship represents. The friend is the surface; the longing is for the underlying quality.

This is why "stop being jealous" never works as an intervention. The system is jealous because it is pointing at a real want; if the want stays unaddressed, the jealousy keeps pointing at it. The work is to identify the underlying want and either move toward having it or grieve its non-availability.

See [[i-cant-stop-being-jealous-of-my-partners-friend]] for the concrete presenting concern and the coaching path. The path begins exactly here: feel the jealousy, identify the underlying want, separate the want from the surface object.

## Connection to Push-Pull Dynamics

Jealousy is one of the configurations in the broader family of push-pull dynamics — the various ways humans want something and simultaneously push it away. The whole family shares the same structural feature: the reach and the recoil occur together.

See [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]] for the broader treatment, including how jealousy sits alongside neediness, independence-as-defense, transactionality, and other forms of the same underlying mechanism. The dating-specific version is the most legible because the wanting-the-partner and pushing-the-partner-away both happen in observable behavior. But the same structure operates wherever jealousy operates.

Also see [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]] for the deeper principle: the grasping itself is a form of rejection. The jealous reach contains the rejection inside it.

## Jealousy and Anxious-Avoidant Dynamics

In partnered configurations, jealousy often shows up most acutely in the anxious half of the anxious-avoidant dynamic. The anxious partner reaches for the partner; the reaching itself pushes the partner away; the pushing-away produces more anxiety; the cycle compounds. See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]].

The jealousy in this configuration is not really about the third party (the friend, the work, the hobby that the avoidant partner is engaged with). The jealousy is the anxious partner's chronic want-and-push-away dynamic finding a specific target. Reading it as about the target loses the actual material, which is the dynamic itself.

## The Kinked Forms

Jealousy that is not allowed to be felt as longing-and-fear takes destructive forms:

- **Possessiveness:** trying to control the surface object so the wanted-and-pushed-away configuration stays under management.
- **Withdrawal:** preemptively cutting off the connection to the surface object so the jealousy does not have to be felt.
- **Attacking the third party:** redirecting the unfelt longing into hostility toward the person who represents the want.
- **Attacking the self:** the "I'm terrible for feeling this" loop that adds shame on top of the original emotion.

All four are the kinked forms. None of them dissolve the jealousy. All four extend the doubled-motion into worse configurations.

See [[kinked-emotions]] for the broader treatment of how emotions take destructive forms when their signal is not read.

## In Coaching

When a person presents jealousy, the first move is the reframe: this is information, not evidence of badness. "Don't buy the story" is the entry-level intervention. Once the shame layer is interrupted, the actual emotional work becomes available.

The coaching path is the three-piece feel: longing, want, fear of loss. The path is somatic, not analytical. Trying to figure out why the jealousy is irrational or unjustified will not move it. Feeling the three components in the body will.

When a person reports chronic jealousy, the read is usually that the underlying want has been unidentified or unhonored for a long time. The work is to identify the want clearly, separate it from the surface object, and either move toward having it or grieve its non-availability.

When jealousy is laced with shame ("I'm terrible for feeling this"), the work begins with separating the two. Shame is a separate signal pointing at separate material. See [[shame-as-disconnection]]. Treating the jealousy and the shame as one undifferentiated badness loses the information of both.

## Tone

Joe holds jealousy with the same affection he holds anger and fear. The "beautiful roadmap" framing carries the tone: jealousy is not a problem to be eliminated; it is a useful pointer to material the person has been avoiding. The directness of "feels terrible" is paired with the immediate redirect to "don't buy the story" — acknowledging the felt experience without letting the felt experience license the self-attack.
