# Forgiveness as Self-Care (Not for Them, For Your Open Heart)

## Definition

Forgiveness is an act of self-care. It is the work of restoring your own heart to openness after it has been closed by hurt, anger, blame, or resentment. It is not for the other person, not for society, not to look good. It is the question: "given what happened, what do I have to do to be good with my heart again?"

"Forgiveness is inherently an act of self-care, and we've lost that." -- Tara Howley, I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

This is the redirect that resolves the central confusion most people carry into the word. Forgiveness has been used as a moral demand on the wronged person ("good people forgive"). The reframe removes the moral pressure entirely and relocates the work in the body of the person doing it.

"It's that it's truly inner personal work. It's not ever about making someone else feel good, or forgiveness isn't even about forgiving them. Forgiveness so that our heart and our soma, that we can be at home in our body and our hearts can remain open to ourselves and the world around us." -- Tara Howley

## What This Is For

The function of forgiveness is to return the forgiver to an open-hearted state. Not to repair the relationship. Not to communicate anything to the other person. Not to declare the harm okay. The single function: my heart was closed because of this, and I want it open again, so I do the work that produces that.

The diagnostic is somatic. Is the heart open? Has the story about the other person dissolved? Is the gut still clenched? See [[track-forgiveness-somatically]].

## How This Differs From the "Stop Making Them a Monster" Framing

Joe's [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]] frames forgiveness as the structural move of releasing the wrongness-judgment so the heart can open and healing can complete. The two framings are siblings:

- [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]] focuses on what releases (the wrongness-frame, the monster-construction)
- This article focuses on what is being cared for (your heart, your body, your interconnectedness)

Both arrive at the same place — the open heart. The mechanisms are the same. The framing differs:

- The monster framing answers "what is the obstacle to my healing?" Answer: holding them as wrong.
- The self-care framing answers "why am I doing this work?" Answer: to take care of my own heart.

Both are useful. Use the monster framing when the person is stuck in a wrongness-judgment that they cannot let go of. Use the self-care framing when the person resists forgiveness because they have been coerced into it and reject the moral demand.

## The Three-Step Process

The work of self-care forgiveness has a structure. See [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]] for the full framework. In brief:

1. **Heart (emotions).** Be with the hurt, anger, fear, resentment. Process them in whatever way you process emotions.
2. **Head (curiosity).** Get curious about what made the other person do what they did. Open the mind. Let the story dissolve.
3. **Gut (boundaries).** Find the boundaries you need so this does not happen again.

When the three steps are complete, forgiveness arrives as a byproduct. It is not the goal that gets aimed at; it is the natural state that returns when the three obstacles are cleared.

"Once you have the curiosity, the emotional processing, and those clear boundaries, forgiveness is an inevitable byproduct." -- Tara Howley

## Why It Does Not Work to Aim at Forgiveness Directly

The coerced version of forgiveness fails because it skips the three-step work. The person tries to feel forgiveness directly, while the anger is still in the body, while the mind still has the story, while the boundary is still un-drawn. The body knows the forgiveness has not actually happened. The performed forgiveness becomes its own residue.

"You put a kid up in front of someone and say, 'Say I'm sorry.' The kid's like, 'I'm sorry.' Right? They're not really. So there's this way that forgiveness has been coerced on many of us." -- Tara Howley

The self-care reframe makes the coerced version structurally impossible to mistake for the real thing. If forgiveness is for the forgiver, then no one can demand it from outside. The forgiver is the only judge of whether the heart is actually open.

## Forgiveness and Boundaries Are Not in Opposition

The most common reason people refuse to forgive is fear that forgiveness will leave them unprotected. If I forgive them, I might end up back in the same situation. The held resentment, the bad-person frame, the refusal to forgive — these feel protective.

The reframe: protection comes from boundaries, not from held charge. See [[resentment-takes-the-place-of-boundaries]] and [[not-forgiving-doesnt-actually-protect-anyone]].

"I think the reason we don't want to forgive is because we want to make sure it never happens again. We want to make sure like, oh, that wasn't okay. And I'm going to be angry at them so that I never put myself in that position again. So that's actually appropriate. Those boundaries are appropriate. But how do we have the boundaries without having to blame the other person or have hatred or resentment or lingering hurt in our own systems?" -- Tara Howley

Once the boundary is in place, the held charge can release. The boundary does the protection job. The blame can stop carrying it.

## The Cost of Not Forgiving

The held resentment lives in the body of the person who refuses to forgive. The drinking-poison framing captures it:

"Holding onto your anger is like drinking poison and expecting the other person to die." -- cliché, surfaced in the conversation by Alexa Anderson

"Holding onto resentment, because, or blame or hatred. Feels protective, and it is protective, but it doesn't necessarily serve us long term." -- Tara Howley

"Blame eats at us. I actually think it releases all sorts of hormones that are not healthy for us long-term." -- Tara Howley

The not-forgiving is not free. The body is paying. The hormonal load of sustained resentment is part of what self-care forgiveness is for.

## Forgiveness Includes Forgiving Yourself

The self-care framing applies inward. Often the work runs in both directions: the wound from the other and the wound from your own role in what allowed it. See [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]] for the spoken practice; this concept covers the framing.

"That actually empowered me to go, oh, I'm, here's how I'm hooking. When I'm responsible, I can hold the boundary and just go, yeah, I don't, I don't want that. And then I don't need to blame the other person. I can just have my wants and my needs and my boundaries." -- Tara Howley

The self-forgiveness in this context is not about taking on blame for what was done to you. It is about seeing where your own un-held boundary participated in the dynamic, forgiving yourself for not knowing how to hold it then, and using the new knowledge to hold it now.

"You're right about responsibility — like, no one's ever responsible for things that happen to them, but for knowing, oh, here is what I did that enabled this to happen. I can actually then take action and create a boundary around it so that I'm not in that position again." -- Tara Howley

The reframe of self-responsibility is empowering rather than burdening when it stays in this form: not "it was my fault" but "here is the boundary I can now hold that I could not hold before."

## What Forgiveness Is Not

- **Not reconciliation.** Forgiveness does not require returning to the relationship. The internal work is separable from the external proximity. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]].
- **Not for the other person's benefit.** They may never know. The forgiveness happened anyway.
- **Not condoning.** "You don't condone the behavior, but you can understand and have compassion for where the behavior came from." -- Tara Howley
- **Not erasing.** The boundary remains. The memory remains. The change is the open heart.
- **Not coerced.** No one else can demand it. See [[dont-coerce-forgiveness]].
- **Not a single event.** It is the work the three-step process completes. It can take time.

## Forgiveness Can Co-Exist With Strong Boundaries

The form: "I forgive you. I see your inherent goodness. I don't like this kind of behavior. I'm not going to spend time with you. I'm not going to make you bad."

"I'm going to forgive you. I see your goodness. I see your inherent goodness. I don't like this kind of behavior, but I'm not going to exile you forever. I'm going to just have these clear boundaries to take care of me, my family, my community. But I'm not going to make you bad." -- Tara Howley

This is the structure that surprises people who have only known forgiveness as reconciliation. The open heart and the firm boundary live in the same body at the same time. They are not in tension. The boundary makes the open heart safe; the open heart makes the boundary clean.

See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] for the underlying distinction.

## When This Lands

The reframe lands when a person has been refusing forgiveness because the word itself has been corrupted for them. They have been told good people forgive, they have seen forgiveness demanded of children before they were ready, they have watched performed forgiveness used to silence the wronged. The reframe gives them back the word by removing every external claim on it.

It also lands when a person has been doing the work — feeling the feelings, finding the boundaries, getting curious about the other — and is asking whether what they are doing counts as forgiveness even though it doesn't look like what they were taught forgiveness should look like.

## Tone

Direct and unapologetic on the reframe. Soft and slow on the work itself. The reframe is the wall that comes down ("fuck coerced forgiveness"). The work is what becomes possible after the wall comes down ("how do you hold yourself super gently and compassionately through a forgiveness process").
