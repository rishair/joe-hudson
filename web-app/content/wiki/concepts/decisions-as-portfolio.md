# Decisions as a Portfolio (Not as Discrete Events)

## Definition

A single decision's outcome contains too much variance to be a meaningful signal about the quality of that decision. Outcomes are influenced by factors the decider never had access to. The only meaningful evaluation of decision-quality happens at the portfolio level: across many decisions of similar kind, the pattern that emerges is the pattern that can be acted on.

The concept inverts the standard frame. Most people evaluate decisions one by one: "Was that decision good? It worked out, so yes." The portfolio frame asks: "If I made this kind of decision 100 times, what would my track record look like?"

"If I made the kind of decision that I made with the company that didn't do so well 10 times, my portfolio would be freaking amazing. If I did 10 decisions with the one that did well 10 times, I would be bankrupt." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## The Skydiving Articulation

Brett offers the cleanest articulation of the principle outside the venture capital context. A skydiver who survived a jump cannot conclude the jump was a good decision based on the outcome alone. They have to ask: would I have survived this jump 100 times?

"You could say that you made a good decision because you survived the jump you just did. And you could also survive a jump and say, 'I made a bunch of terrible decisions because I don't believe that I could have done that 100 times and survived.' And if I had planned on making 100 jumps, then that was a failure." -- Brett Kistler, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

Joe affirms: "That's exactly right."

This is portfolio theory applied to decision quality. The skydiver, the VC, and the everyday person making relationship choices are all running portfolios. The portfolio is implicit; you might not know you're running one. But you are.

## Why Single-Instance Evaluation Fails

Three failure modes:

**Survivorship bias.** The decisions that worked out get remembered as good decisions. The same kind of decision that didn't work out gets remembered as a bad one. The decider concludes that the difference was in the quality of the deciding, when the actual difference may have been stochastic.

**Outcome dominance.** A bad outcome from a good decision feels like a bad decision in the body. The pain of the outcome contaminates the evaluation. The decider learns the wrong lesson: "don't make that kind of decision again," when the correct lesson was "that kind of decision was right, the outcome was unlucky."

**Process erosion.** Over time, single-instance evaluation gradually pulls the decider toward decisions that produce visible short-term wins, even when the portfolio math favors a different kind of deciding. The VC who measures by individual exits drifts toward investments that have a near-term confirmation, even when the long-term portfolio would be better served by investments that take longer to mature.

## The Portfolio Frame in Practice

The shift is to evaluate the kind of decision, not the instance.

- A negotiation that ended in your favor: ask "if I negotiated this way 100 times, would I be ahead on average?" not "did I win this one?"
- A hire that worked out: ask "would this hiring approach produce good hires consistently?" not "is this person great?"
- A leap of faith that paid off: ask "is this kind of leap the kind I want to take routinely?" not "did this one land?"
- A leap of faith that failed: ask "is this kind of leap one I want to take routinely?" same question, different mood.

The question is consistent. The mood the answer arrives in does not change the structural question.

## The Connection to Principled Living

The portfolio frame is what makes principled living defensible against the single-instance objection. A principled decision often produces a worse outcome on a particular instance: leaving money on the table for an asshole client, declining a deal that another deal-maker would have closed, walking away from an opportunity because it violates an internal rule.

The portfolio math redeems the principle. Over a thousand decisions, the principled decider does better -- not on every instance, but on average. The instance-by-instance second-guessing that the unprincipled decider does is itself the cost of not having the principle. See [[principles-free-you]].

"It's like, oh, I have a portfolio of decisions and my job is to make most of them good decisions. And if I'm living principled, I will do that. And it might be hard in those moments, but finding those principles and living by them, making your decisions by them is the shortcut." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## The Connection to Good-Decision-Is-How-You-Make-It

The portfolio frame and the "decision-is-how-you-make-it" reframe are two sides of the same insight. See [[good-decision-is-how-you-make-it-not-the-outcome]]. Both refuse the standard frame that outcome = decision quality. The portfolio frame says: outcomes only become evaluable across many instances. The how-frame says: the only quality you can evaluate at the time of deciding is the quality of how you decide.

Together they produce an operational stance: focus on how you decide each instance, trust that the portfolio will reflect it over time, do not let any single outcome rewrite the how.

## When This Concept Is Needed

The concept becomes load-bearing in specific situations:

- A leader is second-guessing a decision because the outcome was bad, when the decision itself was sound
- A person is over-attributing their success to their skill when stochasticity was a major factor
- A VC, athlete, gambler, or entrepreneur is reading one instance as a verdict on their method
- A coachee is unable to commit to a way of deciding because they keep getting punished by individual outcomes
- A team is reorganizing its process every time a project fails, instead of evaluating whether the process is sound across many projects

In any of these, the move is to widen the evaluation window. What does the portfolio say?

## What This Is Not

This is not a license to ignore outcomes. Outcomes contain real information. A persistent pattern of bad outcomes from a particular kind of decision is data about that kind of decision. The portfolio frame just says: do not let the single instance be the data point.

This is not an excuse for sloppy deciding. The portfolio frame applies most cleanly when the decider has been consistent. A decider who is making each decision differently has no portfolio; they have a sequence of one-offs.

This is not a finance-only concept. It applies to relationships, parenting, career choices, ethical choices, anything where decisions are made repeatedly and outcomes carry stochasticity.
