# Depression Defined Through Four Levels

## Definition

Depression can be defined four different ways depending on which level is doing the looking: intellectual, emotional, nervous system, and community. Each definition is true. Each points at a different feature of the same phenomenon. Together they give a more complete picture than any single definition can, and each one opens a different door for working with depression.

The four are not in competition. Treating any one as the whole produces a partial response that misses what the other three are reporting.

See [[head-heart-gut]] for the broader three-intelligences framework. This concept is structurally parallel to [[anxiety-three-definitions]] -- the same kind of multi-layer definition applied to a different territory, with one additional layer (community) added because depression has a relational signature that anxiety does not have in the same way.

## The Intellectual Level: Negative Self-Talk at Abnormal Volume

From the prefrontal cortex, depression is an abnormal amount of negative self-talk -- abnormal meaning high enough to depress mood and behavior.

"On the intellectual prefrontal cortex mind, it is negative self-talk. It is an abnormal amount of negative self-talk. Abnormal just meaning high enough to make you get depressed." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

This is the layer of the [[voice-in-the-head]]. The voice is running constantly, attacking the person from inside, narrating every action as evidence of inadequacy. The intellectual layer of depression is the volume of that voice plus the person's belief in what it is saying.

The voice characteristically uses:
- A lot of shoulds
- "Why didn't you do this" -- abusive interrogation
- Second-guessing everything that happens
- Hurry-up pressure: "you're 40 and you haven't done X"
- Constant social rehashing: "did you see how they thought of you, they didn't like that at all"

"It's going to be very abusive, very critical, uh, self-talk." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

The work at this layer: notice that the voice is bullshit, that it is not true, that it is not serving the person. The voice is "not a good boss." Tools that work at this layer include [[lawyer-the-voice]], [[untruth-in-every-thought]], [[experimenting-with-the-voice]], [[apply-view-to-the-voice]], and [[voice-as-care]].

### The Stop-Using-Should Hack

A concrete starting practice: notice every time you use the word "should" and change it to "want." This trains the voice to start exposing its own structure. Most shoulds either dissolve or reveal a want underneath. See [[wants-over-shoulds]].

### Debate the Voice

Beyond reframing, the practice of explicitly debating the voice produces fast shifts. Write down the 20 most persistent negative self-thoughts. Literally debate them. Take the other side. After examining each, the voice's logic typically collapses: "Yeah, that's bullshit. That's bullshit. There's a little bit of truth in each of them, but the general premise is bullshit." See [[lawyer-the-voice]].

### Respond to the Voice Differently

A second high-leverage move: most people acquiesce to the voice. "You really fucked up." "Yeah, I fucked up. Okay." The voice gets to deliver its accusations and the person agrees as if it were management feedback.

The alternative is to change the response rather than the content. "You really fucked up" can be met with: "Are you talking to you? Is this the voice in the head talking to itself or is it talking to me?" Or with humor, song, dismissal, or empathy: "I see that you're really scared and that's okay." See [[experimenting-with-the-voice]].

The Fritz Perls framing Joe references: the voice as "overdog, underdog." The overdog says "you should do this." The underdog says "yeah, I should... fuck you" and sabotages. Most personal-development work tries to change what the overdog says. The shift here is changing the underdog's response.

## The Emotional Level: Repressed Anger (and Sometimes Sadness)

From the emotional layer, depression is repressed emotion -- typically anger, sometimes sadness. The energy is moving inward instead of moving through or outward.

"On an emotional level, it's repressed anger typically, sometimes repressed sadness but often repressed anger. The anger is going in instead of the anger is going out or moving through." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

This is the inward form in [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]]: anger that has nowhere to go turns on the self and becomes self-abusive talk. The voice in the head and the held anger are not two separate phenomena. They are the same energy in two registers.

Brett's articulation in the same conversation makes the mechanism vivid:

"I've come to see anger as connected to a broader sense aggression, and on an even broader sense motivation, eros, like our capacity to move towards and want something. And if I'm spending a significant amount of that energy on telling myself how to be, then I might be like, oh, where's all my motivation? I don't have motivation. But all of my motivation is actually going towards telling myself how to be and beating myself up." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A January 2023

Joe's response: "You are very motivated to beat yourself up. What do you mean you don't have motivation? It's like a full-time job. You might not even be sleeping."

The "I have no motivation" report is not accurate. The motivation is fully present. It is just being spent on the inward attack rather than on outward movement. The motivation energy and the depression energy are the same energy in different directions.

The work at this layer: move the anger somewhere it can go without landing on a person. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[anger]], and [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]].

### The 97% Rule

Joe's strong empirical claim: when someone is depressed, the emotional layer is stuck anger about 97% of the time, with stuck sadness in the other 3%. The default assumption when someone presents depression should be that anger is the entry. Sadness may be underneath the anger, and usually arrives naturally after the anger moves.

"If you're listening to this and you're like, 'But anger is bad,' or anything like that, I would say it's a 97% chance that it's stuck anger. And that's the first place to go." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

"Oftentimes underneath the anger there'll be some sadness too. Uh, yeah, so for some, for maybe 3%, they're just, they can't get sad... But typically, like I said, it's anger. And if the anger moves, then the tears will come naturally." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

### What to Do With "I'm Not Angry"

A common move from the depressed person: "I'm not really an angry person. I don't have anger." Joe's response is to point at all the ways the anger is leaking sideways: passive-aggression, being late, despondency, apathy, rebellion, having authority figures constantly upset with them, not being in touch with their own wants. All of that is repressed anger. See [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]] Form 2.

In coaching: "I take away their capacity to be passive-aggressive and I'll just push them. I will keep on pointing to the anger and eventually they get really, really upset with me. They're like, 'Ah!' And then I'll go, 'Yay, look there, you're angry. That's what I'm talking about right there.'" -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

For the person doing this work alone: do not wait until you "feel" angry. Act the anger out. Treat yourself as an actor playing the role of an angry person, performing the part until clarity arrives. The body's resistance ("I'm not really angry") is part of the depression and should not be deferred to. See [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] and [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]].

"Just like pretend you are in a play and your job is to get angry as heck and angry until you have complete clarity. That's the role that you're playing. Your body's going to want to collapse, but see yourself as an actor trying to play this role and give the most compelling performance ever." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

The reframe Joe offers to the listener: you have been listening to the voice in your head for years and this is where it has gotten you. So for 40 minutes, listen to your anger instead and see what happens.

## The Nervous System Level: Constantly Attentive to the Next Attack

From the nervous system, depression is the body in constant vigilance -- constantly attentive to the next foot that will fall, the next attack. And because the head is beating the person up all the time, the attack is constantly coming. So the body is constantly scanning for it.

"On a nervous system, it's constantly attentive to the next foot that's going to fall, the next attack. So on the nervous system, you're constantly -- and it's, by the way, always coming because internally your head is beating you up all the time. So the attack is constantly coming, so you're constantly looking for it. So it's a constant diligence, which is why antidepressant and anti-anxiety medicines are often used to treat depression because it's about that, that's the nervous system side of it." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

This is why anxiety medications and antidepressants are often used together or interchangeably for depression. The nervous system layer of depression is structurally the same as anxiety -- constant scanning for threat. The threat just happens to be coming from inside.

The work at this layer: learn to have deep non-guilty pleasure and sustain it. The pleasure of breathing, walking, the vocal cords vibrating in the chest. Resting in life's intrinsic pleasure teaches the nervous system that there is something else available besides scanning for attack.

"For the nervous system, learning to have a deep non-guilty pleasure and sustain it. How do you be in pleasure? The pleasure of breathing, the pleasure of walking, the pleasure of hearing that your vocal cords vibrate in your chest. Life in its essence is very pleasurable, very enjoyable. And so how do you learn how to rest in that pleasure and enjoyment?" -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

See [[pleasure-capacity]] and [[enjoyment-as-path]] for the practice. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]] for the related but distinct phenomenon of physical depression after sustained stress.

### The Shame-Blocks-Pleasure Mechanism

A specific obstacle at the nervous system layer: shame interrupts simple pleasures before they can register. Joe lists examples: eating, breathing, walking, talking to a friend. Each has intrinsic pleasure available. In the depressed person, each is interrupted with shame ("you shouldn't enjoy this," "you don't deserve this," "you should be doing something productive").

The work is not to manufacture larger pleasures. It is to relax into the pleasure that is already present in the basics. Joe's example: noticing the vibration of vocal cords while speaking, the pleasure in a breath.

"The simple things that you can get a lot of pleasure from, like eating or breathing or walking or talking to a friend, is all like interrupted with shame. So it's like, how do you take pleasure in your daily life and all the little things that are there?" -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] for the broader mechanism. The shame block here is the specific form at the nervous system layer.

## The Community Level: Disconnection

From the community level, depression is a felt sense of disconnection from other people. Not just being alone -- the felt experience of not being connected.

"And then on a community side of it, it's a disconnection. It's a feeling of disconnection with people. So it's feeling not feeling like you're connected with each other." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

This is why a rich social life is one of the most reliable interventions for depression. Connection at the relational level addresses the layer of depression that internal work cannot reach. The person can do all the inner work and still be depressed if they are not in connection with other people.

The work at this layer: build a rich social life. Spend time in connection. See [[connection]], [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]], and [[notice-and-be-grateful-for-connection]].

See also [[shame-as-disconnection]] for the related mechanism: shame is what the body produces when disconnection is happening, and shame is also one of the engines of the inward attack at the intellectual layer.

### Why Community Is Both Symptom and Cause

Joe notes that he is uncertain whether the community layer is properly a cause or a symptom of depression. Both directions of causation operate: depression produces isolation, and isolation produces depression. The feedback works in both directions.

This is the same structure as the feedback loops across all four layers (see "The Reinforcing Loops" below). The practical implication: addressing the community layer is high-leverage because the loop runs in either direction. Building connection breaks the loop from outside even if internal work is also needed.

## The Multi-Level Treatment

A complete response to depression works all four layers. Picking one and ignoring the others usually produces partial relief that does not last.

- **Intellectual:** Recognize the voice is not true. Practice not believing it. Use the voice-work tools.
- **Emotional:** Move the held anger (and sadness) somewhere it can move without landing on a person.
- **Nervous system:** Build the capacity to rest in pleasure. Learn to enjoy basic life experiences.
- **Community:** Stay in connection. Build a social life that is not transactional.

"There's obviously some level of chemical stuff happening in the brain, and I'm not suggesting that for some folks, medicinal treatments aren't great. And this is basically how it works. So if you're working on it with medicines or without medicines, this is -- these are the ways I would suggest it." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The four-level frame is not a rejection of medication. It is a frame for the work that medication does not do. Medication can address the nervous system layer chemically. The other three layers still need attention.

## Why the Four-Level Frame Helps

Most attempts to address depression pick one layer and stay there.

- Cognitive behavioral therapy targets the intellectual layer.
- Antidepressants target the nervous system layer.
- Exercise targets the nervous system and emotional layers indirectly.
- Talk therapy targets the emotional and intellectual layers.
- Group support targets the community layer.

Each helps to the extent that it addresses its layer. Each is incomplete because depression operates on all four. A person who has done the cognitive work but still has held anger will remain depressed. A person who has moved the anger but is socially isolated will remain depressed. A person who has rich relationships but is constantly being attacked by the voice in their head will remain depressed.

The frame is also diagnostic. When working with a person who reports depression, ask which layer they have access to. Some can describe the voice in detail. Some are clearly in held anger but don't see it. Some are isolated. Some are wired hot. Meet them where they have access. Expand from there.

## In Coaching

When someone presents depression, the question is not "what is depression?" but "which layer is the entry point for this person right now?"

- If they are obsessing about what is wrong with them: enter through the intellectual layer. Work with the voice.
- If they describe feeling flat, unmotivated, drained: enter through the emotional layer. Look for the held anger.
- If they describe being on edge, never resting, scanning constantly: enter through the nervous system. Work with pleasure.
- If they describe isolation, no one really knowing them: enter through community. Help them get into connection.

The other layers will come up. They always do. But starting where the person has access keeps the work landing rather than abstract.

## The Reinforcing Loops

The four layers feed each other. Each loop amplifies the others, which is part of what makes depression appear hard to escape.

- Negative self-talk -> belief in the self-talk -> reduced performance -> more negative self-talk
- Negative self-talk -> feeling under attack -> nervous system anxiety -> trying to escape the anxiety -> feeling stuck -> more negative self-talk
- Repressed anger -> inward attack (more voice) -> more shame -> more isolation -> more repressed anger
- Adrenal fatigue -> can't perform -> voice attacks for not performing -> more stress -> more adrenal fatigue
- Isolation -> no external mirror to interrupt the voice -> voice grows louder -> more shame -> more isolation

"The negative self-talk makes you feel like you're under attack on the nervous system, creates more anxiety. The more anxiety you have, the more you're trying to get out of it, which makes you feel stuck. There's so many ways in which this all feeds each other, which I think what makes it apparently challenging to get out of. It's not actually challenging to get out of." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

The loops imply that interventions at any layer also reduce pressure on the others. Moving anger reduces the fuel for the voice. Relaxing the nervous system reduces the urgency of the voice. Connection reduces the shame that amplifies the inward attack. The system is multiply connected; partial interventions still propagate.

## The Premise That Is the Depression

The deepest issue underneath the surface of depression is the premise "there is something wrong with me that I have to fix." That premise, applied as a method, is itself the depression.

"It literally is the depression. There's a way to think of it as there's something wrong with me is the depression. And so any fight to make, make yourself better is part of the self-fulfilling prophecy of depression." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

This is why working on depression from a fix-me frame reinforces it. The work has to start from a different premise: "I am whole. The voice in my head is using bad management techniques and could use better ones. The anger is moving inward and needs another direction. The body needs rest. I need connection." Each of those frames the work without making the person the problem.

See [[symptoms-vs-root-causes-of-depression]] for the full treatment of this distinction and [[inherent-goodness]] for the replacement premise. See [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] for the broader pattern this is an instance of.

## The Symptom Cluster

The visible surface of depression is a cluster of recognizable symptoms: pessimism, forever thinking, the felt stuckness, [[soul-dysmorphia]] (can't see yourself clearly), short attention span, adrenal fatigue, and self-talk that does not survive examination. Each is a symptom, not a cause. See [[symptoms-of-depression]] for the full diagnostic.

The recurring failure mode is treating the symptoms directly: trying to think positively, trying to be more optimistic, trying to focus better, trying to push past the fatigue. Each addresses the surface while leaving the causes running. See [[symptoms-vs-root-causes-of-depression]].

## When Someone Is Supporting a Depressed Person

The four-level frame also clarifies what to do (and not do) when supporting a depressed loved one. The work is the opposite of fixing. Every well-intentioned attempt to fix the depressed person communicates "you are broken," which amplifies the depression at all four layers. The work is sending the message that they are whole.

See [[send-the-message-that-youre-whole]] for the move and [[foot-washing-quaker]] for the canonical example. See [[relating-to-someone-with-mental-health-issues]] for the broader application.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Persistent low mood that doesn't respond to single interventions
- The felt sense of being "depressed" with no clear cause
- High-functioning people who report exhaustion and flatness despite stable external circumstances
- Long-time meditators who have worked on themselves and are still depressed (often the layer they haven't worked on is the one to address)
- People who have tried medication and feel partially relieved but not whole

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and operational. Depression is not pathologized as a uniquely opaque condition. It is broken down into four legible mechanisms, each of which has corresponding work. The frame is practical, not clinical.
