# Belonging Is Trained, Not True

## Definition

The felt-sense of not-belonging is not a perception of the current situation. It is a pattern from childhood being applied to every situation regardless of fit. The person is not noticing that they don't belong here; they are running the not-belonging algorithm and finding evidence to confirm it.

"What you're doing is you're finding that you don't belong in every situation that you go to." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The training is one of the basic identity-formations that comes out of a particular kind of childhood: the kind where what the child essentially was got rejected, not what they did. The training is the protective response to that rejection.

## What It IS

A trained pattern. A learned response. An identity that was useful at some point and is still running. A protection against re-experiencing an original rejection. A move that gets enacted in every new environment automatically.

## What It Is NOT

A truth about the person. A property of any current situation. A trustworthy reading of the social field. A signal worth following. A reason to leave.

"There's no way that any of us in today's world can't find a sense of belonging. And yet, when you've defined yourself as an outsider, what you're doing is you're finding that you don't belong in every situation that you go to." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

This is the structural observation that makes the training visible. The world contains many environments. The person is finding the same not-belonging in all of them. The constant variable is the person, not the environments. The not-belonging is in the reader, not the situation.

## How Joe Explains It

The teaching has two operative moves: name the training, then name what it protects against.

**Naming the training.** The feeling is historic. It has nothing to do with where the person is today. They will feel the not-belonging no matter where they are. This is the diagnostic. If the felt-state is constant across very different environments, the felt-state is not a function of the environments.

"It's an amazing thing when we are hanging out and doing retreats. You can often find the person very quickly who's like, 'Oh yeah, I don't belong here. I'm on the outside.' At the end of any of our week-long retreats, everybody knows they belong. But people will come in with that sense of, 'Oh, I don't belong,' that you know is historic." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

**Naming what it protects against.** The function of the trained not-belonging is to avoid the original felt-experience: the rejection of essence. The original rejection wasn't "I'm not going to do business with you" or "I don't want to go on a date with you." The original rejection was of who the child essentially was.

"The rejection that you received when you were young, which was basically who you are essentially, was not good enough. And so that feeling we're avoiding by constantly going around and proving that we don't belong." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The protective logic: if I decide in advance that I don't belong, I never have to feel that rejection again. The cost is that I never get to feel actual belonging either, because I am always finding the evidence that I don't.

"If I think I don't belong, then I don't have to feel your rejection." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

## The Mechanism: How the Training Generates Its Own Evidence

The training does not just sit in the body waiting to be triggered. It actively produces the evidence that confirms it. The three behavioral expressions are documented in [[three-strategies-of-not-belonging]]: provoking, hiding, and going superior.

Each strategy generates real social feedback that the person then reads as confirmation that they don't belong:
- The provocateur acts in ways that produce distance, then notes the distance.
- The hider becomes invisible, then notes that nobody sees them.
- The superiority-runner finds the group lacking, then notes that they have nothing in common with the group.

The training is self-reinforcing. Each round of strategy + evidence deepens the conviction that not-belonging is just-who-I-am, when in fact it is what the strategy is producing.

## When Someone Experiences This

When you walk into a room and the first thing you notice is who isn't looking at you, that is this pattern. When you join a new community and within the first hour you have already located the reasons it's not for you, that is this pattern. When you have a partner who loves you and you still feel like they don't really get you, that is this pattern. When the material conditions for belonging are present and the felt-state has not shifted, that is this pattern.

The training is impressively portable. It travels with you to every new environment. It will find evidence for itself in places that contain no actual evidence. Recognizing that the feeling is the training, not the situation, is the first opening.

## Tone

Direct and slightly bemused. Joe says this with the matter-of-factness of someone who has watched the same pattern dozens of times across retreats. The bemusement is structural — the constant rediscovery of not-belonging in every environment is, when seen from outside, a recognizable algorithm. The teaching is delivered with no contempt for the person running it. The training was protective once; naming it now is just helping the person see what they are doing.

## Anti-Patterns

- Don't try to argue someone out of the not-belonging feeling by listing reasons they do belong. The person will hear this as confirmation that the listener doesn't get it. The reassurance becomes more evidence of not-being-seen.
- Don't fix the not-belonging by trying to engineer better belonging-conditions. The pattern travels with the person. Better conditions don't dissolve a pattern that is operating on the felt-state directly.
- Don't treat the trained not-belonging as a fact about social skill or charisma. The person is often warm and likeable; they are just running the not-belonging algorithm regardless.

## Distinctions

- [[i-dont-belong-here]] vs. an accurate read of a particular environment: sometimes the person actually has chosen a bad-fit environment. The diagnostic is the constancy. If the not-belonging shows up everywhere, it is the training. If it shows up only here, it is a real read of this place.
- The trained not-belonging vs. healthy preference for solitude: some people genuinely prefer their own company and feel fine. The training comes with a felt-pain and a felt-rejection that solitary preference does not.
