# Faithing (Faith as a Verb)

## Definition

Faithing is the active, present-tense state of trusting that what is needed is already available -- without specifying what is being trusted in. It is faith without an object. The grammar matters: it is not a noun describing a belief held, but a verb describing what the system is currently doing.

Joe makes the distinction between trust and faith deliberately. Trust is a usable word in modern English and most people can land on it. But faith carries a particular quality that trust does not. Faith does not require an object. You can faith without specifying what you have faith in. The state itself is the thing.

"I was told in ancient Greek, I think it was ancient Greek, that faith used to be a verb. You were faithing. Like, it was like, it was something like, I'm walking, I'm faithing. So if you think about like when you're speaking this way, you're just faithing. You're just faithing that the butler is going to [arrive]. And it's not faith in like some external thing. It's just like faith that it's all there for you." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The verb form is the operative move. It puts faith in the same grammatical category as "walking" -- something the system is doing right now, in the body, as a present action. Not a position taken about reality, but a way of moving through reality.

## Faith Is Not Faith In

The conventional English meaning of faith is "faith in something" -- faith in God, faith in a person, faith in an outcome. Faithing strips the preposition. There is no specific object. The state itself is the relationship.

This matters operationally. If faith requires an object, the object can collapse and the faith collapses with it. The person whose faith is in a specific god, a specific person, a specific outcome is vulnerable to that object failing. The person whose faithing is a way of being is not bound to any specific object. They can faith whether or not any particular thing is true.

This is the same structure as [[opposite-of-trying]]: the receiving state is not receiving from a specified source. It is the orientation itself. Faithing is the receiving state with a temporal cast: the receiving is happening continuously, in this moment, while the action is occurring.

## The Distinction From Trust

Joe prefers "faith" over "trust" in this context for a specific reason. Trust, in modern usage, almost always carries an implicit object: I trust *you*, I trust *the process*, I trust *myself*. The word is built to take a direct object.

Faithing dissolves the object. When Joe describes the state of speaking from the butler -- discovering the words as they come out of the mouth at the same time the audience does -- there is nothing specific being trusted. The speaker is not trusting any particular outcome. The speaker is not trusting any specific source. The speaker is simply moving in a particular orientation, and the word for that orientation is faithing.

"Trust is a word that we can use nowadays. That's everybody can get into. But I actually like the word faith more. Not faith in." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

Tristan affirms immediately: "I like faith so much more." The recognition is mutual. Both have arrived at the same observation: the working state requires a word that does not demand an object.

## What Faithing Looks Like in Practice

Faithing is what happens in the body when the speaker pauses and the next thing arrives without effort. It is what happens in the coach when the question comes without preparation and turns out to be exactly what was needed. It is what happens in the writer when the page produces a sentence the writer did not plan. It is what happens in the leader who allows the meeting to unfold rather than driving it.

In each case, the felt sense is the same: the system is moving without grasping. There is no specific object being trusted, no specific outcome being held to. There is also no slackness. The system is alert, present, and engaged. It is faithing.

The closest neighboring concept in the compendium is the listening state described in [[listening]]: spaciousness, expansion, the body soft, the foreground open, the mind running in the background without dominating. Faithing is what that listening state looks like when described temporally as an action the system is performing.

## Faithing and the Butler

The cleanest pairing in Joe's framing is faithing with the [[the-butler-metaphor]]. The butler is what arrives when the system is faithing. The faithing is what allows the butler to be seen.

"When you're speaking this way, you're just faithing. You're just faithing that the butler is going to [arrive]. And it's not faith in like some external thing. It's just like faith that it's all there for you." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The point of pairing them: faithing without the butler image is abstract; the butler without the faithing is a passive image. Together, they describe the dynamic. The faithing is the active orientation. The butler is the metaphor for what the orientation receives.

## Faithing Is Not Belief

A point worth being explicit about. Faithing is not a belief state. The person who is faithing does not have to believe anything in particular. They do not have to believe the words will come. They do not have to believe everything will be fine. They do not have to believe in any source of wisdom.

Belief is mental content. Faithing is a somatic orientation. A person can be faithing while their mind is full of doubt. A person can be entirely confident in their beliefs and not be faithing at all. The two are independent.

This protects against a misreading the AI coach must avoid: faithing is not "tell yourself the answer will come." That is just another form of trying -- using belief to produce a state. Faithing is the state itself, accessed through relaxation, presence, listening. The belief, if it appears, is downstream. It is the report after the fact of having faithed.

## The "Group Has the Same Faith" Extension

Joe's extension of the butler -- it is not just your butler -- has a parallel for faithing. The group can faith together. A room of people can be in the collective state where the next contribution arrives from whichever person is most accessible to it. The faithing is distributed across the room.

"It's such a pleasure to watch how non-personal it can be... I love it when there's like, it's, you can see it in a group that the whole group has the butler." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

This is what produces the well-functioning meeting where everyone is listening, contributions arrive at the right moments from the right people, and the group lands somewhere none of them planned. The room is faithing. The wisdom moves through whoever is most available at each turn.

## Faithing Has Been Going On For Eons

A coda Joe offers in this conversation: the faithing being described is not new. It is the same orientation people have been entering for 200,000 years. People have been pausing and seeing what comes out of their mouth for as long as there have been people. The teaching is not a new discovery. It is a re-articulation of something humans have access to as a structural feature of being human.

This matters operationally because it pulls the practice out of the special-and-rare frame and into the ordinary-and-available frame. The faithing the coach is being invited into is not exotic. It is what humans do whenever they let themselves do it. The work is to remember the capacity, not to acquire it.

## When Each Word Works Best

Both words remain useful. The distinction is about which context calls for which.

**Trust is the right word when:**
- The work is about specific relationships and whether they can be relied on.
- The question is "what would build trust here?"
- The team or relationship needs an honest assessment of what is and is not currently trustworthy.
- The mechanism of demand and accountability is appropriate.

**Faithing is the right word when:**
- The orientation is what matters, not the object of the belief.
- The receiver needs to drop the question "faith in what?" and just be in the receiving state.
- The speaker is in flow and is not generating but discovering.
- The state itself is what is being pointed at, not a belief about something.

In the speaking context, Joe and Tristan converge on faith over trust because trust still implies an object. "I trust myself," "I trust the audience," "I trust the moment" -- each of these still requires a specific evaluation. Faithing as orientation does not. The speaker is simply oriented toward what arrives, listening, allowing the butler to show up. There is no specific object that has to be believed-in.

"It's not faith in like some external thing. It's just like faith that. It's all there for you." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The "faith that" trails off without a complete object. This is intentional. The orientation does not need completion. You are faithing.

## Faithing Across Domains

The verb form of faith generalizes across domains. The pattern is consistent in each: the noun-form (faith in something specific) is fragile and produces gripping. The verb-form (the orientation itself) produces presence and flow.

- **Speaking.** Faithing while speaking is what allows the words to arrive without construction.
- **Coaching.** Faithing while coaching is what makes following the client (instead of leading them) possible. The coach is in the orientation, not gripping a plan.
- **Parenting.** Faithing with children is the orientation that allows the child to develop without being managed.
- **Leadership.** Faithing in a team is not "faith that the team will succeed" -- it is the orientation in which trust can be built and intelligence can surface.
- **Decision-making.** Faithing while deciding is the orientation in which the right next step becomes visible.

## A Subtle Coaching Application

A coachee who comes in saying "I don't have faith in X" is often asking the wrong question. The fix is not to install faith in X. The fix is to drop into the orientation where X-faith is not needed.

A coachee who says "I trust my partner" but is still gripping in the relationship is in a similar bind. Trust as a noun has been installed. The gripping continues because the orientation has not changed. The faithing has not started.

The coaching move: notice when the conversation is happening at the level of objects-of-belief and shift it to the level of orientation. The question is not "what do you have faith in?" The question is "are you faithing?" The first question loads the conversation with content. The second invites a state shift.

## When This Distinction Matters

In most coaching contexts, "trust" works fine and faithing does not need to be introduced. Where the distinction matters is:

- When a person is asking what to trust and finding no satisfying answer.
- When a person's trust has been broken in a specific direction (in God, in a partner, in themselves) and they cannot rebuild it.
- When a person is trying to manufacture trust as a strategy and the trying is what is preventing the state.
- When the teaching is about receiving and the word "trust" is collapsing the teaching back into trying-to-trust-something-specific.
- When a person has rejected religion and now resists the word "faith" because they think it requires belief in a deity.

In each of these cases, the introduction of faithing -- faith as a verb, faith without an object -- reopens the door. The person does not have to find something to trust. They have to enter the state. The state is its own justification.

## Tone

Reflective. The introduction of this distinction in conversation feels like Joe pulling out a word he has been holding and offering it as a refinement. There is no urgency in the offer. It is a vocabulary suggestion for the listener who is ready for it. The delivery is unforced; if it does not land, nothing is lost. If it lands, it tends to land deeply because it gives a name to something the listener already has felt sense of.
