# Parenting as a Deep Tissue Massage of Identity

## Definition

Parenting is a process that dismantles your identity whether or not you cooperate. Whatever you thought was true about yourself — what kind of partner you would be, what kind of parent you would be, how you would sleep, what you control, what is right — gets pulled out of you piece by piece. The metaphor Joe uses is a deep tissue massage. If you resist, it hurts more. If you let it happen, parts of who you thought you were come loose and the underlying essence becomes visible.

"It felt like it was a deep tissue massage, right? Like the more you resisted, you were just fucked. So you don't resist, you just like it's going to tear parts of your identity away. It's going to tear parts of what you think is right away. Like everything just starts shifting. And if you, and I think that the spiritual journey of having, being a parent is allowing all that stuff to be let go and to have it ripped from you so that you can find out what your essence is." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This is one of the most direct framings Joe offers for why parenting is the spiritual practice it is. Not "parenting teaches you patience." Parenting takes parts of you that you considered fixed and demonstrates that they were never fixed. The frame inverts what most people are told to expect from parenthood. The losses are not bugs. They are the practice.

## What Specifically Gets Massaged

A list of what Joe watched come apart in himself and Tara as Esme arrived. Each was something he had thought was firm before the child arrived:

- **What kind of parent you would be.** Joe and Tara had a plan. Tara would be the breadwinner. Joe would be the stay-at-home dad. The hormones shifted within months of pregnancy. By Esme's birth Tara said "I'm not leaving this kid. Fuck making money, you go do that." The roles they had been certain of were gone.
- **What you control.** Sleep, schedule, food, attention. The illusion of control over any of these dissolves within weeks of the baby arriving.
- **What you think is right.** A lot of what people consider correct parenting is just what their parents did. The contact with the actual child often reveals that some of those convictions were inherited, not chosen.
- **The image of yourself as the kind of person who could be patient, kind, present, regulated, etc.** Whatever self-image was built outside of parenting conditions gets tested in conditions where it cannot be performed.

The list keeps going. The principle is: anything you thought was a fixed part of you that does not survive contact with the child was a story you were holding, not a fact about you. The massage reveals which is which.

## Resistance vs. Surrender

The metaphor includes its own instruction. A deep tissue massage hurts when you resist and releases when you let it work. So with parenting.

The parents who resist try to keep their pre-parenting identity intact. The marriage they planned. The parenting style they intended. The hours of sleep they expected. The career trajectory they were on. The self-image they built. The resistance does not preserve any of these. It just turns the dismantling from a release into a wound.

The parents who allow the dismantling lose the same things — there is no version where parents come out unchanged. But what they find on the other side is what was actually there underneath the identity-extensions. Joe calls this "your essence." See [[expanding-sense-of-self]] for how identity transforms across this kind of process.

This is also why some parents look unrecognizable a few years in (in a good way) and others look hardened. The hardened ones are still fighting the massage. The transformed ones surrendered.

## Why This Is the Most Efficient Spiritual Practice Available

The structural argument: most spiritual practices require the practitioner to design conditions that strip identity. A meditation retreat. A long fast. A silent period. A teacher who challenges the student. All of these are external pressures the practitioner has to seek out.

Parenting installs the pressure as a permanent condition. The practitioner does not have to design anything. The conditions arrive every morning. The child does not negotiate. The child does not take a day off. Whatever identity has not yet been seen through is going to get seen through, or the parent is going to suffer trying to defend it.

"That's part of the deep tissue massage." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This is the same general phenomenon as [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]] and [[parenting-as-real-world-test-of-inner-work]], but framed from a different angle. The mirror version is "you cannot lie to yourself with a child in the room." The deep-tissue-massage version is "you cannot keep your identity intact with a child in the room." The mirror reveals what is. The massage dismantles what was.

## What Comes Apart in Order

Joe lists, in the moment, what came apart for him:

"You think this? Nope. You think you control this? Nope. You think you control, you think you control how you sleep? Nope. Like it was just like everything was just taken." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The cadence of that list is the felt cadence of parenting in the first years. Each new dimension of the parent's life shows up to be tested and most do not survive intact. The parent who was going to be the breadwinner becomes the stay-at-home parent. The parent who was going to sleep through the night does not. The parent who was going to feed the child a certain way ends up feeding the child whatever the child will eat. The parent who was going to never raise their voice raises their voice. The parent who was going to stay close to their old friends loses some of them.

Each loss is the practice. The parent who can hold each loss with curiosity rather than grief-with-resistance moves through the massage and finds what is underneath. The parent who resists each loss accumulates resentments and patterns of bracing.

## The Bigger Heartbreak You Sign Up For

There is a particular form of identity dismantling that parenting does that the rest of the spiritual path does not necessarily do. You are also signing up for unlimited heartbreak that has no exit.

"In parenthood, you are opening yourself up to unlimited heartbreak, whether you lose the kid or not lose the kid. If you actually allow yourself to feel the thing of there's just like — and like I've said, the breaking of the heart is like breaking it open. It just increases your capacity to love." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This connects to [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]. The deep tissue massage of identity also includes whatever was protecting your heart from being touched too much by another person. That comes apart too. The parent who allows this finds that their capacity to love has changed deeply. Joe is direct about this: he could not do the work he does in the world if it were not for having kids.

## What Cannot Survive the Massage

A short list of identities that parenting tends to dissolve, regardless of how committed the parent was to them:

- **The independent person.** Parenting makes you dependent on your partner, on your friends, on your community, on grandparents. The autonomy story collapses.
- **The unhurried person.** Parenting is hurried. The slow pace is gone.
- **The intellectually superior parent.** Talking down to a 2-year-old like an adult does not work. The child responds to your emotional presence, not your reasoning. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]].
- **The parent who is going to do it differently than their parents did.** Joe's read: 90% of what most people do as parents is what their parents did to them, and 10% is overcorrection from what they had a problem with. Either way, the early parenting moves are usually inherited, not chosen. The identity of "I am going to be different" does not survive contact with the conditions that produced the inheritance.
- **The parent who is going to keep their identity unchanged.** This is the one that everyone learns about last.

## "Rip Your Ego Out of You Day After Day" (The Live-Coaching Form)

The metaphor also appears in live coaching, where Joe uses it to address the parent who is in the middle of the dismantling and is treating the dismantling as a problem. The compact in-session articulation:

"This is the gift of parenting. You have all the fluidity there. You just have to let it come out of your mouth instead of protecting yourself from it." -- Joe Hudson, He Discovers His Negative Emotions Aren't the Problem

The fuller version, delivered to a father who had been clamping down on his anger and sadness because he believed it would harm his 2-year-old daughter and could not access his excitement about news of a second child:

"Let it break your heart. You don't have to be strong around this. Who would you be if you gave up everything for me — for May? Your identity, all the space you think you need, out of a deep want to, not because you had to, but because that's just the kind of father you are. Who would you be when she's 18? This is the gift of parenting. You have all the fluidity there. You just have to let it come out of your mouth instead of protecting yourself from it." -- Joe Hudson, He Discovers His Negative Emotions Aren't the Problem

The phrasing here adds three things to the conceptual definition above:

- **The deep-tissue work names a specific cost: the parent's space.** "All the space you think you need" is one of the specific identity-extensions the dismantling targets. The parent who has been organizing their life around having enough space discovers, through the practice, that the space they thought they needed was the boundary of the version of themselves the practice is dismantling.

- **The dismantling is framed as a deep want, not a duty.** "Out of a deep want to, not because you had to" — this is the difference between the parent who is suffering the dismantling and the parent who is choosing it. The parent who can find the want underneath the obligation moves through the practice differently than the parent who is enduring it.

- **The future-tense question relocates the practice from drudgery to development.** "Who would you be when she's 18?" is the question that surfaces the developmental destination — the parent the dismantling is producing. The parent who can feel the destination, even briefly, gets a felt anchor for the practice. The session source includes the response Joe was reading for: "I almost don't know if it matters. Yeah, whatever that is. Yes, that's right." The collapse of the future-tense ("I almost don't know if it matters") is the indicator that the destination has arrived as a present-tense felt sense, not as a distant goal.

The visceral phrasing — "rip your ego out of you day after day after day" — extends the deep-tissue metaphor with the parenting-specific cadence. The massage is not a one-time event. It is a daily, ongoing, repetitive removal of the parent's pre-parenting structure. The "day after day after day" cadence is the felt rhythm of the practice. Anyone who has parented a young child recognizes the cadence immediately.

The live use of the metaphor also shows it operationally: not as a teaching to deliver to a parent in distress, but as a structural orientation that lets the coaching move forward. The parent who hears "rip your ego out of you" responds with "I want that" because something in them already knows the dismantling is the practice. The metaphor names what they already feel and gives it dignity.

## The Reframe Joe Hands Pre-Parents Asking What It's Like

When pre-parents or new parents ask Joe what parenting is actually like, the deep-tissue-massage metaphor is the line he reaches for. He has used it so often it has become his standard handoff. In the conversation with Nathan Baschez:

"I remember people used to ask me when our kids were a little bit younger, you know, tell me about parenting. And I would say, parenting is like a deep tissue massage. If you resist it, you're fucked." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The line works because it is compact, physical, and a little funny. The pre-parent who hears it cannot quite tell whether to laugh or brace. Both responses are useful. The laugh receives the line. The brace registers that something serious is being named. Both are part of how the metaphor lands.

The line is also the operational hand-off into the work the parent is going to have to do. The deep-tissue-massage frame implies a stance: do not fight what is coming. The fighting is the source of the suffering, more than the changes themselves. The same stance is what makes [[make-parenting-a-shared-spiritual-practice-with-your-co-parent]] possible — the partner conversation about what is being changed is unbearable when both parents are fighting the change. The conversation becomes generative when they have surrendered to it.

## When This Is the Useful Frame

This concept is the right one to reach for when:

- A parent is bewildered by how much has changed about themselves and is treating the change as loss rather than process.
- A parent is fighting the changes parenting is bringing and the fighting is producing more suffering than the changes would.
- A parent presents as "this isn't who I thought I was going to be" and the right response is "exactly, that's the point."
- A pre-parent is asking what parenting actually does to a person and the honest answer is some version of "it takes you apart."
- A parent is in the middle of the first 2 or 3 years and feeling lost — naming the massage normalizes the lostness.

## When This Is Not the Useful Frame

The frame can be misused as bypass. A parent who is using "it's a deep tissue massage" to justify being checked out, abusive, or chronically overwhelmed is using the frame as a story. The frame describes what happens when a parent receives the conditions; it does not excuse the parent from doing the actual work of meeting the child well. See [[parenting-as-coaching-domain]] for what the actual work looks like.

The frame also does not say that the parent should welcome every difficulty. It says that the identity-dismantling that is going to happen is going to happen, and resisting it makes it worse. There is a separate set of practices for actually meeting children well (Hand in Hand Parenting and others — see [[hand-in-hand-parenting]]).

## Tone

Visceral. Joe says "you were just fucked" if you resist. He is not soft about the difficulty. He is also not heavy about it. The metaphor itself is funny enough that the listener can hear the difficulty without flinching from it. Use that same tone: physical, direct, a little dry. Do not dress this up as spiritual language. The deep tissue massage metaphor does the work.
