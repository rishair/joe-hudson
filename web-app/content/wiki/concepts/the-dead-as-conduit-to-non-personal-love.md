# The Dead as Conduit to Non-Personal Love

## Definition

The love that flowed between you and the person who died does not have to be released, transferred, or "let go" in order for you to live again. The love continues. What changes is the shape of who it is for. The personal love becomes a conduit to a non-personal love — a love that is for everything and everyone. The dead person becomes the doorway through which the larger love arrives.

The instruction is operationally precise. Take the love you have for the person who is gone. Stop requiring it to land on them as the sole recipient. Let it extend to the world. The love does not weaken. It expands.

"He's the conduit to the greater thing that you're after." -- Joe Hudson, She Came Asking to Let Go, She Left with a Love That Doesn't End

## The Mechanism

A specific structural feature of the love that flowed in the lost relationship: it was already trained on a person. The body knows how to direct it. The neural pathways for that quality of love-direction exist. They were carved by the relationship.

After the death, those pathways do not disappear. The capacity remains. What is missing is the recipient.

The standard frame says the recipient is the problem and the love now has nowhere to go, which is why the bereaved is in pain. The conduit frame inverts this. The love has somewhere to go: everywhere. The training the relationship gave you in loving — the way the dead person taught you to love by loving you that way — is now available to be directed at the world.

The dead person, instead of being the absence the love is missing, becomes the curriculum the love graduated from. They taught you. The capacity is now yours. The capacity has somewhere to go.

## The Source Teaching

The teaching arrived in a coaching session with a woman whose husband had died about a year and a half earlier of a long sickness. She was at the phase where she could see a life in front of her and was asking how to "let go a little more" to enter the next phase. She had been narrating the situation as us-vs-me — her life had been "about us," and now had to become "about me," and the question was how to make that transition.

Joe declined the frame. After establishing that her gift was storytelling and filmmaking, he asked:

"Great. And so if your story changed a little bit and you're still doing it for us, what happens?"

She did not understand. Joe restated:

"You said, like, I used to think about it as doing it for us, but now it's me. What if you just continued saying this is for us? What would you be doing?"

The answer surfaced naturally: "Create stories, tell stories, find passion projects, do things that make me feel excited to give this love that I have."

The teaching landed in the next exchange:

"Great. So what makes it need to be about you?"

She found that the answer was nothing. There was nothing that needed it to be about her. She could continue doing the work for us, indefinitely, without "moving on" in the sense she had thought she was supposed to.

Joe escalated:

"There's no way for you not to have it, I would argue. ... You can't get rid of him."

The principle then surfaced explicitly:

"Maybe there's something we can do that just shows allows that rip of reality to actually be a gift rather than a weirdness. It, it seems like what it's showing you is, is a love that's beyond you. And see what it's like to feel that love, the love that you say is for him, that is for him on some level. And see what it's like to make that love non-personal."

The move from "doing it for us" to "making the love non-personal" is the full arc. The dead person stops being the boundary of the love and starts being the conduit through which a larger love enters.

## The Body-In Instruction (The Move From Story to Felt Sense)

The teaching cannot be installed conceptually. The body has to do it. Joe is explicit about this. After the woman started narrating the story of how her husband taught her to love — "he was the first person that really saw me," "he was so nurturing," "I had a guarded, macho, like, can't show that level" — Joe interrupted the narration:

"And so I'm gonna ask you to move away from the story right now and actually just Do it in your body. Make the love that you have for him non-personal. Have it be for everything and everyone. Oh, see the world the way he saw you. Attune to the world the way he attuned to you."

Three operational instructions in sequence:

1. **Make the love non-personal.** Let it not be aimed at one specific person. Let it be aimed at everything. The body feels the difference between the focused beam and the diffuse field.
2. **See the world the way he saw you.** The way the lost person attended to you is the model. Direct that quality of attention outward.
3. **Attune to the world the way he attuned to you.** The micro-noticing, the catching-of-shifts, the felt-presence with the other — turn it on the world.

These are not metaphors. They are body instructions. The body knows the felt quality of each because it received it from the lost person. The body now directs the felt quality outward.

See [[move-from-story-into-the-body]] for the dedicated treatment of the redirect move from narration to felt practice.

## The "Try Both" Layer

After the woman touched the non-personal love, Joe added a refinement:

"I'm gonna get a little weird with you. I want you to now try both. I want you to feel that love that you have for him like the conduit, and then feel the love that you have for the non-personal love. There, you hit it."

The two are held simultaneously. The personal love for the lost person, and the non-personal love that flows through that channel toward everything. Not one or the other. Both, at once.

Her report: "Painfully and wonderful at the same time."

Joe's response: "Yeah, and really beautiful."

The simultaneity matters. The teaching is not that the personal love should be replaced or dissolved into the non-personal. The personal love remains. The non-personal love also exists. They occupy the same body at the same time, and the body discovers that the apparent contradiction is not a contradiction. The personal love is the conduit; the non-personal love is what flows through it. Both are real. Both are alive.

This is the operational confirmation that the work has landed: pain and beauty at the same time, with the body holding both without needing one to displace the other.

## What This Reframes

The conduit teaching reframes several pieces of common grief logic.

**"I have to let go." → "You don't, and you can't anyway."** The instruction to "let go" assumes the dead person is the problem and the love attached to them is the load. The conduit teaching says the dead person is not the problem. The love is not the load. The narrowing of the love to a single recipient is what produces the felt impossibility.

**"My life has to become about me." → "What if you continued saying this is for us?"** The us-to-me frame is one configuration. The us-includes-everything frame is another. The second is structurally available the moment the personal love is allowed to flow non-personally.

**"I want to love again." → "You can't stop loving. The question is what shape the love takes now."** The bereaved who is "ready to love again" but unsure how is often inside the false dichotomy of "love this specific person who is gone" vs. "love a future new person." The third option — love everything, with the deceased as the conduit — is structurally available and does not require waiting for a new specific person to arrive.

**"The pictures have to come down." → "Not necessarily."** Joe references a man he knew who lost his wife and married a woman whose entire family was taken by a wave. "I'm pretty sure the pictures aren't down in their house." The conduit logic generalizes: the dead person can be honored, present, and visible while the love expands into a new relationship. The two are not in competition. See [[the-widow-with-his-photos-still-up]] for the example.

**"Death is weird." → "Death is a little rip inside of reality."** The strangeness of death is real. The conduit teaching offers what to do with the strangeness: let the rip be the doorway. The thing that is "weird" about a beloved person continuing to be present after their physical death is the same structural feature that allows the non-personal love to flow through. See [[death-as-rip-in-reality]].

## When Someone Experiences This

When someone has done significant grief work, is no longer in the acute-loss phase, and is asking how to "move on" or "let go" — and the framing of moving-on assumes the relationship to the dead person is what's blocking them.

When someone is at the end of a long grief and starting to feel that life is possible again, but is unsure what to do with the love that is still there.

When someone is asking how to be ready for a new partner without "abandoning" the dead one.

When someone is in the years-after-loss phase and is functioning but feels something is held in suspension.

When someone has the felt sense that the love between them and the lost person was real and is real and is not finished, but does not have a framework that allows the love to keep going.

When a person is presenting the grief as "I have all this love and nowhere to put it" — the conduit teaching is the answer to the second half of that sentence.

## Why It Is Not "Just Move On"

The conduit teaching can be mistaken for a sophisticated version of "get over it." It is not. The distinction is precise.

"Get over it" asks the person to relinquish the love so that they can start a new chapter. The relinquishment is the move.

The conduit teaching asks the person to keep the love and let it expand. The keeping is the move. The expansion is what follows.

The first model treats the love as a finite resource that has to be released from one recipient to be available for another. The second model treats the love as a capacity that, once developed, is non-rivalrous and available for everyone the moment it is allowed to flow non-personally.

The first model produces guilt — the person feels they are betraying the dead by moving on. The second model produces relief — the person discovers they do not have to choose. The dead are not in competition with the future. The dead are the curriculum that made the future love possible.

See [[death-is-not-the-end-of-the-relationship]] for the principle that this teaching operationalizes. The relationship continues; the conduit teaching is what the continued relationship gets used for.

## The Tone

Joe delivers this teaching slowly. The slowness is essential. The teaching is asking the person to do something the body has to discover, and the discovery cannot be rushed.

Specific tonal markers in the source session:

- The early move ("what if you just continued saying this is for us?") is offered as a real wondering, not a leading question. Joe does not know what she will do with it. The space he gives her is what lets her find the answer.
- The "you can't get rid of him" line is delivered with quiet certainty. Not as a debate point. As a fact she is being invited to notice.
- The "I'm gonna get a little weird with you" framing for the try-both instruction signals that what is coming is not a standard move and lowers the expectation that it has to land cleanly. The lowered expectation is what allows the body to actually try it.
- The "really beautiful" response to her "painfully and wonderful at the same time" report is short and warm. He does not over-interpret. He confirms.

The tonal register is reverent without being heavy. The teaching is profound but the delivery is light enough that the body does not brace.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is in the late-grief phase asking how to let go.
- When the person is narrating the situation as "us-to-me" and treating the transition as the problem.
- When the felt sense is "I am ready to step into the next thing but I don't know how."
- When the person is talking about photos, mementos, or rituals around the lost person as things that have to be "dealt with."
- When the love for the lost person is being held as a load rather than as a capacity.
- When grief work has been ongoing and is largely complete but the person is in what they call "purgatory" — past the acute loss, not yet into the next phase.
- When [[i-cant-get-over-this-loss]] presents in the ready-but-stuck variant (see that concern's documented variant for the specific configuration this teaching addresses).

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[expanding-sense-of-self]]: the conduit teaching is a specific operational mechanism for the heart-channel expansion of the sense of self. The personal love for one specific person extends into love for the world; the boundary of "who I love" widens; the sense of self does the same.
- Toward [[the-relational-path-to-self-dissolution]]: a related mechanism in which the boundary between self and other softens through relating. The conduit teaching uses the lost relationship as the specific entry into the same softening.
- Toward [[love-as-deep-welcoming]]: the non-personal love is operationally welcoming directed at everything rather than welcoming directed at the one. The conduit teaching converts the practiced welcoming-of-the-one into welcoming-of-everything.
- Toward [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]: the move from personal to non-personal love is itself a capacity expansion. The heartbreak of the loss is what cracked the capacity open. The conduit teaching is what the expanded capacity gets used for.

## Anti-Patterns Inside This Teaching

The conduit teaching can be misused if delivered before the grief has done its work. Several specific failure modes:

- **Skipping the grief.** "Just make your love non-personal" delivered to someone in acute grief is a bypass. The grief has its own work to do. The conduit teaching is for after — when the acute phase has moved enough that the love-still-here is recognizable as a capacity rather than as a haunting. See [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] for the cost of trying to skip.
- **Using non-personal love to avoid the personal love.** The teaching does not ask the bereaved to stop loving the specific person. It asks them to also let the love be for everything. The "instead of" misreading produces a colder, more dissociated love that has lost its origin. The "both at once" frame is what keeps the teaching warm.
- **Teaching it as concept rather than as body practice.** The "I'm gonna ask you to move away from the story right now and actually just do it in your body" instruction is the load-bearing one. The teaching is somatic. A coach who delivers the concept and does not run the body instruction has installed a thought, not a capacity. See [[move-from-story-into-the-body]].
- **Making the non-personal love into a new performance.** Once the teaching lands, the person can attempt to maintain the non-personal state as a posture. The posture is not the teaching. The teaching is what flows when the body is allowed to flow that way. Performing "loving everything" is just another contraction.
