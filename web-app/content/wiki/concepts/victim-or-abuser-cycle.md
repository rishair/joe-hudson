# The Victim-or-Abuser Cycle

## Definition

The victim-or-abuser cycle is the recurrent pattern in which a person who experienced abuse and did not process it later either re-enters victim positions or develops the urge (and sometimes the action) to hurt others the way they were hurt. The cycle is the unprocessed trauma's expression in adult life. The same wound that was once being done to them is now either being done to them again (by new perpetrators they unconsciously seek out) or being done by them (to others, in fantasy or reality).

The cycle is not a moral failing of the person. It is the predictable output of trauma that has not been allowed to be felt. The system that holds the unfelt material keeps producing situations where the material might surface and be processed. When the channels available to the person are limited — when there is no place to feel safely, no container that allows the work — the recreation goes outward instead of inward. The trauma travels.

"He's describing something that's like, like the traumatic cycle, right? I was abused and I either become the victim or I become the abuser. And he's describing exactly what happens if the trauma is not dealt with. Then you have this thing in you that you're just like, I want to hurt somebody because I was hurt." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

## The Two Directions

**The victim direction.** The person re-enters situations that match the original abuse. They find partners who treat them the way they were treated. They find workplaces that recreate the dynamic. They find friends who hurt them. The mechanism is described in [[trauma-mapping]]: the body unconsciously selects for the imprint, with such precision that the conscious mind cannot understand how this keeps happening. The recreation is the system's attempt to complete the loop. The person is the receiving end of the cycle each time.

**The abuser direction.** The person develops an urge to do the thing that was done to them. This urge can range from chronic irritability and contempt, to verbal aggression, to violence, to (in extreme cases) the urge to kill. The urge is not just thought — it is felt in the body, as a craving that does not go away. The person knows the urge is wrong. They cannot make it stop. They sometimes do the thing the urge points at; they sometimes spend their life resisting it; they sometimes channel it into violent sport or work where the urge has an acceptable outlet.

Sean in the clip describes the abuser-direction in unusually candid terms:

"You don't understand, like, I truly have a deep down urge to kill somebody. Like, I can't — it doesn't go away. Like, I just — like, I don't know what it is, man. ... The only thing like that would make me happy is the fucking literally cave in your fucking skull and like ... I truly want is the fucking, like, make somebody hurt. Fucking hurt." -- Sean (UFC fighter, in the analyzed Theo Von clip)

The fighter is describing a textbook abuser-direction unprocessed-trauma signature. He has the urge. He has not acted on it (and has channeled significant aggression into UFC, which is one of the relatively safe outlets for the urge). He recognizes the urge as a problem. He cannot make it go away.

## The Blending of Directions

Most people do not stay perfectly on one side. The same person who is mostly in the victim direction may, in one specific domain or one specific phase, swing into the abuser direction. The same person who is mostly in the abuser direction may have one specific relational pocket where they are unable to defend themselves and become the victim.

The two directions are also developmentally related. Joe describes the [[aggressive-victim-stance]] — the person who occupies both positions simultaneously, using their victimhood as a weapon, dominating from the position of the wronged party. The aggressive victim is a hybrid form that draws on both directions.

The general principle: the cycle's two directions are not fixed personality types. They are the two predominant ways the unprocessed material expresses. A specific person is usually predominantly one but capable of the other, especially under specific triggers.

## The "I'm Bad for Feeling This" Loop That Compounds the Cycle

The cycle is compounded by a specific secondary mechanism: the person who has the urge to hurt back concludes that having the urge means they are bad. The badness conclusion confirms the original abuse narrative ("there must have been something wrong with me for this to have happened") and makes the urge harder to process, because now the person is also defending against the conclusion that they are intrinsically defective.

"In a human's mind, if you want to go and kill somebody, then you must be bad. Right? That's the — then the thing is, oh, I'm bad. So not only are you contending with this horrific feeling like that you want to hurt somebody, because that doesn't feel good in anybody's system, so which it is a response to the trauma, it's a thing that he's just like, I don't want to feel that, but you're also then having a very good reason to tell yourself that you're a bad person, that you — that there's something wrong with you, there's something broken with you, and then that story perpetuates the whole thing. It makes the whole thing harder to process." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The loop runs:
1. Abuse happens.
2. The unprocessed material produces the urge to hurt back.
3. The urge feels horrible — the person does not want to feel it.
4. The urge becomes evidence that the person is bad.
5. The badness conclusion confirms the original story that there was something wrong with the person to begin with.
6. The badness story makes the urge harder to feel and process, because now the urge is also a referendum on the person's worth.
7. The unfelt material continues to drive the cycle.

Breaking the loop requires letting the urge be felt without taking it as a verdict on the person. The urge is a response to the abuse, not a feature of the person. The feeling of wanting to hurt is data about what happened to them, not data about who they are. Once the urge can be felt without producing the bad-person conclusion, the urge can finish moving and the cycle can begin to dissolve.

See [[the-im-bad-for-feeling-it-loop-confirms-the-abuse-narrative]] for the developed principle on this compounding mechanism.

## The "I Can't Trust Myself" Outcome

The bad-person loop produces a specific downstream outcome that Joe identifies explicitly in the clip: the person cannot trust themselves. Because they have the urge to hurt, they have concluded they are dangerous. Because they have concluded they are dangerous, they do not let themselves be in situations where the urge might surface or act. They isolate. They avoid relationships. They restrict their lives.

"It makes me volatile, so I don't really — even to this day, like, I don't go out much." -- Sean, in the clip

"You can't trust yourself." -- Theo Von to Sean

"I can't trust myself." -- Sean

Joe's read on this moment: "You can't feel like you can trust yourself if you think you're bad."

The self-distrust is not based on the person being genuinely untrustworthy. It is based on the bad-person conclusion. The person has organized their adult life around the badness story, restricting themselves preemptively, treating themselves as a danger. The restriction has real costs (isolation, foreclosed relationships, restricted experience), and the underlying story driving the restriction is the same loop that the abuse installed.

The work to undo the self-distrust is to undo the bad-person conclusion, which requires letting the urge be felt without taking it as evidence of badness, which requires processing the underlying abuse, which requires the conditions where that processing can happen. The whole thing is connected.

## How the Cycle Dissolves

The cycle dissolves through the same mechanism that resolves all unprocessed trauma: the suspended emotions are felt in a container that allows them. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]].

For the abuser-direction specifically:

**Feel the rage in a container that does not require action.** The rage has been held in the body for years or decades. It needs to move. Pillows, the car, exercise, scream-rooms — there are real options for letting the rage discharge without harm. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]].

**Feel the helplessness underneath the rage.** The rage is often a layer above the helplessness that was unsurvivable as a child. Letting the rage move opens the helplessness underneath. See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] and [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]].

**Feel the grief and the longing.** Under the rage and the helplessness is usually the grief at what was done and the longing for what was needed. See [[inherent-longing-for-parent-love-survives-any-abuse]].

**Be in a relationship that holds the work.** The aloneness inside the original wound was part of the wound. Being present with someone — a therapist, a coach, a partner who can hold this — while the material moves addresses the aloneness directly. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] and [[how-to-receive-someones-trauma-sharing]].

**Stop taking the urge as evidence of badness.** Let the urge be data about what happened. Let it not be a verdict on who the person is. The undoing of the bad-person conclusion is part of the work, not a precondition for it.

For the victim-direction specifically, see [[trauma-mapping]] for the full mechanism and the work that dissolves the recreation pattern.

## Boundary: The Cycle Description Does Not Justify Harm

The principle that the abuser direction is a trauma response does not justify the harm a person in that direction does to others. People with unprocessed abuse who have not done the work are still responsible for the harm they cause. The frame of "trauma cycle" is for the person doing the work on themselves — to understand the urge without taking it as a verdict, to find the work that dissolves the urge, to take responsibility for not acting on the urge during the dissolution. It is not for the person to use as an excuse, and it is not for an observer to use as an excuse for the perpetrator.

The cycle also does not exhaust the picture of perpetrator formation. Not all abusers were themselves abused. Not all abuse survivors become abusers. The cycle is a strong tendency, not a deterministic mechanism. The specific person's path through it depends on many factors.

## Implications for the AI Coach

When someone names having the urge to hurt — partner, child, themselves, a stranger — the AI coach's job is not to be alarmed in a way that closes the conversation. It is also not to dismiss the urge as not real. The urge is real and it is meaningful.

The right moves include:

**Take the disclosure as significant.** Most people will not share this. The person who does is taking a risk and is asking for the urge to be received.

**Do not pathologize the person.** The urge is a response to trauma. It is not who the person is. Treating the urge as the person's intrinsic feature confirms the bad-person loop.

**Do not treat the urge as actionable plan.** Most people who have the urge do not act on it. The urge is the material that needs processing, not a plan that needs intervention. (Exception: when there is specific intent and specific target and specific means, the situation is different and requires safety actions.)

**Point at the underlying work.** The urge points at unprocessed trauma. The work is to find a context where the trauma can be felt and the urge can finish moving. The AI coach can name the structure and point at appropriate resources.

**Hold the bad-person loop visible.** The person is often telling themselves the urge means they are bad. Naming this loop — gently — is sometimes the most useful thing the AI coach can do. The urge is data about what happened, not data about who they are.
