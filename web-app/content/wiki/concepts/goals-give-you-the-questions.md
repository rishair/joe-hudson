# The Job of a Goal Is to Give You the Right Questions (Not Primarily to Be Hit)

## The Concept

The conventional reading of a goal is that it is something to be achieved. The achievement is the point. The goal is the future state you are working toward.

The actual function of a goal is different. The goal's biggest job is not the achievement. The goal's biggest job is to give you the right questions to be asking. Different goals produce different questions. The questions shape the inquiry. The inquiry shapes the choices. The choices produce the eventual outcome, which is often much larger than the goal itself could have specified.

"The first thing, the goals really their biggest job isn't to get to them. Oftentimes the goals are so limited. What we can get to is so much greater than what the goals can even, like, point at. But the goals, what they do is they give you the right questions to be asking." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

This reframes the entire relationship to goals. The goal is not the destination. The goal is the lens through which the current moment is examined. The lens determines what you see, what you ask, what you try.

## The Relationship Example

The most direct illustration is the relationship goal. The same domain produces wildly different questions depending on how the goal is held.

- "I want a good relationship with Brett."
- "I want a long relationship with Brett."
- "I want a deeply intimate relationship with Brett."
- "I want a relationship with Brett that we both feel insanely grateful for."

Each of these is a different goal. Each produces different questions.

"If my goal is to have a good relationship with you, it's going to be very fucking different than if my goal is to have long relationship with you, or my goal is to have a relationship with you that is deeply intimate, or to have a relationship with you that both of us just feel insanely grateful for on a regular basis. That is going to be a very different thing. I am going to ask myself different questions." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The "good relationship" goal might produce the question "did we get along today?" The "deeply intimate" goal produces "what is it that I'm not saying to Brett?" The "insanely grateful" goal produces "what is the thing I could do that would have Brett feeling met today?" Same relationship, different questions, different actions, different relationship five years out.

The corollary failure mode is the goal that produces the wrong questions: "How do I get what I want from you?" or "How do I manage you to avoid my own emotions?" These are questions a goal produces too, just not goals that produce relationships. The questions track the goal exactly.

## The Scale Example

The same dynamic applies in business at the dimension of scale. The goal of reaching 100 people and the goal of reaching 100,000 people are not just different sizes of the same goal. They are different goals that produce different questions.

"If your goal is how do we reach 10,000 people, it is going to be very different than how do we reach 100 people? 100 people, you and I can just go door to door. 100,000 people, you and I can't go door to door anymore. We have to do something different." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The 100-person goal produces the question "who can we visit this week?" The 100,000-person goal produces "what system can scale beyond us?" The questions cannot be derived from each other. The goal determines what kind of company is being built, what kind of work is being done, what skills get developed. The eventual achievement is downstream of the questions, which are downstream of the goal.

## Why "Aim Past the Goal" Works

This is the same principle that operates underneath [[goal-beyond-the-goal]]. The tennis player aiming at the basket asks "did the ball land in the basket?" The tennis player aiming at the quarter inside the basket asks "did the ball land within an inch of the quarter?" The second question produces tighter precision. The goal (the quarter) is more demanding, so the question becomes more demanding, so the execution becomes more precise. The original goal (the basket) is hit anyway, as a byproduct of the more demanding inquiry.

The principle is general. A more specific goal produces a more specific question. A more demanding goal produces a more demanding inquiry. A goal aimed at something larger than the immediate target produces questions that organize the larger work.

## How This Reframes the "Did I Hit It?" Question

If the goal's function is to produce the questions, then the conventional measure of success ("did I hit the goal?") is mismeasuring. The right measure is "did the goal produce the right questions, and did those questions produce the right inquiry?" A goal that was not hit can still have been wildly successful in this measure if the questions it produced moved the work forward in directions that matter.

This is one of the structural reasons why people report ending up in better places than their goals could have specified. Joe explicitly names this:

"I've never been in a situation, well, at least in the last 20-something years, where I look back and think I've gotten everything that I wanted. That's never happened. I've never gotten everything that I've wanted, but I've also never been able to look back and say what I got wasn't a shit ton better than what I wanted. There's a rigidity to goals that doesn't encompass all of life and living by principles and knowing where your North Star is oftentimes gets you to a place much better almost always than what you thought was possible." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The mechanism is that the goal opened up the inquiry, and the inquiry produced things the goal could not have predicted. The destination was always going to be larger than the map could specify. The map was useful only to start the inquiry.

## The Implication for Goal-Setting

If the function of a goal is to produce the questions, then the goal-setting process should be evaluated on a different criterion. Not "is this goal achievable?" or "is this goal specific?" but "what questions will this goal produce?"

The diagnostic test for any goal: speak it out loud, then ask "given this goal, what is the first question I would ask myself?" If the question is one you actually want to be asking, the goal is well-formed. If the question is anxious, defensive, or oriented to a measurement you don't care about, the goal needs revision.

In Joe's own company offsite, the explicit elicitation question was: "What does your heart want for yourself in this business? What does your heart want for the business? What does your heart want for the community we serve?" These questions are themselves the goal-setting move. The goals that emerge from heart-questions produce different operating questions than the goals that emerge from head-questions. See [[the-inner-board]] and [[goals-must-come-from-actual-want]].

## What This Implies for the Mario Brothers Test

The video game example illustrates the same point from the play angle. The goal of "get to the next level of Mario Brothers" produces the question "what's the next move?" The inquiry stays in the game; the play is fun; the levels happen.

The goal of "your worth as a human being is determined by whether you make the next level" produces a different question: "what does this say about me?" The inquiry leaves the game; the play stops being fun; the level becomes hostile.

"If you think about a video game, as soon as you said, wow, you know what, who you are as a human being is going to be determined if you get to the next level of Mario Brothers and you believe that, probably Mario Brothers is probably not going to be as fun as, oh, I'm looking at this goal of like getting to the next level. I want to do that. That's a cool thing. Let's see if we can do that." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The goal is the same in both cases (next level). The way the goal is held determines the question it produces. The question determines whether the activity is play or self-attack. See [[dont-let-goals-define-you]] for the related anti-pattern.

## What Someone Experiences

When a person is in the right relationship to a goal, the goal produces questions that the person finds interesting to investigate. The day is shaped by inquiry rather than by pressure. Missing the goal in some specific way produces curiosity rather than self-attack: "What did I learn? What's the next question?"

When a person is in the wrong relationship to a goal, the goal produces questions that the person dreads to investigate. The day is shaped by anxiety about measurement. Missing the goal in any way produces self-attack: "What's wrong with me?" The goal has stopped functioning as an inquiry-producer and started functioning as a verdict-machine.

The diagnostic shift: pay attention to what questions arise in the head when you think about the goal. If they are questions about the work, the goal is doing its job. If they are questions about your worth or whether you'll be okay, the goal has been weaponized.

## Tone

Joe delivers this concept with the cadence of a practical correction to a common misreading. The reframe is structural, not motivational. The point is to relocate where the value of a goal actually sits. Once the relocation lands, the relationship to goals shifts without further intervention.
