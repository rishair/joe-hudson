# The Aggressive Victim Stance (Topping from the Bottom)

## Definition

A specific configuration of the [[fear-triangle|victim role]] in which the victim uses their victimhood as a tool to dominate others. They "top from the bottom": exercise power over others while preserving the moral cover of being the wronged party.

"They use their victimhood as a way to dominate other people." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The stance is the natural evolution of an abused person who has begun moving toward power but has not yet found empowerment. Power-over is the path they know, because it was the path used on them. The new role they take is the same role, with the victim narrative as the justification.

## The Underlying Pattern

"The abused becomes the abuser." This is observable across scales: countries, families, classrooms. A nation that was conquered begins conquering. A child who was bullied at home begins bullying at school. A child who was abused becomes a parent who abuses.

"As somebody is feeling disempowered, they think the way to get to empowerment is to control other people. And then they use what I would call topping from the bottom. They use their victimhood as a way to dominate other people." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The move out of disempowerment looks at first like power, but it is just the other side of the same coin. See [[empowered-vs-power]]: empowerment is being yourself despite the consequences, not having control over others.

## How It Shows Up

The aggressive victim's behavior pattern is recognizable. The signature: behaviors that constrain others while preserving plausible deniability about being the constraint.

- Showing up late, repeatedly, with reasons that cannot be argued with
- Snarky comments delivered with a smile, often in front of others
- Refusing to change a position because "we agreed" or "that's just how I am"
- Becoming so indecisive that no one else can move
- Naming a felt state ("I don't feel safe," "I feel attacked") in a way that places all responsibility for fixing it on others. See [[dont-use-safety-as-control]].
- Naming the relationship as abusive in a way that forecloses any direct conversation
- Crisis dependency that requires others to constantly rescue

"The aggressive victim does things like shows up late all the time or says the little snarky comment in front of other folks or refuses to change. 'Well, we agreed that this is how it's going to be, so I'm never going to change on this topic.' There's all sorts of little things that they do. They become incredibly indecisive so that the other person can't move freely." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## Why It Is Hard to See

The social field protects the stance. Western culture in particular has internalized the moral primacy of the victim. The person who shows visible distress or distress-language is read as the wronged party. The person who responds with frustration to the chronic small abuses is read as the aggressor.

"Our society so much sees those people as the victim that needs to be saved. Whereas the person who's yelling and doing that kind of thing, who's like, 'Come on, goddamn it, like, what the hell?' they're the bad guy. So there's like a societal norm that makes one of them the bad guy and one of them the victim and the poor person, poor person who this is being done to." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The person inside the dynamic also cannot see it from inside. The victim narrative is the protection. To see one's own behavior as abusive would require dismantling the identity of being the wronged party. That dismantling is exactly what the stance exists to prevent.

"It's really hard for someone who's in the aggressive victim thing for them to see that that's what's happening because they're so defined as the victim. It's very hard for them to see that they're, that it's very hard if they stop putting you in the oppressor role. It's really, really hard for them to see that they might be the abuser in the situation or might be part of the abuse cycle in the situation." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## How Joe Responds in Coaching

In a coaching context, Joe deliberately surfaces the anger underneath. The aggressive victim stance is a layer over genuine anger that the person cannot yet feel directly.

"In a context of coaching, I'll say, you seem pissed, tell me all about it. I will elicit the anger that's underneath it. If that doesn't elicit the anger underneath it, I will call them out in such a way that shows that the victim, that they're topping from the bottom, and then they really do get pissed. You'll notice that anybody who's doing that kind of aggressive victim stance, and you call them out on it, they get really pissed at you then. Then you get to see what's really underneath it all, which is far more beautiful for me than that." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The progression: the surface stance is constricted aggression dressed as suffering. Under it is real anger. Under the anger is usually fear, helplessness, or grief that the anger has been guarding. The path through is to keep moving down the layers.

See [[deconstruct-the-victim-to-release-anger]] for the move.

## The Diagnostic: Take Away the Victim Role and Watch What Happens

A practical test for whether someone is in the aggressive victim stance: try not to participate in the role they have assigned. Specifically, refuse to be the aggressor in their story, and refuse to confirm them as the wronged party. If they are in the aggressive victim stance, they will fight to keep the role.

"If you really want to see someone go from passive aggression to aggression, take away their victim role." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The fight to keep the role looks like: producing more grievances, escalating to "you're attacking me," collapsing dramatically, switching to overt anger if the passive form is no longer working. The escalation itself is the proof that the role was identity-protective, not a report of actual injury.

The case Joe gave in the 2024 episode: a client whose European mother was being relentlessly passive-aggressive while presenting as oppressed. The client started saying "ouch" every time she criticized. The mother fought back: "You can't take that from me. I'm the hurt one. That's me." The line is the diagnostic — the role was the identity, not the situation. See [[say-ouch]] for the move and [[cant-take-the-victim-role-away]] for the read that explains the resistance.

## How Joe Responds in Non-Coaching Contexts

When this is happening in a business or personal relationship and the relationship is not a coaching one, Joe names it directly and refuses to participate.

"I don't wanna be in a power dynamic with you. And I don't agree to be your bully. I don't agree to be the person who's oppressing you. I'm not gonna buy into a situation where you're not empowered and able to do what you wanna do, and I'm not able and empowered to do what I wanna do. And I'm not interested in any relationship where we're trying to change each other." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

What happens next: sometimes the person leaves or gets angry. Sometimes the naming dissolves the dynamic and a real relationship becomes possible.

"Sometimes people get angry or leave or we're not in interaction anymore. And sometimes people see the freedom in it and they're like, a deep loyalty and trust is born because we're meeting each other as humans, not as objects to be controlled." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## Why "Empowerment" Is the Only Real Exit

Replacing one set of dictators with another set of dictators is not liberation. It is the same dynamic with a different name. This is what happens when liberation movements seek power-over rather than empowerment.

"When you see the movements are 'We want to have power over somebody else,' that's when one group of dictators gets replaced with another group of dictators with a different philosophy. It's the same thing as one abuser gets replaced with another abuser inside of a relationship." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The movements that worked (Gandhi, King) were structured around empowerment, not power-over. The disciplined refusal to use the master's tools is what makes the change durable.

The same applies inside personal relationships. The cycle ends when one party stops trying to win the power game and starts being empowered: present, truthful, undefended, willing to accept the consequences.

## Tone

When Joe describes this, his tone has compassion underneath. The aggressive victim is scared. The behavior is wrong but the person is not a monster. The naming is done without contempt. The refusal to participate is done without rejection of the person, only of the dynamic.

"If you can see it all as fear, then like everybody's boundary and your own boundary is just sweetness. It's like, 'Oh, everyone's scared.' Like I see a big, like just dealing recently with a high-level executive, and there was like this big fight in the company. And I kept on coming back to, can you just see everybody is scared? Just scared powerful people throwing big temper tantrums. As soon as you can see it, it's just so easy to see through and navigate." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## Boundary Conditions

Not every victim stance is the aggressive variant. Sometimes a person who is genuinely being wronged describes the wrong without manipulating. The diagnostic is the function: is the victimhood being used to constrain the behavior of others? If yes, this concept applies. If no, the person is just describing what happened to them.

The other diagnostic: does the person become flexible when offered real solutions, or does the problem need to remain unsolved for them to maintain their position? The aggressive victim stance requires the problem to persist. Real victimhood welcomes resolution.
