# Blame Is Not Real

## Definition

Blame is not a fact about a situation. It is a frame people impose on a situation. The frame breaks down on inspection: any attempt to identify "who is to blame" runs into an infinite regress of causes, none of which is the actual locus of responsibility, all of which could be plausibly blamed.

"The reason that blame doesn't work is because it's not true. Like, blame is not true." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The concept is not metaphysical. It is operational. When a person tries to do blame -- to locate it precisely -- they discover there is no place to put it that is not arbitrary. The arbitrariness is the disproof. A frame that yields a different answer depending on where the inquirer chooses to stop is not picking up a real feature of the world.

## The Cascade Demonstration

The clearest way to surface the unreality of blame is to follow the chain backward. Pick something to blame and keep going.

"Am I going to blame you or am I going to blame your parents because of the way they raised you? Or am I going to blame your grandparents because of the way that they raised your parents? Am I going to blame the blood sugar that you have? Is it -- is it the way that, like, your day went at work? Like, what am I blaming? Is it your boss's fault? Who am I blaming for this thing?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The blame cascades. Each level is plausibly to blame. Each level has its own antecedent that could be blamed. The chain does not terminate at a true locus. It terminates wherever the blamer decides to stop, which is the diagnostic that the locus is the blamer's choice, not a feature of the situation.

The cascade demonstration is offered not as a sophistry but as a practical move: if you try to identify the actual blame-target and find you keep moving up the chain, that is the experience of discovering blame is a frame, not a fact. The experience is more convincing than the argument.

## The Comparison to Fairness in Marriage

Blame has the same structure as the question of what is fair in a marriage: an apparently meaningful question that has no answer because the question itself is malformed.

"It's like the same concept of trying to figure out, like, what's exactly fair in a marriage. There's just no way to find it." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The fairness comparison illuminates the blame point. Two partners can both have coherent accounts of what is fair, and the accounts will not match, and no third party can adjudicate the gap. The question "what is exactly fair" produces an arbitrary answer because the question is asking for a property that does not exist as a discoverable quantity.

Blame works the same way. The question "who is exactly to blame" produces an arbitrary answer because the property being asked for is not in the world. The two questions share a structure: both ask for a precise external truth that is actually a contested internal frame.

## The Worst-Case Test

The principle survives the worst-case test. Take an example designed to make blame look obvious -- one partner cheats on the other -- and the cascade still runs.

"Let's just take the worst case scenario, which is she cheated on me. Like, that's an easy one. I blame you for cheating on me. But yeah, the relationship was cold, almost guaranteed before the cheating happened. Or she compulsively cheats because of daddy issues. And I, and I got involved in that for some reason, some way I was attracted into that relationship. And so like, well, is it really her fault? What was, what was I accepting from the beginning? How did I remove my love so that she needed to go to somebody else?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The example is not arguing that the cheating partner is not responsible. It is arguing that the blame frame loses precision the moment it is examined. The cheating happens; the cheating has antecedents; the antecedents have antecedents; everyone involved has some part. The blame frame asks for a single locus and the situation does not have one.

The point is not "no one is responsible." The point is "the blame frame is the wrong tool for locating responsibility." See [[fault-vs-responsibility]] for the better tool.

## Why People Still Do Blame

If blame is not real, why does it run constantly in human minds? The function is emotional, not epistemic.

"There's this idea that we have that if we find blame, then we don't have to be responsible. That's the logical side. But what's actually happening there is what we're saying is we don't want to feel the shame. So if I blame you, I cannot feel the shame." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

Blame is a way to move shame off the self. The accuracy of the blame is irrelevant to the function. The function works as long as the blamer can stay in the frame. The unreality of the frame does not surface as long as the frame is not examined.

This is why the cascade demonstration is so destabilizing. Once a person follows the chain backward and notices that the answer keeps moving, the blame frame loses its function. There is no longer a clean place to put the shame. The shame returns to the blamer, which is the moment the actual work becomes available. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] applied to shame, and [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]] for the internal version.

## What This Concept Replaces

The concept is offered as an intellectual deconstruction that breaks the spell of "but someone is wrong here." When someone in conflict says "but they did X" or "but I did Y" and the blame frame is doing the talking, this concept is what disarms the frame.

"There's something in our mind that says, yeah, but somebody's wrong. And I question that. I question that. Like, if you need somebody to be wrong in a relationship, so then you're, you're married to or dating somebody who's wrong. Is that a turn-on? No, we're just all humans and we're all doing things. And sometimes what we do is a match. Sometimes what we do is not a match. And everything is a symptom of something else." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

"Everything is a symptom of something else" is the structural replacement for the blame frame. The blame frame says: there is a wrong actor and they caused this. The symptom frame says: there is a pattern, the pattern has antecedents, the antecedents are themselves symptoms, and the question is not who-did-this but what-is-happening-here-and-what-do-we-do-about-it.

This is the basis for [[patient-on-the-table]] (the medical depersonalization) and the operational move toward [[how-do-we-want-to-be-together]] (the forward-looking question that replaces the backward-looking blame question).

## The Match Frame as the Replacement

When two people are in a conflict, the blame frame asks "which one of you is wrong." The match frame asks "is this a match or not." The match frame produces information; the blame frame produces shame.

"We're just all humans and we're all doing things. And sometimes what we do is a match. Sometimes what we do is not a match." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

Two people whose behaviors and needs match well are well-matched. Two people whose behaviors and needs do not match are not well-matched. Neither one is wrong. The non-match has consequences -- it may mean they cannot stay together, or it may mean they need to negotiate something specific -- but the consequences are operational, not moral.

The match frame is not a softer version of the blame frame. It is a different frame altogether. It does not ask for fault. It asks for fit.

## When To Use This Concept In Coaching

When a person is locked into "but they were wrong" or "but I was wrong" and the blame is preventing the work, the cascade demonstration is the tool. Walk them backward through the chain. Let them notice that the chain does not terminate. Let them feel the moment the frame loses its grip.

When a person presents a scenario expecting validation of the blame ("she cheated on me, isn't that her fault"), engage with the scenario but expand the frame. Not "no, it wasn't her fault" -- that would be validating the frame by arguing the other side. Instead: what was happening before, what were both people contributing, what was the larger pattern. The blame frame deflates when the situation is described in enough resolution.

When a person has done stage one of [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]] and is now in stage two, this concept is part of what surfaces stage three. The blame is not real; therefore self-blame is not the answer; therefore the work goes further.

## Boundary Conditions

The concept is not "no one is responsible for anything." Responsibility is real. Fault is the frame that is not real. See [[fault-vs-responsibility]] for the distinction.

The concept is not "everything is everyone's fault equally." That would be the same frame with the locus diffused; it is still the frame. The point is to set the frame down, not to redistribute it.

The concept is not "stop having reactions." Reactions are real and informative. The concept addresses the mental motion that turns the reaction into a blame claim. The reaction can be present; the blame frame can be set down.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a slightly amused incredulity. The cascade -- "blame the parents, blame the grandparents, blame Beyoncé" -- is delivered fast and with humor. The humor is the tool. It lets the listener see the absurdity of the construct they have been operating inside without being told they are stupid for operating inside it.

The Beyoncé line, in context: "or is it Beyoncé who taught you that you needed to have a ring on it." The chain is allowed to get absurd on purpose. The absurdity is the point.
