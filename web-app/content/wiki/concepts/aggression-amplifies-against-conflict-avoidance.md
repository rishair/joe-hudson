# Aggressive People Become More Aggressive Against Conflict-Avoidant People

## Definition

Conflict avoidance does not produce less conflict. It produces more, specifically by amplifying the aggression of people who are already aggressive. The mechanism: an aggressive person registers an inability to meet them as evidence that you do not care, cannot hold them, or are below them. The aggression escalates until they get the containment they were unconsciously seeking. When you can actually meet the aggression -- be present with it, not collapse under it, not fight it -- the aggressive person settles. The conflict shrinks.

"The other thing that's interesting is aggressive people become more aggressive and more domineering when you are conflict avoidant. And so you're like, increase, like just perfect golden algorithm. You're increasing the amount of conflict that comes to you because what happens is if there's an aggressive person and you can't meet them, then in their mind you don't care or you can't hold them or they're better than you. And so if somebody's aggressive and you can meet them, you're like, yeah, I'm not, like, I'm right here. I'm holding you, baby. Like your anger does not scare me. Then they can settle down and relax. And so then there's less conflict." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The phrasing "perfect golden algorithm" is Joe's signal that this is one specific case of the broader [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] mechanism. The avoidance of conflict produces the conflict it was trying to avoid, in the exact form of the avoidance.

## Why the Aggressive Person Escalates

When someone aggressive comes at you and you fold, three things register in their nervous system:

1. **You do not care.** If you did, you would meet them. Your folding reads as indifference, not as gentleness.
2. **You cannot hold them.** The aggression is partly a search for containment. If you cannot contain it, they have to keep generating more to find the limit.
3. **They are above you.** The folding establishes a hierarchy. The aggressive person now has to take up the missing position too, which means more aggression.

None of these are conscious calculations on the aggressive person's part. They are autonomic responses. The aggression is an unconscious bid for containment ([[anger-as-containment-for-the-conflict-seeker]]). The folding deprives them of what their system is looking for.

## What "Meeting" Looks Like

Meeting an aggressive person is not aggression-back. It is not fighting. It is being present with what they are bringing without disappearing. The body says "I am right here. Your anger does not scare me." The face does not flinch. The voice does not hedge. The presence holds.

This is not a performance. It requires that you actually be able to be with the aggression without leaving yourself. If you are pretending to be unaffected, the aggressive person reads the pretense and the dynamic does not shift.

The tire shop guy in the source example illustrates this. He works at a tire shop. Customers come in aggressively. He had been treating this as a problem (his own anger, the rudeness). When he started holding the container -- being present with their aggression rather than folding or escalating -- the same customers ended the interaction asking to stay in touch. See [[tire-shop-guy-customer-container]].

"This happens 4 times a week where I literally am like, hey, hold the container. And this person who started off as like the asshole in like the $150,000 car who's like 'Come on, open the door, it's only 5:45, you closed at 5:30,' ends up saying, 'Hey, can we hang out?' You know, at the end of it." -- Tire shop friend, recounted by Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## What This Implies About Anger Capacity

The conflict-avoidant person is usually trying to remove the discomfort of conflict by removing themselves from it. The actual move is the opposite. The capacity for anger -- being able to feel it, hold its presence in the room, not be flooded by it from outside -- is what dissolves the conflict that conflict avoidance was supposed to manage.

This is one of the practical reasons Joe places the work on anger first in the developmental sequence for conflict avoidance. Getting good with your own anger ([[anger]]) is what gives you the capacity to meet someone else's anger without folding or escalating. Without that capacity, the aggressive people in your environment will keep escalating because the containment they need is not there.

For the version of this dynamic where the same principle applies inside intimate relationships and the meeting move is more nuanced, see [[anger-as-containment-for-the-conflict-seeker]].

## The Even-Less-Held Version

The slightly different but related move is holding the energy of meeting without the anger itself. The tire shop guy's anger is the starter version. The more refined version is the same firmness without the personal charge.

"I would say that the thing of like, if he could hold that same energy without the anger, he would be a lot less constricted, correct? And I think that's about -- for him, it's not beating himself up for the anger, but it's also about not taking stuff personally. So slightly different thing that's happening there. But yes, if he could love the anger, it would show up with less ho to it." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The development path: meet aggression with anger first (better than folding, often appropriate); refine over time toward meeting with presence that includes the firmness without the personal charge ("not taking stuff personally"). Both work. The latter is more refined.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You are repeatedly the target of aggression from someone and you have been being more accommodating in response
- You notice that the more you try to placate someone, the harder they push
- A specific person in your life seems to escalate every time you try to soothe them
- You are reading "the bigger person" as "the more conflict-avoidant person" and noticing the relationship has not improved
- You have a customer-facing or client-facing role and the difficult clients seem to get worse over time

## The Compound Effect Over Time

A conflict-avoidant person collects more aggressive people in their life over time. Not because they invite them consciously, but because the aggressive pattern in others has found a place where it can escalate without resistance. The aggressive person stays because there is no boundary that would push them out, and they intensify because there is no container that would let them settle.

This is one of the most counterintuitive consequences of conflict avoidance. The conflict-avoidant person believes they have organized their life to minimize conflict. They have actually organized their life to attract and amplify the people most likely to produce it. The mechanism is invisible to them because it operates at the level of the nervous system, not at the level of conscious choice.

The pattern that the conflict-avoidant person experiences as "I attract aggressive people" or "people walk all over me" is not bad luck. It is the downstream effect of the avoidance, accumulated over years.

## The Coaching Implication

When someone presents repeated patterns of aggression coming at them from a specific person or category of person, the standard moves do not work:

- Telling them to be more patient does not work (the patience reads as not caring)
- Telling them to set firmer boundaries without addressing the underlying capacity does not work (the boundaries collapse under pressure)
- Telling them the aggressive person is the problem does not work (it does not help them shift their part of the dynamic)

The move is to develop their capacity to meet anger -- starting with their own (see [[anger]], [[get-angry-in-the-car]]) -- so that they can be present with someone else's anger without disappearing or escalating. The shift in capacity changes the dynamic without requiring any change on the aggressive person's part.
