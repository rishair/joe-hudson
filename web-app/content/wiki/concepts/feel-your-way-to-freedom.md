# Feel Your Way to Freedom

## Definition

The thing that traps a person is most often their relationship to feeling. The substances they use, the achievements they chase, the dynamics they recreate, the people they please -- a substantial portion of what looks like "the problem" is in service of not feeling. Freedom, in this frame, is not achieved by accomplishing more or by acquiring more. It is accessed by allowing oneself to feel what one has been organizing one's life to avoid feeling.

The phrasing comes from Aaron Taylor, who introduces it as a description of his own decades-long arc:

"Everything that has gotten in the way that has been an inefficient way to create the outcomes that I want has been my desire to not feel. So whether I use emotion, whether I use alcohol, whether I use money and power, control, all of those things were utilized by me to try to elicit something that I wanted, which often involved not feeling. So [...] feeling my way to freedom to me is always the access, the portal to a brighter future, if you will." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The frame matters because it inverts the conventional understanding of what freedom requires. Conventionally, freedom is something achieved through external conditions: enough money, enough autonomy, enough success, enough security. The "feel your way to freedom" frame says: those external conditions, even when obtained, do not produce freedom if the person remains organized around not feeling. The Hall of Fame does not produce freedom for someone who is still avoiding what they were avoiding before the Hall of Fame. The path to freedom is sideways from the conventional path -- it is through the feeling itself.

## What Counts as "Feeling"

The "feeling" in this frame is the felt-experience kind, not the emotional-label kind. It is what the body does in response to its experience. It includes the difficult emotions (fear, shame, anger, grief) and the pleasant ones (joy, wonder, gratitude, love). It also includes the felt sense of being a person in a body in a moment, which is often the thing being most avoided.

Naming an emotion is not the same as feeling it. See [[dont-name-emotion-to-avoid-feeling]]. The "feel your way to freedom" phrase points at the felt experience, not at the verbal labels for it.

## The Common Avoidance Mechanisms

What people use to not feel, in Aaron's autobiographical list and in Joe's broader experience:

- Substances: alcohol, drugs, food, sugar.
- Performance: relentless achievement, identity built on results.
- Roles: caretaking, fixing, providing, being the strong one.
- Stories: the rehearsed narrative of who one is and what happened.
- Control: trying to manage external conditions to prevent the felt experience.
- Distraction: media, work, busyness, sports, anything that occupies the bandwidth that would otherwise feel.
- Spiritual bypass: using practices to skip the feeling rather than to deepen it.

The mechanisms vary by person and by phase. The pattern is consistent: the mechanism is in service of the avoidance. Removing the mechanism without addressing the avoidance produces the next mechanism, not the freedom.

The phrase resonates with many of Joe's central teachings, organized around the same core insight from different angles:

- [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: the operational mechanism of letting an unfelt emotion come through.
- [[love-as-deep-welcoming]]: the relational stance that allows the feeling to come.
- [[stages-of-emotional-development]]: the developmental arc through which the feeling becomes increasingly available.
- [[fear-is-about-feeling]]: the principle that fear of an outcome is really fear of what the outcome will require feeling.
- [[embrace-intensity]]: the broader principle that aliveness requires feeling everything at full volume rather than dampening to manage.

The "feel your way to freedom" phrasing is a useful organizing label for what Joe is teaching across many specific framings. It is short, memorable, and operationally clear: when stuck, find what is being avoided and feel it. The freedom is on the other side of the feeling.

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

The cultural default is the opposite: feel less, accomplish more, manage harder, push through. The default is encoded in childhood ("don't cry"), in education ("just work hard"), in workplaces ("don't let them see you sweat"), in sports ("walk it off"). The result is adults who are highly functional in the conventional sense and largely unable to feel.

Aaron's NFL locker room description captures the cultural pattern in concentrated form:

"The locker room is a sacred place, but it's not a safe place. So feelings weren't welcome. You didn't want to walk into the locker room and say, 'Hey guys, my self-esteem's down today. My girlfriend broke up with me and at the core of my being, I don't feel like I matter. I'm enough. Anybody want to talk about that?' Like, those things didn't tend to come up." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The locker room is one instance. The boardroom is another. The marriage where neither person has felt their actual feelings in years is another. The pattern is so widespread that "feel your way to freedom" reads as advice rather than as the default operating mode, which it would be in a healthier culture.

## What Someone Experiences

The internal experience of beginning to feel one's way to freedom:

- Discomfort. The thing being avoided is being avoided for a reason. Feeling it directly is uncomfortable in proportion to how much energy has gone into the avoidance.
- Surprise. The feared feeling often turns out to be different than expected. The thing avoided for years can move through in twenty minutes when actually allowed.
- Grief. There is often grief about the time spent not feeling, the choices made in service of avoiding, the relationships that were calibrated to the avoidance.
- Relief. The system that has been holding the brace releases. The energy that was holding becomes available for other things.
- A different kind of action. Decisions that felt impossible when the feeling was avoided often feel obvious once the feeling has moved.

## The Direction of the Phrase

"Feel your way to freedom" is directional. It is not "feel and that is freedom." The feeling is the path, not the destination. The freedom is what becomes available as the feeling moves and the system reorganizes around no longer needing to avoid it.

This matters because some forms of feeling-work become their own trap. The person becomes identified as someone who feels. The feeling becomes a practice. The practice becomes a should. The whole thing starts running on the same avoidance engine as before, just dressed in better clothes. See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] for the read on this and [[loving-the-feeling-not-just-with-it]] for the related distinction.

The freedom side is critical. The point is not to be a feeler. The point is to use the feeling as the path through the avoidance and into the actual life that becomes available when the avoidance is no longer running.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not apply uniformly. Some boundary conditions:

- People in acute crisis need stabilization, not deep feeling. The feeling work is a long arc; the next ten minutes may require something more immediate.
- People with significant trauma histories often need a regulated environment and support to feel without re-traumatizing.
- People who have been in feeling-as-practice for a long time sometimes need the opposite invitation: stop trying to feel and let life happen.

The principle is most useful in the middle: the high-functioning adult who has organized their life around not feeling and is starting to recognize what that has cost.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A coachee describes a pattern of "achieving and not feeling fulfilled."
- A coachee identifies a substance or behavior that they are using to manage feeling.
- A coachee names the feeling they are avoiding (or names it indirectly through what they describe avoiding).
- A coachee is asking why their life looks good on paper and feels flat in the body.
- A coachee is in recovery from addiction and the question becomes what the substance was managing.

## Tone

The phrase is delivered with a specific tone in the transcript: lightness, recognition, and the implicit acknowledgment that the speaker has lived the long version of the principle and is offering it as someone who has tested it. The tone for this concept should match: it is not a teaching imposed from above but an offering from someone who has paid the tuition.
