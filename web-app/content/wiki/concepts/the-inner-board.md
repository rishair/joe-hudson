# The Inner Board (The Voice in the Head as a Misaligned Internal Governance Body)

## The Concept

The same dynamic that operates between a misaligned board and a misaligned company also operates inside a single person. In a company, the board is supposed to look at the same data the CEO uses to run the company; when the board is looking at different data, the misalignment shows up as days lost preparing decks that translate one set of numbers into another. Inside a person, the voice in the head is supposed to be aligned with the rest of the system; when it issues goals that are not the wants of the body, the heart, the underlying self, the misalignment shows up as the constant self-abuse of "you should be doing X."

"Oftentimes we have this critical voice in our head, our board, which is making -- yeah, which is saying, here are your goals, but it's not actually the goals of the rest of the system. So your body has a different goal and your heart has a different goal, and maybe even your mind has a different goal. Your trauma has a different goal. So all these goals are not in alignment with one another, and especially not in alignment with the thing that says, I should be married at 35, and why are you so far behind, or whatever that goal-making mechanism, which is just another form of self-abuse, happens inside so many of us." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The voice in the head is not the person. It is one of several subsystems. When it is operating as the only voice, it is the equivalent of a board running a company by fiat without consulting the operating team. The decisions get made; the operating team grinds through them; the work degrades; the friction is constant.

## Why This Reframe Matters

Most people who relate to the voice in the head do so as if the voice were the legitimate authority and the rest of the system the rebellious subordinate. The body resists the diet; the body is at fault. The heart resists the relationship the voice prescribed; the heart is unruly. The trauma resists the achievement schedule; the trauma is dysfunction to overcome.

The reframe inverts this. The voice issuing the goals is one input among several. The body, the heart, the underlying self, the unprocessed material -- each has its own want and its own information. Alignment requires consulting all of them. The voice that is used to running unilaterally will not produce alignment by trying harder. It will produce alignment only by becoming one voice among several.

This is structurally identical to [[wants-over-shoulds]] but stated at the system level. The should is the inner board's decree. The want is what emerges when the rest of the system gets to speak. The discovery is that the system is more intelligent than the voice that has been running it.

## The Goals the Other Subsystems Have

When the voice in the head stops monopolizing the goal-setting, what emerges is a more complete picture of what the system actually wants. The other subsystems each have their own pull:

- The body wants rest, movement, food, breath, pleasure, sleep, sex, contact -- the things that keep the organism alive and functioning.
- The heart wants connection, intimacy, expression, beauty, love given and received, the people who matter, time with what is loved.
- The trauma wants safety -- often through specific compensatory strategies (the achievement, the rescue, the proof, the distance) that the trauma believes will finally make it safe.
- The mind, separate from the inner-board voice, wants learning, understanding, problems to solve, ideas to play with.
- The underlying self wants something harder to name -- often what the spiritual traditions point at, the felt sense of being alive without needing to be doing anything in particular to be alive.

None of these are bugs. Each is a legitimate input. The work is not to silence any of them; it is to let all of them be heard so that the goals that emerge are aligned across the system.

## How the Misalignment Shows Up

Once the inner board frame is in place, several common failure modes become legible as alignment problems rather than as personal weakness:

**Goals that won't stick.** The diet fails not because of weak willpower but because the body never agreed. The voice issued the diet. The body was not consulted. The diet runs into the body's actual wants, and the body wins because the body is the operating team and the voice is just the board.

**Achievements that don't feel good.** The promotion lands and produces nothing. The voice's goal was achieved. The other subsystems wanted something else. The achievement satisfies the voice but starves the rest of the system. See [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]] for the related concept.

**Constant self-attack about being behind.** The voice is issuing goals on a schedule ("I should be married at 35") that the rest of the system never agreed to. The schedule is not real; it's the voice's projection. The self-attack about missing the schedule is the voice trying to discipline the rest of the system into compliance with a target the system never endorsed. See [[shoulds-as-self-abuse]].

**Wants the person feels guilty about.** The voice has issued goals the person is supposed to want. The actual wants -- often closer to the body, the heart, the underlying self -- conflict with the issued goals. The conflict is felt as guilt: "I should want X, but I actually want Y." The way out is not to make the voice's goals win; it is to recognize that the voice was running a campaign and the actual wants are data.

## The Diagnostic for Whether You Are Running on the Inner Board

A useful self-test: list three of your current goals. For each one, check whether the body, the heart, and the underlying self are pulling toward it. If the answer for one or more goals is "the body resists, the heart is indifferent, the underlying self has no interest," the goal is an inner-board goal. It will run on willpower as long as willpower holds, and then it will collapse.

A second test: when you describe what you want, do you describe it from your head ("I should achieve X by Y") or from your felt experience ("I feel pulled toward X, my body lights up around it, my heart cares about it")? The first mode is the inner board operating. The second mode is the system in alignment.

A third test: how does it feel in your body when you say the goal out loud? An inner-board goal often produces a tightening, a bracing, a sinking. A goal that the system has agreed to often produces openness, energy, lightness. See [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]] for the diagnostic move.

## The Alignment Move

Alignment is not achieved by silencing the voice. It is achieved by consulting the other subsystems and letting their input matter.

In Joe's own company's offsite, the goal-setting was conducted by asking each subsystem directly: "What does your heart want for yourself in this business? What does your heart want for the business? What does your heart want for the community we serve?" The questions are designed to elicit input from the heart, which the inner board would not have produced on its own.

"It's not a head want. It's like, oh, it's actually what is this desire that we have? And there's a deep investigation of our own wants. That's one of the best ways to really refine what you're doing in the world." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The deep investigation is what turns the inner-board mode into the aligned-system mode. The investigation cannot be skipped. The goals that emerge after it are different in character from the goals the voice would have issued on its own.

See [[bottom-up-vs-top-down-goals]] for the organizational version of the same principle: goals can come from the CEO or from the teams, but they have to come from somewhere where a real want exists, or they will not function.

## What This Implies for the Inner Critic

The voice in the head is often described as "the critic." The critic frame is useful but partial. The fuller frame is that the critic is the inner board, doing the job of governance for the personality. It issues goals, sets standards, runs reviews. The problem is not that the function exists; the problem is that it is operating without the input of the rest of the system.

The work is not to remove the inner board. It is to reorganize the relationship so the board is one input rather than the sole decider. The voice still gets to speak. The body, the heart, the underlying self also get to speak. The goals that emerge are alignments across all of them.

This is why [[wants-over-shoulds]], [[apply-view-to-the-voice]], and the various practices for working with the inner critic are not about silencing the voice. They are about reducing the voice's monopoly so that what is actually wanted can show up in the goals. See [[voice-in-the-head]] for the broader treatment.

## What Someone Experiences

When the inner board is running unilaterally, life feels like being managed by someone who does not know you. Goals arrive that you are expected to want. Failure to want them feels like a failure of character. Achievement of them produces less than expected. The constant background of "you should be doing more" never stops.

When the system comes into alignment, the experience is different. The goals that emerge are things you actually want. Moving toward them produces energy rather than draining it. Missing one produces information rather than self-attack. The voice is still there, but it is one voice instead of the only voice.

## Tone

Joe delivers this concept conversationally, as a discovery rather than a teaching. The board metaphor lands by analogy from the organizational version. The point is not to assign blame to the voice but to surface that the voice has been operating without consulting the rest of the system. Recognition tends to produce relief: the long-standing self-attack is recognized as misalignment, not as personal failure.
