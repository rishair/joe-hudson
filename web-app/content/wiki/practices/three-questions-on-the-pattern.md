# Three Questions on the Pattern (Hold-Back, Avoidance, Gift)

## The Practice

Three sequenced questions to ask of a pattern you have identified in your life. Each question opens a different angle on the same pattern. Taken together, they move someone from intellectual recognition of the pattern into the relational stance that allows it to update.

Joe offered these as integration questions at the end of a teaching on family dynamics. They are self-coaching prompts. A person can sit with them alone, journal them, or use them as the spine of a session with a coach.

"What's the pattern in your life that you most feel holds you back? And then the second question would be, what's the way that you try to avoid it that's actually keeping it in place? What's the way that you're trying to avoid that pattern that actually holds the pattern in place, would be the other way to say that. And then the third question is, What is it about this pattern that's been serving you, has tried to serve you, and has served you in the past?" -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Through Family Dynamics

## The Three Questions

**Question 1: What is the pattern in your life that you most feel holds you back?**

This locates the working material. The instruction is "most" — pick the largest live one, not the smallest manageable one. Not the curiosity, the pinch. The phrasing "holds you back" intentionally narrows away from the philosophical and toward what the person is actually paying for.

**Question 2: How are you trying to avoid this pattern in a way that is actually holding it in place?**

This is the inversion. The first question identifies a pattern the person wants gone. The second question reveals that what the person is doing to get rid of it is what is keeping it locked in. This is the [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] mechanism applied to the person's specific case. The "avoidance" includes overt resistance ("I work really hard on not being like this"), subtle resistance ("I tell myself I should be past this"), and the entire shame-and-fix loop the pattern lives inside.

**Question 3: What is it about this pattern that has been serving you, has tried to serve you, has served you in the past?**

This is the operational expression of [[honor-the-patterns-intent]]. The question presupposes that the pattern came for a good reason. It directs the person's attention to what the pattern protected, secured, or made possible. The answer is almost always tender: the striving secured love, the caretaking kept the house safe, the disappearing avoided the unbearable. Naming the gift dissolves the war footing.

## Why the Sequence Matters

The order is the work. If a person starts with the gift question, they may bypass the discomfort that motivates change. If they stop at the first question, they have a complaint. If they jump from the first to the third without the second, they miss the recognition that their own resistance is part of the lock.

The middle question is the pivot. It puts agency back without putting blame back. The pattern is not happening to the person; the person is participating, including in the resistance. The third question then re-meets that participation with respect.

## What This Practice Produces

- Recognition that the pattern is not the enemy
- Recognition that the resistance to the pattern is part of the structure that keeps it firing
- An opening to feel what the pattern has been protecting
- A softening that no amount of "trying to fix it" has produced
- The conditions under which the pattern can update its strategy

A person who completes this sequence with sincerity will often arrive at the underlying emotion that has been waiting to be felt. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. The questions create the relational ground; the emotion does the actual moving.

## Common Misuses

The questions can be answered intellectually and produce nothing. The voice in the head can recruit them as new should ("I'm supposed to honor my pattern"). The third question can be skipped because the person does not want to admit that the pattern they hate has done anything for them.

The instruction is to answer each question slowly, in the body, with the actual pattern present. The intellectual answer is a way to perform the practice without doing it. See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]].

## When This Practice Is Useful

- After a person has identified a pattern but cannot get traction changing it
- When the person is locked in self-attack about the pattern
- When [[childhood-imprinting]] has been understood and the question becomes "what do I do with this"
- As a closing integration after a teaching session on family dynamics
- When [[dont-make-family-dynamics-the-story]] is the risk and the person needs a way to use the recognition without it becoming the new identity
- As self-coaching when no coach is available

## Tone

Joe offered the questions in a closing register: not as an exercise to grade, but as a sitting-with. The tone he models is the tone the practice requires — unhurried, kind, with room for the answer to surprise.
