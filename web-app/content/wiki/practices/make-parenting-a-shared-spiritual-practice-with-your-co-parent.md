# Make Parenting a Shared Spiritual Practice With Your Co-Parent

## The Practice

Two co-parents agree to treat parenting as a shared spiritual practice and to have regular conversations about it. The conversations cover what they are learning, how they want to parent, what is changing in them, and how they want to integrate the lessons from parenting into the rest of their lives. The practice is structural: deliberate, recurring, deliberate.

"Tara and I made parenting a self-development practice or spiritual practice, and to have someone to talk to about that on a regular basis was huge. So it is, oh, this is what I'm learning. This is what I want to take away. Here's how I want to parent. This is how I want to take the lessons of parenting into the world. This is how I've grown. This is how it's changed for me." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The practice is one of the highest-leverage investments two parents can make in their parenting and in their relationship. It accomplishes several things at once that no other practice accomplishes together.

## What the Practice Looks Like

The form is loose. Some couples have weekly conversations. Some build the conversation into walks, dinners, dates. Some do it before bed. The frequency is less important than the regularity. What matters is that there is a structural commitment to talking about parenting as a practice, not just as logistics.

Topics that recur:

- **What are you learning about yourself through parenting right now?** The places the parent is being stretched, the places the parent is encountering their own patterns, the places where parenting is revealing what is left to work on.
- **What do you want to take into the rest of your life from this?** The lessons from parenting that apply to work, to friendship, to the marriage itself, to self-relationship.
- **What kind of parent do you want to be?** Not as an aspirational fantasy, but as ongoing calibration. The shape of the parenting changes as the kids grow; the conversation tracks the calibration.
- **What's happening with the kids right now and how do we want to be in it?** The specific situations the kids are in, the patterns showing up, the parental responses being considered.
- **Where are we struggling?** The honest naming of what is hard. The places where the practice is breaking down, individually or together.
- **How has parenting changed me?** The growth that is happening — not as report, but as recognition. The parent who sees their own change in conversation with the co-parent metabolizes the change more fully.

The conversations are not problem-solving sessions in the narrow sense. They are practice sessions in which the practice itself is the conversation.

## What the Practice Accomplishes

Multiple things at once:

**1. It keeps the parents on point with the parenting.** The conversation surfaces what each parent is noticing about the kids, about themselves, about the parenting. Drift gets corrected because it gets named.

**2. It accelerates the parents' own growth.** Parenting changes the parent (see [[parenting-as-deep-tissue-massage]]). The change is metabolized more thoroughly when the parent has language for it, articulates it, hears it back. The conversation is the metabolism.

**3. It strengthens the marriage.** The shared project of becoming the parents you want to be is the same kind of bond as any shared project, but with higher stakes and higher intimacy. The conversation about parenting often goes deep into the conversation about who each partner actually is.

**4. It models for the kid that adults talk about their relationships and their growth.** The kid sees parents engaging with their own development. The modeling lands even when the kid doesn't hear specific conversations.

**5. It prevents the parents from running parallel solo operations.** Without the conversation, two parents can be doing very different things and not knowing it until the kid is confused by the inconsistency. The conversation aligns the practice.

**6. It distributes the load of the inner work.** Each parent is processing parenting through their own lens. The conversation lets them check each other's reads, surface blind spots, support each other's work.

## Why "Spiritual Practice" Is the Right Frame

The frame "spiritual practice" is doing specific work. The alternative frames — "raising kids well," "good parenting," "doing the techniques" — all position parenting as a project the parents are doing to the kid. The spiritual-practice frame positions parenting as something the kid is doing to the parent at least as much as the reverse.

In the spiritual-practice frame:

- The kid is the curriculum. The parent is the student.
- The kid's tantrum is the koan. The parent's response is the practice.
- The places the parent is being broken open are the gifts.
- The growth is the point as much as the parenting itself is.

This is consistent with [[parenting-as-real-world-test-of-inner-work]] and [[parenting-as-deep-tissue-massage]]. The frame is not metaphorical. Joe holds it literally: parenting is one of the most efficient spiritual practices available, because the conditions cannot be evaded and the practitioner cannot fake it.

The conversation with the co-parent is the equivalent of the conversation a meditator has with a sangha or a teacher. The practice happens in the daily encounter. The conversation is the integration.

## Why the Conversation Needs to Be With the Co-Parent Specifically

The co-parent is uniquely positioned for this conversation in a few ways:

- **They are in it with you.** They have the same kids, the same household, the same daily encounter. They know what you are talking about specifically.
- **They have access to your blind spots.** They see you in moments you cannot see yourself. Their feedback is real, not theoretical.
- **They are the partner in the shared spiritual practice.** The parenting practice they are doing and the practice you are doing are the same practice, in the same household, on the same kids.
- **The conversation builds the marriage at the same time it builds the parenting.** Two for one.

The conversation can also happen with a therapist, a coach, a friend. But the conversation with the co-parent is structurally different from those, and the structural difference is part of what makes it work. The other conversations supplement it; they do not replace it.

## What This Practice Is Not

A few things it should not become:

- **A complaint session about the kids.** The complaints are sometimes necessary, but if the conversation reduces to a list of what the kids are doing wrong, the practice frame has been lost. The kids' behavior is the curriculum, not the indictment.
- **A scorekeeping session about who is doing more.** The labor distribution conversation is real and important, but it is a different conversation from the practice conversation. Conflating them dilutes both.
- **An evaluation of each other's parenting.** The conversation is about what each parent is learning, not about whether each parent is meeting the other's standard. Evaluation undermines the safety that makes the conversation valuable.
- **A performance of being good parents.** If either partner is using the conversation to demonstrate they are doing the practice well, the practice has been lost. The conversation is for what is actually happening, including the hard parts.

The conversation needs the same conditions any practice needs: safety, honesty, the willingness to say what is actually true rather than what looks good.

## What the Practice Produces Over Time

Joe describes the long-arc result of having done this with Tara:

"That was an incredibly useful part, and it kept us on point with the parenting, but more importantly, it kept us growing and developing as humans." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The "more importantly" is doing real work. The parenting outcome was good. The bigger outcome was the parents' own development. The practice produced two adults who had been changed by parenting in deeper ways than they would have been without the deliberate conversation.

This compounds across years. The parents who do this for a decade are different humans than they were when they started — not because parenting alone would not have done it, but because the deliberate conversation metabolized what parenting was already doing. The unmetabolized version of parenting also changes parents, but often in less intentional and more painful ways.

## Setting Up the Practice

Practical steps for couples who want to start:

1. **Have one explicit conversation about wanting to do this.** Not just "let's talk more about the kids." A conversation about wanting to treat parenting as a shared practice and wanting to set up regular conversations about it.
2. **Pick a structure.** A walk on Sunday morning. A weekly dinner without phones. The drive home from drop-off. The structure matters because without it, the conversation gets crowded out by logistics.
3. **Pick a few opening questions.** "What did you notice this week?" "What are you learning?" "Where are you struggling?" The questions don't need to be elaborate. They need to open the conversation.
4. **Let the form evolve.** What works in the first six months may not be what works in year five. The practice itself is iterated.
5. **Be willing to do it imperfectly.** Missed weeks are not failures. The practice is the orientation, not the rigid schedule.
