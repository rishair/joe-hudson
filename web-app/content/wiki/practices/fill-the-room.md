# Fill the Room

## The Practice

When stage fright -- or any anxiety that has the flavor of stepping into a bigger room -- arrives, the instruction is to fill the whole room with your felt presence. Not to push outward, not to project, not to perform. Just to let your felt sense extend to occupy the entire room you are in.

"I gave them this like very, very simple little exercise to do. I said, hey, next time you get up on stage, I want you to fill the whole room. I want you to feel like you are the entire room. That's it. Just put your feeling into the entire room." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The instruction is short. The execution is internal and somatic. The person on stage does not visibly do anything different. What changes is the radius of their felt presence inside the body.

## What It Does

The anxiety of stepping into a bigger room is excitement that has been constricted. See [[fear-vs-excitement]] and [[two-sources-of-anxiety]]. The body is producing the activation that matches the size of the room. The person is holding their felt sense small inside that activation. The mismatch between the size of the activation and the size of the felt-self is what reads as anxiety.

Filling the room resolves the mismatch directly. The felt-self expands to match the activation. What was reading as anxiety is then read as excitement -- the same energy, now allowed to occupy the space it was always trying to occupy.

The reframe is not cognitive. It is somatic. The person does not think their way out of the anxiety. They expand their felt body and the anxiety converts.

## The Source Story

The teaching is delivered as a story about a friend who always had stage fright. Joe gave the friend the instruction; the friend, who was somatically aware, was able to receive it; the result arrived in the room:

"Luckily, they were somatically very aware. They understood what I was saying. They were like, oh, the anxiety's gone. Actually, I was really excited to be up on stage." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The friend's report names the mechanism precisely: the anxiety was gone, and what was underneath it was the excitement that had been there all along. The friend did not stop being activated. The activation was the same. The label and the felt experience of the activation shifted because the felt-self was now the size of the room.

## When To Use It

- Before walking on stage. The instruction is given in advance and run silently in the moment.
- Before a high-stakes presentation, pitch, or speech. Any moment where the person is stepping into a bigger room than usual.
- When entering a new job, new relationship, new way of being, or new level of responsibility. The room is bigger; the body is producing the activation for it; the instruction is to fill the room you are now in.
- When the person is already on stage / in the meeting / in the room, and the anxiety is currently firing. The instruction can run in real time.
- As a complement to [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] when the verbal practice is not enough, or when the setting does not permit speaking out loud (mid-presentation, mid-meeting).

## Prerequisites

The instruction requires somatic awareness. The friend in the source story is described as "somatically very aware" -- the friend can feel where their felt-self is, can locate the radius of their presence in the body, and can expand it on instruction.

For a person without that baseline awareness, the instruction may not land cleanly. The remediation is to build the somatic awareness first ([[body-awareness-as-the-base]], [[coming-to-your-senses]]) and then teach the room-filling move. Or to pair the instruction with body movement that does the work the awareness would otherwise do -- the hand-pumping, the breath, the discharge moves taught alongside [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] and in [[nervous-system-prep-before-high-stakes]].

## Distinction From "Say I'm Excited"

Both practices address the same underlying state -- excitement that has been constricted, presenting as anxiety -- and both convert the state in the moment. They differ in mechanism:

- **Say I'm excited ten times** ([[say-im-excited-ten-times]]) is verbal. The breath required for the repetition opens the constriction. The label "excited" routes the activation through a different framing. The mechanism is breath plus relabeling.

- **Fill the room** is somatic-only. There is no verbal component. The felt-self expands to match the activation. The mechanism is the resolution of the mismatch between the size of the activation and the size of the felt-self.

The two practices can be used together. Say "I'm excited" ten times to open the breath and route the label; then fill the room with the now-acknowledged excitement. The combination addresses both the verbal-cognitive layer and the somatic layer in sequence.

For a person who is somatically very aware, the room-filling practice alone often suffices. For a person who is mostly in their head, the verbal practice is the better entry point. For a person who needs the discharge, the body-movement version paired with one or both is the move.

## What It Does Not Do

The practice addresses the bigger-room form of anxiety -- the form that is excitement misread. It does not address anxiety that is signaling a genuine misalignment, a real danger, or an unmade ask. See [[two-sources-of-anxiety]] for the sorting frame. If the anxiety is the body's report that a specific action has not been taken (the first source), filling the room will not resolve it. The person needs to take the action.

The diagnostic: if filling the room actually shifts the felt state to excitement, the anxiety was the bigger-room form. If filling the room does not move the state, the anxiety is one of the other forms and needs a different response.

See [[grounded-excitement-vs-ungrounded-excitement]] for the second-layer check -- if filling the room produces excitement that is bouncy rather than amped, there is still anxiety underneath that has not been integrated.

## Tone

The instruction is delivered simply. No build-up. No explanation. Just the instruction. "Fill the whole room. Feel like you are the entire room. That's it." The minimal framing is part of the practice -- the more elaborate the explanation, the more cognitive the receiving of the instruction, and the more the somatic move gets short-circuited.
