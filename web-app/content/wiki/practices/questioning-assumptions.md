# Questioning the Assumption

## Definition

Inside every problem statement, every complaint, every "I am X" or "this is Y," there are assumptions that are not being questioned. The default response is to try to solve the announced problem. The practice is to question the problem itself — the assumptions embedded in the way it is being held.

"In any statement that we make, any problem statement that we make — I'm slammed right now, I don't have enough money, I'm not enlightened — there are some assumptions in there that usually aren't questioned. What we usually do is we go and try to solve the problem that's been announced instead of actually question the problem itself." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The practice has two surface uses and one deeper use. At the surface it is a problem-solving tool and a way to access [[wonder]]. At depth it is one of the practices that produces awakening, freedom, and the dissolution of self-identification. The same instrument operates at all three levels.

## Two Distinct Uses (Same Instrument, Different Depth)

This is a single practice but it operates in two different registers, and the distinction matters for the AI coach. Conflating them produces a confused practice.

**Surface use: problem solving and out-of-the-box thinking.** Used in business, management, design. Useful, valid, well-covered in many books. "Are we asking the right question? What's the actual problem statement here? What am I missing?" The frame is pragmatic. The aim is better solutions.

**Self-inquiry use: seeing through one's own thoughts and identity.** Same act of questioning, but turned on the assumptions inside one's own beliefs about self, world, right, wrong, and freedom. The frame is different: not "let's get to a better answer" but "let's see whether this is true at all, and what becomes available when the assumption loosens." The eventual effect is identity-level rather than answer-level.

"There is a distinction between the way it's used in business, which is really, really useful, and problem solving, which is really, really useful, and when it's used for self-inquiry." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The two uses do not require different mechanics. They require different orientations. The business version aims at outcomes. The self-inquiry version aims at freedom from the assumption itself.

## How It Works

Any statement carries assumptions. "Life is challenging because I don't have enough work" contains at least three: that less work is challenging, that I do not have enough, that I should have more. Each assumption, once surfaced, opens a field of [[wonder]].

Alternative assumptions exist for the same facts: free time to start a business, an opportunity to improve marketing, space to rest. The assumptions are not right or wrong. Surfacing them reveals that the "obvious" reading was one of many.

Context shifts produce the same effect. "I don't have enough work" means something different in suburban America, rural Africa, and coastal Iceland. The statement that felt absolute becomes relative. The knowing loosens.

"Questioning the assumption that's in your mind is one of the quickest ways to get to wonder." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder

At the self-inquiry depth, the same procedure is run on the structures that are typically taken to be self-evident: that there is an enlightenment to be achieved, that there is a you who would achieve it, that this thought is yours, that right and wrong are stable categories, that you know what is true.

"Are you enlightened? Are you not enlightened? Is there a you that gets enlightened? Is that how it works? Is enlightenment something that you achieve or is enlightenment something that just happens? How would you know if you were enlightened? There's all sorts of assumptions to the question." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

## When To Use It

- When you or the person you are coaching is stuck in a fixed interpretation.
- When a conversation is looping: the same complaint, the same framing, the same conclusion.
- When you cannot find your way into [[wonder]] through other doorways.
- When someone presents their situation as though only one reading is possible.
- When the person is ready to question something deeper than the surface problem — not just "is my goal right?" but "is the I that has this goal real in the way I assume?"

For self-inquiry depth, the practice is also recommended as a daily lifestyle rather than a session move. Joe describes a period when he questioned every assumption hardcore for about a week and a half. Then it became a continuous orientation rather than an effort. See [[disorienting-middle-of-self-inquiry]].

## How To Practice

In conversation, listen for the assumptions inside what someone says. Rather than responding to the surface content, ask about the assumption underneath it.

Example: someone says "I need to figure out how to keep my job." The assumption is that keeping the job is the goal. A question that surfaces the assumption: "What would it mean if you didn't keep it?"

In self-reflection, take a belief or worry and list its embedded assumptions. Question each one. Notice what opens when the assumption is no longer treated as given.

For self-inquiry, the practice generalizes: question every thought as it arises, not as an attack on the thought but as a check on its assumption-content. Most thoughts of the type "this is true, that is false, this is right, that is wrong" have assumptions that, when surfaced, reveal the thought is partial — not entirely true and not entirely false. "All thoughts have some truth and all thoughts don't have truth."

## Family Resemblance to Other Inquiry Forms

Question the assumption sits in a lineage of inquiry practices. Joe names several explicitly:

- **Vichara** (the Hindu "what am I" inquiry). See [[what-am-i]].
- **Byron Katie's four questions.**
- **The Socratic method.**

"There's lots of forms of inquiry out there. There's vichara, which is the 'what am I' that the Hindus did. There's Byron Katie, the four questions. There's the Socratic method. There's all sorts of inquiry forms. And question the assumption is a way to congeal a lot of those different forms of inquiry." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The practice is not unique. The framing as "question the assumption" is what makes it portable across the surface and the depth registers without losing coherence.

## What It Produces

At the surface: access to [[wonder]] and [[impartiality]] simultaneously. The fixed frame loosens. New information enters. The person often discovers that their "problem" contains options they could not see while the assumption was invisible.

At depth: progressively less identification with the thoughts that previously felt like self. Each assumption seen through is a small dissolution of identity. See [[transparent-identity-vs-rigid]]. The belief does not disappear — it becomes [[belief-integration-not-elimination|integrated, transparent, optional]] — and so does the identity it was holding in place.

"To be able to see through that also, in its core, is seeing through your identity a little bit each time. Because most of us identify with the thoughts that we're thinking." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

A subtler product: the thoughts you trade one assumption for another assumption, but the swapping itself makes both assumptions more transparent. The lens you are looking through becomes visible. "You can see the lens you're looking through. And so that creates a way in which [assumptions] dissolve. They just become less real, less personal."

## The Pay-Off That Makes It Worth the Discomfort

The case for doing this work, in Joe's framing:

- A tremendous amount of freedom from having to defend your thoughts.
- Nothing to defend, so nothing to be right about.
- Ability to see the truth in different perspectives, which makes relationships much better.
- Easier problem-solving: when your thinking is flexible, you can say "yes, here's exactly how I'm full of shit, and here's what I see" and the conversation moves.

"It's a tremendous amount of freedom to not have to defend your thoughts, to not have to defend yourself, to be able to see through the things that cause you pain, to be able to shift your worldview and see the truth in different perspectives." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The freedom is not abstract. It is the felt absence of needing to be right.

## Paired Move: Question Your Own Knowing

The same practice applies inward. When you find yourself certain about something, ask: what is the context of this knowing? "Lying is wrong," for example, assumes a context (suburban living, ordinary relationships). In the context of hiding a person from an authoritarian government, the certainty becomes a question. Every "truth" you hold is true within a context.

This makes wonder durable. You stop defending your knowing because you can see that your knowing is one slice of reality, not all of it. When someone challenges you on something you said earlier, the response is not defense but a shift: "Yeah, I don't entirely agree with myself either" — you can see the context in which the new framing is also true.

"Knowing is only relevant based on context. To think you know something is to not only assume that you know the right answer, but it's also to assume that you know the context of the person that you're going around with. And it's also to assume that they don't have some wisdom in what they're saying, which is ridiculous." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder

## Litmus Tests for Whether You Are Actually Doing It

Question the assumption is easy to mistake for. The practice has external symptoms that look similar to other things (skepticism, contrarianism, debate). Joe gives concrete tests for whether the real thing is happening.

**Discomfort.** The assumptions that are most important to question are the ones you hold most dear. Questioning them is uncomfortable. If you are not uncomfortable, you are likely not questioning the load-bearing assumptions, only the convenient ones.

"The way that you know that you're doing it while you're doing it is that it gets uncomfortable. Like the assumptions that are the most important to question are the ones that you hold most dear, and to question them is uncomfortable. So there's moments of discomfort, there's moments of embracing intensity that come with this practice." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

**Defensiveness signals an unquestioned assumption.** If someone attacks your idea and you get defensive, that idea is still operating as assumption rather than as transparent thought. The defensiveness is the diagnostic.

"If someone attacks your ideas and you get defensive, that's an idea you haven't seen through. So that defensiveness is another really good litmus test of like, because you need to be right, you're valuing correctness." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

**Aggression is suspect.** If you are questioning an assumption with force, with attack-energy, with the need to make the other person see, you are likely defending another assumption. The real practice is more joyful and heart-opening, even when scary.

**Fear of losing connection.** At some point in deep work, the thought "I might not be able to relate to people I love anymore" arises. "I don't know anybody who's really deeply questioned their assumptions and not thought to themselves, oh, I might not be able to — I may be going too far out there and I might lose people I love." This is not a sign you are doing it wrong. It is a sign you are touching real load-bearing material.

## The Disorienting Middle

For most people who go deep with this practice, there is a phase of disorientation. The ground feels ripped out. Simple decisions become hard because the assumptions you used to make decisions are visible as assumptions. Joe describes a week and a half of his own hardcore inquiry leaving him asking "who, what, like, how do I make a fucking decision from this place if there's no clear right and wrong and what the fuck's going on?"

Joe has worked with someone who, after going deep, had a hard time at the grocery store deciding what to buy. Even those mundane choices revealed themselves to depend on assumptions that were no longer load-bearing.

This phase passes. See [[disorienting-middle-of-self-inquiry]] for full treatment of the pattern and what comes through it. The destabilization is part of the practice, not evidence the practice has failed.

## What Eventually Emerges (The Morality Flip)

A specific structural shift comes through the practice that is non-obvious enough to require its own framing: see [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]] and [[mind-wide-as-sky-action-fine-as-barley]].

Briefly: as thoughts about right and wrong become transparent, the locus of moral discernment moves from the head (where it was content) to the body and action (where it becomes felt necessity). You stop having a moral *opinion* about closing your heart or lying. You feel that closing your heart hurts, and there is therefore nothing to do but keep it open. The morality stops being a thought-thing and becomes a body-thing.

This is what the practice is producing under the surface of the discomfort. The work is not nihilism. It is the transfer of moral discernment from a fragile thought-layer to a more reliable body-layer.

## Common Failure Modes

See [[dont-use-inquiry-to-win]], [[skeptical-inquiry-vs-self-inquiry]], and [[dont-use-story-frame-to-bypass]] for the major anti-patterns.

The short list:

- **Questioning to be right.** Lawyering. Using inquiry to win debates. Skepticism wearing the costume of inquiry.
- **Questioning everything except your sacred cows.** Becoming "a questioner of assumptions" as a new identity, while a few load-bearing assumptions stay invisible.
- **Questioning as rebellion.** When the questioning is fueled by rebellion against an authority figure (often a parent), the practice is still controlled by what it is rebelling against and cannot see the truth in both sides.
- **Stopping at intellectual seeing-through.** The mind releases first, the emotions and body lag. See [[mind-unsees-first-body-last]]. Without the body work, the seeing-through is partial and the belief reinstalls.
- **Treating the destabilization phase as proof the practice is broken.** The destabilization is the practice working. Stop, and you stop short of what the practice was producing.

## Tone

The practice is held with curiosity, not aggression. The investigative orientation is "what is here?" not "what is wrong?" Joe's tone when teaching it has lightness — playful with mortality, playful with absurd assumptions like "I'm going to live forever" — because the underlying recognition is freeing rather than grim.

The destabilizing phase is held with gentleness for oneself. The practice asks for being merciless with assumptions, not with the self.
