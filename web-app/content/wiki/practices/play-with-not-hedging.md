# Play With Not Hedging

## The Practice

For someone whose speech is full of hedges, qualifiers, and softeners — "maybe," "kind of," "like," "sort of," "I guess," "in a way" — the experiment is to speak without them for a defined period, and notice what happens.

The instruction is exact: *play* with it. Not force yourself. Not eliminate hedges from your life. Try the experiment in a contained way, with curiosity, and observe what arises in the body when the qualifier is removed.

"To play with not hedging is a very useful set of experiments to do." — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

The "set of experiments" framing matters. This is not a single rule to follow. It is multiple small attempts to remove specific hedges in specific contexts and notice the effect.

## Why This Practice Works

Hedging is doing a job. See [[linguistic-caveat]]. The qualifier is at the precise spot where the speaker would otherwise have to feel something they are not feeling. Removing the qualifier surfaces what was being avoided.

The avoided material is almost always the same thing in different costumes: the want, the claim, the assertion that the speaker has the right to be a person with desires and views. The hedge softens these things to make them less inviting of imagined attack. The practice puts the speaker into direct contact with what they were softening.

The practice is particularly useful for the [[two-layers-of-shame-about-being-seen]] pattern. The hedges are the verbal form of the wanting-shame. Each hedge says "I want this but please don't take it that I really want it." Removing the hedge means owning the want. The owning is the work.

## How to Run It

Several forms of the experiment, in order of difficulty:

1. **One-conversation form.** In a single conversation today, drop the hedges. Just say the thing without "maybe," "kind of," or "I guess." Notice what happens in your body. Notice how the listener responds. Notice what arose in you that the hedges had been protecting against.

2. **One-day form.** For a day, attempt to speak without hedges. Catch yourself when you use them. Either restate without the hedge or just notice. The point is awareness of the pattern, not perfect execution.

3. **One-hedge form.** Pick a specific hedge word that you overuse (often "maybe" or "kind of"). For a week, notice every time you use it. Some of the time, restate without it. Track what kinds of statements the hedge attaches to. Patterns will emerge — the hedge always shows up around a specific kind of content.

4. **Email-and-message form.** In written communication for a defined period, do a final pass that removes hedges before sending. The asynchronous form lets you see the difference between the hedged and unhedged version on paper, which often surfaces what the hedge was protecting.

The size of the experiment is less important than running it as an experiment. The point is data, not discipline.

## What Tends to Surface

When the hedges drop, several things tend to surface depending on what the hedges had been doing:

- **The want.** Most often. "I want to go to the movie" instead of "I kind of want to go to the movie maybe." The want becomes ownable.
- **The opinion.** "This isn't working" instead of "I sort of feel like maybe this isn't working." The opinion becomes claimable.
- **The boundary.** "No" instead of "I think maybe not right now if that's okay." The boundary becomes a boundary.
- **The bid for connection.** "I want to be seen" instead of "I guess I want to be seen, like, sort of." The bid becomes available to be met.

The somatic signal: when the unhedged statement leaves your mouth, what happens in your body? Often a small surge of fear or aliveness. The surge is the felt content that the hedge had been smoothing over. The point of the practice is to make that content available, not to eliminate it.

## What Tends to Surface in the Listener

The unhedged statement also produces a different response in the listener. Hedged speech reads as someone managing themselves. Unhedged speech reads as someone present. Most listeners receive the latter more openly than the former, even when the content is the same.

The data the speaker collects: the imagined attack that the hedges were protecting against usually doesn't arrive. The hedges had been managing a danger that wasn't there. Once this is observed across several attempts, the body's automatic hedging starts to relax on its own.

## Variations

- **The "what makes you say maybe?" version.** When the hedge does arise in conversation with someone safe, ask yourself (or have a coach ask): what makes me say "maybe" here? Investigate the hedge specifically. See [[question-the-caveat]] for the move-form of this.
- **The single-sentence version.** Before sending an important message or making an important statement, write it once with all your normal hedges, then rewrite it without any. Read both versions. Send the version that is true.
- **The body-check version.** Before speaking, briefly check the body. If the body is bracing for the imagined attack, name that internally. Then speak without the hedge. Notice what happens to the brace after the unhedged statement lands.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a campaign to eliminate hedges. Some hedges are accurate ("I'm not sure" when you actually aren't sure is not a hedge, it is a statement). The practice is about removing the unnecessary hedges that are doing protective work, not the necessary ones that are doing communicative work.
- It is not about forcing yourself to be blunt or aggressive. The opposite of hedging is not blunt — it is direct and warm. "I want to spend more time with you" is direct without being aggressive. The unhedged version is usually softer, not harder, than the hedged version, because the hedged version was carrying anxiety.
- It is not a moral practice. The hedges were learned. They were once protective. The practice is not about being better; it is about gathering data about what the hedges have been managing.

## When This Practice Is Useful

- For someone presenting [[how-do-i-love-myself]] with visible hedging in their language
- For someone who reports difficulty being seen and whose speech is full of qualifiers
- For someone in [[i-cant-ask-for-what-i-want]] or other ask-related concerns
- For a leader whose direct reports cannot tell what the leader actually wants
- For someone who has done extensive inner work and noticed that their language is still hedged

## Tone

Light. Curious. The practice is named as "play" deliberately. Trying to do this perfectly converts the practice into another should and adds another layer of management on top of the original hedging. The play frame keeps it as an experiment, which is the only mode in which the data is useful.
