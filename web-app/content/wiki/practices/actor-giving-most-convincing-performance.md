# Pretend You're an Actor Giving the Most Convincing Performance of the Emotion

## The Practice

When the body is stuck and an emotion is not arriving, do not try to find the emotion. Pretend instead that you are an actor whose job is to give the most convincing performance of having that emotion. The framing does two things at once that nothing else does cleanly: it produces real sincerity in the body (the actor commits), and it releases identification (the actor knows the part is not who they are).

The most reliable framing of the practice Joe gives:

"The way I describe it that I think works the best is pretend you're an actor and your job is to give the most convincing performance of sadness, but you're just an actor. And what it does is it does the two things that we just — that you just talked about. The first thing that it does is it creates a sincerity, but the second thing it does is it allows you not to believe any of your thoughts because you're an actor." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

"It's like you're outside observing it, so it's — your identity is not involved. Right? Because your identity is the one observing the actor, and you are have the sincerity, or it's not a good performance. And you don't believe any of the thing because it's just a story. And though, if you're gonna fake it, that's the most effective way I've seen to fake it, is pretend you're an actor playing the part of you right now, and your job is to give the most sincere performance, best performance of anger, best performance of sadness." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

## What Makes This Different From Manufacturing

[[manufacturing-the-feeling]] is the failure mode where the person tries to produce the emotion they think they should be having. The trying is the resistance; the manufactured version blocks the actual feeling. This practice is different because it removes the demand for the emotion to be "real." The actor is not required to feel anything. The actor is required to give a good performance.

Paradoxically, this is what lets the actual feeling arrive. The system that was tightening around "I need to feel this" relaxes. The body, no longer being asked to produce a state, is free to enter the state the actor is shaping.

Joe explicitly addresses Brett's concern about the fine line:

"There's a fine line between just full-on faking it, where on some level you know you're faking it and like you're trying to get yourself somewhere, and so the resistance happens and then you get nowhere. But there's something — there's a subtle fine line where if you're faking it with sincerity there's an invitation like, hey, I'm — if there's hay in their muscles, if there's a tension that's kind of like this one that wants to be felt, let's try feeling." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3

The actor framing solves the line problem by changing what is being asked of the body. Not "feel this." Not "perform this for the audience to see how sincere you are." Just "play this character convincingly." The character is sincere by construction. The actor is not personally invested by construction. The body has room to do what it wants.

## The Two Things It Does

**1. Sincerity.** A good actor commits. They are not winking at the audience. They are giving the performance everything. This commitment lands in the body. The body that has been holding the emotion away is invited to inhabit the shape of the emotion fully. The committed shape is the entry.

**2. Freedom from identification.** Actors know the part is the part. They do not become the character. They embody the character and then step out. This frame is exactly what the body needs to allow the emotion fully. The system stops defending against "becoming" the emotion (and thereby admitting it as part of identity) because the framing makes clear that the actor is not the character. See [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]] for the related move.

The combination is the practice's specific value. Sincerity without identification. Both at once. Each component alone is insufficient. Sincerity with identification produces the manufacturing trap. Non-identification without sincerity produces detachment that bypasses the feeling.

## What This Move Practically Looks Like

The body's first question once given the instruction: "Okay, how am I gonna act like me having this emotion?" That question naturally directs attention into the body — what does my body want to do if it is really feeling this? — without going through the head's evaluation.

Brett's articulation of this entry:

"The pointer of being an actor, acting as you also is just like a great way to drop into the body. Cuz okay, how am I gonna act like me? The first place I'm gonna go, at least maybe that's me, but the first place I'd go is into my body to be like, what is, what does my body wanna do if it's that? You know, if I'm, if I'm feeling that. And then I'm most likely to actually take the shape that, that is actually what my body wants to take to move that emotion." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3

So the instruction operationally produces a body-first inquiry without naming it. The actor needs to know how the character moves. The only way to find that is to consult the body. The body's answer is the shape the emotion has been wanting to take.

## When to Use This Practice

- When a person reports they cannot find an emotion they sense is there.
- When [[every-thought-about-not-feeling-is-an-emotion-to-feel]] has been applied and the descent is producing more thoughts about not-feeling rather than the feeling itself.
- When the body has been numb to a particular emotion for a long time.
- When the person has done the work intellectually but the emotion is not arriving in the body.
- When [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] applies but the person needs a broader framing.

## How to Deliver

The instruction is short:

"Pretend you're an actor playing the part of you having this emotion right now. Your job is to give the most convincing performance you can."

Then let the body do its work. Do not over-instruct. Do not specify what the performance should look like. The actor framing already gives the body permission to find its own shape.

If the person asks "but is this real?", redirect: "Don't worry about whether it's real. Just give the best performance you can. Your job is to be convincing, not to be sincere about whether you're sincere."

The redirect is important. The question "is this real?" is the identification-with-the-feeling trying to reassert itself. The actor stance does not need the question answered.

## What This Practice Produces

- The body finds the shape of the emotion without the head's interference.
- The shape becomes the door through which the actual feeling arrives.
- The transition from performance to felt state is often imperceptible — the actor realizes mid-performance that they are no longer performing.
- The identification trap that [[dont-believe-your-emotions-as-identity]] warns about is structurally avoided because the framing pre-empts it.

## The "Old Crowbar"

Joe explicitly calls this practice the crowbar — the tool you use when nothing else is working.

"That's the old crowbar. Yeah." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The crowbar framing is honest about what the practice is. Not the natural way emotions move. The mechanical tool you use when the natural way is jammed. Once the practice opens the door, the natural way takes over.

## Connection to Other Practices

This practice is the more general form of [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]. The anger version is the specific case for one emotion. The actor-most-convincing-performance version is the broader framing that works for any emotion.

It is also the operational answer to [[discipline-starts-engine-then-let-gravity]]: the actor's discipline is the engine-start; the natural feeling that arrives is the gravity that takes over. Both phases are present. The discipline is sincere effort. The release happens once the engine catches.

## Tone

Light, playful, undemanding. The actor framing has built-in lightness because acting itself is play. Joe delivers this as a hack, not as serious instruction. The lightness is part of what makes it work — the body cannot drop into a heavy practice the way it drops into a playful one.
