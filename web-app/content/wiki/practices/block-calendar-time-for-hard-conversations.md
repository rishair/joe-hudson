# Block Calendar Time for Hard Conversations

## The Practice

Put three hours on your calendar every month. The slot is named "hard conversations." During the slot, scan for what doesn't feel right inside the company or the team, identify which hard conversations you have been avoiding, and have them. Pick up the phone. Call the person. Open the drawer.

"I literally on my calendar have 3 hours set aside every month to have hard conversations. I literally, it just says hard conversations." -- Joe Hudson

"And I think about, okay, what are the hard conversations I'm not having? And I call people up and have them." -- Joe Hudson

The practice is structural. It does not depend on the leader's mood, on the conversation being easy to enter, or on the other person bringing the issue up first. Three hours a month, the leader sits down and does the work the rest of the month makes it tempting to defer.

## What the Slot Is For

The slot is for conversations that meet two conditions:

1. **Something doesn't feel quite right.** The leader has noticed friction, tension, an unaddressed pattern, a misalignment, a thing the team is dancing around, a relationship that has cooled, a piece of feedback they have been sitting on. The not-quite-right is real but small enough that the default move is to ignore it.

2. **The leader has not had the conversation yet.** The slot is not for re-doing conversations that have happened. It is for the ones that have not happened because the leader has been waiting for the right moment, for the issue to resolve on its own, for the other person to bring it up, for things to calm down enough that the conversation will be received.

"What doesn't feel quite right? I'm going to have that conversation. I don't care if it's about you. I don't care if it's about me. All I care is it's not quite right. And it needs attention, and I'm gonna go put attention on it, and we're gonna have the difficult conversation, and we're gonna learn something." -- Joe Hudson

The scope is the leader's own discomfort radar. The slot processes the backlog of "I should probably bring that up" items the leader has been carrying.

## Why a Standing Calendar Block

The standing block exists because hard conversations have a specific time profile. They are always urgent in some sense (the unresolved thing is costing the team) and never urgent in the immediate sense (no meeting will collapse if the conversation waits one more week). The "never immediately urgent" property means they get deferred indefinitely without a structural counterweight.

The calendar block is the counterweight. It is the structural answer to the leader's own avoidance pattern. The leader does not have to find motivation each time; the slot is already there, and the only question is which conversation to take during it.

"If you wait to have them, it'll have hurt the team. If you wait to have them, you will be much angrier. But if you wait to have them, the relationship will have resentment in it. If you have them as soon as you see them, less resentment, less problems in the team, better execution." -- Joe Hudson

The cost of waiting is not a one-time cost. It is a per-day cost that accumulates. The conversation that would have taken twenty minutes early becomes the conversation that takes hours late, plus the months of dysfunction that grew in the interim.

## The Practice as Opening-the-Drawer Institutionalized

The practice is the institutionalized form of [[open-the-drawer-as-shared-verb]]. Opening the drawer is the moment-to-moment verb the team uses to surface what's not being said. The calendar block is the leader's own structural commitment to do the same thing at scale, every month, regardless of whether the moment-to-moment opportunity arises in passing.

The leader cannot rely on hard conversations surfacing themselves through opening-the-drawer in regular meetings. Some conversations are too specific to one relationship, too cross-cutting to fit in a meeting, or too charged to surface in a group setting. The standing slot exists to address the conversations that need a one-on-one channel and that the leader keeps putting off.

## Why This Is High-Priority Time

The practice ties directly to [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]] and to the leader's role in keeping the team's connective tissue healthy. Unsaid things accumulate as resentment, misalignment, and disengagement. The leader's three hours per month spent on hard conversations does more for the team's effectiveness than the same three hours spent on almost any other leadership activity.

"Very high priority. It's very much to me, like you said, oh, I need to get my house in order. Like if people aren't feeling connected and the thing that prevents people from feeling connected is this, is not having those conversations on accountability. This didn't feel good. This didn't work. This didn't, this doesn't feel good. This doesn't work. Those conversations aren't being had. Then there's less connection, and therefore people are less likely to actually do the things that they're supposed to be doing for the team. Cause there's resentment and all that stuff builds up." -- Joe Hudson

Connection is the operational substrate of team performance. The hard conversations are what keep the substrate clean. The standing block ensures the substrate is being maintained on a regular cycle rather than only when the dysfunction has grown large enough to demand attention.

## How to Run the Slot

The slot is unstructured by design. The leader uses the time to:

1. **Scan.** What is the leader carrying that they have not addressed? What patterns have they noticed and not named? What feedback have they been sitting on? What relationship feels distant? The scan can be quiet review, or it can be writing, or it can be conversation with a trusted other.

2. **Pick.** Of the things surfaced in the scan, which ones need a direct conversation with a specific person? The leader picks the highest-leverage one to address first.

3. **Call.** Pick up the phone (or the equivalent for a remote team). Have the conversation. Do not wait for the right moment. Do not over-prepare. The slot itself is the right moment.

4. **Continue.** When the first conversation is complete, return to the scan. If there is a second one ready to be had, have it. The slot ends when the time is up, not when the leader's discomfort with the practice peaks.

## What the Practice Produces

When the practice is run consistently:

- The leader's backlog of unsaid things stays small. Issues get processed in the month they arise rather than accumulating for years.
- The team's relational tissue stays clean. Resentment does not have time to ossify because the leader is regularly surfacing and addressing the items that would otherwise calcify into resentment.
- The leader's own avoidance pattern gets exposed and worked on. The leader sees, month after month, what they were tempted to keep avoiding. The pattern becomes legible.
- The team develops trust that hard things will be addressed. The leader's reliability on this point teaches the team that the leader does not pretend things are fine when they are not.

The practice also accelerates the team's culture. When the team sees the leader regularly initiating hard conversations from a position of care rather than aggression, the team learns the form. The team begins to initiate their own hard conversations using the same shape.

## Why This Pairs With Prioritization

The practice is one form of the leader's broader prioritization discipline. The leader who knows what the first domino is for the company also knows that the connective tissue of the team is one of the first dominoes. Spending three hours a month on the conversations that keep the tissue healthy is a high-leverage use of leadership attention.

"Very high priority. It's very much to me, like you said, oh, I need to get my house in order." -- Joe Hudson

The metaphor is the leader's own house. The house has to be in order for the work to happen. The standing block is part of keeping the house in order.

## What This Is Not

**Not a slot for performance reviews.** Performance reviews are scheduled, structured, and pre-existing in most companies. The hard-conversations block is for the conversations that fall outside the structured channels — the ones that have not been institutionalized because they are too situation-specific.

**Not a slot for delivering bad news as a category.** This is not "the firing slot" or "the negative feedback slot." It is the slot for conversations the leader has been avoiding. Some of those involve giving feedback, some involve receiving it, some involve clarifying a misalignment, some involve repairing a relational rupture. The category is "what I have not said," not "what is hard to hear."

**Not a slot for the leader to dump.** The conversation has to be had in an open-hearted way, with the leader's expectations clear and the other person's experience welcomed. The slot is not permission to operate from frustration. See [[no-doctrine-leadership]] for the inner posture required.

**Not a substitute for in-the-moment elephant-naming.** When the leader notices a thing in the meeting that needs to be named, the leader names it then. The standing slot is for the things that did not get named in the moment and have been carried since. Both channels matter. The standing slot is the safety net for what the in-the-moment channel misses.

## Boundary Conditions

The practice works for leaders who:

- Have done enough internal work to enter hard conversations from a position of care rather than reactivity
- Have a team large enough that hard conversations do not get processed naturally in daily interaction
- Have a calendar discipline that holds standing blocks against the pressure to fill them with other meetings
- Are willing to actually have the conversations during the slot, not just identify them

The practice does not work when:

- The leader is in a chronic state of frustration with the team and would use the slot to vent rather than to address
- The leader is unable to hold the boundary against the calendar pressure that wants to absorb every available block
- The hard conversations the leader has been avoiding are about the leader's own behavior and the leader is not yet ready to receive feedback in them

## The Joe-Specific Detail

In the conversation, the interviewer asks Joe which exact hours of the week the slot occupies, half-joking that he wants to be available if he gets a call during them. The detail is small but communicates the practice's seriousness: the slot is on the calendar, at specific hours, and Joe genuinely uses it to make the calls. The interviewer reports having received one such call himself.

The realism of the practice is part of its teaching. It is not an aspiration or a metaphor. Three hours, on the calendar, every month, with the leader making actual phone calls during the time.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. Joe does not present this as inspirational. He presents it as one of the things he does because the math works. The hard conversations have to happen. The calendar is the mechanism that ensures they do. The practice is not heroic; it is operational.
