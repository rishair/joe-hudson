# VIEW as a Solo Meditation Pointer

## The Practice

VIEW is normally taught as a relational practice — vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder applied to conversations with another person. The same four components are also the most useful meditation instructions Joe knows, applied to one's own experience on the cushion.

Each component becomes a question to bring to the sit:

- **Vulnerability:** Can I be vulnerable with myself while meditating?
- **Impartiality:** Can I be impartial about my agenda in meditation?
- **Empathy:** Can I have empathy for myself in meditation?
- **Wonder:** Can I be in wonder in meditation?

"View. Can you be vulnerable with yourself while you're meditating? Can you be impartial about your agenda in meditation? Can you have empathy for yourself in meditation? Can you be in wonder in meditation? That modality while sitting is just as useful as it is the way we teach in the Connection Course, which is, you know, in relationship." -- Joe Hudson, What Happens When You Wake Up

The relational form and the solitary form are the same modality. The "other" in solitary practice is one's own experience. VIEW around what is arising in the sit is structurally the same operation as VIEW around what another person is presenting in a conversation.

## The Two Additional Pointers

Joe adds two pointers beyond the four-letter framework. Both are versions of the same thing as impartiality, but with different felt textures:

**Non-management.** The job in meditation is not to manage anything. Not to control the breath, not to control the thoughts, not to control the body's sensations. Whatever is happening is allowed to happen.

"To, in meditation, the job is to not manage anything, to not try to control anything, which is impartiality. But I had emphasized that one." -- Joe Hudson, What Happens When You Wake Up

This is the operational form of impartiality at the level of the sit. The agenda being released is the agenda of producing any particular state. See [[meditation-as-welcoming-not-management]] for the broader principle.

**Enjoyment.** What is required for me to enjoy the heck out of this meditation? What is the most enjoyable way to sit still right now?

"What's required if you're going to sit still? What's the most enjoyable way to sit still? Michael, how do you get the most enjoyment from your meditation? Often, and this, I don't think this is something that you want to do all the time whenever you're meditating, but it's a good one to do once a week is to like ask that question. Say, oh, here, I'm going to meditate. What's required for me to enjoy the heck out of this meditational experience? Because typically it's not a management. It is an unwinding of management that allows us to enjoy the moment." -- Joe Hudson, What Happens When You Wake Up

The enjoyment pointer is not for every sit. Joe suggests asking it about once a week, as a recalibration. The question structurally undoes the management orientation that quietly accumulates in regular practice. The way the question lands is to make enjoyment the criterion, which dissolves the should-structure that meditation easily picks up.

## How These Map to Head, Heart, Gut

The six pointers (VIEW plus non-management plus enjoyment) collapse onto the three centers Joe uses elsewhere. See [[head-heart-gut]] and [[three-paths-of-awakening]] for the parallel framework around awakening.

- **Head:** Impartiality and non-management. The instruction is to the thinking center — release the agenda, stop managing.
- **Heart:** Vulnerability and empathy. The instruction is to the feeling center — be vulnerable with yourself, have empathy for yourself.
- **Gut:** Wonder and enjoyment. The instruction is to the body — be in wonder, find the enjoyment.

The mapping was noticed by Brett during the conversation. Joe confirmed it as a clean parallel.

"I like the way that you just kind of went head, heart, gut there. Like view, mindset, non-management, like whatever's happening, let it happen. And then enjoyment. With that, like, safety." -- Joe Hudson, What Happens When You Wake Up

"Yeah, didn't notice that, but that's great. Yeah, that's exactly right." -- Joe Hudson, What Happens When You Wake Up

This means the same practice works each of the three doors of awakening simultaneously. The head pointer works the head channel. The heart pointers work the heart channel. The gut pointers work the gut channel. A practitioner who runs all six is doing one practice that addresses all three centers at once.

## Why This Form of Meditation Avoids the Trap

Joe's primary complaint about how he was taught meditation: it became a form of self-management. He spent years trying to manage himself into a state, even though the teaching was non-management. See [[meditation-as-welcoming-not-management]].

The VIEW form of the instruction structurally prevents the trap. "Can you be vulnerable with yourself?" cannot be done as a management move. Vulnerability is not a state you produce; it is a posture you adopt. Same with impartiality — you cannot manage your way into having no agenda. Same with wonder — wonder is incompatible with the goal-orientation that management requires.

By framing the practice in terms of postures rather than states, the management trap loses its purchase. The practitioner stops trying to produce something and starts orienting toward something. The producing is what makes meditation hell. The orienting is what makes it the beach-and-breeze experience Joe describes after the shift.

## The Suggested Weekly Question

For practitioners with established sits, Joe's recommendation is to ask one specific question about once a week, not at every session:

"What's required for me to enjoy the heck out of this meditational experience?"

The frequency is deliberate. Asking it every sit makes enjoyment itself a project — a new thing to produce, a new agenda. Asking it weekly is a recalibration. It catches the management orientation that quietly accumulates and resets the practice toward its actual point.

The question's structure is interesting. It asks "what is required" — meaning, what conditions need to be in place. The practitioner answers from inside the sit. The answer is usually some form of letting go of something they have been holding. The releasing is the meditation, not the producing of a state.

## When to Use This Practice

- When your meditation has become an obligation or a should.
- When you notice you are managing yourself into states rather than welcoming what is.
- When the practice has lost its enjoyment and feels like a chore.
- When you want to use your meditation to work all three centers (head, heart, gut) at once.
- When you want a structural alternative to state-seeking on the cushion. See [[state-seeking]].

## What This Tends to Produce

The first reported shift: the felt sense of meditation changes from effortful to spacious. The cushion stops being where you go to do work and becomes where you go to be unworked-on.

The second shift: the questions accumulate cross-session insight. Asking "what's required for me to enjoy this sit?" once a week, over months, reveals patterns. The practitioner starts to see what they consistently bring to the cushion that prevents the enjoyment. The patterns become workable in the rest of life.

The third shift: the line between meditation and the rest of life thins. If the same VIEW posture is the meditation, and VIEW is also the relational practice, then there is no real boundary between the cushion and the conversation. The chop-wood-carry-water teaching becomes operationally true. See [[meditation-as-welcoming-not-management]].

## Tone

Joe presents these pointers casually, almost in passing. They are not framed as a separate teaching but as the natural extension of VIEW into another domain. The implication: if VIEW works in relationship, it works in any context where there is an experience to be in relationship with — including the experience of being alone with one's own consciousness.

The enjoyment pointer in particular is delivered with warmth. The "enjoy the heck out of this" phrasing is the signature. Meditation is not supposed to be punishing. The practice that produces awakening is the practice that the practitioner can sustain, and the practice that the practitioner can sustain is the practice that has some pleasure in it.
