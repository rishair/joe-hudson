# Nervous-System Prep Before High-Stakes Performance

## The Practice

Before a known high-stakes moment -- a stage, a board meeting, an investor pitch, a fundraiser, a major presentation -- prepare the nervous system, not just the content. The protocol has three phases and several layers within each.

The phases:

- **Days before.** Nutrition, sleep, stimulant management.
- **The day-of and minutes-before.** Body movement, breathwork, internal permissioning.
- **After.** Reregulation and celebration.

The full practice depends on knowing yourself well enough to know what your nervous system actually needs. The list below is a starting taxonomy; the specific mix is personal.

## Days Before the Performance

Tara's pre-stage protocol:

"If I know I'm taking on something big that's going to make me anxious, I'll cut out coffee, tea, chocolate, all of those stimulants, or cut them down. And adding really nutritious food and bananas, potassium-rich foods, root vegetables, grounding foods. So really taking care of yourself nutrition-wise." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

The principle: do not arrive at the stage with a nervous system that is already partially activated by other inputs. The performance itself will provide all the activation needed. Coffee, sugar, sleep deprivation, alcohol the night before -- each adds activation on top of what the performance will produce. The total often crosses the threshold into freeze.

Substitute inputs that ground the system: potassium-rich foods (bananas), root vegetables, complex carbohydrates, whole proteins, regular sleep. The goal is not to be calm; the goal is to be regulated enough that the performance activation has somewhere to land.

Pair the nutrition with deliberate physical practice: "Also exercising, like sweaty exercise can give a place for all of that, like, ha, ha, ha, to go. So before you're getting on stage, going and having a run or doing something sweaty can be quite useful. Cold showers, doing Wim Hof work and breathwork all are super supportive for it." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

Sweaty exercise discharges baseline activation. Cold showers and Wim Hof breathing build nervous-system capacity -- they train the system to meet activation without freezing. The repeated practice in the days leading up to the performance increases the bandwidth available for the activation that will arrive.

## The Day-Of and Minutes-Before

Two layers in this phase: body and internal voice.

**Body.** Move the held energy out before the performance starts. The classic version: pump the hands over the head, flap the wrists like bird wings, pant the breath, jump. The somatic detail in [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] is the formal version of this. The minimum effective dose Tara describes for the COVID-era executives: two minutes of hand-movement and deep breathing under the table before the Zoom call.

The mechanism: the body is producing activation; the activation needs an outlet; the movement provides the outlet. Without the outlet, the activation locks into freeze. With the outlet, the activation moves and the body returns to a regulated state from which it can re-engage with the moment.

**Internal voice.** Reclaim the right to be on stage. This is the verbal-internal layer that pairs with the body movement.

"I get to be fucking on stage. Like, I get to. Fuck off anyone who says I don't. Fuck off, judgmental thoughts. Fuck off, shame. Fuck off, you know, whoever judged you for being big, bigger than normal. And actually reclaiming a part of yourself that gets to go on stage, that gets to take control of a meeting in big. Like, I fucking get everybody off my fucking back. I get to be big. I get to go on stage. I get to fucking do this thing. Shut up, everyone. Shut up, judgment. Shut up, everyone. Fuck off." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

The function: most of the shame-layer that produces stage fright is internalized judgment from someone in the person's history who could not tolerate them being big. The pre-stage voice work moves that judgment out of the way before the stage. The person who walks on stage carrying every parental and teacher and friend's voice about being too much will perform under that weight. The person who has spent the previous five minutes reclaiming their right to take the space walks on with the weight already shed.

Joe's distillation: "I get to take this space. This is mine."

## After the Performance

The most-skipped phase. The overachiever pattern is to push through and feel relieved it is over. That collapse trains the body that performance is something to survive rather than something to integrate. The next performance is then scarier, because the system has not received the signal that the previous one was actually okay.

"What do I need on the back end? Because as a recovering perfectionist, but also the overachiever type, it's like I will push my way through an experience and just be glad I survived it rather than celebrate the fact that that just happened. That was -- this was great." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Stage Fright

The post-performance protocol has two layers:

**Reregulation.** Whatever the nervous system needs to come down. Tara's: "Mine is always like, go into nature and exercise, like, you know, gentle walk or hike." Other versions: a hot bath, a quiet hour, a meal with someone who is not going to ask about the performance, sleep. The principle: the activation that fueled the performance is still in the body. It needs to be released, not held. The activation-release-rest cycle has to complete -- see [[activation-release-rest-cycle]] -- or the integration window is missed.

**Celebration.** Mark that it happened. Not "I did it without dying" -- actually celebrate. Tara's framing: "And the celebration, that's a really key piece. Like, yeah, did it!" Joe: "Did it!" Tara: "Yeah, woohoo! Well done."

The celebration includes the mistakes. The team-level version is [[celebrate-failures-publicly]] -- celebrating misses alongside wins. The personal version: name what went well and what did not go well, and applaud both. The mistakes were the iteration data. They were what made the performance real. They get celebrated too.

The combined effect: the body learns the loop. Activation -> performance -> release -> celebration -> rest. The next performance starts from a body that knows this loop completes well, not from a body that survived the last one.

## Personalization

The practice list is a taxonomy, not a prescription. Tara: "It seems like everyone has their own tricks and hacks on ground. Finding really what works for you is what I'm hearing in that for the nervous system... Actually trying a bunch of different things to find out what works for you."

Some performers need more body movement; some need more nutrition; some need more verbal permissioning. The protocol is to try the categories, find what reliably regulates your specific nervous system, and build the personal pre-stage routine from those elements. Over time, the routine itself becomes a regulating signal -- the body recognizes "we are in pre-stage mode" and the regulation begins before the specific practices do.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a substitute for the deeper work on stage fright. The protocol regulates the body so the performance can happen; it does not address the underlying shame layer that makes the activation feel like a threat. See [[i-want-to-be-authentic-on-stage]] for the full coaching path.
- It is not "make the fear go away." See [[stage-fright-as-fuel-cell]] -- the activation is the fuel. The protocol regulates the system so the fuel can be used, not so the fuel disappears.
- It is not a one-time fix. The practice has to recur with each performance. The body forgets between performances; the routine has to be re-done.
- It is not a guarantee. A well-prepared body can still freeze if the underlying material is unresolved. The protocol helps; it does not insure.

## When To Use This

- Before any high-stakes performance moment.
- When stage fright has been severe enough to compromise past performances (panic attacks, throwing up, freezing).
- When a person is about to enter a sustained period of high-frequency performance (a tour, a fundraising run, a series of board meetings) and needs a sustainable nervous-system protocol.
- When someone is asking for "how to overcome stage fright" -- this is the body-level half of the answer; the relational half is in [[i-want-to-be-authentic-on-stage]] and [[stage-fright-as-fuel-cell]].
