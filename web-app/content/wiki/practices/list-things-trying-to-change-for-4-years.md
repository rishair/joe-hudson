# List Things You've Been Trying to Change for at Least Four Years (Then Check the Shames)

## The Practice

A two-step writing exercise that makes visible, on a single page, the structural cause of long-running unsuccessful change efforts. The first step produces an inventory of things the person has been trying to change for years. The second step exposes that nearly every item on the list has a should or a shame sitting on top of it.

The diagnostic value is the visibility itself. The person is not told that their shoulds are blocking their change. The person sees, on their own paper, that every item they cannot change has the same signature.

## How to Do It

**Step 1: List everything you've been trying to change about yourself for at least four years.**

Take a piece of paper, a notebook, or your phone. Write down every part of yourself you have been wanting to be different for at least four years. Be exhaustive. Categories that often surface:

- How you eat
- How you exercise
- Who you hang out with
- How nice you are
- How embodied you are
- How proactive you are
- How forthright you are with your opinion
- How disciplined you are
- How you handle conflict
- How you spend your money
- How present you are with people
- How you talk to yourself
- Habits you have been trying to stop (drinking, scrolling, late-night eating, smoking)
- Habits you have been trying to start (meditation, journaling, calling people, going to bed earlier)

Aim for at least ten items. The list should include the obvious things and the smaller things. Both count.

The four-year minimum matters. Things you have been working on for a few months are noise. Things you have been trying to change for years are the signal — they are the items where something structural is holding them in place.

**Step 2: Check off every item that has shame or obligation attached.**

Go through the list. For each item, ask: do I feel a should around this? Do I feel like there's something wrong with me if this doesn't change? Do I feel like I have to or have got to change this?

Mark every item where the answer is yes.

**Step 3: Notice the pattern.**

In almost every case, the pattern is the same: every item — or nearly every item — has the shame or obligation marker. The items that have been resistant to change for years are precisely the items that have a heavy should sitting on them.

This is the diagnostic. The list itself produces it. The person is not being told what to conclude; the person can see it.

## What This Practice Produces

**The exposure of a uniform pattern.** Items the person was treating as unrelated turn out to share the same structural feature: a should on top. The person had been thinking of each item as its own separate failure; the practice shows it is one failure mechanism with many surface forms.

**The reframing of the "why can't I change?" question.** The person had been asking the question as if each item required its own solution. The practice produces the answer that none of them require a better solution; all of them require the same shift — dropping the should that has been doing the locking. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

**The relief of universalization.** It is easier to look at the list when the conclusion is "I have been running the same loop on twelve different things" than when the conclusion is "I am a uniquely defective person who can't change anything." The list reveals the structural rather than personal nature of the stuck.

**The cost made visible.** Looking at the list in aggregate often produces a moment of "I have been trying to change all of these for a decade." The cost of the unsuccessful effort — the years of self-criticism — becomes legible all at once.

## Why It Works

The practice operationalizes the time-scale diagnostic from [[shame-drives-behavior]]: anything you have been trying to change for years and have not changed has shame holding it in place. Knowing this in the abstract is one thing. Seeing your own paper with twelve items, all of them years-old, all of them checked for shame, is another.

The writing externalizes what has been internal. Internally, each item lives in its own context — the eating thing happens at meals, the exercise thing happens in the morning, the scrolling thing happens at night. On paper, they all line up. The lining-up is what reveals the shared structure.

The shame-check in Step 2 makes the diagnostic falsifiable. If the person had an item that they had been trying to change for years without any should around it, that item would falsify the principle. The practice almost never produces such an item. The convergence of the result across all items is what makes the principle vivid.

## When to Reach For This Practice

- A client describes one item they have been trying to change for years and cannot.
- A client says they have "tried everything" and nothing has worked.
- A client is bracing for the next technique that will finally work.
- A client is in [[ive-been-trying-to-change-this-for-years]] and the diagnostic case from [[shame-drives-behavior]] needs an empirical demonstration the client produces themselves.
- A teaching context where the abstract principle needs an embodied form.

The practice is also useful as a session opener: when a client presents a list of things they want to work on, asking them to first list everything they have been trying to change for years can reorient the work upstream of the items.

## The Instruction Format

Joe's own delivery of the practice in the teaching video pauses the person between steps. The instruction is to do step one, pause, then come back for step two — explicitly not to read or hear step two before doing step one.

This matters. If the person knows in advance that the second step is "check the shames," the first step gets filtered through that knowledge and the items that surface are skewed toward the shame-loaded items. The diagnostic loses its power because the list has already been pre-filtered.

When running the practice with a client (live, in a session) or recommending it as homework, preserve the two-step structure. Do not reveal step two before step one is complete.

"Don't listen to the next step before you've done the first step, or it'll destroy the experiment for you." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Change

## What To Do After

The list is the diagnostic. The actual work is what follows from the result.

- For each item: feel the unfelt emotion the should has been managing. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[emotional-inquiry]].
- For the daily form: install [[notice-where-you-shame-yourself-be-gentle]] as an in-the-moment practice.
- For the underlying belief that the shoulds are necessary: address [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] and [[inherent-goodness]].
- For finding what you actually want underneath each item: see [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] and [[wants-over-shoulds]].
- For the items that are addiction-shaped: see [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] and [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]].

The list itself is not the work. The list is the door. The work is what happens once the door is open.

## Connection to Related Practices

This practice has two close siblings, and the distinctions matter for choosing which one to assign:

- [[joes-26-year-old-list]] is Joe's personal story of having done this exact practice (with a slight variation) at age 26. The story is what gets told before or after this practice to show the discovery moment. The practice in this article is the assignable form of the experiment Joe ran on himself.

- [[list-the-shames-you-avoided]] is the time-reversed version. Where this practice asks "what have you been trying to change and not changing?", that practice asks "what shames have stopped you from taking actions you wanted to take?" Both produce the lock-as-mechanism insight. The choice depends on the angle: this one starts from the change effort and finds the shame; the other starts from the shame and finds the avoided action.

For a client in the [[ive-been-trying-to-change-this-for-years]] concern, this practice fits. For a client in stagnation or avoidance with no specific change frame, [[list-the-shames-you-avoided]] often fits better.

## Tone

The practice is delivered in a matter-of-fact, almost clinical tone. The person is being invited to run an experiment, not to receive a moral. The instructions are precise. The pauses are real. The discovery is produced by the data, not by the instructor's claim.

"You might notice how ineffective that feeling of shame and obligation is if so many of the things on this paper also are full of that shame and obligation." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Change

The conditional ("if so many of the things... also are full of that shame") preserves the empirical stance. The person checks the data. The data does the convincing.
