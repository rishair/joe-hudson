# VIEW Around Yourself as an Identity Diagnostic

## The Practice

Pick a topic about yourself. Notice whether you can be in VIEW with that topic -- vulnerable about it, impartial about it, empathetic toward others around it, in wonder about it. If yes, that topic is probably not a strong piece of identity for you. If no, it is.

This inverts the usual question. Instead of asking "what are my identities?" (which produces only the identities you are already conscious of), you ask "where can I not be in VIEW with myself?" The places where VIEW collapses are where identity lives.

"If you're in VIEW around a topic about yourself, there's less of a chance that you're in identity. If it's easy for you to be vulnerable around it, if you're impartial about it, if you have empathy for others around it, if you are in wonder about it, it's far less likely to be a strong part of your identity." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## How to Use It

Pick a self-description. Examples: "I am a good parent." "I am hardworking." "I am vulnerable." "I am the responsible one." "I am not anxious." "I am the egoless one." "I am a successful person."

Then run each component of VIEW (see [[view]]) against the topic:

**Vulnerability.** Can I be vulnerable about this without bracing? Can I admit where I fall short of this self-description, or where I am uncertain about it, or where I am secretly the opposite? If the vulnerability does not flow, the identity is holding.

**Impartiality.** Can I observe this topic about myself without an agenda? Can I be okay with finding evidence that contradicts it? Can I notice it neutrally rather than needing it to come out one way? If I am attached to the answer, the identity is holding.

**Empathy.** Can I have empathy for others who are different on this topic? Can I feel into the person who is not hardworking, not responsible, not vulnerable? If I judge them, distance from them, or feel superior, the identity is holding through contrast.

**Wonder.** Can I be in wonder about this aspect of myself? Can I be genuinely curious about why I am like this, what it serves, what it might become? Or am I closed and certain? If wonder is absent, the identity is holding.

When any component collapses, that is the signal. The collapse itself is the data.

## What This Produces

The most useful output of the diagnostic is the topics where you cannot do it. The collapse is often immediate. Asked to be vulnerable about "I am a good parent," the person finds themselves defending instead. Asked to have empathy for someone who is not hardworking, the person finds themselves judging.

The defense and the judgment are the identity becoming visible. Before the diagnostic, the person might not have known the identity was there. After, it is in view.

The other useful output is locating topics where VIEW flows easily. These are likely not strong identity for the person. They are areas of natural flexibility. This is also data: it shows what is possible in other areas with work.

## Why VIEW Is the Right Probe

VIEW is normally taught as a communication state for being with another person (see [[view]]). Joe extends it here as an inward diagnostic. The logic: VIEW requires a kind of open, undefended contact. Identity is the opposite -- defended, partial, closed, certain. They are mutually exclusive in any given moment. So the presence or absence of VIEW, applied to yourself on a topic, is a clean read on whether identity is in play.

This is also why VIEW practice tenderizes identity over time (see [[transparent-identity-vs-rigid]]). Every time the practice succeeds on a topic where identity was previously holding, the identity has softened a notch. The diagnostic and the practice are the same move.

## What Joe Does With the Result

Once a topic is identified where VIEW collapses, the work begins on that topic specifically. The options:

- Trace the identity to its origin somatically (see [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]])
- Feel the underlying emotion that the identity has been protecting
- Practice vulnerability about the topic with safe others
- Apply VIEW deliberately, noting where it collapses each time, gently expanding the range

The diagnostic tells you where to point the work. It does not do the work itself.

## When to Use

- When a coachee is doing identity work and does not know which identities to address
- When someone is operating from a self-concept that is producing problems but the person cannot see it
- As a self-inquiry tool between sessions
- When a person reports "I don't know what my identity is" -- this gives them a method
- When a person claims to have transcended identity (the diagnostic will reveal where they have not)
- When a coachee has had a breakthrough and is now identifying with the breakthrough

## The Self-Application

The diagnostic can be used by the coach on themselves before sessions. Where can I not be in VIEW about myself today? Those are the places I am holding identity that the coachee will likely encounter as resistance in our work. Loosening them in myself first makes the work cleaner.

## Tone

Casual. Almost playful. The practice does not require ceremony. Pick a topic, notice what happens. The point is the noticing, not a heavy investigation.
