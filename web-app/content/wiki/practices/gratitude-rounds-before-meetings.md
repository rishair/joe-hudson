# Gratitude Rounds Before Meetings

## The Practice

Before a meeting begins, the team goes around in a circle. Each person names three things they are grateful for. The circle goes around three times total, so each person speaks three times, naming three gratitudes each round. The orientation of the gratitudes can be open, or it can be focused: gratitude around the team, gratitude around the problem being worked on, gratitude around resources.

The practice was Joe's standard meeting opener during his venture capital years. It was applied to internal team meetings, not pitch meetings.

"The way we used to do it is that before a meeting, everybody would say 3 things that they were grateful for kind of in a circle. So it'd be like 1, 2, 3. So everybody would go one time, everybody would go a second time." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

## The Critical Requirement: Actually Feel It

The practice fails if people list gratitudes without feeling them. A child reciting "I'm thankful for the food" while waiting to eat is performing the form without the substance. The body has to actually drop into the felt sense of being grateful for the listed thing.

"The most important part of a process like that is you actually have to feel the gratitude. You can't be like a 5-year-old at the table who just wants to eat. You have to be like, really feel into, oh, this is something I'm really grateful for. That's the key part of the process." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

This is what distinguishes the practice from a corporate icebreaker. The icebreaker is satisfied with the social form. The practice requires the somatic reality. A leader who lets the team perform without feeling is running a different practice that produces different results.

## Three Orientations of Gratitude

The practice scales differently depending on what the gratitude is oriented around:

**Open / neutral times:** Gratitude around the team itself. "What are we grateful for around the team so that we could continue to become more and more functional?" This is the maintenance use of the practice, building cohesion when nothing in particular is broken.

**Around the team's functionality:** When the team is generally in a good place, gratitude can be directed at what is working on the team, as fuel for becoming more functional. See [[functional-team]].

**Around a specific problem:** When there is something difficult the team is working through, gratitude can be directed at the difficulty itself. The product is not selling -- gratitude for the customers telling you what is wrong, gratitude for the salespeople surfacing their concerns, gratitude for learning what makes products sell before building the next one. See [[gratitude-around-the-problem]].

The third use is the most counterintuitive and the most powerful. It is also the hardest, because it requires welcoming the difficult thing into the field of appreciation rather than treating it only as a problem to fix.

## Why It Works

Two paths to a solution: (1) see what is wrong and fix it, (2) see what is right and build on it. Humans are evolutionarily biased toward the first. The apple-versus-snake asymmetry means survival required catching the snake; missing the apple was rarely fatal. Intelligence amplifies this bias rather than correcting it.

See [[see-whats-right-to-build-on-it]] for the underlying principle. See [[intelligence-amplifies-self-criticism]] for the negativity bias enhanced by intellect.

Gratitude rounds force the second path into the room. Without the practice, the team will default to the negativity bias and miss most of what is right. The data is incomplete. The decisions made on incomplete data are worse than the decisions made on complete data. The practice is, in effect, a data-collection discipline.

"It's really important in any kind of analysis is to really be able to see the data clearly... in this case, it means to see the positive stuff clearly." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

## What It Produces

When run with the felt requirement intact, the practice produces:

- Team cohesion (the cohesion is a byproduct of mutual visibility into what each person actually values)
- Better decisions, because the data set includes the positive evidence
- Faster solutions to problems, because the resources and assets become visible alongside the deficits
- A different emotional baseline for the meeting that follows

The cohesion claim is specific. The practice is not generic teambuilding. It is data-collection that happens to also be cohesion-building.

## Instructions to a Team Trying This

1. Set up the circle at the start of the meeting. Five to ten minutes for the full three rounds.
2. The first round can be open. The second and third can be oriented toward the team or the problem at hand.
3. Each person names three gratitudes in their turn.
4. The leader names the rule: you have to actually feel the gratitude, not just say it. If you cannot feel it, find one you can feel.
5. The leader models the felt version, not the performed version.
6. If someone is half-assing it, the leader names it. See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]].

## The Three Sub-Instructions That Make the Round Land

Within the gratitude round, three additional rules govern how each individual gratitude is spoken. These rules apply equally to the two-person form (see [[two-person-gratitude-practice]]) and the team-round form.

**Keep each gratitude short. Less than a sentence.** Long sentences accumulate hedges and pull the practitioner into the head. The body-event of gratitude needs short language to coexist with it.

"It's really important to make the gratitude very short, always less than a sentence, definitely not a run-on sentence or a compound sentence. I mean, you might mess up once or twice, it doesn't matter, but generally, you're being as concise as possible." -- Joe Hudson, Gratitude

The mechanism: if you make the gratitude long, you have to think it. If you have to think it, you are not feeling it. The short form prevents this slippage. The leader of the round can name this rule explicitly: "Keep each one short. Less than a sentence."

**Feel the gratitude before you say it.** The body-event of gratitude arrives first. The verbal naming follows. If the order reverses -- words first, hoping the feeling shows up after -- the practice has reverted to recitation.

"The other thing is to feel the gratitude before you say it... if you need to say something to yourself for a while to get yourself into the gratitude or think of something to get yourself into the gratitude, that's fine. But have the visceral sensation of it and then speak the thing you're grateful for." -- Joe Hudson, Gratitude

Internal silent preparation is permitted. The participant can think for a few seconds to find the felt-state before speaking. The thinking just has to happen before, not instead of, the felt-state arriving.

**Savor each gratitude after it is spoken.** Do not immediately move to the next person. Stay in the body sensation for a few breaths. The savoring is what allows the gratitude to register as a sustained body event rather than a passing mental note.

The savoring also applies to listening. When another person speaks their gratitude, the listener savors too. The whole round is a shared savoring exercise punctuated by verbal naming, not a sequence of verbal disclosures.

If these three sub-instructions are not held, the round produces verbal performance rather than felt practice. The benefits are conditional on the sub-instructions.

## Joe Names the Resistance

The expected resistance is anticipated: "I would say the real core as to why people resist gratitude, besides it's strange and awkward at the beginning, is the fact that they're scared that they won't be prepared for the inevitable doom that is always around the corner. If they are happy, if they are content, if they're looking for something good to happen." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

This is [[fear-of-joy]] showing up in a team setting. The practice is not just data-collection; it is exposure to a feared emotional state. For many people, sustained positive attention to what is good is more uncomfortable than sustained attention to what is wrong.

The leader's job is to have empathy and compassion for that resistance, not to override it. "Having empathy and compassion for that perspective is also really important." Naming the resistance as understandable lowers the friction. Pretending the resistance does not exist increases it.

## Introducing It to Skeptics

The team member most likely to resist this practice is also the team member whose intellectual buy-in matters most. The approach is to frame it as an experiment, not as a mandate. See [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]].

"For the next 3 weeks, we're gonna start our meetings this way and we're gonna note how our decision-making changes and if it's effective. If it's ineffective, then we won't do it. Also, because we're being positive, we're not being Pollyannish. I want to hear everything that's going wrong. I just also want to hear everything that's going right." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

The framing makes two distinctions explicit. First: this is a test, not a policy. Second: positive does not mean exclusively positive. The negative data is still welcome. The practice is adding the positive data that was missing, not substituting positive for negative.

## When This Is Used Outside Teams

The same practice works in families. "This works with your husband and wife and kids, and it's just an incredibly useful, really just beneficial thing." -- Joe Hudson. The mechanism is the same: forced visibility of what is good, requiring the felt quality, in a small group that does it together. See [[family-as-team]].

## The Gratitude-Around-the-Problem-in-a-Meeting Variant

A specific use Joe describes for the round: when a meeting is about an intractable problem or when two team members are in conflict, do a gratitude round oriented at the difficulty itself.

"If you have a problem that's intractable or like two people are fighting or something like that, to share a gratitude about what's the gratitude for the opportunity in this situation. So, you can start solving problems. If you have a problem that you're all solving together that feels hard or something like that, you can say, 'Hey, let's just do a gratitude for the problem.' And then, everybody can show gratitude for what is the potential of learning or what they get to do because of this problem. And then, solve the problem. And that changes -- that usually helps solve the problem a lot quicker." -- Joe Hudson, Gratitude

The structure: name the difficulty out loud. Run a round in which each person finds gratitude inside that specific difficulty. Then proceed to the problem-solving.

The effect: the problem-solving that follows starts from a different state than it would have started from. The team is no longer braced against the problem; they are oriented toward what the problem makes possible. The same data is now visible inside a different frame, and the frame is what determines what solutions are available.

This is the team-meeting application of [[gratitude-around-the-problem]]. The pre-meeting round is a special case: instead of running open gratitude, you run targeted gratitude oriented at the specific stuck thing.
