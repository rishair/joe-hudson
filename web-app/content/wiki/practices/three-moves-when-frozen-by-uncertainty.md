# Three Moves When Frozen by Uncertainty

## The Practice

When a person is paralyzed by uncertainty -- in chronic cortisol, unable to think clearly, looping in catastrophic thoughts -- the practice has three moves. The moves are ordered. Each one prepares the system for the next.

The practice answers the specific question Brett asks Joe in the episode: "How can somebody who heard what we were just saying about how do you want to be through the uncertainty and feels completely frozen, or completely dysregulated, what can that person do?"

The three moves are Joe's direct answer.

## Move 1: Face What Is Actually Real

The person who is frozen by uncertainty is almost always frozen by an imagined catastrophic future, not by what is actually here. They have been imagining the disaster for so long that the imagining has become the operative reality. The first move is to face what is actually real, which usually includes the recognition that the catastrophe has not, in fact, arrived.

"The first one is most likely in that incapacity is not facing a reality. So part of the reality you're probably making up is things are going to go horribly wrong. And if you've been in it for years, you've been making that up for quite a long period of time. So you might want to notice that things have not gone horribly wrong." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

The diagnostic: how long has the person been catastrophizing? If they have been predicting disaster for years and the disaster has not arrived in the form they predicted, that is data. The predictive apparatus is not accurate. The accumulated unmaterialized predictions are evidence that the catastrophic future is not the reality.

A specific application: when the catastrophe is something the person did not cause (a diagnosis, a market shift, geopolitics), the first form of facing reality is recognizing that they are not to blame for the situation arriving. See [[fault-vs-responsibility]]. The self-blame piece is often the first thing that has to drop before the other two moves can be done.

"Facing reality is don't blame yourself. A lot of people in the face of uncertainty, their first move is to blame themselves. Why didn't I support so-and-so more? Why didn't I do this? Why didn't I do that? First form of certainty is facing the truth of the situation, which is I'm not to blame for this scenario." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

## Move 2: Feel the Emotional Experience You Are Resisting

The second move surfaces the unfelt emotion underneath the chronic worry. Almost always, the chronic worry is a defense against a specific feeling the person has been refusing: grief about something already lost, fear about something already present, anger about something already happening.

"There's almost guaranteed an emotional experience that you are resisting in this. There's a -- I don't want to be so out of control or uncertainty so much that I have to feel that grief or that fear or that anger. So there's also that component of it typically that's at play." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

The chronic worry takes less energy than the specific feeling, in the short term. It distributes the difficulty across a vague indefinite future rather than asking the system to be in the specific present-moment feeling. But the chronic worry never resolves; the specific feeling does, once it is allowed.

The diagnostic: what is the person not letting themselves feel? Often the answer is the obvious one. The person worrying about cancer for a parent is not letting themselves grieve the parent. The person worrying about the marriage is not letting themselves feel the anger about the partner's behavior. The person worrying about the company is not letting themselves feel the fear about their own competence.

Bringing the feeling in -- gently, with titration appropriate to the person's window -- is the move. The chronic worry usually drops a notch the moment the underlying feeling is actually touched. The full resolution often takes multiple passes, but the first contact with the actual emotion produces an immediate change in the body's relationship to the situation.

This move also includes the connection to depression-and-anger work. The chronic flat state that often accompanies long-term uncertainty paralysis can be the form anger takes when the system is not allowed to feel it. See related material on anger work.

The same patience principle applies. Brett's account of his brother's diagnosis illustrates the titration: there was a week-long retreat to facilitate first. He could not feel everything immediately. He compartmentalized adaptively, then when the retreat ended, he asked his partner to hold him and cried for an hour. The titration was not avoidance; it was matching the feeling to the available container. See [[compartmentalizing-can-be-adaptive]] for the related principle that not-feeling-everything-all-the-time is not failure.

## Move 3: Find the Certainty That Is Actually Here

The third move is the recovery of the actual certainty that the false-certainty-seeking has been blocking. The person has been chasing certainty about an unknowable future. They have been ignoring the certainty that is fully available about the present.

"There is some certainty that you do have and you're not noticing it. Let's look at what's certain. What's certain is right here, right now. Like, you're here, you're safe in this moment. You're with somebody who loves you at this moment. So the brain catastrophizes because we've got too much cortisol going on. Our capacity to problem solve goes away. But if you can focus on the certainty, that often can relieve the system enough so that you can get into regulation." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

What is certain in this moment:

- The person is here.
- The person is safe in this moment (assuming no acute physical danger).
- The person can name someone they love.
- The person can identify how they want to be in this.
- The person has a body, a breath, a chair, a room.

The naming of these is not a positive-thinking exercise. It is the recovery of access to perception that the cortisol had occluded. Once the system has the available certainty in view, the cortisol drops. Once the cortisol drops, the problem-solving capacity comes back online. Once the problem-solving capacity is back, the person can actually respond to whatever the situation is.

See [[the-certainty-that-is-actually-here]] for the underlying principle.

## Why the Order Matters

The three moves must be done in order. Each prepares the system for the next.

If you go straight to Move 3 (find the certainty) without Move 1 (face the unreality of the catastrophic prediction), the certainty-naming feels like denial or platitude. "You're here, you're safe" lands as a deflection rather than as a recognition.

If you go to Move 3 without Move 2 (feel the resisted emotion), the certainty-naming bypasses the underlying feeling. The person reports feeling slightly better but the underlying anxiety returns within hours.

If you go to Move 2 without Move 1, the person can feel afraid of an imagined catastrophe rather than feeling the actual present-moment emotion. The feeling is misdirected and does not resolve.

The order: reality first, then the feeling that was being avoided, then the certainty that is actually available.

## How to Apply Each Move

**For Move 1 (face reality):**

- Ask: "What have you been predicting? How long have you been predicting it? Has it arrived?"
- Ask: "Are you blaming yourself for this situation arriving? Is that accurate?"
- Notice the gap between the predicted catastrophe and the actual present moment.
- Let the gap be the evidence. The system has been wrong about the future for a long time.

**For Move 2 (feel the emotion):**

- Ask: "What are you not letting yourself feel about this?"
- Common answers: grief, fear, anger, helplessness, shame.
- Let the feeling be present in the body for a minute. Do not try to resolve it or analyze it.
- The chronic worry should reduce as the specific feeling is touched. If it does not, more layers of resistance are present.

**For Move 3 (find the certainty):**

- Ask: "What is certain right now? What is here in this moment that you know is here?"
- Walk through the body, the room, the relationships present.
- Let the system register what it has been ignoring.
- The body should shift toward regulation as the actually-certain comes into view.

## The Worked Example: Cancer in the Family

Brett walks through his brother's cancer diagnosis as a worked example of the three moves.

**Move 1 (face reality):** Brett did not cause the cancer. The worrying about cancer in newspapers earlier in life did not cause it. "Cancer happens with or without you." The first form of facing the truth was releasing the self-blame, which made room for the second move.

**Move 2 (feel the emotion):** The longer Brett was in it with his brother, the more he faced his emotional pieces. Moments of "I'm not going to face it because it's going to be fine." Moments of "okay, I gotta hold it together." Then, after the retreat ended, the full grief broke through. The patience -- not demanding to feel it all immediately -- was part of the practice.

**Move 3 (find the certainty):** Brett did not start with the story "I'll never be close because now he's dying." He started with: "There's a way we could have a deeper relationship by the end of your life because of this than otherwise." That was the certainty he could find: that closeness was possible regardless of timeline. "I'm certain I can spend more time with you. I'm certain that I love you. I'm certain that I want to be close to you. I'm certain that this could mean your death." Multiple certainties at once, including the certainty of the loss, alongside the certainties about love and connection.

The three moves in this example were not done in twenty minutes. They were done over weeks and months. The practice can scale to a single conversation or to a long arc of working through a major life event. The structure is the same.

## When This Practice Is Most Useful

- When a person is in chronic cortisol from sustained uncertainty
- When the catastrophic-prediction loop is running and not stopping
- When the person is frozen and cannot access the [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty|how-question]] directly
- When self-blame is loading the situation in addition to the actual uncertainty
- When the person has been "in it" for so long that they have stopped noticing how much they have been bracing
- When [[take-the-adventure-stance]] has been offered and the person cannot access it because they are too dysregulated

## What This Practice Does Not Replace

The practice does not replace [[take-the-adventure-stance]] or [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]]. The practice prepares the system for those moves. Once the practice has restored some regulation, the stance work and the how-question become available.

The practice also does not replace longer-term work on the patterns that have produced the chronic uncertainty-paralysis in the first place. It is a way to get out of the immediate freeze so that the longer work can happen.

## Tone

The practice is delivered quietly and at the pace of the person's regulation. Each move is given time. The pace of the moves matches the pace of the system's recovery, not the pace of the coach's wanting-to-help.

There is also a specific reassurance built into the practice: the catastrophic prediction is not accurate, the underlying feeling can be met, and there is more certainty available than the person currently sees. The tone communicates: "this is not as bad as it has been feeling, and here is how to get back to functional."
