# Chase Your Own Approval

## The Practice

Replace the question "do they approve?" with the question "do I approve?" as the central organizing question of your life. Every decision, every action, every interaction. Where the attention has been running outward to the other person's verdict, redirect it inward to your own.

"If you really take a minute and you say, 'Hey, I'm just gonna chase my own approval,' it's a whole different game. It's a whole different game to say, 'Oh, hey, do I approve of that?' If that's your central question rather than, 'Do they approve of that?' 'Do I approve of that?' 'Does that make me feel good?' Is this how I wanna be? That is a life where you feel very empowered, very grounded, and very able to have the life that you want." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The questions Joe lists are the operational form of the practice:

- **Do I approve of that?** — applied to actions, decisions, ways of being
- **Does that make me feel good?** — the body check, not the conceptual check
- **Is this how I wanna be?** — applied to the way you are showing up, the version of yourself you are running

These are the questions that get asked when the externalizing strategy is reversed. The center of gravity moves from out there to in here. The same scorecard structure that was running on others' approval now runs on the person's own.

## Why This Is Not Self-Centeredness

The reframe is structural, not moral. Approval-seeking from others is not actually generous behavior. It is the externalization of self-approval, which leaves the person unable to give either generously to others or honestly to themselves. The person running on external approval is shaped by others' expected reactions, which means they are not genuinely available to others. The chase makes real contact impossible. See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]].

Chasing your own approval is not selfishness. It is the relocation of the question to where the question can actually be answered. Other people cannot tell you whether you approve of your life — only you can. They can tell you whether they approve, which is information about them. The only useful approver in the matter of your own life is you.

The downstream consequence is paradoxically more, not less, presence with others. The person who has their own approval can listen to others without needing them as the approver. The contact becomes real. See [[wanting-others-to-listen-is-the-block]] for the parallel mechanism.

## Why It Requires the Prerequisite Work

The practice cannot be done by an act of will if the structural prerequisites are not in place. Two prerequisites:

**The capacity to feel the avoided emotion.** Approval-seeking is the strategy a person uses to not feel a specific emotional substrate (small-self, not-enough, shame). Telling that person to chase their own approval without addressing the substrate is like telling them to stand on the floor that is missing. They will fall through. See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] for the structural account.

**The capacity to receive.** A person whose system has been deflecting recognition for years cannot suddenly receive their own. The receiving channel needs to be opened, usually first by practicing receiving from others (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]). Once the channel works in one direction, it can work in both. The self-approval lands the same way an external compliment lands: somatically, fully, without deflection.

This is why Joe puts the practice third in the sequence in the source teaching. First, see how approval-seeking pushes people away (the motivation for stopping). Second, let compliments all the way in (the receiving capacity). Third, feel the emotion underneath thinking someone is better than you (the substrate work). Only then does "chase your own approval" have somewhere to land.

## The Instruction

Asked at any decision point: do I approve of this? Does this feel good in my body? Is this how I want to be?

The answers are not always immediately available. The mind that has been running on external approval has trained itself to not ask the internal question. The answers may have to be waited for. The body may have to be allowed to register what it is registering.

The practice is also not all-or-nothing. The instruction is to make the do-I-approve question the *central* one, not the only one. Other people's perspectives, feedback, and care still matter. They are inputs. But they are not the verdict. The verdict comes from inside.

When you notice yourself asking "do they approve?" — that itself is the cue. Use the cue to flip the question. The flip is the practice. Repeated, it rewires which question runs by default.

## What the Result Feels Like

Joe names three downstream qualities of a life organized around self-approval rather than external approval:

- **Empowered.** The center of decision is no longer outside the self, which means decisions can actually be made rather than negotiated around.
- **Grounded.** The body has a place to be (in itself) rather than being constantly cast outward, which produces the felt sense of having a home.
- **Able to have the life that you want.** The life you actually want can only be located by you. The externalized strategy was always producing the life others would approve of, which is not the same thing as the life you want.

The phrase "have the life that you want" is operative. The approval-seeker has typically built a life optimized for others' approval and is unable to locate what their own want actually is. The self-approval practice surfaces wants that have been suppressed. Those wants then become the basis of an actually-wanted life.

## What Joe Does Not Mean

The practice is not:

- A new performance. "Look at me, I'm chasing my own approval now" is the same approval-seeking move with a new costume. The practice happens in private, in the body, in the moment of choosing. The visibility of it is irrelevant.
- A justification. The do-I-approve question is not "I approve of this so I do it whether or not it hurts anyone." Real self-approval includes the integrity of considering impact. The practice is not narcissism. See [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the misuse.
- A rejection of feedback. Others may have useful information about your blind spots. Receiving feedback is different from running on their approval. The practice keeps the verdict yours while leaving the input available.
- A withdrawal from relationship. The person doing the practice is more available to others, not less. The hunger that made every interaction an extraction is no longer running.

The misuses tend to install the same approval-seeking with a different surface. The genuine practice is quieter. It is the move of asking the question inward and letting the answer come from inside.

## How to Begin

Start with one decision per day. Pause before it. Ask: do I approve of this? Does this make me feel good in my body? Is this how I want to be? Wait for the answer. Act on the answer.

When you notice the externalizing question running — when you catch yourself asking "what will they think?" — flip it. Ask what you think. The flip itself is the practice, regardless of how clear the answer is.

When a compliment arrives, receive it (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]). When an emotion arrives that you would have used approval-seeking to avoid, feel it for a few seconds (see [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]]). When a decision needs to be made, ask the inverted question.

The practice compounds. Each successful flip makes the next one easier. Eventually the do-I-approve question runs as the default and the do-they-approve question becomes the noticeable exception rather than the operating system.

## The Thousand-People-Beautiful Test (Why External Approval Cannot Substitute)

A crisp formulation of why the practice is structurally necessary, not just preferable: external approval cannot do the job that self-approval does, because the same identity that rejects internal self-approval will reject external approval through the same filter.

"A thousand people can call you beautiful, but if you think you're ugly, you're gonna call them all a liar and you're never gonna let it in. You're never gonna get to actually see your own beauty." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Most Want

The principle is structural: the substrate that determines whether approval lands is the self-approval, not the supply of external approval. Quantity does not solve a digestion problem. If the internal verdict is "I'm ugly," every external "you're beautiful" gets filtered through that verdict and reclassified as a lie (see [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]]). The deflection mechanism is the same whether the approval comes from one person or a thousand.

This is the reason the practice is the operational form of self-approval, not a supplementary one. The person hoping to gather enough external approval to finally believe they are okay is running a strategy that cannot succeed because the gathering itself is structurally limited by the very deficit it is trying to fix. The internal "I approve" is the only verdict that can actually land — and it can land only because the same identity that filters external evidence does not filter the verdict that comes from inside.

The deeper layer: "Underneath all of this deep wanting for appreciation is this deep wanting for your own appreciation, for seeing yourself as you truly are, to see what is beautiful about you, what is intelligent about you. That's the deep want." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Most Want

The wanting that the external chase has been pointing at has been the internal want all along. The chase is the misdirection. The relocation of the wanting to its actual target — your own approval of yourself — is what makes the want finally fulfillable. The fulfillment was structurally unavailable through the external route because the want was for a verdict only the self can issue.

For the structural reason why other-given recognition cannot increase self-esteem the way demonstrated competence can, see [[compliments-dont-build-self-esteem-competence-does]].

## The Present-Moment Variant: "Be Good With Yourself Right Now"

A specific in-the-moment instantiation of the practice that bypasses the cognitive question and goes directly to the felt-sense. The question shifts from "do I approve?" to "what do I need to do to be good with myself right now?" The two questions are doing the same work — relocating the verdict to the self — but the present-moment variant has a different on-ramp.

See [[what-do-you-need-to-be-good-with-yourself-right-now]] for the full treatment. The variant is included here because it is the operational form of the practice that requires no prior conceptual setup. A person can be asked the present-moment question without having heard the principle of self-approval. The felt-sense answer arrives directly, and the principle can be installed retroactively from the felt-sense.

The structural observation that authorizes this variant: the feeling chased through external approval is identical to the feeling produced by being-good-with-self-right-now. See [[what-you-seek-from-approval-is-self-presence]] for the principle. The "right now" constraint in the question is what closes the future-and-past routes the approval-mind would normally take, and the closing of those routes is what reveals the always-available internal substrate.

The variant is particularly useful for:

- People who get stuck in the conceptual "do I approve?" question (they can't quite tell what "approve" means in the abstract — the present-moment question is more concrete)
- People who are mid-chase and need an immediate re-grounding (the present-moment question can be asked silently in a tense moment in a way "do I approve?" cannot)
- People who have done the conceptual work and need the somatic reinforcement
- People who have not yet been introduced to the practice at all (the present-moment question is the gentlest possible entry)

The two forms can be alternated. The conceptual question is useful when there is time to consider. The present-moment question is useful when the chase is firing and there is no time for consideration.

## Connection to Speaking Your Truth

The do-I-approve question can be asked silently as a check at the threshold of any decision. But it has a more potent operational form: speaking your truth out loud. See [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] and [[speak-truth-twice-a-day]].

The mechanism: when you speak the perception that is actually true for you, the act of voicing it is itself the act of approving it. Saying it is treating it as valuable enough to leave your mouth. The speaking is the self-approval, made operational. The silent "do I approve?" question becomes the louder "I approve enough to say this."

The two practices are paired. The silent question is the inner discrimination ("is this how I want to be?"). The speaking is the outer enactment ("yes, this — said aloud, in the room, to the people present"). Both are the same self-approval mechanism in two forms. Combined, they install the structural shift faster than either alone.

The downstream signature when both practices are running: the felt sense of speaking shifts. The speech starts to feel less like self-expression and more like [[channeling-quality-of-truth]]. The "do I approve?" question stops needing to be asked because the speech is already self-approving by the time it is spoken.

## Connection to Self-Trust

This practice is also one of the practical paths into self-trust. See [[how-do-i-trust-myself]]. The person who is chasing others' approval cannot trust themselves because they have never let their own verdict carry weight. The practice gives the inner verdict weight. With repetition, the inner verdict becomes reliable enough to trust. Self-trust is the downstream of asking and acting on one's own approval over and over.

The substrate scorecard described in [[i-need-a-scorecard]] also relocates here. The scorecard that cannot be taken away is self-approval. The practice is the path to that scorecard.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a quiet conviction. The phrase "it's a whole different game" is the tone marker — said with the slight relief of someone describing a way out. He is not pitching the practice. He is naming what changes when the inversion is made.

The delivery is also slightly hopeful. The promise of the practice is not modest. A life that is "empowered, grounded, and able to have the life that you want" is the full payoff. Joe says this as the obvious downstream of the inversion, not as an aspiration. The inversion produces the life. The work is just to make the inversion.
