# The Five Triggers Projection Experiment (Map, Find the Projection, Apologize Upright)

## The Practice

A three-step experiment designed to systematically convert triggers into self-knowledge and freedom. The structure is simple. The work happens in the second step.

1. **Write down five ways you got triggered by somebody** (recent triggers, specific enough to name what they did and what you felt).
2. **For each one, see how the projection worked**: what is it that you got triggered by that you also have and that you cannot fully accept and love in yourself? Write that next to each trigger.
3. **Apologize to each of the five people in an upright way** — not from shame, just clean acknowledgment of your part. See [[upright-apology]].

"The first thing that you're going to do is write down 5 ways that you got triggered by somebody. And then the next thing you're going to do is see how the projection worked in each of them. What is it that you got triggered by that you also have and that you can't fully accept and love in yourself? And then the third thing you're going to do is apologize to the person who you're triggered by, and you're going to do it in an upright way." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

## Why Five

The number is not arbitrary. One trigger can be a fluke. Two can look like two unrelated incidents. By the fifth, the pattern is unmistakable: the same kind of trait shows up in the projections, or the same posture toward unloved parts of self repeats. The list is what makes the structure visible. A single trigger worked is useful. A list of five reveals the operating system.

The list also defeats the "this one is different" defense. When five disparate triggers all turn out to be the same projection, the projection becomes hard to deny.

## Why Apologize (And Why Upright)

This is the step most people would skip. The apology is what closes the loop. The recognition without the action is intellectual. The apology delivered with shame would make the trigger worse. The upright apology is the form that does the work.

"You're going to apologize to those people, and then all of a sudden you'll see all of those times you're triggered, it's just going to become less and less and less, and you'll be more and more at peace and loving and accepting of yourself and others." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The mechanism: the trigger was your nervous system insisting that the other person was the problem. The apology is your nervous system updating to the truth that you also held the thing. The body recalibrates around the new information, and the trigger threshold rises.

The form of the apology matters. Joe links explicitly to the [[upright-apology]] teaching and warns specifically: "If you do it with shame, it'll just make it worse." A shame apology reinforces the underlying configuration that produced the trigger in the first place. An upright apology dissolves it.

## What the Practice Surfaces

For each trigger, the practice surfaces:

- **The specific trait you have projected.** Named in your own words.
- **Your relationship to that trait in yourself.** Not the trait itself — the rejection of it.
- **The opportunity to take ownership.** The apology is the action that finishes the recognition.
- **The cumulative pattern across five.** The same trait or the same posture often recurs.

The practice does not require you to love every trait at the end. It requires you to see the projection clearly enough to apologize from upright. The loving usually follows from there. See [[love-the-aspect-through-someone-else]] and [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]] for the moves that help the loving land.

## The "Go Down One Level" Caveat

Sometimes the projection is not obvious on the surface. The person who is triggered by a liar has "actually been honest" — they never told a lie. The practice does not stop there. Go down one level. Have you been transparent? Have you shared the important things? If not, that is a form of lying. See [[go-down-one-level-to-find-projection]] for the full move.

If you cannot find the projection at all, the practice has not failed. It has surfaced a trait you have made invisible to yourself. Sit with the trigger until the level you can see at deepens.

## What This Is Not

This is not a way to dismiss the other person's behavior. They might have lied. They might be an asshole. They might be ignorant. The practice does not require them to not be those things. It requires you to recognize that your own trigger has its own source.

"They might have lied and it might not be okay. And I'm not saying that you should excuse it, but I'm talking about your internal trigger. I'm talking about the fact that you are controlled by their actions to the point of getting frustrated. I'm talking about the fact that you're no longer able to have an open heart with them." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The practice is about your freedom, not their behavior. The boundary you draw, the action you take, the leaving you do — all of that can still happen. The practice addresses what is happening inside you regardless of what you choose to do about them.

## What This Is Not (II): Not an Extraction

The apology is yours to give. It is not extracted from the other person. It is not given to extract one from them. If the practice becomes a way to get the other person to apologize back, the upright dimension has been lost. See [[dont-extract-apologies]].

The cleanest form of the practice: you do not even need to expect anything from the other person in response. You apologize, you receive whatever response comes, and the loop closes on your side. What they do is theirs.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Doing the first two steps and skipping the third.** This is the most common failure mode. The recognition without the apology stays intellectual. The apology is what installs the new configuration in the body. See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]].
- **Apologizing from shame.** Joe is explicit: "If you do it with shame, it'll just make it worse." If the body cannot find upright in the apology, do the [[love-the-aspect-through-someone-else]] or [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]] work first to reduce the shame, then apologize.
- **Treating the projection as the verdict.** The fact that you are also dishonest does not mean the other person was not dishonest. Holding both is the work.
- **Performing the practice without sitting with the recognition.** Filling in the worksheet quickly. The practice works when each line is genuinely worked.
- **Skipping the practice because "I already know all my projections."** The list is what reveals patterns the discrete recognitions miss.

## When To Assign This

- A person describes recurring frustration with multiple people in their life
- A person has been working with triggers conceptually but has not made the move to apology
- A person wants a structured practice to apply the [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] principle
- A person reports being triggered "all the time" and needs a way to convert the triggers into useful material
- A person has just learned the projection framework and asks "what do I do with this"
- After [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] has surfaced more triggers than the person knew they had

## When Someone Experiences This

- The list of five looks repetitive on first reading
- The projections turn out to be uncomfortably similar across the five
- Strong resistance to the apology step (this is the diagnostic that the apology is the work)
- A specific physical lightness after each upright apology
- Noticing later in the week that things that would have triggered you do not anymore
- The discovery that what you most accused others of is what you most do

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person has learned [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] and wants to apply it
- A person is doing the [[projections]] work and asks for a practice

- A person presents [[i-keep-running-into-the-same-wall]] with relationships

## Tone

Joe presents this with practical confidence, almost as a recipe. The video frame is teaching, not coaching, so the tone is instructional. The repeated phrase "test it out for yourself" carries the spirit: this is a hypothesis you can verify, not a doctrine to accept. The "and it's a little bit scary, but that's what makes it work so well" is the only acknowledgment that the third step is the hard one.
