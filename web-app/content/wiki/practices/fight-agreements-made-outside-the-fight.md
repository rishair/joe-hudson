# Make Fight Agreements Outside the Fight

## The Practice

Sit down with your partner at a time when you are not fighting. Ask each other: how do we want to fight together? Make explicit agreements about how conflict will be conducted between you. Write them down. Use them.

"One of the biggest tricks for fighting well is just making some agreements before you fight. And that doesn't mean like, oh, we're about to get in a fight now, let me — how are we going to do this? That doesn't work because by the time you're getting in a fight, your nervous system is already wild. So this is about like sometime when you're not fighting, sit down and say, how do we want to fight together?" -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The practice has a specific timing requirement: the agreements have to be made when both nervous systems are calm. Mid-fight agreement-making does not work; see [[cant-make-agreements-during-the-fight]] for why.

## Purpose

Fight agreements do three things at once:

1. **They lower the cost of conflict.** Specific moves that would otherwise escalate (threatening divorce, using a particular word, raising volume) are taken off the table by mutual consent.

2. **They install repair instruments.** Specific phrases or actions get pre-loaded as moves either partner can reach for mid-fight to interrupt a trauma loop (see [[repair-phrase-as-trauma-deactivator]]).

3. **They make conflict less shameful.** Having a structure for how you fight reduces the meta-anxiety that fighting itself is a failure. The fight becomes a known process the couple has prepared for, not an emergency.

## How It Works

The conversation is open-ended. The couple decides what they want to agree to. There is no prescribed list. The agreements have to be co-created (see [[agreements-must-be-co-created-not-prescribed]]).

Categories of agreements that tend to show up:

**Words and threats off-limits.** Specific words you do not say to each other. Specific threats you do not make. The most common is the agreement not to threaten divorce or ending the relationship as a fight move.

"You can literally have agreements of like the words that you're not going to say, and you're not going to make threats, you're not going to threaten divorce." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

**Repair phrases.** A specific sentence one partner says when they notice the other is in a particular trauma pattern, designed to land as recognition rather than confrontation. Joe gives the example of Tara saying "I see how much you care" when Joe gets into rushing mode, which dissolves his urge to control her speed.

**Body and presence agreements.** Specific physical moves the couple has agreed to make during conflict. The most striking example: getting naked.

"They said at one point our agreement was that whenever we got in a fight, we get naked. So like, if we're gonna fight, we're gonna be completely unclothed and be completely vulnerable. And they said it totally changed the way they fought, and it changed the way Tara and I fought too." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

**Trauma-naming agreements.** When somebody says "I'm in my trauma right now," the other one goes over and touches them. See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] for the more developed move; this practice operationalizes it as a pre-agreed response.

**Emotion-with-emotion agreements.** When somebody is crying, you don't try to stop them, you don't try to fix it, you just be with them in their tears. When somebody is angry and it feels like abuse, you say "hey, I need that to stop, can you please go into a different room?" -- and they go into a different room, get angry, and come back.

**Visibility agreements.** Whether to hide your fights from others or not. The friends who taught Joe and Tara this practice "didn't hide their fight from us. They weren't going to go into the shame. They weren't going to say fighting was shameful. And they could do that because they weren't abusing each other, right? They weren't trying to control each other. They were just having a conflict."

## The Originating Story

Joe learned this from friends in Colorado. He and Tara saw the friends get into a fight, and in the middle of the fight, one of them said, "Oh, yeah, hey, we agreed not to do that. We agreed not to use that word." The other one said, "Oh, yeah, sorry." And then they continued the conflict. The fight ended in a short period of time.

"Tara and I looked at each other like, 'What the fuck just happened? That's not how you fight.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

When they asked the friends about it after, the friends said: "We have an agreement. We have our agreements on how we fight." They had literally written them down on a piece of paper.

The visible-fight piece matters. The friends were not hiding the fight. They were not abusing each other. They were just having a conflict, in front of guests, and using their pre-agreed structure to navigate it. The whole thing modeled what it looks like to fight without shame and without harm.

## Write Them Down

Joe notes that he and Tara never wrote theirs down, but that this would probably be a good idea, because the agreements get forgotten.

"They literally had a piece of paper, which Tara and I never have done, and that would probably be a good idea to have them written down because I think we forget them often." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The paper is not the magic. The paper is the prosthetic for the memory. Without it, the agreements drift and you stop being able to point at them.

## The Iteration Rule

Your first set of agreements will not be great. The practice is to make them, use them, learn from where they fail, and remake them. This is the same principle as [[agreements-renegotiate-as-you-change]] applied to the specific case of fight agreements.

"Your first set of agreements are not going to be great. You're going to learn some shit and you're going to have another set of agreements. You're going to have to go back to that set of agreements over and over again." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The expectation that the first set is not the final set is built into the practice. The practice is iterative. The agreements are working documents.

## The Don't-Be-Rigid Rule

Two paired cautions on how the agreements get used in practice:

**Don't expect to live up to all the agreements on your first fight.** The agreements are aspirational structures. The first fight after making them will probably violate several of them. This is normal.

**Don't use that as an excuse not to try.** "Don't make it an excuse not to — it's our first fight, so I haven't — you really want to try to maintain the agreements." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The two cautions sit together: be forgiving about the imperfection of the first uses, and be serious about reaching for the agreements anyway. The agreements are not a moral test; they are a tool the couple is trying to learn to use.

## The Meta-Fight Trap

A specific failure mode: you violate an agreement in a fight, and then the fight becomes about the violation of the agreement, instead of about the original content. See [[dont-fight-about-breaking-the-fight-agreement]].

"It's really important to be able to say, that's not our agreement, and be able to stop and say, okay, yep, that's true, we agreed to this. And it's also important that those agreements are actually something that are creative and make you both feel good and that you both actually agree to." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The clean shape: one partner notices a violation, names it as a reminder, the other partner acknowledges, both continue the conflict. The unclean shape: the noticing becomes a new line of attack, the acknowledgement gets defended against, and now there are two fights instead of one.

## The Co-Creation Requirement

The agreements have to be both partners' agreements. Not one partner's prescription for how the other should fight. Not Joe's recommended list. Not therapist-prescribed rules.

"It's also important that those agreements are actually something that are creative and make you both feel good and that you both actually agree to. So it's not like a cajoling, like, okay, so this is how we're going to fight, right? Good. It's literally like, oh, when you do this, I can say this." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The shape that works is collaborative discovery: oh, when you do this, I can say this. Each agreement emerges from a specific dynamic between the two of them and contains a specific move that helps with that specific dynamic. See [[agreements-must-be-co-created-not-prescribed]] for the principle underneath this.

## Example: The "I See How Much You Care" Agreement

The most specific example Joe gives from his own marriage:

"If I feel like in a rush and that's creating conflict, she knows, like, our rules are — she just says, 'I see how much you care.' And it totally just makes me go soft. Because I'm in a rush because of the way that I was raised, not because of what the fuck is going on right now, right? It's my trauma, it's not the real thing. And she knows that that one thing, that she's just acknowledging my care, can totally disintegrate that rush for me and disintegrate my urge to try to control her to speed up." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The structure of this agreement: Tara recognized a pattern in Joe (rushing from old trauma material). She and Joe co-created a phrase she can use when she sees it firing. The phrase is acknowledgement-shaped: she names what is actually true under the rush (he cares), which dissolves the rush and the controlling behavior that comes with it. See [[repair-phrase-as-trauma-deactivator]] for the general move this exemplifies.

## The Generative Question for Building These Agreements

When sitting down to figure out what specific agreements to make, the central question that surfaces the right material is:

"What's the thing that you want most that you never got as a kid?" -- Joe Hudson, Unlocking your trauma pattern

The question routes directly under whatever recurring pattern keeps showing up in the fights. Whatever each partner names as the answer becomes the underneath that the agreement is built to address. If the answer is "to be told my care is seen," the agreement loads a phrase like "I see how much you care." If the answer is "to be told I'm right," the agreement loads some form of "you're right." If the answer is "to be physically held when I'm scared, not fixed," the agreement loads a specific kind of embrace. The question is the on-ramp to building the specific agreements.

The corresponding in-the-fight question, which the noticing partner can ask the trauma-firing partner, is: "What is the thing that I can say to you right now?" or "What do you most want in that moment?" These mid-fight questions only work if both partners have built enough trust and language for the trauma-firing partner to be able to answer in the heat. Without that prior work, the answer in the moment is usually not available; the partner is too activated to surface it. The outside-the-fight version of the question is what makes the in-the-fight version usable later.

## Examples of Agreement Shapes

Drawn from Joe's description, examples of the kinds of agreements couples can make:

- Specific words that are off-limits in conflict
- No threatening to end the relationship as a fight move
- No threatening divorce
- Get naked when fighting (a specific couple's choice — not a recommendation)
- When somebody says "I'm in my trauma right now," the other one goes over and touches them
- When somebody is crying, you don't try to stop them, you don't fix it, you be with them in their tears
- When somebody's anger feels like abuse, the receiving partner says "I need that to stop, please go into a different room" — the angry partner goes, gets angry there, comes back

The list is illustrative, not prescriptive. Each couple builds their own.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is twofold:

**Decision-making is offloaded from the activated state.** When two nervous systems are calm, they can think about what they want. When two nervous systems are activated, they cannot. By making the agreements when calm, the activated state inherits the calm state's decisions. The activated state does not have to figure out what to do; the calm state already figured it out.

**Pre-agreed moves bypass the trauma loop.** When Tara says "I see how much you care," Joe's rush dissolves not because the words are magic but because both Joe and Tara have already agreed that this phrase carries a specific meaning. The agreement turns a phrase into an instrument. Without the agreement, the same phrase might land as condescension or appeasement; with it, it lands as the recognition Joe's trauma was reaching for.

## The Safe Playing Field

The function of the agreements, at the highest level, is to create a safe playing field inside which the disagreement can become transformative work rather than damage.

"That was like the first and most important step in turning our fights into something that became healing, that became evolutionary for us, that became transformative, that helped us become better people, is just like making it a safe playing field. And so that is absolutely critical." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

The playing-field metaphor matters. Inside the marked field, the partners can play hard — push back, name difficult truths, get angry, cry, demand, retreat, return. The play is the work. The work cannot happen if the field is not safe. The field is safe because the lines are held by the agreements. See [[disagreement-that-heals-vs-retraumatizes]] for the distinction the lines protect, and [[nervous-system-codes-threats-as-real]] for why the lines are mechanical rather than aspirational.

The "safe playing field" framing also explains the temporal positioning of the practice in the [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]]: making the agreements is "the first and most important step" that opens the door to the later stages. Without the safe playing field, the trauma-healing fights cannot happen because each one risks adding fresh trauma on top of the old. With it, the medium becomes available.

## The Boundary As Enforcement of the Agreement

When an agreement is being violated mid-fight and the reminder-and-acknowledge move from [[dont-fight-about-breaking-the-fight-agreement]] is not landing — the violating partner cannot stop, the fight is heading past the line — the move is to enforce the agreement by leaving.

"Holding the boundary of saying like, we're not doing that in a fight, or if you're doing that, I'm going to leave. I'll be back in 20 minutes, or I'll be back when you call me and tell me you're ready to do this without threatening me, without insulting me. Holding that boundary is incredibly important, or you're going to start going in the wrong direction. It's going to get re-traumatizing instead of healing of the trauma that the fight is eliciting in you." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

The shape:
1. Name the line: "we're not doing that."
2. State the consequence: "if you're doing that, I'm going to leave."
3. Leave with a timeline ("I'll be back in 20 minutes") OR with conditions of return that re-establish the in-the-lines requirement ("I'll be back when you call me and tell me you're ready to do this without threatening me, without insulting me").
4. The fight resumes only on the healing side of the line.

The conditional-return form is the more rigorous version when a timeline alone is insufficient — when 20 minutes might not be long enough for the violating partner to come back inside the lines. The condition replaces the time: the return happens when the partner can demonstrate they can be inside the lines again.

The leaving is not punishment. It is the only available way to keep the body of either partner from being given more material to code. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for why the timeline or condition matters and how the leaving needs to be delivered to avoid landing as abandonment.

This is the moment in the practice where the agreement stops being a piece of paper on the kitchen counter and starts being an instrument that one partner can use to protect both of them from the fight going past the line. The agreement was made when both nervous systems were calm; the boundary is the calm-state's pre-installed move that the activated-state can execute.

## When To Make Them

The practice is recursive. Make a first set when calm. Use them. Notice what worked, what did not work, what new patterns surfaced. Make a second set. Use them. Continue.

The triggers for sitting down to remake them include:
- A fight where the existing agreements failed in a specific way
- A new pattern showing up between the two of you that the current agreements do not address
- A life change (new child, new job, illness, move) that has shifted what either of you needs
- The recognition that one of you has changed enough that an old agreement no longer fits (see [[agreements-renegotiate-as-you-change]])

## What This Is Not

This is not a technique for couples in abusive relationships. The friends who taught Joe this practice could do it "because they weren't abusing each other... they weren't trying to control each other." See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the boundary. Fight agreements presume two people both willing to fight well; they do not work as a tool to restrain an abusive partner.

This is not a substitute for the in-the-moment work. The pre-agreements are scaffolding. The actual fighting still requires the partners to do [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]], [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]], and the other in-the-fight moves. The agreements lower the friction; they do not replace the work.

This is not a moral framework. The agreements are practical tools. There is no right or wrong agreement. The only test is whether it works for both of you.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The relief of finding that conflict can have a known shape rather than being formless and frightening
- The discovery that a specific phrase from a partner can completely change the trajectory of a fight
- The recognition that the agreements made early in the relationship no longer fit
- The shame about fighting starting to lift once the fights have a productive container
- The first fight where one partner names a violated agreement and the other partner cleanly acknowledges, and the fight just continues

## Tone

Joe describes the practice with light enthusiasm, the way you talk about a thing that surprised you with how well it worked. The Colorado-friends story is told with comedic shock — "what the fuck just happened? That's not how you fight." The "get naked" example is delivered with humor at its boldness. The underlying message is that fight agreements are not a heavy therapeutic intervention; they are a practical, playful, creative thing two people who love each other can do together.
