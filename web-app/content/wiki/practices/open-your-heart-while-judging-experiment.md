# Open Your Heart While Thinking You're Better Than Someone (The Incompatibility Demonstration)

## The Practice

A live, immediate experiment that takes less than a minute. It demonstrates that superiority and an open heart are structurally incompatible.

**Step 1: Pick a specific person you think you are better than.** Joe is direct that you know the person: "Think you're better than somebody, or pick that person that you think you're better than, you know the person, right?" The body has someone available. The instruction is to actually bring them to mind, not to think about superiority abstractly.

**Step 2: While holding that person in mind with the superiority intact, try to open your heart.** Try to feel the warmth, openness, expansion, or contact that an open heart produces. Try to do both at the same time.

**Step 3: Notice what happens.**

Almost universally, the heart cannot open while superiority is being held. The two states require contradictory configurations of the body. The defense required to maintain superiority is the same defense that closes the heart. You cannot do both at the same time.

"Literally, you can just do the experiment right now. Think you're better than somebody, or pick that person that you think you're better than, you know the person, right? And then open your heart and see if you can do both at the same time." -- Joe Hudson, How NOT To Be Happy

## What It Demonstrates

The principle the experiment proves: thinking you are better than someone requires constant defense, and defense closes the heart. The unhappiness produced by superiority is not a moral consequence; it is a structural one. You cannot be defended and open at the same time. The closed heart is the felt cost of the superiority position.

This sits inside a larger teaching about why the better-than position keeps people unhappy:

"To feel like you're better than other people, you have to judge them. And if you judge them, every single time we have that judgment, we're cutting off another feeling. So it's also an emotional repression. So the last reason that thinking that you're better than other people keeps you unhappy is it requires you to constantly be defensive. You have to constantly have a defense up to prove that you're better than other people. And so it closes your heart." -- Joe Hudson, How NOT To Be Happy

The experiment is the felt verification of this argument. The listener does not have to take Joe's word for it. The body shows them in real time.

## The Mechanism

Two configurations of the body are at play.

**The superiority configuration.** Some muscular and energetic posture is required to hold "I am better." There is a slight lifting up, a slight pulling back, a slight closure around the chest. The defense is present even when no overt argument is happening. The body is held against contact with the other person as a peer.

**The open-heart configuration.** Some muscular and energetic relaxation is required to open the chest, soften the front body, and allow contact. The defense is dropped. The contact with the other person is allowed to land.

The two configurations contradict at the muscular level. The body cannot lift up and drop down at the same time. It cannot close and open at the same time. The defense cannot be both up and down.

When the instruction "open your heart" arrives while the superiority is being held, one of two things happens:

1. The superiority drops, and the heart opens. The person feels the contact. The defense was always optional.
2. The superiority stays, and the heart will not open. The person feels the impossibility of the both-and. The defense is felt as the obstacle.

Either result teaches the principle. The first shows that superiority is droppable. The second shows that superiority is a cost.

## Why This Lands Faster Than Argument

Most people, when told that judging others closes their heart, can intellectually agree without changing anything. The agreement is at the cognitive layer. The behavior continues.

The experiment converts the cognitive claim into a felt event. The body's inability to perform the both-and demonstration is direct evidence. The person cannot argue with what they just failed to do. The proof is in their own muscles.

This is structurally similar to [[try-to-feel-scared-then-stop-experiment]]: the in-the-moment somatic demonstration produces conviction that intellectual agreement does not produce. The body teaches what the mind cannot.

## When to Use This

- Inside [[5-ways-to-stay-unhappy]] when delivering the fifth point (thinking you are better than others).
- When a person is judging another person and presenting the judgment as insight or discernment. The experiment punctures the "I'm just seeing clearly" frame by showing what the seeing is costing.
- When someone has the spiritual version of superiority -- "I'm more awakened than they are," "they have so many limiting beliefs" -- and the position is producing isolation. See [[dont-identify-as-awakened]] and [[using-limiting-belief-frame-to-judge-others]].
- When a person reports a closed heart, a loss of contact with a specific person, and is not yet seeing the role of their own judgment in producing the closure.
- When [[comparative-mind]] has been named and the person needs a felt experience of the cost.

## Variations

The experiment also works with the inverse position:

- **Worse than.** Pick a person you think you are worse than. Try to open your heart. Notice what happens. The result is often the same: defense closes the heart even when the defense is shame-based rather than pride-based. See [[comparative-mind]] for the teaching that all comparative positions (better than, worse than, equal to) produce the same closure.
- **Specific judgment.** Pick a specific judgment you are holding about a specific person ("she is so self-centered," "he is so weak," "they are so stupid"). Try to open your heart toward that person. Notice what happens. The specific version is often sharper than the generic "better than" framing because the judgment is felt.

## Anti-Pattern

The experiment can be performed as a thought-experiment rather than a felt one. The person mentally pictures a person they judge, mentally pictures an open heart, decides intellectually that the two seem compatible, and concludes the principle is wrong. This is the cognitive bypass. The instruction is to actually do both in the body, not to imagine doing both.

The somatic correction: ask the person to put a hand on their chest while running the experiment. The hand makes the chest a felt object. The chest's actual state -- closed or open -- becomes harder to bypass.

## What to Do After the Experiment

The experiment surfaces the cost of holding superiority. It does not, by itself, dissolve the superiority. Once the person has felt the cost, the next move is to find out what the superiority is protecting.

This connects to [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]]: judgment (of self or other) usually protects against a feeling underneath. The superiority position is no exception. The person who holds "I am better than them" is usually defending against a feeling they do not want to have -- often shame, often a fear of being equal-and-vulnerable, often the loneliness that being-above produces and then has to keep producing in order to justify itself.

The follow-up question: "If you were not better than them, what would you feel?" The answer points at the actual work. Feel the feeling. The judgment relaxes on its own.

## Tone

Playful and immediate. "Literally, you can just do the experiment right now" is an invitation, not a demand. The lightness signals that the experiment is small and recoverable. The person is not being asked to dismantle their identity; they are being asked to try something for thirty seconds and see what happens.

The lightness also disarms the defensiveness that the topic naturally produces. "Are you saying I am judgmental?" is a conversation that goes nowhere. "Want to try a quick experiment?" is a conversation that produces data.
