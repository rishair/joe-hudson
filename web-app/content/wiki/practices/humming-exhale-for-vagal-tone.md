# Humming on the Exhale to Tone the Vagus Nerve

## The Practice

Take a full breath in through the nose. Exhale by humming through the nose, sustaining the hum to the end of the exhale.

That's the whole practice. One or two cycles is usually enough to produce a felt shift. It can be done anywhere — at a desk, in a car, in a meeting before responding, in a cold tub trying to find your breath in the morning.

## What It Does

Two mechanisms operating together:

- **Nitric oxide release.** Humming on the exhale releases nitric oxide in the nasal passages. Nitric oxide is a vasodilator that supports oxygen uptake and downregulates the sympathetic nervous system.
- **Vibrational toning of the vagus nerve.** The vibration in the throat from sustained humming travels along the vagus nerve corridor, providing direct stimulation to the parasympathetic pathway. The constriction of the humming both pulls air through more slowly (lengthening the exhale, which itself triggers parasympathetic activation) and creates vibration on the structure responsible for downshifting.

Together, these two mechanisms make humming one of the most efficient available practices for moving from sympathetic activation toward ventral vagal (the social/connected state). See [[polyvagal-nervous-system-framing]].

A related practice with similar effect: Ujjayi breath (the yoga ocean-sounding breath through a slightly constricted throat). The constriction creates similar vibration in the same nerve corridor.

## In Practice

Joe and Jonny demonstrate this on the podcast:

"Full breath in through the nose, inhale, and then humming through the nose." -- Jonny Miller, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

Joe's response after one hum: "Fuck that, it feels too good. Let's do another."

The point being that the felt shift after a single hum is significant enough to be obvious. The body settles. The system softens. The shift is immediate.

"The constriction that the hum causes also creates like a vibration in that nerve corridor." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

## When to Use It

- Before a difficult conversation, to settle the system enough to be present
- After a triggering interaction, to begin completing the activation cycle (see [[activation-release-rest-cycle]])
- Mid-meeting when overwhelm is rising and there's no space for a longer practice
- In the morning before getting out of bed
- In a cold tub trying to find the breath (Jonny's specific use)
- Any moment of activation when downshifting is what's wanted
- As a regulatory move before any deeper inquiry, when the system is too activated to feel cleanly

## What This Practice Is For

This is one of the cleaner cases of "breath as fertile soil for healthy emotion" rather than "breath as control over emotion" — when used for its intended purpose. See [[breath-as-fertile-soil-vs-breath-as-control]]. The point of the humming is to bring the nervous system into a state where the person can actually be with what is happening, not to override what is happening.

The same practice can become control if it's used to make sure a feeling never arrives. The fix is the same as for any breath practice: use the regulation to make space, then return to whatever you were postponing. The hum is a bridge, not a destination.

## What It Doesn't Do

This practice is not:
- A replacement for deeper somatic work
- A way to skip emotional inquiry
- A long-term solution to chronic dysregulation
- An alternative to the broader activation-release-rest cycle

It is a small, in-the-moment regulatory tool. Treat it like a glass of water — useful when thirsty, not a substitute for nutrition.
