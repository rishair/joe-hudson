# Should vs. Want Body Check

## Definition

A direct somatic comparison: ask yourself "how should I be right now?" and notice what the body does. Then ask yourself "how do I want to be right now?" and notice what the body does. The two states feel completely different. The body knows which one is producing life and which one is producing stagnation, even when the mind cannot tell.

The exercise is repeated. Back and forth. Should. Want. Should. Want. Each iteration deepens the contrast and makes the felt-distinction sharper.

## Instructions

1. Sit or stand. Bring attention into your body.
2. Ask out loud or silently: "How should I be right now?" Let the answer come. Notice what happens in your body when the should arrives. What contracts? What heaviness shows up? What energy shifts?
3. Now ask: "How do I want to be right now?" Let the answer come. Notice what happens in your body when the want arrives. What opens? What lightens? What energy shifts?
4. Repeat. Should. Want. Multiple times. Stay with the body, not the content of the answers.
5. Notice the difference. Notice that one is constricting and one is opening. Notice that this is true regardless of what specific answer comes up.

## What Joe Says It Does

"You ask them over and over again, 'How do you want to be right now? How should you be right now?' And they get to experience the difference physically in their body, what it feels like to tell themselves they should do stuff and what that creates in their body and their mind. And then how do you want to be right now? And then what does that create in your body and your mind. And you get to feel it and you get to feel like how horrible shoulds are and how stagnant they create a life and how wants can open things up." -- Joe Hudson, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

The point is not to arrive at the right answer to "how do I want to be." The point is to feel the difference between the two modes of asking. The intellectual case for [[wants-over-shoulds]] is one thing. The somatic case is another. The somatic case is what produces actual change.

## Why It Works

[[should-as-threat]] explains the mechanism intellectually: a should is an internal threat, and the body responds to threats with contraction. A want is an invitation, and the body responds to invitations with expansion. The exercise puts the person inside the two responses sequentially so the body can register the difference directly.

The body cannot lie about this. The mind can argue that shoulds are necessary, that shoulds are how things get done, that wants are selfish or unreliable. The body responds to the actual energy of the question regardless of what the mind says about it. The contraction on "should" and the opening on "want" happens before the mind can frame the answer.

Once the body has registered the difference, the case for [[wants-over-shoulds]] does not have to be made intellectually anymore. The body has made it.

## The Two Discoveries

The practice reliably produces two discoveries:

**Shoulds are physically horrible.** People who have lived with shoulds as background noise for years do not notice how much constriction they produce until they actively feel one and then feel its absence. The contrast reveals what was hidden in the background.

**Wants open things up.** The opening is not metaphorical. There is a real shift in body state when the question changes. Breath drops. Shoulders soften. Energy moves. This is the felt evidence that wants are the engine, not shoulds.

After running this experiment a few times, the relationship to one's own shoulds changes. They become detectable. The body starts to flag them. The cost of running on shoulds becomes visible because the alternative has been felt.

## The Question Variants

The practice generalizes to other should-want pairs:

- "What should I do right now?" / "What do I want to do right now?"
- "What should I say?" / "What do I want to say?"
- "How should I feel about this?" / "How do I want to feel about this?"
- "What should I work on?" / "What do I want to work on?"

In each case, the body's response is the data. The content of the answer is secondary to the felt-difference between the two modes.

The "how do you want to be" version is the most general and often the most useful, because it is about the quality of being rather than the specific action. It connects directly to [[how-do-you-want-to-be]].

## The Clean-Want vs. Constricted-Want Variant

A second version of the body check operates within the want frame itself. Two wants are compared, both stated as wants, but they produce different body responses. The clean want opens the body; the constricted want produces tension, speed, and tightness. The contrast reveals which wants the person has been freely wanting and which wants the system has prohibitions on.

The move: name a want the person can speak cleanly ("I want my partner to be happy"). Then name a want the person finds harder to speak ("I want $250,000 a year"). Have the person say both out loud. Watch the body each time.

> Joe: "If you say, I want my fiancée to be happy, let's hear you say that and see what happens."
> Client: "I want my fiancée to feel happy."
> Joe: "Yeah. Okay. Now I want $250,000 a year. What's that one?"
> Client: "I want $250,000 a year."
> Joe: "Okay. How is it — how are those two things different?"
> *(Source: Why You Keep Sabotaging Your Own Success)*

The client's body did the reporting. The first statement was loving and soft. The second statement required tightening and acceleration. The client noticed the difference in real time: "even me saying it the second time, I'm catching myself trying to be more gentle and calming and loving."

The diagnostic feature: when the want is constricted, the person speeds up, tightens, and drops the love from the system. The acceleration is the body's report that it does not feel safe saying this thing. The clean-want speed and posture is the baseline; deviation from that baseline is the data.

Joe then asks the broader categorization question: "What is it okay for you to want and what is it not okay for you to want?" The categorization surfaces the unspoken rules the system has been running about which wants are permissible. The rules are usually structural ("it's okay for me to want good things for others / not okay for me to want good things for myself") and predate the specific topic at hand.

This variant works at the level of want-permission rather than at the want-vs-should level. Both variants are body checks; one tests the want-vs-should distinction, the other tests the permitted-want-vs-prohibited-want distinction. The same instrument; different applications.

## Why This Is a Body Practice, Not a Thinking Practice

If the practice is done as an intellectual reflection ("hmm, what should I do, what do I want to do, interesting"), it produces nothing. The exercise requires the body's participation. The body has to actually register the should and the want as different states.

This is one specific instance of [[exercises-need-body-voice-emotion]]: the practice requires verbal articulation (the question is asked), physical attention (the body is felt), and emotional contact (the difference is registered). Take away any of those and the exercise becomes a thought experiment that does not produce change.

## When This Practice Is Used

- When a client has been describing their life in should-language and needs to make contact with the cost of it
- When the intellectual case for [[wants-over-shoulds]] has been made and is not landing
- When someone is stuck in a behavior they think they should change but cannot make themselves change
- When the conversation needs to move from analysis to somatic data
- As an exercise to establish the body as a reliable diagnostic instrument
- When working with leaders who have been operating on duty for years
- When someone says "I know I should but I can't" as a way to demonstrate why the should is the problem

## What It Produces

- Direct body-felt evidence that shoulds constrict and wants open
- A new diagnostic instrument: the body can now detect a should before the action is taken
- An emotional understanding of [[wants-over-shoulds]] that no intellectual argument could produce
- Increased trust in one's own wants because their effect on the body has been felt
- Often: spontaneous identification of specific shoulds the person had not been noticing

## Boundary Conditions

The practice does not work for someone who has lost contact with their body. In that case, body-reconnection work has to happen first. See [[staying-in-your-body]].

The practice can also produce discomfort when the want answers conflict with the life the person has been building on shoulds. The cost of running on shoulds gets revealed, and that revelation has its own emotional weight. The person running the practice should be willing to be moved by what is found.
