# Open the Drawer as a Shared Verb

## The Practice

Take a principle that already exists and translate it into a phrase short enough that anyone in the company can say it in a meeting, in a 1:1, or in a Slack message. The phrase, once shared, becomes a verb that gives anyone permission to start the conversation the principle is pointing at.

The canonical instance: the "Embrace Intensity" principle (see [[embrace-intensity]]) became "Open the Drawer" inside Johannes Langgraf's company (ONA, formerly Gitpod). The phrase came from the kitchen-drawer metaphor Joe had used to describe the work of a CEO -- the drawer where everything you do not want to look at gets shoved, and the job is to open it and bring clarity to the mess. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the original metaphor.

"Our job as a company is to go to that drawer, open it, and bring clarity into the messiness of that drawer, which is an analogy for like conflict, difficult decisions, fear, emotions you're avoiding, and so on." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

The reason the phrase travels: it is short, concrete, image-based, and -- critically -- usable as a verb in real time. "Let me open the drawer with you on that" is something an engineer can say to a product manager mid-conversation. "Let me embrace the intensity with you on that" is not.

## Why This Practice Works

The principle itself, even when fully internalized by the leadership team, does not propagate through an organization unless people have an in-the-moment way to invoke it. Without the phrase, the principle stays in the heads of the leaders who learned it directly. With the phrase, the principle becomes a tool that anyone can pick up at the moment they need it.

The phrase also pre-handles the permission question. The hardest part of starting a difficult conversation is the social cost of starting it. The shared phrase removes that cost. When the phrase is part of company vocabulary, the listener already knows what is about to happen and consents to it by recognizing the phrase. "Let me open the drawer with you on that" announces: a real conversation is about to start, you can prepare yourself, this is what we do here.

"Open the drawer by now is probably one of the most used sentences in the company because it allows people to do also that. It's like, hey, let me open the drawer with you on that. It kind of gives like permission to have those conversations." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles

The permission structure matters because it converts the principle from a private commitment into a shared protocol. The shared protocol is what scales. Private commitments do not scale.

## How to Design the Phrase

The phrases that work share specific features.

**Short enough to say in real time.** Three to four words maximum. If it cannot be said in a passing comment or written in a Slack message, it will not get used.

**A verb or verb-adjacent.** "Open the drawer" is verb-shaped. "Raise the bar" is verb-shaped. "Embrace intensity" can be said but is harder to use as a real-time signal because it does not have a clean object. The verb-shape is what makes the phrase usable as a move, not just a value.

**Image-based.** The drawer is a concrete object. The bar is a concrete object. The image gives the phrase a hook in memory. Abstract phrases (like "be transparent" or "lean into discomfort") do not stick the same way.

**Co-developed, not imposed.** The phrase has to feel like the team's phrase, not the CEO's slogan. The drawer phrase emerged from Joe's coaching, was tested by Johannes and his team, and was adopted because it captured what they were already doing. The same phrase, dropped into a company that had not done the underlying work, would have died as a buzzword.

**Sayable in conflict.** The phrase has to work when someone is annoyed or scared. If the phrase only works in calm strategy meetings, it does not solve the problem the principle is pointing at. The drawer phrase works because it acknowledges difficulty without judging anyone for the difficulty being there.

Johannes names the design constraint directly:

"We try to phrase all of them in a way that people can say them. So raise the bar, open the drawer, ask, was wunderbar, etc. So that people can use it in their day to day." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles

## What This Practice Replaces

The default mode in most companies: the principle exists in the executive team's heads, gets mentioned at off-sites, appears on slides, and does nothing in the actual flow of work. The team operates by the principles they actually have, not by the principles management has named.

The shared-verb practice closes the gap between the named principle and the operating principle. The phrase carries the principle into the rooms where the principle was supposed to operate but did not.

This is a specific instance of [[direct-intervention-vs-structural-change]]. The direct version is the CEO modeling difficult conversations and hoping the team picks it up. The structural version is giving the team a verb that any member can use to initiate the difficult conversation. The verb scales. The modeling does not.

## The Boomerang Story

Joe's own teaching changed because of how Johannes's company used the phrase. Joe had been teaching "Embrace Intensity" as the principle. He watched Johannes's team use "Open the Drawer" as the in-the-moment phrase and noticed how naturally it spread. The phrase came back into Joe's own company's principle set.

"I remember we switched our -- we had Embrace Intensity, we switched it to Open the Drawer. And one of the reasons we did it was because I was watching one of your meetings and someone's like, I got to open the drawer on that. And it was just -- it had become such a regular part of your culture for someone to say it, and everybody immediately knew what it was. Everybody could immediately prepare themselves to have the real conversation." -- Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles

In the second conversation about this, Joe and Johannes openly tease each other about who borrowed the phrase from whom -- the bidirectional borrowing is itself a sign of the kind of principle-iteration that keeps the system alive. The phrase is good enough that both parties want credit for it and neither minds attributing it back to the other:

"And the analogy here is, which I think I stole actually from one of your podcasts, and then you stole it back." -- Johannes Langgraf, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

"And then I stole it back from you." -- Joe Hudson, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The story illustrates something about the iteration of principles (see [[wrestle-with-your-principles]]): a phrase that works better than the original phrase can replace the original even at the source. The principle is not the words. The principle is the move the words enable. When better words enable the move more reliably, the words update.

## The Toxic-Niceness Origin

The phrase emerged at Ona out of a specific cultural failure pattern. Before the principle existed, the company had what Johannes calls "toxic niceness" -- walking on eggshells, not wanting to talk about the hard thing, a culture of avoiding what needed addressing. The post-COVID environment of cheap money had allowed the pattern to persist because almost everything was working anyway.

The 30% layoff in January 2023 forced the company into a different mode. The decision itself required hard conversations the team had been avoiding. The successful execution of the layoff -- including writing what Joe called "the most important email you'll ever write to your team" -- gave the company a reference point for what it looked like to open the drawer. The phrase became the principle's permanent verb-form.

"Back then we had, there was too much of, I would say, like toxic niceness in a way in the company. So walking on eggshells, you know, not wanting to talk about the hard thing. Money was so free and cheap, you know, before of that coming out of COVID. So there was a lot of it just like, you know, works. And we had a lot of that walking on eggshells in our culture. So out of that very, very difficult decision came the operating principle of Open the Drawer." -- Johannes Langgraf, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The lesson generalizes: the strongest principles often emerge from the moments when the absence of the principle was most costly. The principle is then named in a way that the team can never forget what it was trying to address. See [[walking-on-eggshells]] for the dyadic version of toxic niceness, and [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]] for the broader pattern.

## Other Examples Joe Names

The same design pattern shows up across his principle set and across the companies he works with:

- **"Raise the bar"** -- the verb form of an excellence or growth principle
- **"Open the drawer"** -- the verb form of an embrace-intensity or transparency principle
- **"Ask, was wunderbar"** -- the verb form of a curiosity or wonder principle (German-language adaptation in Johannes's company)
- **"Connect, connect, connect"** -- Joe's verb-doubled phrase for the connection principle (see [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]]). The triple-repeat makes it sayable as a reminder in any moment.

Each of these is the same move: take the principle, find the phrase the team will actually use, install it.

## What This Practice Does Not Replace

This is not a substitute for the underlying work. A team that adopts the phrase without doing the trust-building and conflict-leaning work that the principle requires will use the phrase as theater. "Let me open the drawer with you on that" said in a low-trust meeting becomes a soft attack disguised as a teaching reference. The phrase is downstream of the principle being lived. Without the living, the phrase is decoration.

The practice is also not a one-time installation. The phrase keeps its meaning by being used in the way the principle intends. If the phrase becomes a euphemism for "I'm about to criticize you," it loses the permission-structure and stops working. The maintenance of the phrase's meaning is the same as the maintenance of the principle: keep doing the actual move, not just the verbal gesture.

## When This Practice Is Useful

- A company has rolled out principles and they are not landing in daily work
- A leader notices that hard conversations are still avoided despite stated commitment to having them
- A team has adopted the principles but does not have a way to invoke them in real time
- A CEO is asking why the principles work in the executive team but not at scale
- A coach is being asked how to install a principle in an organization, not just teach it

## Boundary Conditions

The phrase has to be supported by the rest of the structure. If a manager uses "open the drawer" to start a difficult conversation and is met with retaliation or stonewalling, the phrase will not get used twice. The phrase requires that the response side of the protocol works too -- that when someone opens the drawer, the team's response is to engage rather than to defend.

The phrase also has to be allowed to evolve. The original phrase may not be the final phrase. The team may discover a better phrase through use. The wisdom is to let the better phrase replace the original rather than holding onto the official version. This is also [[wrestle-with-your-principles]] applied at the vocabulary level.
