# Love Yourself the Way You Always Wanted to Be Loved (The 3-Year-Old Pointer)

## The Practice

A specific entry point into loving yourself when the question "how do I love myself?" produces a blank or a generality. Instead of trying to construct self-love from scratch, locate the felt sense of the love you wanted but did not receive as a young child. Then give yourself that.

"Learning to love yourself unconditionally is a huge part of it. The best pointer I can give you there is to love yourself the way you've always wanted to be loved. Literally go into a 3-year-old version of yourself. Say, 'how do I want to be loved? How did I want love then?' And give that kind of love to yourself. It's a felt sense. It's an experience." -- Joe Hudson, How Love Gets Confused

## Why This Pointer Works

When someone is told "love yourself," the imprinted definition of love kicks in. The person tries to love themselves through criticism, control, or whatever was wired in as love. The attempt fails because it is the imprint, not love. The advice produces frustration: "I keep trying to love myself but I'm just doing what was already there."

The 3-year-old pointer bypasses the imprint. The child knows what they wanted before the imprint had finished installing. The wanting was specific: this kind of presence, this kind of attention, this kind of holding, this kind of words. The wanting is still in the body. Asking the question "how did I want to be loved?" surfaces it.

The pointer reaches for the love that was missed, not the love that was modeled. The love that was modeled is the imprint. The love that was missed is the actual welcoming the child needed but did not get. The body knows the difference even when the mind has lost track.

## How to Run It

The instructions:

1. Drop into the felt sense of being three years old. Not the memory of being three. The felt sense of being small. The body remembers.
2. Ask: how did I want to be loved? What was missing? What did I need that I did not get?
3. Notice what comes. It may be specific (held in arms; spoken to gently; allowed to be sad). It may be more general (just to be seen; just to be allowed to be myself).
4. Give that to yourself now. Not as performance, not as concept. As felt experience. The body relaxes the way it would have relaxed if the love had arrived then.
5. Repeat. The first attempt usually produces something. Subsequent attempts find more.

The practice can be done in seconds when needed. It can also be a sustained sitting practice for as long as the person wants. The work is in the contact between the adult capacity to provide care and the child's specific unmet need.

## The Felt-Sense Emphasis

Joe is explicit: "It's a felt sense. It's an experience." The practice is not a cognitive exercise of listing what was missing. The list, taken alone, can re-traumatize. The point is to feel the missed love arrive now. The body is the indicator. If the body softens, the love is arriving. If the body stays braced or analytical, the practice is still in the head.

The practice is also not the same as identifying what the parents did wrong. The orientation is toward the love that is wanted, not toward the wound. The wound is upstream; the practice addresses what to do once the wound is acknowledged. The actual practice is forward-facing — what would you give yourself now? — rather than backward-facing.

## What This Practice Pairs With

This practice is one of two complementary moves Joe offers for the path out of imprinted love. The other is allowing heartbreak (see [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]). The third is taking contrary action (see [[take-contrary-action]]).

Together they form a small cluster:

- Give yourself the love you always wanted (this practice).
- Allow the heartbreak about not having gotten it then and about how long you have gone without it.
- Take contrary action so the imprint stops being reinforced in the present.

The three together address the imprint at multiple layers: the loving provides the new content, the heartbreak releases the old, the contrary action updates the behavior. Working only one of them produces partial results. Working all three together moves the work much faster.

## What This Practice Is Not

It is not inner-child therapy in the sense of constructing dialogues with the inner child. The practice is simpler: locate the wanting, give the love. There is no scene, no script, no production. The relationship is direct.

It is not retroactive parenting. The point is not to imagine your actual parents giving you what they did not. The point is to give yourself what was wanted, from your current capacity. You are the source of the love now. The wanting from then is the pointer to what to provide.

It is not blame of the parents. The wanting was specific to who you were as a child. The parents had their own imprint that constrained what they could provide. Surfacing the wanting does not require litigating the failure. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]].

It is not a one-time exercise. The first attempt produces something. Subsequent attempts find more. The work continues. The accumulation over time is what shifts the imprint.

## When This Practice Lands

When a person reports trying to love themselves and not knowing how — the pointer provides a concrete entry.

When a person reports feeling stuck in the same self-treatment pattern despite years of work — the pattern is the imprint reinforcing itself; this practice introduces an alternative.

When a person has done significant emotional work and is at the stage of building self-love rather than dismantling defenses — the practice provides the building material.

When a person is in the middle of grief over a relationship or a long pattern — the practice often arises naturally; the person discovers what they were really wanting in the relationship was the love they never gave themselves.

## Tone

Tender. The 3-year-old pointer is delivered with softness. Joe does not push the person to do it; he offers it as a pointer. The practice itself is gentle by nature. The body does not respond well to forcing self-love. The orientation is permission and presence rather than effort.
