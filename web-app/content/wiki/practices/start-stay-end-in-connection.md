# Start, Stay, End in Connection

## The Practice

Make connection the operating principle at three points of every interaction: the opening, the middle, and the close. The mantra is three words because there are three positions.

1. **Start with connection.** Open the interaction in connection. Do not begin with the agenda, the request, the criticism, the catch-up. Begin in connection with yourself and with the other person.
2. **Stay focused on connection.** When the interaction gets hard, when results are not landing, when the other person resists, return to connection as the fulcrum. "Things aren't going well. I focus on connection." -- Joe Hudson
3. **End in connection.** Close the interaction in connection. Do not end with the conclusion, the action item, the silent withdrawal. Close in the same field you opened in.

The repetition is functional. Each point is a place where connection is commonly dropped. The practice marks all three.

## Joe's Words

"I actually repeat it 3 times because you start with connection, you stay focused on connection, you end in connection. So it's a principle means that for me, it means -- can I act on it? And so it's like, oh, things aren't going well. I focus on connection. If things aren't going well with my customers, I focus on connection. If things aren't going well with my marriage, I focus on connection. I start my interaction with connection. I end my interactions with connection. So that's why I have it as 3 times." -- Joe Hudson, Connection: A State Beyond States

## Why Three

The interaction has three structural positions where connection is most easily lost.

**The opening.** People often skip the opening of connection because they are nervous, in a hurry, or focused on what they want to extract from the interaction. Starting with the agenda communicates "you are an instrument of my goal." Starting with connection communicates "I am here with you." The opening sets the field.

**The middle.** When tension arises mid-interaction, the reflex is to push through to the result, to win the argument, to fix the problem. The connection drops. The practice is to use the middle moment as the trigger to return to connection. The problem usually resolves more easily from a connected state than from a pushing state.

**The close.** The exit is often where people break connection most subtly. The handoff to the next thing, the quick goodbye, the unfinished feeling at the end of a meeting. Ending in connection means staying present until the actual end, not pre-leaving while still in the room.

## How to Practice

The practice is not a script. It is an orientation.

- Before entering any interaction (a meeting, a phone call, a conversation with a partner), take a moment to feel yourself. The connection starts inside.
- Open the interaction with whatever produces connection in that context: eye contact, a real question, a moment of silence, a comment that meets the other person where they are.
- During the interaction, when you notice tension rising, ask: am I optimizing for being right, or for connection? See [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]] for the heuristic.
- At the close, do not rush the exit. Mark the ending. Stay until you are actually gone.

In a marriage or close relationship, the practice often means the morning greeting and the goodnight matter as much as the conversations in between. In a business meeting, it means the first minute and the last minute carry weight far out of proportion to their duration.

## Connection Is Not a Step in a Sequence

The practice is not "do connection first, then move on to the real work." The connection is the field within which the work happens. The work does not happen in spite of the connection; it happens because of it.

This is the same pattern as [[view]]: VIEW is not something you do before the conversation. It is the state of mind from which the conversation arises. Start-stay-end is the temporal version of the same principle. Connection is the substrate, not the prologue.

## What Goes Wrong Without This

Interactions opened without connection feel transactional. The other person registers that they are a means to an end and adjusts their availability accordingly. The interaction may complete but the relationship erodes.

Interactions in which connection drops mid-stream and is not restored produce the felt sense of "we needed to come back together but didn't." This builds residue. Over time, the residue becomes the default state of the relationship.

Interactions ended without connection leave the other person with the felt experience of being dropped. They may not name it. They will register it.

The cumulative cost across many interactions is what most people call "the relationship is not what it used to be" without being able to point to what changed.

## When Joe Uses This

The principle is for daily use. Joe applies it to customers, to his marriage, to his children, to coaching, to teams.

The most useful test is: when something is going wrong, can you bring this principle to bear in the next ten seconds? If yes, you can act on it. If it requires elaborate planning, it has become a concept rather than a principle.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A noticeable shift in the felt quality of interactions when the opening is unhurried.
- The discovery that hard conversations are easier when the connection is restored mid-stream rather than after the fact.
- Cleaner endings. Less residue between interactions.
- The relationship-with-self gets the same treatment: starting the day in connection with self, returning to it during difficulty, ending the day in connection.
