# Daily Upright Apology to Yourself for a Bad Habit

## The Practice

For a habit you have wanted to change for years but cannot, give yourself a daily upright apology for it. Not a self-flagellating apology. An empowered one, from an upright body, with no shame in it.

"If you're out there and you're like, I have a bad habit, see what it's like to give yourself an upright apology for that thing on a daily basis. 'I'm sorry for hurting myself. I'm sorry for hurting you for smoking. This isn't how I want to be with you, or this isn't how I want to be with myself.' Whatever feels good. What is that like to really be in that empowered apology? See what it does to the habit." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## Why This Differs From Self-Criticism

Most people already self-criticize their bad habits constantly. That self-criticism is shame, and shame holds the habit in place. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] and [[negative-self-reinforcement]]. The daily apology is the opposite of that mechanism: an upright acknowledgment without shame, which loosens rather than tightens the lock.

The difference is the body. Self-criticism happens with the back hunched and the voice attacking. The upright apology happens with the back straight and the voice steady. The same words ("I want to stop smoking") feel completely different in those two bodies, and produce completely different effects on the habit. See [[upright-apology]] for the body signature.

## How to Practice

1. **Pick one habit.** Choose something you have been trying to stop or change for years. Smoking, eating at night, snapping at your kids, doomscrolling, the recurring fight with your partner. One thing.

2. **Set a time daily.** Same time each day, even briefly. The repetition is part of the practice.

3. **Find uprightness in your body.** Sit or stand. Back straight, eyes level. Notice the difference between this posture and the hunched shame posture.

4. **Speak the apology aloud.** Out loud, not in your head. To yourself, or to whoever is affected by the habit. The example phrasings:
   - "I'm sorry for hurting myself."
   - "I'm sorry for hurting you for smoking. This isn't how I want to be with you."
   - "This isn't how I want to be with myself."

5. **Stay with what arises.** The upright apology often cracks open emotion. Grief about lost time. Fear about who you would be without the habit. Tenderness for the part of you that the habit was protecting. Let what comes come. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

6. **Do not check whether the habit is changing.** Checking is its own form of trying-to-fix, which reinstalls the dynamic this practice is meant to dissolve. Just keep the practice. The change is a byproduct, not the goal.

## Pair with Full Felt Experience of the Behavior

The daily upright apology is the verbal, posture-based half of the work. The complementary half is the in-the-act practice: next time you are doing the behavior, do not interrupt it, but feel it all the way through. See [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]].

The two practices reinforce each other. The apology is the upright acknowledgment from a calm body. The full felt experience is the upright acknowledgment from inside the act. Both bypass the willpower-and-shame approach that has been failing. Together they often produce a release that neither alone would.

## What It Tends to Produce

The practice tends to produce two things:

**Emotional movement.** Grief for years of self-attack often comes first. Then a softening toward the part of yourself that has been carrying the habit. Sometimes anger at whoever installed the shame that locked the habit in place.

**A loosening of the habit.** Not in the form of willpower. In the form of decreasing interest. The habit becomes less compelling because the emotional dynamic underneath it is being addressed directly. This mirrors Joe's own experience with his pot habit: once he stopped fighting it and addressed the underlying emotions, the habit dissolved without effort. See [[joes-pot-habit]].

## Anti-Patterns During Practice

- Do not let this become another self-improvement project. The moment "am I doing this right" appears, the practice has slipped back into shame. See [[tools-to-change-not-love]].
- Do not measure success by whether the habit changes. Measure by whether the apology felt upright. The habit change is downstream.
- Do not skip the body. An apology done in your head while hunched at your desk is the shame apology. The body posture is the practice.
- Do not pile multiple habits into one apology. One habit. The practice is about the felt quality, not the volume of items addressed.

## When To Recommend This

- When someone has been trying to change a habit for years and shame-based methods are clearly not working.
- When someone is locked in [[negative-self-reinforcement]] about a habit and needs a structurally different relationship with it.
- After [[upright-apology]] has been introduced as a concept and the user wants to know how to develop the capacity.
- When the user has identified the underlying emotion driving the habit but wants a daily container to work with it.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the user presents a 10-year-old habit they cannot stop.
- When the user reports self-criticism about a habit and is willing to try something different.
- After teaching [[shame-drives-behavior]], this is the practice that operationalizes the principle.
- When the user has tried willpower repeatedly and is open to a non-effort approach.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[upright-apology]] for what the apology must be.
- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] when grief or fear arises during the practice.
- Toward [[joes-pot-habit]] as the worked example of the principle.
- Toward [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]] when the practice reveals what is actually underneath the habit.

## Boundary With the Interpersonal-Pattern Framework

This practice is the bad-habit form, where the apology is to yourself and the daily container is the structure. The [[four-as-of-behavior-change|4 A's]] are a different structure entirely, for interpersonal patterns where the apology is to specific other people and the wide announcement restructures the relational field. Bad habits don't have the right shape for the 4 A's because the apology target is unclear and the action step doesn't fit (you can't take five immediate counter-actions to "exercise every day"; the bad habit needs a different operational form). The two practices are siblings — both use the upright apology mechanism — but they apply in different domains. See [[four-as-of-behavior-change]] for the interpersonal-pattern complement.
