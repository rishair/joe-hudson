# Lawyer the Voice

## The Practice

Write down what the [[voice-in-the-head]] is saying. Then become an argumentative lawyer to it. Not an adversary. A lawyer doing their job: examining the claim, asking what evidence supports it, finding the small untruths inside it. The goal is not to win against the voice. The goal is to expose the false logic on which it has been operating.

This is the operational form of [[untruth-in-every-thought]]. Every statement the voice makes contains a small untruth. The practice is to find it.

"Find like be an argumentative lawyer to it, not an adversary, but an argumentative lawyer, and find out what's a little untrue in each of the statements." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

## How to Run It

**Step 1: Capture the voice on paper.** Write what it has been saying. Use its actual phrasing. "I should lose weight." "I'm bad at this." "They think I'm stupid." Get the statements out of your head and onto the page.

The depression-specific variant: write the 20 most persistent negative self-thoughts you can identify. The 20-thought constraint forces excavation past the first three or four obvious ones, into the loops that have been running quieter. The list itself is often a revelation -- many people are surprised by how much they can produce and how repetitive the underlying message is.

"Literally write down, here's the 20 negative, most persistent negative self-thoughts, and then literally debate them with yourself. Take the other side and say, 'I'm going to see if the voice in my head is actually accurate,' and really debate with it. At some point, you get to a place where any of the negative self-talk, you're just like, 'Yeah, that's bullshit. That's bullshit.' There's a little bit of truth in each of them, but the general premise is bullshit, bullshit, bullshit, bullshit. And that makes the change happen really quickly when you can see that." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

**Step 2: Pick a statement.** Start with one. The most-repeated one is usually a good place to begin.

**Step 3: Ask lawyer questions.** Joe demonstrates with "I should lose weight" in the transcript:

- "Should according to whom?"
- "What do you mean by should?"
- "Shouldn't you be the weight you are? Because the definition of should is what is."
- "Why? So you don't die early?"
- "What makes it that dying early is bad?"
- "And what — so I have to lose weight because if not, I'll die early?"
- "Maybe I should say I want to lose weight."
- "What's the response to 'I should lose weight'? Most people's response is rebellion. So is that even working?"
- "I've been saying it for a decade. It doesn't work. So what makes me keep on saying it?"

"You know, so someone says, 'I should lose weight.' Well, you should lose weight? According to who? What do you mean by should? Shouldn't you be the weight you are? Because the definition of should is what is, right?" -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The lawyer is not trying to prove the statement wrong. The lawyer is examining the statement with full attention to what it actually claims.

**Step 4: Notice what is true.** Often something is true inside the voice's framing, but the framing distorts it. "I should lose weight" might contain "I want to feel better in my body." The lawyering surfaces what was actually wanted, in cleaner form.

**Step 5: Move to the next statement.** Or sit with the discovery from this one. There is no rush.

## What the Practice Surfaces

Common discoveries when running this:

**The "should" has no source.** When pressed, the voice cannot say who or what is doing the shoulding. The authority dissolves on examination.

**The catastrophic consequence is unproven.** "If I don't, then disaster." When asked for evidence, the disaster is rarely well-supported.

**The binary is false.** The voice presents two options. The lawyer asks: are those really the only two? Almost always there is a third, fourth, fifth.

**The voice is internally inconsistent.** "I should be more assertive" and "I shouldn't have said that" both addressed to the same person in the same day. The lawyer catches the contradiction.

**The want underneath becomes visible.** Most shoulds, lawyered, reveal a want. "I should call my mom" reveals "I'd like to feel connected to her." See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[the-want]].

**Most negative thoughts contain a kernel of partial truth from selective data.** The depressed voice rarely makes things up entirely. It pulls real data points and weighs them disproportionately. "I'm 40 and I don't own a house" is data. Treating that single data point as a verdict on a whole life is the move the lawyer can name. The voice has been operating on selective evidence; the lawyer surfaces what was being left out.

"You're treating yourself the way you wouldn't treat somebody else typically." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

A useful test the lawyer can run on any negative self-thought: would I make this verdict about my closest friend on the same evidence? If not, the verdict is the voice's selective application, not the truth.

## The Lawyer Is Not an Attacker

The role matters. An attacker is trying to destroy. A lawyer is trying to test. The energy is different.

If the practice becomes "I'm going to prove this voice wrong and then attack myself for ever having listened to it," the practice has been hijacked by the voice in new clothes. The voice now has a sophisticated tool to use against you. See [[dont-fight-the-voice]].

The lawyer energy is investigative, curious, slightly amused. The questions are real questions, not gotchas. The discovery is interesting. The voice losing credibility is data, not victory.

## Why Journaling Helps

The voice operates faster in the head than the hand can write. Slowing it down to writing speed exposes the structure. Many of the voice's claims, once written, look obviously thin. They held up only because they were running too fast to inspect.

The page is also a witness. The voice does not like being seen. Writing it down brings it into a different register, where its mechanics become visible.

## When to Use

- When a specific recurring thought has become an obstacle.
- When the person wants something more active than "just notice it."
- When the voice has been believed for a long time and needs to be examined directly.
- As a journaling practice, weekly or daily.

## What This Does Not Replace

This practice is one tool among many. It is not the only or best approach to the voice. For someone who needs warmth and not analysis, [[apply-view-to-the-voice]] is the move. For someone who needs lightness, [[experimenting-with-the-voice]] is better. For someone who needs depth, [[whats-looking-out-behind-my-eyes]] or [[what-am-i]] is the right instrument.

This practice fits the moment when the person is ready to investigate the voice's content with rigor.
