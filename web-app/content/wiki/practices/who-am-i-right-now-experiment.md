# Who Am I Right Now? (The Present-Moment Identity Experiment)

## The Practice

A short, live experiment that surfaces how much of the felt-sense of "who I am" depends on past or future evidence rather than present-moment reality.

The instructions:

1. Close your eyes for a minute.
2. Without going into the past for any evidence, and without going into the future for any evidence, feel who you are right now.
3. Any thought you have about it has to come from the past (something you did, felt, thought) or the future (something you will do, feel, think). Notice those thoughts arrive and let them go.
4. Stay only with what is here in the body, in this moment.
5. You can silently ask: "who am I right now?"

The funny thing the experiment reveals: who you are right now is always who you are. The "you" that needs the past and future to be defined is one version of self. The "you" that is here in the body without those references is a different version. Both are real. The first one is built on story. The second one is built on presence.

"Usually when we think about who we are, we have to go into the past or the future. Something that we've done or something that we're going to do, something that we felt or something that we're going to feel, something that we thought or something that we are going to think. But what I want you to discover is who are you right now? So close your eyes for a minute, and without going into the past for any evidence or the future for any evidence, just in your body, feel who you are right now. Any thoughts you have about it has to come from the past or the future. So it's just in your body. Who are you right now? You can even ask that question silently to yourself. Who am I right now. And the funny thing is, who you are right now is always who you are." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Causes of Stress

## What It Demonstrates

The experiment makes visible the architecture of identity. Most of what we mean by "who I am" is a story constructed from memories (past) and projections (future). The constructed self is the one that gets defended, that produces the stress of identity-protection, that has to be the thing being identified with. See [[identity]] and [[every-identity-has-limits]].

The present-moment self is a different thing entirely. It does not need defending because it is not constructed from anything that can be contested. It is the simple felt-sense of being here, alive, in the body, right now. This self does not have positions to protect or scores to keep.

The discovery is that this present-moment self is always available. It is not contingent on having time to meditate or on being in a particular state. The body is always right now. The breath is always right now. The sensations are always right now. The "you" that is right now is always there to be felt, if attention goes to the body instead of to the story.

## Why This Belongs in the Stress Framework

The experiment is the identity-level intervention in [[four-causes-of-stress]]. The framework's fourth cause of stress is identity: the harder you hold the self-definitions, the more your life is stressful, because each definition is a piece that has to be protected.

This experiment offers a direct route around the protection. The present-moment self has nothing to protect. Spending even a minute in the present-moment self gives the system a break from the constant identity-maintenance that the constructed self requires.

The experiment is also a small dose of what the deeper identity work eventually produces. The transparent identity ([[transparent-identity-vs-rigid]]) is, in some sense, the identity that has learned how to live more in the present-moment self and less in the constructed self.

## What Tends to Happen

When people do this experiment, several common things arise:

- The mind reaches for past or future and has to be redirected
- A felt sense of "more space" or "less holding"
- A discovery that "I" am not as defined as I usually think
- Sometimes anxiety, because the constructed self feels threatened by being set down
- Sometimes peace, because the constructed self has been a burden the person did not realize they were carrying

If anxiety arises, the experiment is still working. The anxiety is the constructed self protesting the loss of its position. Notice the anxiety, let it be there, return to the body.

## Variations

The experiment can be extended:

- Do it for five minutes instead of one
- Do it with eyes open in the middle of a stressful situation
- Do it while looking at someone you have an identity-conflict with
- Pair it with the question "who is the one asking?" when noticing a defensive reaction

The variation in the middle of a stressful situation is particularly useful for testing whether the stress is identity-stress. If dropping into the present-moment self softens the stress significantly, the stress was identity-protection. If the stress remains, the stress is likely in another layer (emotional, nervous-system).

## When Joe Uses This

The experiment is used in teaching contexts to demonstrate the identity layer of stress. It is also useful in coaching when:

- The person is in a heated identity-defense and needs to find ground that is not the identity
- The person is asking "who am I really?" in a way that suggests they are looking for a better self-definition (the experiment shows that the better answer is to step out of the framing)
- The person is wrapped up in past stories or future projections and needs to come back to now
- The person has been doing identity work intellectually and needs a felt-sense version

## Anti-Patterns

Do not turn the experiment into a goal. "I have to find my true self in the present moment" makes the experiment another should. The instruction is light: just notice. The discovery happens by attention, not by effort.

Do not use the experiment to dismiss the constructed self. The constructed self has its uses. It is what speaks, holds memory, plans, makes commitments. The point of the experiment is not to eliminate the constructed self but to discover that it is one layer, not the whole.

Do not expect the present-moment self to be dramatic. It is usually quiet. It usually feels like nothing in particular. That is correct. The constructed self is the noisy one. The present-moment self is the quiet one. People sometimes assume they have failed the experiment because nothing dramatic happened. The quietness is the data.

## What Comes Next

After the experiment, the natural extensions:

- Notice when in daily life the constructed self is running the show
- Notice when in daily life the present-moment self is available
- Practice dropping into the present-moment self for one breath in the middle of a difficult moment
- Use the present-moment self as the place where identity work happens (you cannot work on identity from inside identity; you need a place outside it to stand)

See [[view-around-self-as-identity-diagnostic]] for the related diagnostic that uses VIEW to find identity edges. The two practices work together: the present-moment self is the ground from which the VIEW diagnostic is most useful.

## Tone

Light, curious, inviting. Joe delivers this as a small discovery, not as a heavy spiritual practice. The "funny thing is, who you are right now is always who you are" is delivered with a smile. The experiment is a small wonder, not a grand revelation.
