# Put Your Attention in Your Inner Ear

## The Practice

While listening, speaking, or being in any conversation, place your attention in your inner ear. Keep the focus inside the ear itself. Continue doing whatever you were doing. The comprehension does not collapse. The pace does not change. But the quality of listening shifts — spaciousness opens, the body comes back online, the meta-position of monitoring how you are being received dissolves.

"If you want a hack, there's a simple hack, which is right now while we're sitting here, just put your attention in your inner ear. So all you have to do is put your attention in your inner ear. I'll talk and that's it. That's all you have to do. Super easy." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

The instruction is small enough to be deployed anywhere. No special posture. No preparation. No meditation cushion. Just the redirect of attention to one specific body-part already involved in any conversation.

## Instructions

1. Notice you are in a conversation, listening to a podcast, or about to speak.
2. Direct your attention into your inner ear. Locate the ear from inside. Notice what it is like to have your attention there.
3. Stay with that attention while the conversation continues. Notice you still understand what is being said. Notice you can still respond.
4. When the attention drifts away (it will), return it to the inner ear. The drifting is normal. The returning is the practice.

The practice can be done while doing anything else. It is not a separate activity.

## Why It Works

The inner ear is in the body. Placing attention there places attention in the body. The result is the same as the larger [[staying-in-your-body]] practice, but anchored to a specific location small enough that the instruction is unambiguous.

The mind that has been at a meta-position monitoring how the speaker is being received cannot occupy two places at once. When the attention is in the inner ear, it is not at the meta-position. The default monitoring pattern is interrupted. The body opens. The breath drops. The conversation becomes audible from inside the listener rather than from above the conversation.

The ear is also the body-part most directly involved in receiving speech. Choosing the ear has a clean metaphorical coherence — the listening organ is where the attention goes — that makes the instruction easy to remember and easy to return to.

## What You Will Notice

- A sense of expansion in awareness. The forward edge of attention opens.
- Slower, more deliberate processing of what the other person is saying, without any reduction in comprehension.
- Easier registration of tone and pace, not just content.
- Reduction in the impulse to plan the next sentence or rehearse the response.
- When applied while speaking: the words become slower, more grounded, more present. The speaker hears themselves.
- A felt sense that resembles meditation — not because meditation is happening, but because the conditions for it are arriving naturally.

"There's a kind of a feeling of expansion that occurs. It's almost like your awareness becomes bigger and it becomes more in the forefront. There's more spaciousness that happens." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## When to Use It

- During a difficult conversation in which you notice yourself leaving your body.
- While listening to someone in pain, when you need to be with them without losing yourself.
- In meetings where the urge to perform or impress is high.
- Mid-presentation, mid-speech, mid-interview — any moment when the body is bracing for the audience response.
- As a continuous low-intensity practice during ordinary daily conversation.
- When self-listening has gone abstract and a body-anchor is needed.

## Companion to the 10% Move

This practice and [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] are pointing at the same shift from different angles. The 10% move is the percentage form ("listen to yourself more, by this much"). The inner-ear practice is the body form ("listen from this location"). Both produce the same result: a return to presence in the self while remaining in conversation.

A coachee who finds one easier than the other should use whichever opens the door. People with strong intellectual orientations tend to find the percentage instruction more tractable. People with stronger body orientations tend to find the inner-ear hack more natural. Both work. Switching between them keeps either from becoming an empty habit.

## What This Is Not

This is not a meditation technique to produce a special state. The state it produces is just ordinary listening from inside the body — what listening was always supposed to be. It is not exotic.

This is not a technique to perform during a conversation in order to be perceived as a better listener. The performance itself would put the attention back at the meta-position. The practice has to be private and quiet. The listener cannot tell you are doing it. They can only feel the shift in your presence.
