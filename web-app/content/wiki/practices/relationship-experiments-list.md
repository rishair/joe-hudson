# Pre-Build a List of 10-20 Relationship Experiments Together

## The Practice

Sit down together and pre-build a list of 10 to 20 experiments you will run on the relationship. Not "we'll try this thing and if it doesn't work it's over." A numbered, agreed-upon list of distinct things to try, agreed in advance, so that no single attempt is carrying the verdict on the relationship.

The list converts the relationship work from a pass-fail trial into an iteration. With the list in front of both partners, no individual attempt can collapse into "we tried, it didn't work, we're done." There is only "we ran experiment #1; here's what we learned; now we run experiment #2." The next iteration is always already on the page.

"Instead of having it like, I'm going to try this and if it doesn't work, then it's over. You need to think about it as like, oh, here are the 10, 20 experiments that I'm going to do working with the relationship." -- Joe Hudson, The Mindset Shift That Transforms Dead Relationships

## Instructions

1. **Confirm both partners are in.** This practice depends on both people wanting a better relationship. If only one partner is willing, this is not the right practice. See [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]] -- if the answer for both is yes, proceed; if only one is yes, the conversation is a different one.

2. **Sit down together. Write 10 to 20 experiments.** Not 3. Not 5. The number matters because most couples have already tried the 2-3 obvious things and concluded those did not work. Get past those. Write the weird ones. Write the ones from books and friends. Write things one of you has wanted to try but never proposed. The list itself is the work.

3. **Make them specific.** "Be more loving" is not an experiment. "Every night this week, before sleep, say one thing you appreciated about each other today" is an experiment. The specificity is what allows the experiment to be evaluated.

4. **Order them.** Pick any order. The order is less important than the existence of an order. Knowing what is next is what makes any individual experiment safe.

5. **Run them.** One at a time. Notice what each one reveals -- about you, about your partner, about the dynamic between you. Each experiment is information.

6. **Treat each completed experiment as a win.** Even the ones that "don't work" produce learning. The win is the iteration, not the outcome of any single iteration.

## Why It Works

The practice has the same structural effects as [[write-50-experiments-before-starting]], applied to the relationship domain:

**Pre-empting the habenula.** The [[habenula]] suppresses retry after perceived failure. A single failed attempt at fixing a relationship -- "I tried being more vulnerable and it backfired" -- registers as failure and shuts down the next attempt. The pre-built list changes what each event registers as. The same outcome that would have registered as "the relationship can't be fixed" registers instead as "experiment #1 complete; experiment #2 next." The retry happens because the brain did not get the suppression signal.

**Defusing the verdict on the relationship.** Without the list, every failed attempt risks becoming "this relationship is over." With the list, no single attempt carries that weight. The relationship is not on trial in any one experiment. The relationship is the field in which the experiments are being run.

**Converting the binary into a process.** The pass-fail framing produces two states: working and broken. The iterative framing produces one state: learning. The relationship is no longer in the dangerous binary where every attempt could prove it broken. It is in the iterative loop where every attempt produces information.

## The Habenula in Couples

This practice is specifically designed against the brain mechanism that makes couples give up. Joe makes the parallel explicit: the dieter who has the piece of chocolate cake and thinks "ah, fuck it, I failed" and then eats chocolate cake every day for a week is running the same suppression circuit as the couple who tries one round of marriage counseling, finds it didn't fix things, and concludes the marriage is unsalvageable.

"It's the thing that makes us, like, when we're trying to diet and then we, like, have that piece of chocolate cake, we're like, 'Ah, fuck it, I failed.' And then, like, you just, ah, just eat chocolate cake every day. That's the part of the brain. And so you need to know that going into the relationship." -- Joe Hudson, The Mindset Shift That Transforms Dead Relationships

The list is the structural pre-empt of this mechanism in the relationship domain. The dieter who has the chocolate cake while running experiment #4 on a list of 20 does not register that as failure. The same is true for couples.

## What It Produces

The most striking promise Joe makes about this practice:

"I guarantee you, by the time the list of experiments is done, you will absolutely be in a different place than when it started. They don't have to work even, but you will have learned about yourself. You will have learned about each other. You will learn things where you can create new experiments that will work. And it's just like one over the other." -- Joe Hudson, The Mindset Shift That Transforms Dead Relationships

The outcomes are not contingent on any single experiment working. The outcomes are contingent on the iteration happening. After 10 to 20 experiments, both partners have substantially more information about themselves, about each other, and about the dynamic between them than they had at the start. That information itself is the change. Future experiments built from that information have higher leverage than the original list could have had.

## Both Partners or Don't Bother

This practice is fundamentally a couples practice, not a solo practice. The structural protection only operates if both partners are running the iteration together. One partner pre-building a list and running experiments while the other partner is not on board does not produce the same result. The unilateral version becomes [[i-keep-trying-to-fix-the-relationship-but-they-wont]], which is a different concern.

"It's much better if it's together, right? Like if both people are saying, hey, let's work on this relationship and that's what we want to do. Then great. Then you both have it. You both have the iterations so that you're never failing. You're just trying the next thing, trying the next thing that's going to work for you." -- Joe Hudson, The Mindset Shift That Transforms Dead Relationships

If only one partner wants to work on the relationship, the conversation to have is different: it is the conversation about whether they actually have a shared agreement that this is a relationship both people want to invest in. See [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]] and [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]].

## Use as a Pre-Marriage Diagnostic

Joe uses a version of this practice as the question he asks when someone is considering whether to marry a person:

"When I say to people, they're like, oh, should I get married to a person? One of the things that I'll ask is like, are you both willing to work and change, you know, yourselves and the relationship so that it gets better and better and better? Because if you don't have that agreement upfront, you know, the chances of success are not great." -- Joe Hudson, The Mindset Shift That Transforms Dead Relationships

The upfront agreement to iterate is itself the diagnostic. A couple that cannot agree before the marriage that they will both work and change is a couple that will hit the inevitable bumps without the structure that makes the bumps survivable. The pre-marriage version of the practice is the conversation about whether the iteration commitment is real.

## What to Watch For

- **The couple that writes 3 items and stops.** The number matters. If the list is short, the structural protection is weak. The first one that doesn't work will collapse the whole thing. Push for the 10-to-20 range even if generating them is uncomfortable. The discomfort of generating them is partly the point: it surfaces what each partner has been holding back.

- **The couple that picks the "right" experiment to start with.** The premise of waiting for the best item is the achievement-frame returning. Pick any order and start. The order is less important than the existence of an order.

- **The couple that runs experiment #1 with everything riding on it.** The list has been built but the verdict frame is still operating. Reaffirm: experiment #1 is one of many. The whole list is the sequence. No single experiment carries the weight.

- **The couple that picks experiments designed to prove the other partner wrong.** The point of the experiment is mutual learning, not evidence-collection for the prosecution. If the experiments are constructed adversarially, the data they produce will be adversarial. The framing has to be genuine inquiry into what works for both people.

- **The couple where one partner is going through the motions.** Like [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]] in the team context: an experiment one partner is performing without genuine engagement produces no data. The leader (in this case, the more committed partner) has standing to call this out. If the half-assing cannot be addressed, the practice is structurally compromised.

## What Tends to Happen

Each experiment touches a different dimension of the relationship. After running through 10 to 20, the couple has accumulated enough information that:

- The original presenting complaint often becomes irrelevant. The dynamics it was pointing at have shifted under the iterations.
- New experiments emerge from what the original experiments revealed. The list extends itself.
- Both partners have a felt sense of progress they did not have before, even if no single experiment was the magic one.
- The iterative orientation generalizes. The couple starts treating other long-stuck areas of their life together the same way.
- The aliveness that was leaking out of the relationship returns, often without either partner having tried to bring it back directly.

## Tone

Joe delivers this practice with the energy of a structural fix. The voice is concrete and slightly impatient with the alternative ("I'm going to try this and if it doesn't work, then it's over"). The practice is offered as the obvious operational form of how iteration works applied to a domain where iteration is unusually neglected. The lightness comes from the guarantee at the end: "by the time the list of experiments is done, you will absolutely be in a different place." It is not a hopeful claim; it is a structural one.
