# The 2-3 Day Connect Trip With Your Kid (Every 6 Months)

## The Practice

Take a 2-3 day road trip with your kid every six months. Leave the phones behind or minimize their use heavily — limit them to a defined evening window like 5 to 6 pm. Spend the days actually being with each other. No scheduling agenda. No fix-it conversations. Just time together.

"A really great tactic is the thing that I do with you is like take a 2-day, 2, 3-day road trip with your kids every, every, every 6 months where you actually get to connect and don't bring phones or minimize your phone use to like between 5 and 6 at night and really have time to be with each other and enjoy each other." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

Esme's confirmation: "Those have been awesome."

## What the Practice Accomplishes

The practice solves a problem most parents do not realize they have: the day-to-day life with a kid, especially a teen, is full of logistics. Schedules. School updates. Phone notifications. Sports. Meals. Errands. The amount of actual undistracted presence between parent and kid in a normal week is often surprisingly low. Even families that eat dinner together are often eating dinner with phones present, with conversations about scheduling, with one or both parties partially elsewhere.

The connect trip removes the logistics layer. There is nothing scheduled. There is nowhere else to be. There is no notification stream pulling attention away. What is left is the actual relationship.

The 2-3 day duration is important. The first half-day is usually still oriented around the trip itself. By day two, both parties have dropped into a different rhythm and conversations open up that would not have happened in a 90-minute dinner. The depth is a function of the duration.

The every-6-months cadence is important. Less often and the relationship drifts back into the logistics-dominated default. More often and it becomes a chore. Every six months produces a regular reset that keeps the connection from accumulating distance over time.

## What Happens On the Trip

The trip is not programmed. The point is not to do specific activities. The point is to be available to each other.

Some things tend to happen organically:
- Long unstructured drives are good. The lack of eye contact actually opens conversation. Hard things get said in cars more easily than across tables.
- Meals are slow because there is nothing to rush toward.
- The kid talks about things they would not have brought up at home, because there is no rush, no audience, and the parent is fully present.
- The parent learns things about the kid they did not know. New friends. New interests. New concerns. The information accumulates because the parent is in [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]] mode.
- Stuff that was unresolved between parent and kid gets resolved, often without it being made into a Big Conversation. The space allows it to come up naturally.

## The Phone Discipline

Phones break the practice. A parent who is half on email, or a kid who is half in their group chat, is not on the trip the way the practice requires.

Joe's specific protocol: minimize phones to a defined evening window (5-6 pm). Both parties get the connection time they need to maintain their other relationships, but the rest of the time the phones are not in the equation. The window approach is cleaner than "use phones less" because it makes the rule unambiguous.

The phone discipline also models something for the kid: undistracted time with someone you care about is a real thing, and adults can do it on purpose. Most kids have rarely seen this modeled. The trip is the demonstration.

## Why This Is Not Just "More Family Time"

A common misreading: "we already spend time together as a family." This is usually true and usually not the same thing. Family time often has logistics, multiple kids, divided attention, and an agenda. The connect trip is one-on-one, agenda-free, phone-free, multi-day. The structural conditions are different and the outcomes are different.

A second common misreading: "we will just do this when something needs to be talked about." The practice does not work this way. The reason the trip generates depth is that it has been planned in advance, removed from any particular crisis, and entered into without the kid feeling they are being summoned for a Conversation. The pre-emptive cadence is what makes the trip a default reset rather than a triggered intervention.

## Why Every 6 Months Specifically

Six months is long enough that the relationship has accumulated some drift. The kid has changed. The parent has changed. The day-to-day has not given them space to update each other. The trip catches them up.

Six months is short enough that the relationship has not gone so far adrift that the trip becomes an attempted repair. The trip is maintenance, not surgery. Maintenance keeps repair from being needed.

The cadence also gives both parties something to look forward to. The kid knows the trip is coming. The parent knows the trip is coming. The anticipation itself contributes to the felt sense that the relationship is being prioritized.

## When to Adjust the Practice

- **For younger kids**, the trip can be shorter (a day, an overnight) and the phone discipline matters less because young kids are not on phones the same way.
- **For older teens with active social lives**, the trip may need to be scheduled around their lives rather than parental convenience. The kid choosing to come is part of what makes it work.
- **For families with multiple kids**, the trip is with one kid at a time. This is part of the point — each kid gets the undivided parent. Doing it with both kids at once converts it back into family time, which is a different thing.
- **For divorced or co-parenting situations**, the practice still works but requires coordination with the other parent. The trip is still 1:1.
- **In stretches when 2-3 days is not possible**, a shorter version (an overnight) is better than nothing. The principle is undistracted dedicated time; the specific duration is a recommendation, not a rule.

## The Failure Modes

The practice fails when:

- The parent uses the trip to deliver a Big Talk they have been saving up. The kid will register the trip as a setup and resist the next one.
- The parent over-programs the trip with activities, leaving no unstructured time. The unstructured time is where the depth happens.
- The parent does not actually disconnect from work or other obligations. Half-presence undermines the structural condition.
- The parent expects the kid to perform connection. The trip is the conditions, not the result. The depth happens when it happens. Some trips will be lighter than others.

## Connection to Other Parenting Moves

The connect trip is the structural complement to [[dont-chase-your-teen-stay-available]]. The parent who is not chasing in daily life builds availability through the trip rather than through constant pursuit. The kid knows the connection time exists and is scheduled, so the parent does not need to extract connection from every interaction at home.

The trip is also the natural context for [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]]. The phone-free, agenda-free duration is what makes the rabbit-hole questions possible.

It is also a parental version of [[joe-and-tara-weekend-reset]]. The same structural intervention — sustained undistracted time — works for the marriage and for the parent-child relationship. The mechanism is the same: relationships need maintenance time that is not embedded in logistics.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent realizing they cannot remember the last real conversation they had with their teen
- A parent whose family life has become entirely logistical
- A parent of a teen who is about to leave for college and wanting to deepen the relationship before they go
- A parent whose work-from-home or always-on phone has been hollowing out their family time
- A parent who has read about the practice and wants to start it but is unsure where to begin
