# Notice Pleasure and Sensation as Grounding (Re-Entry After Opening)

## The Practice

Close your eyes for a minute. Notice the sensations moving through your body. Notice the pleasure and the expansiveness that the body is producing. Stay with it.

The instruction is simple. The function is specific: after a release of long-held suppression (composure, bracing, contraction), the body is full of sensation it has not had access to in years. The sensation can be overwhelming if it has nowhere to land. The practice gives it a place to land — in the felt pleasure of the body itself, savored as ground.

"So close your eyes for a minute. Notice the sensations moving through your body. Your job is just to notice sensations moving through your body. And notice the pleasure and the expansiveness that you feel in this. That is an important part of how you need to spend some of your day as you start to not compose yourself, as you start to allow your sensitivity to feel everything. That's what's going to keep you grounded — that technique is to feel that pleasure." -- Joe Hudson, I'm Tired Of Taking Responsibility

## Purpose

The practice serves a specific structural function. When a person who has been locking down their sensitivity their entire life begins to release, the released material includes both pleasure and difficulty. Without a practice that orients toward the pleasure, the person tends to organize the new perceptual flood around the difficulty — and either re-locks (because the difficulty is too much) or pendulates wildly (because they have no anchor).

The pleasure is the anchor. The body, once the brace begins to release, is a more capable producer of pleasure than most people realize — pleasure shows up in subtle ways (warmth, spaciousness, tingling, expansiveness, ease). The practice trains the attention to notice and savor these signals, which gives the system somewhere stable to return to as the larger opening proceeds.

This is not a positivity bypass. The practice is not telling the person to ignore difficult feeling. It is teaching them that there is also a layer of bodied pleasure available, and that this pleasure is the ground from which difficult feeling can be metabolized. Without the pleasure-as-ground, the difficulty has nowhere to be held; with it, the difficulty can move through.

## Instructions

1. Close your eyes.
2. Bring attention to the body.
3. Notice the sensations that are moving through — warmth, tingling, pressure, ease, openness, whatever is actually there.
4. Notice specifically any pleasure or expansiveness in those sensations. Even small amounts.
5. Stay with the pleasure. Let attention rest in it.
6. If difficulty arises, do not fight it. Return attention to whatever pleasure is also available alongside it.
7. Do this regularly throughout the day. Not as a sit-down meditation only. Brief touch-ins many times per day.

## When Joe Assigns It

After a coaching session in which a long-held suppression has released. The practice is what makes the release sustainable rather than transient. Without the practice, the person leaves the session in an opened state, has no method for staying with the opening, gets overwhelmed by the unfamiliar flood, and re-suppresses — often more tightly than before.

The practice is also for people with constitutional sensitivity that has been locked down ([[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]]). The system needs to learn that pleasure is also available in the sensitivity, not only the difficulty that produced the lockdown in the first place. The practice retrains the association.

The practice is assigned as part of the daily life of the person, not as a session-specific exercise. The framing is: "that is an important part of how you need to spend some of your day." The frequency is more important than the duration. Many brief contacts beat one long sit.

## What It Produces

When the practice is done consistently:

1. The person begins to associate releasing-the-brace with available-pleasure rather than with overwhelm.
2. The opening proceeds at a sustainable pace rather than in disorganized bursts.
3. The sensitivity that comes back online has somewhere to be held — in the pleasure of the body — rather than being immediately overwhelming.
4. Difficult feeling, when it arises, can move through more cleanly because there is a stable somatic ground to return to.
5. The system gradually re-learns that being open is not the same thing as being overwhelmed, that sensitivity does not have to mean exhaustion.

When the practice is skipped or done sporadically:

1. The release from the session erodes within days.
2. The person re-composes, often without noticing.
3. The next session has to do the same work over again, and the body learns that opening is unsustainable.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not turn the practice into a goal. "I have to feel pleasure now" is itself a brace. The practice is to notice what is actually there, including small amounts of pleasure if they are there. If there is no pleasure available in a given moment, that is fine — notice the actual sensation without forcing.
- Do not skip the practice when feeling bad. The practice is especially useful in those moments. The available pleasure in the body is small but it is there; finding it gives the difficult feeling a context to move in.
- Do not require a meditation cushion. The practice fits inside any thirty-second pause. Walking, in line at the grocery store, during a transition between tasks. The brevity is part of the design.
- Do not treat this as a substitute for the underlying release work. The practice grounds the work; it does not replace it. A person who has not yet released the long-held suppression will find the practice abstract because there is nothing yet to ground.
