# Visualize Being In Your Purpose Right Now

## The Practice

Close your eyes. Imagine something you do every day — a meeting, programming, talking to your spouse, doing the dishes. Then ask: "How do I live this moment in my purpose?"

Visualize yourself being in your purpose while doing that ordinary activity. Not changing the activity. Doing the same activity, but in your purpose.

Then shift to a big-deal moment — making a big pitch, asking for a raise, inventing something new — and imagine being in your purpose during that moment.

Notice the felt quality. The visualization is not asking the mind to construct a theory; it is asking the body to register what "in purpose" feels like as a quality of presence layered onto the same actions.

Then take the experiment into daily life. Bring the question into consciousness five or six times a day. Use a reminder system if needed. The question, repeated: "In this moment, how do I live in my purpose?"

"Imagine something that you do every day. Maybe it's having a meeting, or it's programming, or it's talking to your wife. And ask yourself, how do I live this moment in my purpose? And visualize yourself being in your purpose while you're working at your desk, or while you're in the meeting, or while you're talking to your husband. See what it's like to be in your purpose in that moment." -- Joe Hudson, You Won't Find Your Purpose By Searching For It

## The Two Moments

The practice deliberately uses two contrasting kinds of moments — ordinary and big-deal — because purpose is being trained as a quality available in both.

**The ordinary moment** (meeting, dishes, programming) shows that purpose is not contingent on the activity being important. The same email written from a different inner orientation is in purpose or out of purpose depending on the orientation, not the email.

**The big-deal moment** (pitch, raise, invention) shows that purpose is not lost when stakes go up. The system tends to leave the body and go into performance mode when stakes rise; the visualization rehearses purpose surviving that pressure.

If purpose can be felt during both, it can be felt during anything between them. The two ends of the spectrum bracket the whole range.

"Imagine a big deal, one of those big deal moments where you're making a big pitch or you're asking for a raise or you're trying to invent something new. And I want you to be in your purpose while you're doing it. What is it like to be in your purpose during those big moments?" -- Joe Hudson, You Won't Find Your Purpose By Searching For It

## Not in the What, in the How

The practice operationalizes [[purpose-is-how-not-what]]. The question is never "what should I be doing instead of this?" The question is always "how do I do this — the thing I am already doing — in my purpose?"

"How do you find your purpose in this moment right now? Not in the what, but in the how. How do you right now even do this experiment with that sense of purpose. That's the experiment." -- Joe Hudson, You Won't Find Your Purpose By Searching For It

The instruction "do this experiment with that sense of purpose" is itself an example of the principle. The experiment is mundane. The experiment can be done in purpose or not. The choice of how to do the experiment is the same choice as the choice of how to do anything else.

## The Reminder Scaffolding

The practice is meant to leave the visualization session and infiltrate ordinary life. Joe explicitly recommends a reminder system:

"Bring that into your consciousness 5, 6 times a day. Use a reminder system if you need to, and just constantly be asking yourself, in this moment, how do I live in my purpose?" -- Joe Hudson, You Won't Find Your Purpose By Searching For It

The reminders are the operational scaffold for the principle that purpose is a present-moment availability, not a future destination. Without the reminders, the mind reverts to the search-frame (where is my purpose, when will I find it). With the reminders, the question stays in present tense (how am I in this moment).

This pairs naturally with the more disciplined version of the same scaffolding in [[purpose-checkin-alarm-practice]]. That practice uses 2-3 alarms a day for a week and asks "what is my purpose asking me to do right now?" This practice uses 5-6 prompts a day and asks "how do I live this moment in my purpose?" The two questions are complementary — one is about action, the other is about quality of presence. Both work the same in-the-moment frame.

## What the Visualization Trains

Three things get trained by running this experiment repeatedly:

1. **The somatic memory of "in purpose."** The body learns what "in purpose" feels like by being asked to feel it. The first few times the felt quality may be vague or constructed. Repetition gives the body more data. Over time, the felt quality becomes recognizable in the same way other emotional states are recognizable.

2. **The decoupling of purpose from activity-selection.** Each successful visualization of being in purpose during an ordinary activity is evidence that the purpose-experience is not produced by the activity. The decoupling weakens the search-for-the-right-activity frame.

3. **The availability of purpose during high-stakes moments.** The big-deal visualization rehearses the maintained-purpose-under-pressure state. When the real big-deal moment arrives, the system has practiced the state and can find it again. Without this rehearsal, big-deal moments default to performance-mode, which is the opposite of in-purpose.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a goal-setting exercise. The visualization is not about imagining what you will achieve in the meeting or the pitch. It is about the inner quality you bring to it.
- It is not a calming practice. The purpose-quality is not always calm. It can be intense, scary, alive. The point is to feel the in-purpose quality, whatever its texture, not to impose a peaceful overlay.
- It is not a visualization-for-success practice. The pitch in the visualization can go badly and the purpose can still be present. Purpose is the quality of how you are being, not a guarantee of how the activity turns out.
- It is not a substitute for action. The visualization is meant to ride along with action, not replace it. The point is to take the in-purpose quality into the actual meeting, the actual pitch, the actual conversation — not to feel it in private and stop there.

## Failure Modes

- **The mind constructs an image of "looking purposeful."** The visualization produces a picture of a poised, confident, glowing version of oneself doing the activity. The body, meanwhile, registers nothing different. This is the visualization staying in the head. The correction is to drop attention from the picture down into the body and ask what the body feels.
- **The reminders get ignored.** The five-or-six-times-a-day prompt is silenced or dismissed as "not a good moment." This is the most common failure. The exit is to set reminders that are harder to silence (calendar invites with notifications, written reminders in places that are unavoidable) and to commit to actually pausing for thirty seconds when they fire.
- **The "in purpose" quality is reported as binary.** "I was in purpose" or "I wasn't in purpose." The actual experience is more like a gradient — more in purpose now, less in purpose then. Treating it as binary cuts off most of the useful data. See [[what-feels-more-in-purpose-right-now]] for the gradient frame.
- **The practice is held as a should.** The reminders become evidence of failure ("I missed three of them today, I'm bad at this"). The practice was meant to operationalize purpose, not to install a new flavor of self-monitoring. If it has become a should, drop it for a week and come back with a different orientation. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]] for the parallel pattern in another practice.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone presents [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] and needs an embodied experiment instead of more thinking
- When someone has just landed on [[purpose-is-how-not-what]] in principle and needs an operational way to live it
- When someone is preparing for a big-deal moment (pitch, raise, presentation) and wants to bring purpose into it rather than performance
- When someone has been doing the [[purpose-checkin-alarm-practice]] and wants a complementary visualization to deepen the in-the-moment availability
- As a closing experiment in a teaching session — Joe ends the segment with this experiment so the listener leaves with something to do rather than something to think about

## Tone

Calm, instructional, brief. Joe delivers this as a short experiment at the end of a teaching segment, with no fanfare. The framing is "let's just try this" — low stakes, immediate, available right now. The reminder instruction is offered as practical operations, not as a moral imperative. The whole practice fits in under two minutes of guidance.
