# Three Reflection Questions on Anger

## The Practice

Three questions to sit with after working with anger as a topic. Each opens a different door. None is meant to be answered quickly. Each is meant to live in the body for days or weeks.

**1. What's necessary for you to express your anger without hurting anybody or doing it at anybody? What would it take for you to feel safe doing that?**

**2. How can you love your anger unconditionally? How can you love your anger the same way that you wish to love the kids that you have or the kids that you hope to have?**

**3. What would it take for you to never get angry at somebody again and to allow yourself to be angry whenever you were?**

"I have 3. The first one is, what's necessary for you to express your anger without hurting anybody or doing it at anybody? What would it take for you to feel safe doing that? And then another question I would ask is, how can you love your anger unconditionally? How can you love your anger the same way that you wish to love the kids that you have or the kids that you hope to have? And the last question I would put out is, what would it take for you to never get angry at somebody again and to allow yourself to be angry whenever you were?" -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

## What Each Question Opens

**Question 1: the container question.** What's necessary for you to express your anger without hurting anybody. This question presupposes that anger will be expressed (not whether). It moves the inquiry to logistics: where, when, how, with what safety. It surfaces the absence of containers in the person's life. The answers are often practical: a car, a beach, a time of day, a phrase to tell the family. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the entry-level container most people end up with.

The second half ("what would it take for you to feel safe doing that?") is the deeper question. Often the safety the person needs is not external. It is permission. It is knowing the body will not be punished. It is being convinced that what arrives will not destroy them. See [[fear-of-self-destruction-by-anger]].

**Question 2: the love question.** How can you love your anger unconditionally, the same way you would love a child. The comparison is not accidental. Most people have an easier time imagining unconditional love for a child than for their own anger. The question uses the easier case to point at the harder one. If you can imagine welcoming a tantrumming child without trying to fix or silence them, you can imagine welcoming your own anger that way. See [[loving-all-parts]].

The "unconditionally" is the load-bearing word. It rules out the conditional welcomes that look like welcome but are not: "I'll love my anger when it produces clarity," "I'll love my anger when it's reasonable," "I'll love my anger when it doesn't hurt anyone." Each condition is a refusal. The question asks: what would it look like to drop all conditions.

**Question 3: the both-and question.** What would it take to never get angry at somebody again and to allow yourself to be angry whenever you were. Two things at once: refuse to aim anger at people, and refuse to suppress it. This is the integration question. Most people collapse the two into one: either get angry at people, or do not get angry at all. The question refuses the collapse. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for one half and [[kinked-emotions]] for what happens when the other half is missed.

What it would take is the actual investigation. Some likely answers: a daily practice, a private place, the felt experience that anger does not destroy you, language for telling family you need to leave the room, a relationship with the emotion itself that is not adversarial. The question is open-ended on purpose; the work is the inventory.

## How to Use the Questions

These are not questions to answer in one sitting. They are questions to live with.

The recommended approach: pick one. Carry it for a week. Notice what arises. Notice the resistance. Notice the moments when an answer appears unbidden. Write what comes. Move to the next one when the first has done its work.

Working all three at once flattens them. Each one is doing different work. Holding them sequentially lets each one open what it opens.

## When the Questions Stop Working

If the questions become intellectual loops, the practice has drifted. The signal: the person is producing clever answers without the body shifting. The move is to drop the question and feel the anger that is present. The questions are pointers, not the work. See [[dont-block-emotion-waiting-for-reason]].

If the questions produce shame ("I cannot answer this, I am bad at anger"), the practice has been hijacked by the inner critic. The move is to notice the shame as shame and let the question rest. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]].

## Tone

Joe offers these as questions, not as instructions. He puts them out and lets them be. The tone is invitational. The questions are not tests. They are doors.
