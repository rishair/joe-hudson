# Defend vs. Open to All Past Judgments

## The Practice

A two-stage somatic experiment that lets the body register, by direct comparison, the felt difference between defending against judgment and being open to it. The experiment uses the practitioner's full history of being judged — every person who ever thought anything about them — as the material, then asks them to try both stances and feel which one is more alive in their body.

## The Setup

Make yourself comfortable. Take a breath. Get into your body, from the bottom of your feet to the top of your head. Now imagine all the people who have ever judged you. That includes basically every friend, every family member, every coworker, countless strangers on the street. Some thought you were too ugly, some too pretty. Some thought you were too smart, some not smart enough. Some thought you were too emotional, some not emotional enough. Some thought you were too loud, some too quiet. Imagine all of those people and all those millions of times you have been judged.

The full scope of the imagining matters. The practice is not "think about one judgment." It is "feel the field of all judgments." The body needs to register the scale.

## Stage One: Defend

Feel what it is like to defend yourself against all of those judgments. You can literally say, out loud or to yourself, "No, your judgments aren't true." You can build a nice piece of armor that makes all the judgments bounce off. Feel what that is like in your body — to construct the wall, to push back, to argue against, to refute, to refuse to let anything in.

Sit in this stance for long enough that the body has fully entered it. Notice the physical signature:

- Where is the tension?
- What is happening in your chest?
- What is happening in your jaw?
- What is your breath doing?
- How does your aliveness feel?
- How does your energy feel?
- What is the felt-quality of this posture?

Take a deep breath. Shake it off.

## Stage Two: Open

Close your eyes again. Imagine all those same people who have judged you. This time do the exact opposite of defending. Be completely open. It does not mean you have to believe their judgments. It does not mean you have to agree with them. The job is just to not defend. To be open. To be open-hearted.

You can put attention into your heart. You can put your hand on your heart. Think of all those people with no defense. Feel what it is like in your body when you do not defend against the judgment.

Sit in this stance for long enough that the body has fully entered it. Notice the physical signature:

- Where has the tension released?
- What is happening in your chest?
- What is happening in your jaw?
- What is your breath doing?
- How does your aliveness feel?
- How does your energy feel?
- What is the felt-quality of this posture?

## The Closing Question

The question that gathers the experiment:

> "Which of these two things was most comfortable? Which of these two things made you most alive? When you defended against the judgments or when you stayed open?"

The body answers without thought. The opening produces aliveness. The defense produces depletion. The data is immediate and visceral.

## What the Experiment Reveals

The structural insight that lands: the defended stance is the source of the suffering, not the judgment itself. The judgment is information that arrives. The defense is what the practitioner does with the information. The defense is what is producing the chronic tension, the depletion, the fight-readiness. The opening releases the tension and produces aliveness.

The body has been holding a wrong belief: "defending against judgment protects me." The experiment shows the body the actual cost of the defense — chronic tension, lost energy, deadened aliveness. And shows the body what is available when the defense releases — space, breath, life.

The point is not that the judgments are good or that the practitioner should welcome being judged. The point is that defending against the judgments is itself the suffering. The practitioner has been organizing their life around preventing the judgments from landing, and the organizing has been costing more than the landing would have cost. See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for the underlying principle: attack requires defense to attack against; without defense, the attack has nothing to land on.

## Why the Full-Field Setup Matters

The experiment uses "all the people who have ever judged you" rather than a single judgment for a specific reason. A single judgment can be argued with, dismissed, or rationalized. The full field is too large for any of those moves. The practitioner cannot defend against millions of judgments simultaneously without entering the chronic-defense state. The full field forces the recognition that the defense has been operating continuously, against an effectively infinite stream of judgments, for the practitioner's entire life.

The same is true of the openness side. Opening to one specific judgment is a narrow experiment. Opening to the full field of all judgments is a wholesale shift in operating stance. The body has to choose between two operating modes, not between two specific responses.

This is also what makes the experiment more powerful than a thought-exercise about a particular criticism. The thought-exercise stays in the cognitive layer. The full-field somatic experiment is operating at the level of the chronic posture the practitioner has been holding for their whole life.

## What to Notice in Stage One

The defended stance often produces:

- Tightening in the chest or solar plexus
- Jaw clenching
- Held or shallow breath
- A bracing in the shoulders
- A pulling-in or hardening of the body
- A sense of needing to be ready for the next attack
- Fatigue or depletion
- A felt-sense of smallness, isolation, or being besieged

The fact that this is the chronic state for many people is part of what the experiment reveals. The defended state is not exceptional; it is the operating baseline. The exceptional thing is to feel it as exceptional, because the defense has been backgrounded.

## What to Notice in Stage Two

The open stance often produces:

- Release in the chest
- Breath returning to fullness
- A sense of expansion
- Lightness
- A felt-sense of vitality or aliveness
- A surprising warmth
- Sometimes tears: the body releasing the long-held tension
- A felt-sense of safety, even though the judgments are still being imagined

The aliveness is often striking. The body has been holding the defense at such a low-grade chronic level that the practitioner does not recognize how much energy it was consuming until the defense releases. The release produces the kind of energy-return that a person does not realize they were missing until they get it back.

## The Pairing With Real Life

After the experiment, the practitioner can carry the openness stance into actual moments of being judged. The body has now experienced both stances. When a judgment arrives — from a colleague, a partner, a stranger, an internet commenter — the practitioner has the choice the experiment installed.

The choice is not abstract. The choice is: do I tighten or do I stay open? The body now knows what each option feels like. The practice in real time becomes "drop the defense, stay open." The practice can be repeated indefinitely because judgments are constant.

The signature of doing it well is the same signature the experiment surfaced in stage two: breath returning, aliveness returning, sense of expansion. The signature of doing it as another performance (be-the-ocean-as-strategy) is the same signature stage one surfaced: bracing, tightness, sense of being above. The body is the diagnostic. See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for the principle and its tone-discrimination ("if you don't feel empowered and invigorated, you're still in the shame").

## What to Do With What Surfaces

The experiment often surfaces grief — the recognition of how much energy has been spent defending, how much aliveness has been suppressed. The grief is part of the work. Let it move.

The experiment also often surfaces fear: "if I don't defend, won't I be destroyed?" The fear is based on a misreading of what gets destroyed. The defended self is at risk in the move; the actual self is not. See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for the structural account. The fear can be held without being resolved; the experiment continues to work even when the fear is present.

The natural follow-on is to keep the stage-two stance available in real life. Each instance of judgment becomes a rep. Over time the defended posture weakens. The body's default begins to shift toward openness. See [[immune-to-shame-through-non-defense]] for the long-arc result.

## A Variation: For Self-Judgment

The same structure works for self-judgment. Imagine all the judgments you have ever made of yourself — every harsh thought, every internal criticism, every contempt for yourself. Feel what it is like to defend against them (which is structurally complicated — you are the source of the judgments, so the defense is internal civil war). Then feel what it is like to be open to them — to let them land, to let them be felt, to not defend against your own inner critic.

The internal version is harder because there is no other-to-blame. The judgments are the practitioner's own. The opening to them is the practice of receiving one's own contempt without arguing with it. This is the work of [[find-the-emotion-under-self-judgment]] and [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]].

Use the external version when the practitioner is over-managing external reputation. Use the internal version when the inner critic is the dominant source of suffering.

## Tone

Joe delivers this slowly, with long pauses. The setup is the longest part — the imagining of all the judgments has to land before the stances can be tried. The voice is warm but spacious, leaving room for the body to enter each stance.

The stage-one instructions have a slight intensity: "build a nice piece of armor." Joe is asking the practitioner to fully enter the defended stance, not to think about it. The intensity is what makes the felt-contrast available in stage two.

The stage-two instructions are softer, slower, more invitational. The opening is not forced; it is offered. The voice models the stance.

The closing question is asked simply, without pressure. The practitioner's body will answer.
