# Approach the Edge and Say No (With the Group Honoring the No)

## The Practice

A participant in a fear-practice setting -- a retreat, a workshop, a group facing a hard collective task -- approaches the edge of the difficult thing (the cliff, the conversation, the decision) with the explicit intention not to do it. They walk up to the brink, say no, and the group responds: "we love your no." The no is fully received. The participant's right to not do the thing is honored explicitly and warmly.

What often happens next: the participant, having had their no fully honored, finds themselves choosing the yes. The thing they were blocked from doing becomes available. The block was not the absence of will; the block was the unmet no that had to be acknowledged before the yes could be authentic.

The setup, in Brett's words:

"One of the people who was jumping, it was suggested like, hey, like, why don't we just do this? Like, you approach the edge of the cliff and you're ready to jump. You don't have to jump. In fact, you can intend not to jump. But just have the experience of walking up and saying no. And then the group says, we love your no." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The outcome, in this specific case:

"And this person went up to do that experiment and they found themselves jumping. Like, that was just having that no honored by the group and brought fully to the surface and loved in themselves." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The practice succeeded by the unconventional criterion (the no was honored) and unexpectedly produced the conventional outcome (the jump happened) as a side effect.

## Why It Works

The mechanism: a person blocked from doing a hard thing often has an unmet no underneath. The block is the no that has not been allowed to register. As long as the no is not registered, every yes is contaminated by the unregistered no. The yes that arrives without the no being acknowledged is partial, anxious, gripping. The body knows the no is there and refuses to move forward cleanly.

When the no is fully honored -- not as a fallback option, not as a polite acknowledgment, but as a fully-received choice -- the no completes. The completion releases the block. The yes, if it arrives, arrives clean. It is no longer the yes that is fighting the no; it is the yes that has been chosen after the no was honored.

This connects to [[no-doctrine-leadership]] and [[lead-with-the-no]]: the no is the door to the yes. Skipping the no and trying to extract the yes produces neither. Honoring the no produces both the freedom of the no and, often, the natural arrival of the yes.

## What Distinguishes This from Permission

The practice is not "you have permission to not do it." Permission still positions the not-doing as a failure mode that is being tolerated. The actual no in the practice is not a fallback. The participant is being asked to intend the no, to plan for the no, to walk up to the edge with the no as their actual choice. The group then loves the no as the chosen action it is.

This distinction matters because permission-language preserves the framing in which the yes is the "real" desired outcome. The no-as-actual-choice framing dissolves that hierarchy. Both the no and the yes are legitimate outcomes. The participant is freed to discover which one is actually theirs.

The "we love your no" line carries the work. It is not "we are okay with your no" or "we support your no." It is "we love your no." The love is the operative ingredient. The no is being received with the same warmth that a yes would be received with. The participant's nervous system can feel that the no is genuinely welcomed, not endured.

## The Group's Role

The practice requires a group capable of actually honoring the no. A group that is secretly disappointed when the participant says no -- even unconsciously -- contaminates the practice. The participant senses the disappointment and the no fails to complete. The yes that emerges, if any, is the yes of caving to group pressure, which is the same problem in a new costume.

The group preparation for this practice is therefore as important as the participant preparation. The group needs to have done their own work on the no enough that they can actually love it. They cannot perform loving the no while secretly wanting the participant to jump. The discrepancy will be felt.

This connects to [[fear-hot-potato]] in its inverse form: a group that has been throwing fear around cannot honor an individual's no, because the no triggers their own un-felt fear. A group that has each person holding their own fear can honor another person's no because the no does not threaten anyone's emotional regulation.

## The Conditions Under Which It Produces a Yes

The practice does not always produce a yes. Sometimes the no is the actual choice and the participant walks away without jumping. This is also a successful run of the practice. The participant has had the experience of their no being honored, has acted from their actual choice, and has not been coerced by group pressure or self-pressure into doing something that was not theirs to do.

The cases where the no produces the yes have a particular structure. The participant wants to do the thing. The wanting is real. What is blocking them is not the absence of want but the presence of an unprocessed no. Once the no is processed (acknowledged, loved, allowed to be the actual choice), the want becomes available again. The participant then chooses the yes from a place of want rather than from a place of overriding the no.

This is the same structure Brett describes in the broader principle:

"The person who's like, hey, I just learned that when I'm scared I get angry. Or this person who had pattern X. They needed to move through that and feel and love the whole thing. To be able to step off of the cliff." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The "feel and love the whole thing" includes loving the no that wants to stop. The action that follows is the action that is left when nothing has been overridden.

## What This Practice Replaces

The default frame in fear practice: encourage the participant to push through. "You can do it." "Just take the step." "Don't let the fear win." These framings position the no as the enemy. The yes is extracted by overpowering the no. The participant may or may not do the thing; if they do it, they do it from override rather than from choice. The body carries the override forward into the action. The post-event report is often "I did it but it didn't feel like mine."

The approach-and-say-no practice inverts this. The no is not the enemy. The no is part of what is alive in the participant. Honoring it is honoring them. The action that follows is theirs. The post-event report, when there is a yes, is often "I jumped, and it was the cleanest thing I have ever done."

## In Coaching

The structural form of this practice extends well beyond fear retreats. Any time a coachee is blocked from doing something they "should" want to do, the practice can be adapted:

- Coachee: "I can't bring myself to have the hard conversation with my partner."
- Coach: "What if you don't? What if the conversation just doesn't happen?"
- Coachee: "But I have to..."
- Coach: "What if you don't?"
- (The coachee's no is invited fully. The coach does not lobby for the conversation. The coach honors the possibility of not having it.)

What often emerges, if the coachee genuinely sits with the not-having-it: clarity about what they actually want. Sometimes the conversation becomes available because the no has been honored. Sometimes the coachee discovers that the conversation was not actually theirs to have and they can move on. Either outcome is a successful coaching move.

The same structure works for: a career decision the coachee feels they should make but can't; a project they feel they should start but won't; a relationship they feel they should end but haven't; a habit they feel they should change but don't. In each case, the "should" is what is blocking the move. Honoring the no -- the actual choice to not do the thing -- often produces either a clean exit from the should or a clean entry into the action.

See [[every-yes-needs-a-real-no]] for the broader principle this practice operationalizes.

## What This Practice Does Not Mean

The practice does not mean: collapse every commitment into "well, do you really want to?" Some commitments are made because they are commitments, and re-litigating them every time the activation rises is its own dysfunction. The practice is for blocks, not for ordinary friction.

The practice also does not mean: groups should always honor every no. A group can have legitimate expectations of its members. A team has commitments to each other. A community has agreements. The practice is for moments where a participant is on the edge of a hard, personal, voluntary action and the question is whether to take it. It is not a license to renegotiate group agreements unilaterally.

The practice also does not work without the rest of the container. A participant who says no in a group that has not done the preparation work will not have the experience of being honored. The group's capacity is the practice's prerequisite. Without it, the practice cannot run.

## In Facilitation Design

For facilitators designing a fear retreat, a difficult workshop, or any group practice involving voluntary hard actions, the approach-and-say-no exercise is worth building in as an explicit option. Naming it as a possible practice, before the difficult action arrives, lets participants know that the no is genuinely available. The availability itself relaxes the activation. Some participants will use the exercise. Others will know it is there and not need it.

The facilitator's preparation work: getting the group to a place where they can genuinely honor a no. This is usually done through earlier exercises in which the group practices loving each other's choices, including small choices like "I don't want to share right now" or "I want to skip this round." The honoring muscle has to be built before it is asked to carry a load like "I am not going to jump off the cliff."

## Tone

Brett describes this with warmth and a kind of delighted surprise. The practice produced something neither he nor the participant expected. The lightness of "we love your no" is the operative quality; said heavily, the line would not work. Said with genuine warmth, it can dissolve a block that pushing would have hardened.
