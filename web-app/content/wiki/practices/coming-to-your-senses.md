# Coming to Your Senses (Put Attention on the Sensations on the Outside of Your Body)

## The Practice

The instruction is simple. Take a deep breath. Make a little sound on the exhale. Put attention on the sensations on the outside of the body. That's it. Within about a minute, the nervous system is calmer.

The "outside of the body" is the operational part. Not the inner sensations the person is already churning on. The skin. The fingertips. The soles of the feet. The air against the face. The texture of whatever is under the hand.

Several specific entries make the practice concrete:

- Put a hand on anything and feel the texture. Move the hand across the surface and stay with what the fingertips report.
- Wiggle the toes and notice how the soles of the feet feel against the shoe or the floor.
- Notice how the air feels on the skin.
- Put the hands together and rub the thumbs together slowly, staying with the sensation.
- Put all available attention on the bottom of the feet, and keep returning to it.
- Listen to the silence around the words being spoken — the higher frequencies, the lower frequencies that are silent right now. Pay attention to those.

"Your job is just to put your attention on the sensations on the outside of your body... and you'll notice that already your nervous system is more calm. Already everything is less stressful. It took about a minute, and all that's required is that you slow down and feel the sensations on the outside of your body." -- Joe Hudson, How To End 60,000 Negative Thoughts a Day

## What It Does

The practice downshifts the nervous system by occupying attention with sense-data rather than thought-data. The voice in the head ([[voice-in-the-head]]) runs on attention. When attention is directed to a sensation that is genuinely happening right now, the voice loses its operational fuel. The hum of cognition that produces most stress quiets without being fought.

This is one of the three categories of [[three-categories-of-downregulation]] — the sensation category, distinct from the breath category (humming, box breathing, 6-6 breath) and the attention category (listening to silence between words).

The "outside of the body" specification matters because much of inner-body attention is already in the field that produces the stress. The chest is tight; the stomach is in knots; the jaw is clenched. Going to inner sensation can deepen the contact with the stress that is already happening. Going to outside-of-body sensation produces a different operation: it gives attention something fresh, something that is happening now, something that is not the stress.

## Why It Works in Under a Minute

The body's sensory apparatus is exquisitely sensitive. Once attention is directed to the fingertips, the fingertips report a lot of data — texture, temperature, micro-pressure, the way the skin feels against itself when the thumbs rub. The brain has not been processing this data because attention has been on thought. When the data is allowed in, the system has something to engage with that is real and present, and the threat-response that has been running on thought-content begins to quiet.

This is also why creating the sensation works as well as finding one already happening: rubbing the thumbs together produces sensation that the person can attend to. The point is not that the sensation was always there waiting to be noticed. The point is that attention on sensation, however generated, downshifts the system. The practice is sometimes more reliable with deliberately created sensation because there is no question what to attend to.

## When to Use It

- In the middle of a stressful moment when stepping away is not possible
- Before a call or meeting that has been producing background activation
- When the inner critic has been firing and the person needs a reset before the next conversation
- While dialing the help desk or any specific recurring stress trigger that can become a cue for the practice (see "Make It a Trigger-Cue" below)
- As a preparation for sleep — combined with breath, the practice helps the system stop producing the activation that prevents falling asleep
- When the body has been bracing without the person noticing — the redirection to outside-the-body sensation surfaces the bracing and allows it to release
- As an in-session tool when a coachee is too activated to feel a more interior sensation cleanly
- When grounding is needed before doing deeper emotional work — see [[notice-pleasure-and-sensation-as-grounding]] for the related re-entry practice

## Make It a Trigger-Cue

A specific operational form: pair the practice with a recurring stress trigger so that the trigger itself becomes the cue. The example: a person who knows they get frustrated calling the help desk can install humming or rubbing the thumbs together as the act of dialing.

"It's really nice to get into the pattern of doing these things when a certain thing triggers it, the same way that you would do coffee, right? You wake up in the morning and you walk past the coffee place, you smell the coffee, you go in and you get the coffee. It's like, oh, I know that I get frustrated when I call up the help desk and the person doesn't understand me and they never help me out. So as I'm dialing, I'm hmm. Just these little tricks to downregulate yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How To End 60,000 Negative Thoughts a Day

The trigger-cue form is high-yield because it uses an already-established neural pathway (the trigger) to deliver the new pattern (the downshift). Trying to remember to do the practice on its own usually fails — there is no cue. Letting the existing stress-trigger become the cue is structurally cleaner: the system already notices the trigger; the practice is what changes about the response to the trigger.

## What This Practice Does Not Do

This practice handles the immediate activation. It does not handle the underlying issue producing the activation. See [[downregulation-handles-the-symptom-not-the-source]].

A specific cautionary signature: a person who downshifts effectively and then notices that the original frustration is still there once the practice ends. The frustration is the data. The practice cleared the surface noise so the deeper signal could be heard. The mistake is to use the practice to keep clearing the deeper signal as it arises. See [[dont-let-downregulation-become-avoidance]] and [[breath-to-self-regulate-instead-of-feel]] for the related anti-patterns.

The right use of this practice is as a bridge — the system needs to come down enough to be able to address what is actually loaded, then the address can happen. The wrong use is to keep the system permanently in the downshifted state to avoid the address.

## Tone

Slow. Spacious. The instruction itself is given in a slowed-down voice — the practice is doing what it's describing as it's being described. "Slowly feel the sensations" is the operational tone. The pace of the instruction is part of how the practice works.

There is no urgency in the delivery. Speed in the instruction would contradict the practice. The teaching has to embody the state it is producing.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not turn this into a meditation discipline. The practice is for in-the-moment regulation. The 20-minute formal-sit form is a different practice with a different intent.
- Do not chase the calm. The point is not to feel calm; the point is to be with sensation. Calm tends to follow as a byproduct. Chasing it makes it elusive.
- Do not skip "the outside." Going inward to the body — to the tight chest, the clenched jaw — is valuable somatic work but it is not this practice. This practice is specifically outside.
- Do not use it to avoid feeling what is loaded. See [[dont-let-downregulation-become-avoidance]]. The downshift opens space; what fills the space is the next move.
