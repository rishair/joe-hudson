# Progressive Build of Somatic Awareness (Skin, Gut, Heart, Held Memory)

## The Practice

A three-step progression for building a relationship with the body from scratch. The progression is calibrated for the head-dominant beginner who has no current relationship with the body and needs an on-ramp that starts simple, gets a small win, and adds layers. The progression is: (1) the skin as the simplest starter, (2) the gut or heart as the next layer of internal sensation, (3) the body's response to a remembered event as the integration layer.

Each step is a small unit. Each unit can be done in a few minutes. The build is cumulative — each layer becomes available because the previous layer was practiced.

## Step One: The Skin

The simplest possible somatic access point. The skin is the most external layer of the body and the easiest to feel. Sensations in the skin are direct, undeniable, and pre-emotional. The starter is below any threshold of difficulty.

The instruction:

"Start simply just bringing attention to your hand. And you can kind of even close your hand and open your hand. What's the sensations on the hand? Maybe you close your hand and you even feel your fingers on your palm. Like, oh, that's somatic awareness, just listening to the sensory information of the fingers rubbing the palm." -- Tara Hawley

Variations on the same theme:

- Rub the fingers together. Feel the friction.
- Rub the hands on the legs. Feel the texture of the pants.
- Feel the warmth or coolness of the skin.
- Feel whether the skin is soft or coarse.
- "Put your ears in your hand" — listen with the hand the way you listen with the ears.

The framing matters: the practitioner is not trying to feel something dramatic. They are noticing what is already there. The skin is constantly reporting. The practice is just turning attention to the report.

## Step Two: The Gut or Heart (Internal Sensation)

Once the skin layer is accessible, attention can move inward to internal sensation. The two starter locations are the gut and the heart. Different people find one easier than the other; both are equally valid entry points.

**The gut.** Drop attention down to around the belly button. Use breath to be there — feel the belly expanding slightly. Notice subtle sensation: a gurgle, a warmth, a fullness, a tension. The standard for "feeling something" is low. Subtle digestive activity counts. The point is the attention is there, not that something dramatic is happening.

"Dropping awareness down to the gut. So dropping the thinking mind. You can think of it as taking like an elevator ride all the way down to around your belly button and then using awareness to look around. So you can be looking visually, how the gut feels, but also sensing how does the gut feel. And you can use your breath here, even like bringing breath to it, and you might sense your belly expanding slightly." -- Tara Hawley

**The heart.** Bring attention to the chest. The chest is also easy for breath access. The heart area is the center of emotional sensation. Attention here may surface ache, tension, crinkles, warmth, sleepiness, softness. Old heartaches may show up as constriction or tension in the front of the heart.

"For some people, the gut is easiest. For some people, the heart. You can bring your attention to the chest. And again, the chest is an easy area to feel the breath moving through. And this is the center of our heart, the center of emotions. So sometimes just by bringing that awareness down we start to sense ache." -- Tara Hawley

The choice between gut and heart is made by trying both and noticing which is more readily available. Whichever is more accessible becomes the working location. The less-accessible one becomes available later as the practice deepens.

## Step Three: The Body's Response to a Remembered Event

Once internal sensation is available, the practice can integrate emotional history. The instruction: bring to mind a recent hard conversation or event, and notice how the body responds.

"And then third step, for me at least, is thinking about like, oh, I said XYZ to so-and-so, and that was a really hard conversation. And I can notice like, oh, my body wants to close up as I think about that hard conversation." -- Tara Hawley

This is where the practice becomes therapeutic. The body holds the residue of past events. Attention to the body while remembering the event makes the residue visible — and, more importantly, gives it a channel to release.

The full move:

1. Bring to mind the event.
2. Notice the body's response. (Shoulders closing in? Heart protecting? Jaw tightening? Breath catching?)
3. Let the body take the shape it wants to take. Do not correct it.
4. Breathe with it. Be company for what the body is doing.
5. Allow the body to sequence. As attention and breath are present, the body will often complete what it was holding. Shoulders drop. Posture softens. Breath deepens. Tenderness arrives.

"With my awareness and my breath, the body can start to sequence and move in a way it needs to. Like, I just noticed my shoulders dropping. They're rolling back a little. Everything's softening. My heart's coming on. And I can notice a tenderness for myself having that hard conversation. That was hard. And I can notice compassion and tenderness for the other person from this place too." -- Tara Hawley

The sequencing happens in seconds when the attention and breath are there. The body knows how to release. The practitioner's job is to let it.

This is the same mechanism as [[emotional-inquiry]] applied to held material rather than to an arriving emotion. The body is given the conditions it needs to complete what it has been carrying. See also [[emotions-live-in-muscles]] for why the body holds residue in muscle in the first place.

## The Gym-Reps Approach

The practice is built like physical training. Short reps. Frequent. Recovery time between sessions. Not all-day sits.

"Learning body awareness is like learning any new skill. It's like going to the gym, go once, do a few reps, take a break. Sometimes what people do is they come in and do it all day long. I encourage you to try it. Usually in the beginning, people get really bored because we're so used to being entertained by thoughts." -- Tara Hawley

The reason for short reps: the head will fight long sits. The boredom of attention-in-the-body is the head's protest. A short rep finishes before the protest can mount a successful resistance. Many short reps compound into a real skill faster than one long forced sit.

"So really the first muscle to build is like, okay, thank you, mind. I usually give you all my attention. Right now I'm gonna put my attention on my core or my body and my gut. And so just building that muscle of, oh, a head just took me to a drifting thought. I'm gonna come back and what is happening there?" -- Tara Hawley

The instruction to the mind is not aggressive. It is a polite redirect: thank you, mind, I am putting attention elsewhere for a moment. The relationship to the mind stays cooperative. The mind is not the enemy. The mind is just being asked to step aside briefly so the body can be heard.

For the principle that makes these reps stick over time, see [[make-the-practice-juicy]]. The shortness of the reps and the pleasure of the experience together determine whether the practice survives the first two weeks.

## What This Practice Differs From

- From [[staying-in-your-body]]: that practice is the ongoing background attention to the body throughout daily life. This practice is the explicit dedicated build for the person who does not yet have the access required to do the ongoing version. This practice is the foundation; staying-in-your-body is what becomes available after.
- From [[attention-is-feeling]]: that move is a Socratic intervention for the person who claims they cannot feel their body. This practice is the structured progressive build for after the access has been established (or for the person who has access but no organized approach).
- From [[emotional-inquiry]]: that practice is the full VIEW-applied-inward protocol for meeting an arriving emotion. This practice is the body-access build that makes emotional inquiry possible. Step three of this practice is the simplest form of emotional inquiry directed at held material.
- From [[daily-emotional-yoga]]: that practice is the scheduled dedicated time to feel suppressed emotion. This practice is the beginner's somatic on-ramp before emotional yoga becomes possible.

## Failure Modes (and Their Reframes)

Three patterns Tara names that derail the practice. Each has a specific reframe:

**"I don't feel anything."** The practitioner drops attention into the body and reports nothing. The reframe: nothing is data. See the dedicated section below.

**"I get flooded by the nervous system."** Attention into the body triggers anxiety, panic, or activation. The reframe: this is the system being innervated for the first time. It is not a problem. Calm the nervous system (breath, slow down, name that it has not been safe to do this before), be gentle, and try again later in smaller doses. See [[nervous-system-support-during-intense-inquiry]].

**"I get flooded by an emotion."** Attention into the body triggers grief, anger, or fear that is too much. The reframe: there is a lot of held material. The practice can continue, but it needs to be balanced one-to-one with self-care. See [[one-to-one-grief-and-self-care]].

The common error across all three: treating the response as a sign that something is wrong with the practitioner. The response is the practice working. Each variant has a specific care path.

## "I Don't Feel Anything" Is Data

The most common derailment is the report "I don't feel anything." Tara's reframe inverts the framing:

"If you don't feel anything, that's awesome. What's it like not to feel anything? And actually bring the attention to nothing and to the absence of feeling and explore that. If you think you don't feel anything, that's some, you're getting data somewhere that tells you you don't feel anything." -- Tara Hawley

The somatic information includes the quality of the not-feeling. Is it quiet? Is it void? Is it muffled? Is it numb? Each of those is a distinct felt experience. Exploring the absence is itself the practice.

"You can just, I like thinking of it as exploring the raw data. So instead of letting the mind tell you, I don't feel anything, go and explore what's — What are the data points that tell you I don't feel anything? So you might bring your gut down, like, I don't feel anything in my gut. I'm like, oh, how do I know I don't feel anything? Because it's quiet. Feels like a void. I don't sense anything. Everything just goes quiet. Well, that's somatic information." -- Tara Hawley

This is the same logic as the [[attention-is-feeling]] move applied as a self-instruction. The "nothing" is something. Attend to the nothing. The not-feeling has texture.

## What This Produces

- A working relationship with the body that can be deepened indefinitely.
- Access to emotional information that was previously offline.
- Access to gut wisdom and intuition as deliberate instruments.
- The capacity to be present in conversations, decisions, and work without effort.
- The capacity to release held material from past events in a few seconds.
- Bliss and joy on the other side of the initial discomfort, as the body's natural state becomes available.

"On the other side of being with those feelings in the body is peace and joy and bliss and wisdom and insight and intuition. So if you can stick with the discomfort in the body, there is an amazing wealth of — it's like a gold mine of information that you're walking around with. And I can vouch for the bliss that is in a body, absolute bliss. And the just the simplicity of our breath can become incredibly pleasurable, or just sensing our toes." -- Tara Hawley

## Tone

Tara delivers this with warmth, patience, and an explicit anti-rush message. She models the practice as gentle. She uses "yummy" and "juicy" deliberately to mark that this is not another should. The whole protocol carries a tone of "take your time, this is for you, there is no rush."
