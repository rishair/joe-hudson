# Respond to the Voice in Your Head in a Way You Never Have Before

## The Practice

The instruction is simple. The execution is hard at first and then becomes a doorway.

Most people have a repetitive negative self-talker. The Mayo Clinic estimate Joe cites is around 50,000 to 60,000 thoughts a day, most of them repetitive. People notice the voice at some point. They want to get rid of it. The more they resist, the more it persists. The voice does not actually change.

The practice: every day, or every three days, pick a way to react to that voice that you have never reacted to before. Not to silence it. Not to argue with it. Not to comply with it. Just to react in a way you have never reacted before.

"Pick a way you want to react to that voice in the head that you've never reacted that way before." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

The variations are infinite. Joe gives examples to demonstrate the range:

- "Wow, I could really use you to be a better boss."
- "No." (just no)
- "I see how scared you are and I'm right here with you."
- Clap. Have a ton of applause arrive for the voice.
- Throw the voice a surprise birthday party in your mind.
- Bow. Thank it sincerely.
- Laugh.
- "What do you actually want?"
- Sit and wait for it to finish.
- "Tell me more."

The point is not which response. The point is that the response is new. The voice has been receiving the same two or three reactions from you for years (compliance, suppression, resistance, fear, agreement). A new reaction interrupts the pattern at the level of the relationship, not at the level of the content.

## Why It Works

The practice operates on several layers at once.

**It empties the self.** Joe describes the practice as "a great way that starts emptying you out really quickly." The voice in the head is one of the most load-bearing structures of the constructed self. Most people identify with it (either by following it or by fighting it). Responding in unprecedented ways breaks the identification by treating the voice as separate from the response. The space between voice and response is the space where the self loosens.

**It bypasses the resist-persist trap.** The standard moves with the inner critic (trying to silence it, trying to argue with it, trying to be "positive") all reinforce its grip because they treat it as something that needs to be managed. The new-response practice does not try to manage it. It just experiments. The lack of management is what loosens the grip.

**It surfaces what the voice actually is.** When you respond to the voice with "I see how scared you are and I'm right here with you," the voice is revealed as a scared part of you. When you respond with applause, the voice is revealed as a hungry-for-approval part of you. Each new response makes a different facet visible. Over time, the voice stops being a monolithic enemy and starts being a structure of parts the person can actually meet.

**It builds the experimentation muscle.** The practice is a specific operational form of the broader [[experimentation]] orientation. The capacity to try things in your own inner life and watch what happens is the capacity that underlies most of the work. The inner-voice practice is a daily rep of that capacity.

## What Tends to Happen

Joe reports that people who do this practice unfold faster than people doing some early stages of meditation. The result is not a dramatic event. It is a steady loosening. The voice that used to feel like a tormentor becomes a part of self that can be related to. The space around the voice expands. The grip of the voice on behavior loosens.

The critical conditions Joe names:

**You are not trying to get rid of it.** The practice fails the moment the orientation becomes "I will respond in new ways until the voice stops." The new-response practice is not a technique for elimination. It is an investigation. The investigation has to be sincere. If it is covert elimination, the practice converts back into resistance and the voice persists.

**It is experimentation.** The mental stance is wonder. What happens if I respond like this? What if I respond like that? The voice becomes the laboratory. The findings are interesting in themselves, regardless of whether they produce the outcome the person originally wanted.

"It's really important. The critical point is you're not trying to get rid of it. And the critical point is that it's an experimentation. You're learning about it. You're in wonder about it." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

## The On-Ramp When the Practice Is Too Hard

A specific concern: the inner-voice material is often the hardest material to engage with directly. The thoughts that come up are sticky. The reactions are habitual. The person tries to respond in a new way and finds they cannot.

Dr. K offers an on-ramp in the same conversation: brush your teeth with your non-dominant hand. Not because brushing teeth is the work. Because the experience of doing a familiar thing in an unfamiliar way is the warm-up for doing the inner work the new-response practice requires. The neuroplasticity engaged by the non-dominant-hand brushing is the same neuroplasticity the new-response practice will require. Brushing with the non-dominant hand for a few days is a low-stakes way to build the capacity to respond differently to something familiar.

"So it's so hard with the cognitions that we have... so I just start with a little, a touch of neuroplasticity and practice the skill of responding to things in different ways. And the easiest way to do that, brush with your non-dominant hand, right? You can only do it for a couple of days. Joe will give you something that'll work for 20 years. Do mine for like 3 days." -- Alok Kanojia (Dr. K), Charisma isn't learned

The on-ramp is not the practice. The on-ramp is a way to build the capacity to do the practice when the inner-voice material has been resistant. If a person can go directly to Joe's practice, go directly. If not, brush with the non-dominant hand for a few days, then try the inner-voice practice.

## The Practice Has a Lifespan

Joe is explicit that the practice, like most practices, has a lifespan. He estimates 10 to 20 years. The practice serves the person up until some recognition that makes the practice no longer the right tool. This connects to [[every-epiphany-is-a-rut-waiting-to-happen]] -- even the practice that is the right practice now will eventually need to be released.

The practice should be done as long as it is producing movement. When the practice itself becomes a discipline being maintained rather than an experimentation that is alive, the practice has shifted into the rut phase. Notice and let it become something else.

## What This Is Not

The practice is not "be nicer to your inner voice." That is a single new response that becomes another rut if it becomes the answer. The practice is the *variety* of responses. The point is that the responses keep being new, not that the responses keep being kind.

The practice is not "argue better with the voice." Arguing is one of the most common existing responses; the practice tries responses that are not arguing.

The practice is not "ignore the voice." Ignoring is a form of suppression that the voice will persist through.

The practice is not a technique to feel better. It might produce feeling better. That is incidental. The practice is to learn about the voice and to loosen the identification with it.

## When to Reach for This

The practice is useful when:

- The person has a persistent inner critic and the standard tools (positive self-talk, meditation, ignoring) have not worked.
- The person is interested in inner work and ready for a daily practice with a small footprint.
- The person is loosening the constructed self and the inner voice is one of its load-bearing structures.
- The standard relationship with the voice (compliance or resistance) is producing exhaustion.
- The person has done some basic work on noticing the voice and is ready to start experimenting with the relationship.

The practice is *not* useful when:

- The person is in acute crisis and needs different stabilization first.
- The person would convert the practice into a discipline-style management without the experimentation orientation.
- The person needs to start with even more basic work like noticing that the voice is happening at all (try [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]] first, then graduate to this practice).

## What This Produces

Joe's framing: "a very cool practice that starts emptying you out really quickly." The emptying is the key result. The person becomes less full of the constructed self. The new space is what makes deeper self-knowing possible. Other capacities (attunement, listening, the [[sensitivity-as-byproduct]] capacities) become available as the cup empties.

"What I notice is that we react to it differently? And I find just the experimentation will help you unfold the thing very, very quickly. A lot quicker than -- than for me, I notice people unfold a lot quicker than some early stages of meditation even." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned
