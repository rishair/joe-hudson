# Surround Yourself With People Who Make Fun of You

## The Practice

Deliberately keep close relationships with people who make fun of you, and make fun of yourself regularly. The practice operates as a continuous corrective against the gravitational pull toward becoming the authority-figure that students, audiences, and communities want their teachers to be.

The practice is small and ongoing rather than structured. It is not a meditation. It is who you spend time with and how you talk about yourself.

"You do have to remind yourself, or allow yourself to be surrounded by people who make fun of you. One of my biggest joys is to be surrounded by people who make fun of me on a regular basis. Or I make fun of myself, like with the Fuck You Joe handle. Because that's something that really helps in keeping it level when there's some group of people out there who think that you're some authority figure, or somebody who's going to judge them, or somebody whose approval they need. It allows that to dissipate. And having two teenage daughters does that really, really well -- they make fun of you all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Humility Q&A

## What This Looks Like

- Keep close friendships with people who knew you before the public role and who treat you the same.
- Be around family members (kids, partners, siblings) who do not relate to you as the teacher.
- Make fun of yourself publicly. Joe's example: the @fu_joe Twitter handle. The handle is a continuous reminder that he is not the figure of authority some people project onto him. See [[the-fu-joe-handle]] for the specific example.
- Let people roast you. Do not protect the dignity that wants to be protected. The dignity being protected is the identity.
- Notice the impulse to position yourself above the joke (or to make the joke about something else) and let it pass. Receive the joke. Let it land.

## Why This Works

The practice operates through two mechanisms.

**Mechanism 1: Counterweight to projection.** People in your community will project authority, perfection, halo onto you. The projection is gravitational. Without a counterweight, the projection slowly reshapes how you see yourself. Being around people who do not project these things is a continuous corrective. They see you as a person. Their seeing protects you from being absorbed into the role.

**Mechanism 2: Continuous sanding of the identity.** Being made fun of is, at the body level, the same kind of corrective the [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] move uses. Each time a joke about you lands without you defending against it, a small piece of the identity gets sanded away. The accumulated effect over years is the prevention of identity build-up that would otherwise calcify.

The mechanism is structural, not psychological. The practice does not require the person to consciously remind themselves to be humble. The presence of people who make fun of them does the work automatically.

## Why This Matters (the Stakes)

The structural risk for any teacher, coach, or public figure is the [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]]. The community's preference for certainty, the community's tendency to attack, the community's projection of the halo -- all push the teacher toward defensive armoring and toward identification with the role. Without continuous corrective, the spiral happens by default.

This practice is one of the few reliable structural correctives. It does not require the teacher to be a particular kind of person. It just requires them to deliberately keep close relationships outside the role.

The teacher who has only relationships inside the role -- only students, only people who treat them as the teacher -- has no counterweight. The role takes over by default. The teacher who keeps teenagers, old friends, family who do not care about the work, and people who roast them, has a built-in protection that operates whether or not they are paying attention.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not pretend to be made fun of.** Hiring sycophants who deliver scripted teasing does not work. The mechanism requires the person to actually not be operating from the projected role. If they are still treating you as the authority while pretending to roast you, the corrective is not present.
- **Do not make humility theatre out of the self-mockery.** "I am so humble that I make fun of myself" is the identity reinstalling itself. The self-mockery has to be light and continuous, not staged.
- **Do not use the practice as a defense.** "I make fun of myself, so I cannot be arrogant" is exactly the trap. The practice is a structural support, not a moral credential.

## In Coaching

When a coachee is in a public role and noticing themselves hardening, the question is: who in your life sees you as not-this-role? Who roasts you? Who treats you the way they did before? If the answer is no one, that is itself the diagnostic. The corrective is not interior. It is relational. The repair is to actively cultivate relationships outside the role.

When a coachee is a teacher whose community is going wonky, the practice is part of the corrective. The teacher who is being eaten by the role needs people who can see them outside it. Often the practice has to be deliberately rebuilt -- old friendships re-cultivated, family relationships re-attended to, time deliberately spent in contexts where the role does not exist.

## What This Produces

- Continuous, low-grade sanding of the identity that would otherwise build up
- Built-in protection against the [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]]
- A reservoir of relationships that do not require the role-self to operate
- A natural lightness in the public role because the identity is not being defended
- An ability to be more present in the role precisely because the role is not the whole self

## Tone

Joe describes the practice with affection and lightness -- his teenage daughters making fun of him is named as "one of my biggest joys." The tone signals that the practice is not a discipline. It is a pleasure. The being-made-fun-of is enjoyable when there is no identity that has to be protected from it.
