# Put Your Hands on Your Body to Calm Your Nervous System

## The Practice

When the nervous system is activated — in a trigger, mid-fight, in acute anxiety, in the puppet feeling of being out of yourself — put your hands on your body and feel your body. Hand to chest, hand to belly, hands to shoulders, hands to face. Any contact between your own hands and your own body. The contact pulls the nervous system back toward regulation.

"You can put your hands on your body, feel your body, that can completely calm your nervous system." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

The mechanism is somatic, not symbolic. The body responds to physical contact differently than to thought. The pressure, warmth, and proprioceptive signal of your own hands on yourself is information the nervous system can use immediately.

## When to Use It

- In the moment of a trigger when you notice the puppet feeling beginning
- Mid-fight when you have noticed the activation but the conversation cannot stop
- In acute anxiety when the body has started spinning up and you are losing access to thought
- Before a difficult conversation when you can already feel the system bracing
- During a session of emotional work when the body needs more grounding to continue
- When [[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]] alone is not enough and you need an additional channel of input

The practice pairs well with breath. Many people find that doing both at once (one hand on chest, slow equal breathing) is more effective than either alone.

## How to Do It

1. Notice where in the body the activation is most present. Often chest, belly, throat, jaw, or shoulders.
2. Place a hand there. Just rest it. No pressure required, but firm contact is usually more useful than light contact.
3. Feel the contact. Notice the warmth, the weight, the pressure. Pay attention to the sensation rather than to the story that is running.
4. Stay for a few breaths. The nervous system needs a beat to register the new input.
5. Notice the shift. The racing slows. The constriction loosens slightly. Thinking begins to return.

Common positions that work for different activations:

- **Hand on heart** for grief, sadness, or relational pain. The contact addresses the chest tightness that comes with these emotions.
- **Hand on belly** for fear or anxiety. The belly is where many people hold the freeze response. Hand on belly addresses it directly.
- **Both hands on shoulders** (self-hug) for shame or shrinking. The wrapping shape signals safety to the body.
- **Hand on cheek or forehead** for overwhelm. The contact with the head can interrupt rumination.
- **Hand on the spot that hurts** when there is a specific somatic location. The hand follows the body's report of where the activation is.

There is no wrong way. The practice is contact between your own hands and your own body, held with attention. The specific position matters less than the consistency.

## Why This Works

The nervous system is wired to interpret touch as information about safety. Touch from a trusted other can downregulate the system. Self-touch operates on the same channel. The body does not strictly distinguish between being held by another and holding itself. Both deliver signal that contact is happening, which the system reads as "not alone" and "not in danger."

For people whose original wounding was relational — the absence of soothing contact in childhood — the practice has a second layer of effect. The hands-on-body move provides the contact the original caregiver could not. The adult becomes, in that moment, the source of the soothing that was not available. This is one of the simplest forms of [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] applied to the self.

This works in concert with what the polyvagal frame describes. See [[polyvagal-nervous-system-framing]]. Hands on body brings ventral vagal input back online. The system shifts out of pure sympathetic activation toward a blended state in which contact and thought become possible again.

## The Larger Sequence This Belongs To

This practice is a regulation tool. It is the on-ramp to the actual work, not the work itself. The work is feeling what could not be felt. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]].

The sequence is:
1. Notice activation.
2. Use regulation tools (breath, hands on body, naming) to come back into enough capacity to be present.
3. Feel what is underneath the activation.
4. Allow the emotion to move and complete.

Skipping step 2 means trying to feel from a dysregulated state, which usually produces flooding rather than felt-through processing. Skipping step 3 and 4 means using regulation as ongoing suppression. The practice works when it is used as a bridge to feeling, not as a substitute for feeling.

"The opportunity is calm your nervous system so you can be back in yourself. And then feel the thing that you didn't get to feel." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

## Variations

**Partner version.** When the prerequisites are in place (the relational version of [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] has been built), the partner's hands can do the same work. The mechanism is the same; the source of contact shifts. The partner-version requires the partner to be present and not also activated.

**Self-massage version.** Slow, deliberate kneading of the chest, neck, or shoulders. Adds motion to the contact. Useful when the activation includes muscular bracing.

**Hand-on-hand version.** Place one hand on top of the other (over chest or belly). Doubles the proprioceptive signal. Useful when single-hand contact has not been enough.

**Self-hug version.** Both arms wrap across the body, hands on opposite shoulders. The whole-body shape signals containment. Useful in shame or in the feeling of being too exposed.

The practice tolerates experimentation. Try the variations. Notice which produces the most shift. Use that one.

## What This Is Not

This is not a way to make the underlying emotion never arrive. If hands-on-body is consistently used to keep the emotion suppressed, it has become the same trap as [[dont-use-breath-to-repress]]. The diagnostic is the same: after the regulation, do you return to what was underneath? If yes, the practice is functioning. If no, it has been weaponized as bypass.

This is not a replacement for processing the underlying material. A nervous system that is consistently being calmed without the underlying emotion being felt will keep producing the activation, because the activation is the body's request for the emotion. See [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]].

This is not a substitute for professional support in severe dysregulation. For acute PTSD, dissociation, or panic that is not responding to basic regulation tools, the practice is not enough. It is a starting tool, useful in the wide range of small-to-moderate activations that make up everyday life and relationship.

## What Someone Experiences

Within 10-30 seconds of contact, most people notice some shift. The racing slows slightly. The chest loosens slightly. Some access to thought returns. Sometimes the shift is dramatic; more often it is just enough to be able to do the next thing.

A common surprise: the contact itself often triggers tears or a wave of emotion. This is the practice working at the next layer. The contact tells the body it is safe enough for the held-back emotion to come through. The tears are not a sign the practice failed; they are a sign the practice succeeded and is now opening into step 3 (feeling what is underneath).

When that happens, the instruction is to keep the contact. Let the emotion move while staying in touch with the body. The combination of contact and emotion is the healing form.

## Tone

Joe presents this with the same matter-of-fact tone as breath. It is a basic tool, not a deep teaching. The simplicity is part of why it works. The practice does not require special training, special conditions, or special equipment. The hands are always available. The body is always there to be touched.
