# Require an Action at the End of Every Internal Message

## The Practice

In internal team communication, every message ends with an action assigned to the recipient. The rule applies to everyone regardless of seniority. A part-time assistant writing to the CEO assigns an action. The CEO writing to the assistant assigns an action. The structural feature: anyone can delegate to anyone.

"At the end of every email, people have to give somebody else an action. So if you write to me, or a part-time assistant writes to me, they have to give me an action to take at the end of that. They have to assign me, they have to delegate to me. That's part of like what we're doing to say, oh wait, we're all a team here. I have a particular role, you have a particular role, but anybody gets to assign anybody a task and anybody gets to say no and anybody gets to say, but it's so important that, oh, to realize that relationship is going both ways." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The reciprocity of the assignment is the point. The CEO can be assigned tasks by the assistant. The assistant can say no. The relationship in every direction is "we are both members of this team with different roles." The default hierarchy of who-can-assign-what-to-whom has been removed at the communication layer.

## Why This Works as an Authority-Issue Corrective

The practice is a structural counterweight against [[authority-issues]] developing in the organization. The default org pattern: people lower in the hierarchy assign nothing to people higher; people higher assign tasks to people lower. The asymmetry installs the authority frame in every interaction. Each email reinforces "I am below you" or "I am above you" without anyone naming the dynamic.

The reciprocal-assignment rule interrupts the installation. Every message asserts in form what the company asserts in principle: we are a team with different roles, not a hierarchy with different ranks. The repeated assertion, embedded in the daily mechanics of communication, does what announcements and town halls cannot do — it changes the felt experience of the relationship.

This connects to [[spit-at-the-top-is-a-waterfall-at-the-bottom]]: the leader's relationship to authority distorts every level below them through accumulated authority management. The reciprocal-assignment practice reduces the accumulation at each layer. The CEO who receives tasks from the assistant cannot easily become the figure-of-authority the assistant orients around; the assistant who assigns tasks to the CEO cannot easily collapse into the approval-seeking position.

## The Three Components

The practice has three components, each of which carries weight:

**1. Anyone can assign.** Not "you can request" or "you can ask." The form of the message is an assignment. The assistant tells the CEO "please do X by Y." The CEO tells the assistant "please do X by Y." The form is symmetric.

**2. Anyone can say no.** The assignment is not a command. The recipient can decline. The asymmetry that would normally distinguish a request from a command — "I have to say yes to my boss but my boss can say no to me" — is removed. Either party can decline either party's assignment.

**3. Anyone can negotiate.** The recipient can also push back, ask for clarification, propose a different action. The assignment opens a conversation rather than closing it.

The three together produce a relational shape: every interaction is a two-person collaboration where both parties have agency. The role differences are preserved (the CEO is still the CEO, the assistant is still the assistant) but the agency differences are equalized.

## What This Replaces

The default communication norms in most organizations:

- **Hierarchical asks only.** Senior people assign; junior people request. The form encodes the rank.
- **Diplomatic softening of upward asks.** Junior people use elaborate framing ("when you have a moment, would you be willing to consider..."). The softening is necessary because the form is foreign to the hierarchy.
- **Acceptance-by-default downward.** Junior people accept senior assignments without explicit negotiation. The decline option is theoretical but rarely exercised.
- **Asymmetric clarity.** Senior assignments are concrete; junior asks are vague. The asymmetry reflects who is allowed to be specific about what they need.

The practice replaces all four. The form is symmetric. The asks are direct. The acceptance and decline are equally available in both directions. The specificity is required from everyone.

## What This Is Not

**Not a flat hierarchy.** Roles still exist. The CEO is still the CEO. The accountability still flows in particular directions. What changes is the communication layer, not the structural layer. See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for the broader principle.

**Not a permission to overload the leader.** The CEO is not now obligated to do everything the team assigns. The "anyone can say no" component is real. The practice produces conversation; it does not produce obligation.

**Not a manipulation tactic.** The practice only works when the leader is genuinely available to be assigned. A leader who installs the rule but never accepts an assignment from a subordinate is performing the practice without enacting it. The team registers the performance and the practice fails.

**Not a substitute for clear roles.** The reciprocal-assignment rule presupposes that everyone knows what they are responsible for. If the roles are unclear, the practice produces chaos. The roles have to be defined first; the practice operates inside the role structure, not in place of it.

## The Faffing Origin (Why Joe Designed This)

The practice originated as the structural response to a specific pattern Joe noticed in his own company's internal correspondence: messages full of "we could kind of do this or this, I don't know," responded to with "oh, that's a cool idea, I guess." Joe called this pattern "faffing" -- the diffuse, non-committal communication that fills the gap between intention and action.

"I was reading these emails and I noticed the following behavior, which was, we could kind of do this or this, I don't know. And somebody else going, oh, that's a cool idea, I guess. but there's also this thing and I call that faffing. I don't know if that's the appropriate -- and then, and so we just put one structural change in, which is at the end of every correspondence, you write action needed, colon, what the action is, who's supposed to do it, when they're supposed to do it by. And in doing that, all the, almost all, maybe 90% of the faffing stopped. And that was the purpose of it." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The 90% number is the leverage statement. A direct intervention on each faffing instance would have addressed each instance and missed the rest. The structural change reduced the entire category by an order of magnitude with one design decision. See [[direct-intervention-vs-structural-change]] for the underlying distinction this exemplifies, and [[ask-how-do-i-change-this-so-it-never-happens-again]] for the question Joe asked that generated this design.

The explicit format Joe uses: "action needed:" followed by what the action is, who is supposed to do it, when they're supposed to do it by. The action recipient can be anyone -- the part-time assistant assigning the CEO, the CEO assigning the assistant. The reciprocity (anyone can assign anyone) and the specificity (what, who, when) together produce the result.

## The "I Hated It at First" Diagnostic

A specific report from someone who joined a team running the practice and encountered it for the first time:

"I hated that at first. I knew the moment I hated it, I was like, oh, this is exactly what we need." -- a leader-coachee describing his own first encounter with the practice, "I Block 3 hours a Month for Hard Conversations"

The diagnostic is informative. The practice produces an immediate negative reaction in many people new to it. The reaction is itself the signal of why the practice is needed. The person hated being asked to specify an action because the specificity exposed the hedging they had been doing. The hedging was the pattern; the specification interrupted it; the interruption felt uncomfortable; the discomfort was the cost of breaking the pattern.

This is useful information for leaders installing the practice: expect initial resistance, expect the resistance to come from the people for whom the practice is most needed, and expect the resistance to soften as the people who recognize the diagnostic value of their own reaction work through it. The leader who treats the initial discomfort as a sign the practice is wrong will reverse the install. The leader who treats the discomfort as a sign the practice is doing its job will hold the install through the adjustment period.

## What This Produces

When the practice is genuinely operating:

- The hierarchy becomes less psychologically loaded. The form of communication does not constantly remind people of rank.
- The leader gets useful asks. The team feels empowered to surface things the leader actually needs to handle.
- The team becomes more direct. The hedging that usually accompanies upward asks drops out.
- The leader's time gets allocated by the team's actual needs, not by the leader's guess about what the team needs.
- The team operates with more agency. Each member experiences themselves as a co-creator of the work, not as an executor of others' decisions.

The practice is one of several structural interventions Joe describes for keeping authority issues from accumulating in his own company. See [[surround-yourself-with-people-who-make-fun-of-you]] for the related personal-level practice.

## When This Practice Fits

The practice fits organizations that:

- Have a leader who genuinely wants reciprocal authority and is not just performing flatness
- Are small enough that the leader can actually receive and act on team-assigned tasks (the practice scales with deliberate adaptation, not by default)
- Have clear enough roles that the assignments do not become chaotic
- Have a culture where the "no" is real and the assignments are honest

The practice does not fit organizations where:

- The leader expects to be the one who decides who does what
- The roles are so fluid that adding more reciprocal asks would produce confusion
- The culture is so politically charged that the assignments would be used as tactical moves rather than as genuine asks
- The leader is unable to internally accept being assigned by a subordinate (the practice will fail and the failure will install resentment rather than reciprocity)

## How to Install It

The practice is installed by demonstration, not by announcement. The leader starts ending their own messages with an action and starts honoring actions assigned to them by subordinates. The team registers the new shape of communication and begins to mirror it.

A formal version: the leader names the practice in a team meeting and asks everyone to start including an action at the end of internal messages. The naming makes the practice explicit, but the demonstration is what makes it stick. A practice that is announced but not modeled by the leader fails within weeks.

The team needs to experience the leader accepting a "no" from a subordinate before the practice fully lands. The first refusal is the test. If the leader responds with frustration or quietly withdraws the practice, the team learns the form was symbolic. If the leader accepts the no cleanly and the conversation continues, the team learns the form is real.

## The Internal Move Behind the Practice

The practice requires the leader to have done internal work on their relationship to being assigned tasks by subordinates. A leader who internally resents being assigned tasks by junior staff cannot install the practice; the resentment leaks through and the team detects it.

The internal work: noticing the impulse to dismiss or override the assignment, asking why the impulse is firing, recognizing where the [[authority-issues]] pattern is operating in oneself. The practice is partly a corrective for the leader's own authority issues — installing it requires confronting them; running it sustains the confrontation.

This is why the practice is more powerful than its mechanics suggest. It is not just a communication norm. It is a daily exposure to the leader's own authority-issue patterns, with the team as the mirror. Each assignment from a subordinate is a small invitation to inhabit the role differently.
