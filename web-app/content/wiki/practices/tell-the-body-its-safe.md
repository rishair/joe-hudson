# Tell the Body It's Safe Before Anger Release

## The Practice

Before releasing anger, speak directly to the body. Out loud. Tell it that the current situation is not the original situation that taught it to be afraid of anger. Name the specific things that are not happening here.

"Literally telling your body, hey, it's safe. It's okay to literally say, hey, I'm not gonna be abused here. I'm not gonna get attacked by my abusive brother. I'm not going to, I'm not gonna destroy anybody, I'm not hurting anybody, nobody's here to hear me. Like literally to just tell your body that, give your body that message. It seems simple, but it can totally work." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The message has two halves: what is not happening (the old danger is not here) and what is not at risk (no one will be hurt by this release).

## Why This Works

The body's fear of anger release is wired to the original scene where anger was dangerous. When the body starts to release, it scans for the original conditions: is the abuser nearby, am I about to be punished, am I about to hurt someone, am I about to be rejected. If the scan returns ambiguous results, the body shuts the release down.

Explicit, out-loud reassurance gives the body specific data: the original conditions are not present. The scan returns clean. The release can proceed.

The body responds to spoken language more than to thought. The verbalization makes the data audible. The nervous system updates faster with audible input than with internal narration.

This connects to the broader pattern in [[three-childhood-origins-of-anger-repression]]. Each origin produces specific body-level fears at the moment of release:
- Origin 1 (abusive caretaker): the body fears becoming the abuser. The reassurance: "I am not going to hurt anyone. No one is here."
- Origin 2a (anger removed love): the body fears being abandoned. The reassurance: "No one is here to leave. I am not at risk of losing love by doing this."
- Origin 3 (punished for anger): the body fears being attacked. The reassurance: "There is no one here who will punish me. I am safe."

The specific reassurance has to match the specific fear. Generic "you're safe" does less work than specific "no abusive brother here."

## How to Do It

1. Find the private container. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]].
2. Before starting the release, speak directly to the body. "Hey body, listen. Here is what is true right now."
3. Name what is not happening. Whatever the body's specific fear is.
4. Name what is not at risk. No one will be hurt. Nothing will be destroyed.
5. Pause. Let the body register the information.
6. Begin the release.

The pause matters. The body needs a beat to update. Rushing past the reassurance into the release is the same as not doing the reassurance.

## The Specific Sentences

The sentences depend on the person's specific history. Common examples:

- "I am not going to be attacked by [original aggressor] here."
- "No one is here to see this. I am alone."
- "Nothing is going to be destroyed. I am not going to hurt anyone."
- "If I get loud, no one will leave."
- "I am an adult. The people who told me anger was bad are not here."
- "I am in my car. I am safe."

The sentence is most effective when it names the specific fear that the body is presenting. Generic safety language is less useful than precise reassurance.

## Connection to Other Practices

This practice often combines with others.

**With [[get-angry-in-the-car]]:** the verbal reassurance is the on-ramp. The car already provides physical privacy; the spoken reassurance addresses the body's residual concerns about that privacy being enough.

**With [[i-exist-affirmation-against-collapse]]:** the safety reassurance is preventative; the affirmation is responsive. The reassurance prevents the collapse from forming; the affirmation breaks it once it has formed.

**With [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]:** the reassurance helps the body permit the performance. Many bodies cannot even fake anger until they have been told the conditions are safe.

## When to Use It

- Before any session of anger release, especially in the early stages of the practice.
- When the body is hesitating, the breath is shallow, the energy is not arriving.
- When previous release attempts have produced fear or shutdown.
- When the person has a history of anger being dangerous in their environment.

## When It Is Not Enough

If the reassurance is being said but not believed, the body does not update. Common reasons:
- The person is in their head, not speaking to the body. The verbalization is performative without being addressed.
- The current environment actually is not safe (a partner is nearby, walls are thin, the person could be overheard). The reassurance contradicts reality. The body knows.
- The person is rushing through. The pause to let the body register is being skipped.

The practice requires actual privacy, slow delivery, and addressing the body as if it were a being that needs to be reassured. All three matter.

## The Larger Principle

The body responds to information. Information about safety, about who is present, about what is at risk. The mind is fluent in this information; the body sometimes needs to be told.

The practice generalizes beyond anger. The body can be told it is safe before doing any difficult emotional work. Crying. Shaking. Speaking a truth. The reassurance is the same: name what is not happening, name what is not at risk, give the body a moment to register.

## In Coaching

When working with someone whose body keeps shutting down during release:
- Suggest the practice. "Before you start, try just talking to your body. Tell it specifically what is not happening here."
- Help locate the specific fear. "What is your body afraid of right now if it lets the anger come?"
- Help formulate the reassurance. The sentence has to match the fear.
- Have the person say it out loud. The verbalization is the practice.

## Tone

The practice is gentle. The speaker is addressing their own body as a being that needs care. The voice is soft, not commanding. The body responds to the tone as much as to the words.
