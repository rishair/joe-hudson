# Rolling Out Principles in an Organization

## The Practice

There is a specific multi-step method for getting a set of principles to actually shape behavior in a company. The method is bottom-up experimentation followed by top-down codification, not the reverse. The typical corporate failure -- a slide deck of principles that nobody references -- is what happens when the order is inverted.

The method has been used in companies ranging from small executive teams up to 1,200-person organizations. Skipping any step produces the failure pattern.

## The Steps

### Step 1: Independent Drafting

Every executive (including the CEO) independently produces five principles they think should govern the company. No collaboration first. The independent drafting produces independent thought.

The presentations happen together. The result is typically surprising: people independently arrive at substantially the same five principles, with different wording and different framings.

"Pretty soon, you would find out that everybody really probably agreed on the same 5 principles, that different wording, different things, and maybe 2 become 1. But, pretty much, you're going to find there's not going to be a lot of disagreement typically. I've never had it." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The convergence matters because it grounds the principles in the actual collective sense of what the company needs, not in any one person's preference.

### Step 2: Executive Team Experimentation

The executive team runs experiments with the candidate principles. One experiment per principle per day, for roughly five days, then cycling. A month of experimentation covers all the candidates several times.

A shared channel (Slack or equivalent) holds the experiments. People post: "I did this experiment with the connection principle. Here is what happened." The team reads each other's experiments and learns from them.

The point is not to validate the principles. The point is to discover what they mean in practice, what their edges are, where they break down, and where they consolidate. The principles get refined through use, not through debate.

### Step 3: CEO Codifies After Evidence

After the experimentation phase, the CEO -- as the final decision maker -- writes the final form: "These are the principles. This is what they mean. This is what they do not mean."

"Then the CEO, because they are the CEO, is the final decision maker, says, 'These are the experiments. This is what they mean and what they don't mean.'" -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The CEO's codification is informed by the experimentation, not preceding it. The CEO is consolidating what the team discovered, not imposing what the CEO thought before the team experimented.

The "what they don't mean" part is critical. See [[defining-principles-well]] for why every principle must be defined by what it IS and what it IS NOT.

### Step 4: Company-Wide Experimentation

Once codified, the principles are released to the whole company with the same experimentation structure: shared channels, ongoing experiments, learning from each other.

"And the whole company experiments with these principles and you do it on -- and I've done this in like a 1,200-person company where they're all -- like there's channels, Slack or whatever channel, and where they're talking about the experiments they've done and they're learning from one another." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The CEO continues to refine based on what surfaces. Each round of experimentation produces clearer edges, clearer applications, clearer boundary cases.

### Step 5: Distributed Accountability for Living the Principles

After the rollout is done, everyone in the company is responsible for finding and committing to one way they will remind the company to live the principles. The accountability is distributed and self-chosen.

"Everybody in the company is responsible for finding and committing to one way of reminding everybody to live to the principles. And maybe that's making a sign that you put in the bathrooms, or maybe that's having a discussion group once every quarter, or maybe that's changing the way that you do reviews so that the principles are a part of the review process, or maybe that's changing the sales pipeline so that, let's say, connection is a bigger part of the sales." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The mechanisms get embedded into actual workflows -- reviews, sales pipelines, meeting structures -- so the principles operate through systems rather than only through individual will.

The self-chosen quality matters. If the CEO assigns the mechanisms, they do not stick. If people volunteer their own mechanism, they own it.

## The Variation: Pre-Executive Brainstorming Phase

In one flat organization Joe worked with, an earlier phase was added: the whole company watched a video explaining how to make principles, then suggested principles the executives could consider before the executive drafting began. This is appropriate for organizations with very flat structures or strong cultures of co-creation.

The principle still gets codified by the CEO after experimentation. The earlier brainstorming just widens the input.

## The Quarterly Re-Wrestling

After the initial rollout, the organization revisits one principle each quarter for renewed experimentation.

"Every quarter or so, they'll take one of the principles and say, 'Here's a principle that we're living by.' And they'll all experiment with it. And do we want to change it? Is this still -- or here's a new one that we want to try out. Does this work? Do we add it? Do we subtract something?" -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The quarterly cadence keeps the principles alive. Without it, the rollout produces six months of attention followed by reversion to default behavior. See [[wrestle-with-your-principles]] for the underlying principle.

## What Stops It From Working

Joe is specific about the failure modes.

**No experimentation.** The principles get announced and people are expected to live by them. Without experimentation, people do not learn what the principles mean in their actual work. The principles stay abstract and get ignored.

**Top-down imposition.** The CEO or a small group writes the principles and presents them as finished. The rest of the company experiences this as a mandate to obey rules they did not co-create. Compliance happens; integration does not.

**No accountability for experimentation.** People are told to experiment but nobody is responsible for making it happen. The shared channel goes silent within two weeks.

**Assigned mechanisms.** The CEO assigns who is responsible for embedding which principle into which workflow. The assignments produce performance, not commitment.

**Competing frameworks.** The company already has a mission statement and core values that are not aligned with the new principles. The signal-to-noise ratio becomes too high. People do not know which framework to act from.

"There is one other thing that has happened in some of the companies where they have like we have our mission statement, which is meaningless that nobody looks at and we have our core values, which is meaningless and nobody looks at or it's meaningful and nobody looks at. And then they're like, 'Okay, now we're going to do principles.' And it's like, 'Shit, now we have a mission and we have the -- what do I listen to?' It's just confusing. The signal-to-noise ratio is just way too high. And so I think it's really important if a company is going to do it to really prioritize how you're doing this and what's really needed and what's not needed and what's being listened to. And like anything, you have to edit really well. You have to throw stuff out to make it work." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

When introducing principles, ruthlessly prune the existing frameworks. If the mission statement is not living, retire it. If the values are not changing decisions, archive them. The principles need clear air to operate in.

## Why Upfront Time Pays Off

The method is time-consuming. A month for executive experimentation. Several months for company-wide experimentation. Quarterly re-wrestling forever. The upfront investment is substantial.

The return is measured against the alternative: a company that keeps making the same decisions over and over because no one has codified what they are optimizing for.

"It's kind of like being a great parent or training a dog, it takes a lot of upfront time, but it saves a tremendous amount of time on the back end." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The decisions that get pre-handled by living principles save more time than the principles took to install. The opportunity cost calculation favors the rollout, but only if the rollout is done well enough that the principles actually live in the organization.

## Why the Long-Term ROI Far Exceeds the Upfront Cost

The standard objection to this practice is the upfront time. Joe answers it directly with the dog-training analogy:

"It's a lot like training a dog. If you don't spend the time up front, it's a lot of time up front, but if you do it, the rest of your dog's life is a lot easier, but there is upfront cost to it." -- Joe Hudson, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The analogy is operational. The dog that has not been trained costs more time across the dog's life than the dog that has been trained, even though the training itself was time-intensive. The trained dog handles 80% of situations without intervention. The untrained dog requires intervention in every situation.

The same arithmetic applies to companies. The company without principles requires the CEO to weigh in on every decision because there is no shared logic for making the decision without the CEO. The company with rolled-out principles has 80% of its decisions pre-handled. The CEO's attention is freed for the 20% that actually requires it.

The hidden upfront cost is also smaller than it appears. The principles work happens at the executive level over a few months and at the company level over a few additional months. The compounding return runs for the life of the company.

Johannes' direct testimony on this: most of his ROI came in two specific areas: recruitment (the principles let candidates self-select before the interview process) and quarterly touch-bases (the principles are the basis for the manager-and-employee conversations). The principles also reduced the amount of internal documentation he needed -- "I just need to have those hundreds of documents when I can just like codify, you know, culture, which is how to build relationships and how to make great decisions in that set of principles."

The replacement is structural. The principles do the work that previously required policy documents, decision-by-committee, and CEO intervention. The principles compress an enormous amount of organizational scaffolding into a small number of well-defined commitments.

## The 80%-of-Decisions Effect

A specific operational claim Joe makes about what well-installed principles produce: 80% of decisions get pre-handled, and the remaining 20% become much clearer because the 80% is no longer in the way.

"If you have a good set of operating principles, 80% of your decisions get made almost automatically and they get made well, consistently." -- Joe Hudson, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The number is approximate but the structural claim is precise. Most decisions in a company are recurring decisions in slightly different forms. The principles, once internalized, dissolve the recurring decisions into automatic choices. The decision-feel evaporates because there is no actual decision to make -- the principle has already answered the question.

The remaining 20% are the genuinely novel decisions where the principles point in directions that need to be weighed against each other. Those decisions get the CEO's full attention because they are not buried under hundreds of routine decisions that should have been pre-handled.

See [[80-percent-of-decisions-are-automatic-via-principles]] for the full treatment of this effect.

## What Makes a Phrase Spread Through the Company

A specific design insight Johannes names about principle-naming: the phrase has to be sayable in real time, by anyone, in any meeting. The criterion is not "does it capture the principle abstractly?" but "can a developer or a product manager or a customer-success person actually say this phrase out loud and have everyone immediately know what it means?"

"We try to phrase all of them in a way that people can say them. So raise the bar, open the drawer, ask what's wunderbar, etc. So that people can use it in their day to day." -- Johannes Langgraf, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The phrases at Ona share specific features:
- **Short.** Three to four words.
- **Verb-shaped.** They can be used as actions, not just as values.
- **Image-based.** A drawer, a bar, a question.
- **Permission-granting.** The phrase, when said, gives the room permission to start the conversation the principle is about.

The result: the principles become daily verbs rather than wall decorations. "Open the drawer" is now one of the most-used sentences in the company. Each use is the principle operating in real time.

See [[open-the-drawer-as-shared-verb]] for the full treatment of this design principle.

## The Principle-Iteration in Practice

A specific example Johannes gives of how principles evolved at Ona: an earlier principle was "We, not me" -- which captured something true but produced a side effect of people not showing up fully empowered. The principle implied that ego was the problem, when actually the absence of end-to-end ownership was the problem. The team iterated the principle to "Connect, Connect, Connect" -- which kept the connection element (with customers, with each other) and added the connection with self that the earlier framing had inadvertently suppressed.

"We changed that and iterated that to Connect, Connect, Connect, which keeps the connection part... but also, we spoke about that previously, connecting with yourself. I mean, I think that's the whole thing. If you really, really understand yourself, you would just be way more effective in everything." -- Johannes Langgraf, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The example illustrates the [[wrestle-with-your-principles]] practice in operation. Principles that miss something get refined as the missing dimension becomes visible. The refinement is the work.

## When This Practice Is Useful

- A CEO is starting a new company and wants to design culture deliberately
- An existing company is reorganizing or going through a major transition
- A leader notices the company is running on the leader's personality (see [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]])
- A company has values that nobody references and is considering replacing them with principles
- A team is functional but lacks shared language for why they make the decisions they make

## Boundary Conditions

This method works for organizations where the CEO genuinely wants distributed input. If the CEO already has the principles they want imposed, running the method as a charade is worse than not running it at all -- the team learns that "co-creation" means "perform agreement with the predetermined answer." Subsequent invitations to participate get treated with cynicism.

The method also requires sufficient bandwidth for several months of focused attention. A company in a survival crisis cannot run this method. The crisis itself is too consuming.
