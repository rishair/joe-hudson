# Five Minutes Every Two Hours

## The Practice

The simplest prescription Joe and Brett offer for [[time-scarcity]]: take 5 minutes every 2 hours, and during that 5 minutes, do nothing.

"Take 5 minutes every 2 hours, sit and do nothing. Like, do absolutely nothing for 5 minutes every 2 hours." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The structure:
- A 5-minute block, deliberately set apart from the rest of the work.
- Spaced approximately every 2 hours through the working day.
- During the 5 minutes: no productivity, no input (no phone, no podcast, no audiobook), no planning the next 2 hours.
- The instruction is "do nothing." Not "rest in a particular way." Just do nothing.

In a 16-hour waking day, this is 8 pauses, totaling 40 minutes.

## Why This Specific Prescription

The 5-minutes-every-2-hours format is not arbitrary. Several features make it work:

**5 minutes is small enough to never be a real obstacle.** Anyone who claims to be too busy for 5 minutes is making a claim that does not survive scrutiny. The claim itself is the diagnostic. If 5 minutes is impossible, the relationship to time has become the problem.

**Every 2 hours is frequent enough to prevent buildup.** Time scarcity compounds across the day. Each rushed hour adds to the next. The 2-hour interval interrupts the compounding before it has time to crystallize into the end-of-day overwhelm.

**Doing nothing is the actual prescription.** Not stretching. Not deep breathing. Not journaling. Not affirming. Doing nothing. The reason: anything else becomes another task to perform, another item to do well, another loop to close. The point of the 5 minutes is the absence of doing. Without that absence, the system never gets a break.

**40 minutes is achievable.** Anyone reporting hours of daily entertainment time who claims they cannot find 40 minutes for nothing is bumping into the actual issue. The minutes are there. The willingness to be present without distraction is what is not there.

## The Time-Math Argument

The standard objection: "I don't have time."

Brett's response, designed to expose the objection's structure:

"If you say that you don't have the time to spend 5 minutes every 2 hours, so let's say you're awake 16 hours, so that's 8 times 5, so that's 40 minutes. If you don't have time, then I'm assuming that you don't have time to do 40 minutes of Instagram, you don't have time to do 40 minutes of television, you don't have time to do 40 minutes of working out. Like, I don't believe it. I just don't believe that that's the case." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The arithmetic is the intervention. 40 minutes out of 16 waking hours is 4% of waking time. Almost everyone has at least 4% of their waking time in passive consumption (scrolling, TV, music while doing nothing else). The 40 minutes can be reallocated from that consumption to dedicated stillness. The reallocation is not a productivity loss. The consumed time was already low-quality time. The stillness is high-leverage time.

Joe's variation on the same point, drawn from an older saying:

"If you don't have time to meditate for 20 minutes, meditate for an hour." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The saying captures the paradox: the busier you feel, the more the practice is needed. The need increases as the resistance increases. The resistance is itself part of what makes the practice valuable when actually done.

## The First-Day Test

The specific intervention to propose:

"Do that 5 minutes for one day. Like, carve out the 40 minutes somehow for one day and notice how much extra time you have." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The frame is experimental, not committing. The person is not being asked to adopt a new daily practice forever. They are being asked to try it for one day and see what happens. The asymmetry is favorable: the downside is 40 minutes; the upside is direct experience of whether the practice produces what is claimed.

The expected outcome on Day 1: the person reports feeling like they had more time, not less. The day felt less rushed. They got things done more easily. The body was less depleted at the end.

This outcome contradicts the felt-experience prediction (which was that the 40 minutes would cost time). The contradiction is what produces the behavior change. One experiential disconfirmation often does more work than a hundred theoretical arguments.

## What "Do Nothing" Actually Means

The instruction is deceptively simple. Most people, on first attempt, do not actually do nothing. They:

- **Plan the next two hours.** The mind keeps working on the to-do list. This is not nothing. The body is still, but the system is still running.
- **Rehearse a conversation.** The mind chews on something said or about to be said. Not nothing.
- **Notice and judge.** The mind notices the breath and starts evaluating whether it is the right kind of breath. Not nothing.
- **Try to relax.** The mind issues commands to the body about how it should be feeling. Not nothing.
- **Check the time.** The mind continually checks how much of the 5 minutes has passed. Not nothing.

Actual nothing is harder. It includes:
- Letting whatever arrives arrive without doing anything about it.
- Not trying to be present, not trying to be relaxed, not trying to be anything.
- Allowing the breath to do what it does without interference.
- Sitting with the discomfort of not-doing if discomfort arrives.
- Allowing emotion to surface if it surfaces, without redirecting or processing.

The 5 minutes is not a meditation. There is no technique. There is no goal-state. The practice is the simple absence of activity for a defined window. What arises during that window is information about what the rest of the day has been suppressing.

## What Often Arises

In the first few attempts, the 5 minutes is mostly experienced as restlessness. The body wants to move. The mind wants to work. The hand wants the phone. This is not a failure. This is the visible-form of what was running invisibly during the work.

After several attempts, what arises tends to include:

- Tiredness that was being suppressed (often the first thing to surface).
- An emotion that was being held at bay (often anger or fear).
- A clarity about something that had been confused.
- A spontaneous next-step on a problem that had been stuck.
- A sense of permission that was not available during the work.
- A tear that needed to come out.

Joe and Brett name the same phenomenon in the larger frame: the 5 minutes is partly an opportunity for emotional processing that the day did not allow.

"And in that same 5 minutes, you could scream, cry, feel the fear. ... You could be present with what is. Enjoy yourself. It's just about being present." -- Joe Hudson and Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The 5 minutes is not committed to any particular content. Whatever arrives is what arrives. The practice is the not-resisting and the not-redirecting.

## Common Failures

**Treating the 5 minutes as a task.** The person sets a timer, sits down, and the system goes into checking-the-box mode. The same buzzing that runs the rest of the day runs the 5 minutes. Nothing relaxes. The practice was performed but not done.

"If you sit down for those 5 minutes, you're probably going to initially rush through those 5 minutes the way you rush through everything else, right? Which is to think about the things you're going to do next. You might find yourself constricting and being like, okay, I'll hold still for 5 minutes, checking the box to get the thing done. And that's the stance that is creating that situation in your entire life." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The recognition: the stance that produces the rushing through 5 minutes is the same stance that produces the rushing through everything. The 5 minutes is a clean laboratory for noticing the stance. Once the stance is visible, it can be released. The release happens in the 5 minutes and then spreads to the rest of the day.

**Filling the 5 minutes with content.** Reading. Music. Podcast. A short video. Any of these defeats the practice. The point is the absence of input, not the presence of better input. The system needs to drop, and dropping requires no input.

**Skipping the 5 minutes when the day gets busy.** This is the most common failure. The instinct under pressure is to skip the pause and push through. The instinct is exactly the wrong response. The busier the day, the more the pause is needed. The skipping is the symptom of the time scarcity that the practice is designed to interrupt.

**Treating one good attempt as completion.** The practice works through repetition, not through a single successful instance. The pattern is built over weeks, not over one day. The single-day experiment is to demonstrate the principle. The longer practice is what changes the relationship to time.

This practice is the minimum-viable form of a larger family of presence practices. It pairs naturally with:

The practices are not redundant. They are sized for different windows and different triggers. The 5-minutes-every-2-hours is the most accessible. The others are appropriate when the window or the trigger is different.

## When This Is the Move

The practice is the right intervention when:

- The person presents [[i-never-have-enough-time]] and needs a concrete first step.
- The person has tried productivity systems and is still time-starved.
- The person reports doomscrolling or other passive consumption that is consuming time they claim not to have.
- The person is in [[time-scarcity]] and needs an experiential disconfirmation of the "I don't have time" belief.
- A leader is trying to change a team culture of constant rushing and needs a structural recommendation that anyone can implement.
- The person says they want to slow down but does not know what slowing down would even look like.

## Tone

Practical and slightly amused. The practice is small and the delivery is small. Joe and Brett present it as obvious-when-stated, almost embarrassingly simple. The unspectacular nature is part of why it works. The person is not being asked to commit to a 10-day silent retreat. They are being asked to do nothing for 5 minutes. The smallness of the ask is what makes the experiential test possible.
