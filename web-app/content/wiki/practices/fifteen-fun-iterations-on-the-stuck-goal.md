# Write 15 Iterations on the Stuck Goal, Make Each One Fun (The Habenula Workaround)

## The Practice

For something you have been trying to do for a long time and have not been able to do — lose weight, stop smoking, be nicer to your friends, listen better — write 15 distinct iterations you could try to achieve the goal. Then next to each one, write what you would have to do to make that iteration fun, enjoyable, something you actually want to do. If you cannot find a way to make an iteration fun, replace it with a different one. The final list is 15 iterations you would enjoy. Then do them in order, one until it fails, then the next, until either one works or you have run the list.

This is the small-format sibling of [[write-50-experiments-before-starting]]. The 50-list is the long-arc structural intervention. The 15-list is the 3-minute version, gated by enjoyment, designed to be writable in a single sitting and runnable in ordinary daily life.

"I want you to come up with 15 of them, and I want you to write them down in order. And then right next to that, I want you to write down what you would have to do to make each one of those things fun. And if you can't think of a way to make it fun, enjoyable, something that you want to do, then you have to come up with a different one." -- Joe Hudson, The 3-Minute Test

## Instructions

1. **Pick something you have been trying to do for a long time and have not been able to do.** Lose weight. Stop smoking. Be nicer to your friends. Listen better. The thing where prior attempts have collapsed and the inner verdict is "I can't do this." Pick one.

2. **Write 15 distinct iterations.** Not one thing in 15 different ways. 15 different ways. For weight loss the examples Joe gives are: play pickleball, walk with a friend, play dodgeball, try an Ayurvedic cleanse, do a juice fast. The distinctness matters because the brain's "I already tried that" objection collapses the list into "I have done this before and failed" if the items are too similar.

3. **Next to each iteration, write what would make it fun.** Not "what would make me successful at it." What would make doing it enjoyable. Pickleball with which friend, on what court, with which playlist. The Ayurvedic cleanse with which cookbook, with which morning routine. The fun-column is not a luxury; it is the gate.

4. **If you cannot make an iteration fun, replace it.** The enjoyment requirement is non-negotiable. An iteration you cannot enjoy is not a candidate. The replacement is part of the practice: the constraint reveals which iterations you were including from obligation versus actual interest. The list compresses toward the iterations you actually want to run.

5. **Number them. Do them in order.** Run iteration #1 until it fails. Then iteration #2. Then #3. Maybe one of them does not fail. Maybe they all fail. The point is to be iterating, not to find the magic one.

## Why the Fun-Gate Matters

The 15-list adds the fun column to the 50-experiments practice for a specific reason: it preempts the habenula at two layers instead of one.

The 50-experiments practice ([[write-50-experiments-before-starting]]) preempts the habenula by making the next iteration structurally available before any single attempt can register as "failure." But the iteration itself can still be miserable. The dieter who lists 50 weight-loss approaches and runs them as obligations is still running each one in the failure-frame internally, even if the structural frame has been changed.

The 15-list with the fun-column closes that gap. Each iteration is also enjoyable. The activity itself sends a different signal to the body than an obligation does. The iteration is being run because the person wants to, not because they should. The habenula does not fire on enjoyment; it fires on perceived failure. An iteration that the person is enjoying does not produce the failure-perception even when it does not produce the goal-outcome.

This is the same structural insight as [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] applied to iteration. Enjoyment is not a luxury overlay on the work. It is the per-unit-of-energy reading that determines whether the work is sustainable. An iteration list of 15 enjoyed activities is more likely to be completed than a list of 50 dutifully-pursued ones.

## What This Practice Tends to Produce

- **A different starting point than the person would have chosen.** The fun-column often makes the obvious-but-dreaded first item (running, calorie counting, gym membership) drop off the list. The replacement is an iteration the person would not have chosen as the "right" answer but is happy to actually do.
- **Movement on a goal that has been frozen for years.** The combination of iteration-frame and enjoyment-frame is more leveraged than either alone. Many of the long-stuck goals are stuck precisely because the obvious approaches were not enjoyable enough to sustain.
- **The discovery that the original goal was not the actual goal.** If the only iterations on a weight-loss list that pass the fun-gate are social activities (pickleball with a friend, walking with a friend, dodgeball), the actual underlying want may be connection, not weight loss. The list surfaces the real motivation by showing which iterations the person can actually inhabit.
- **A felt shift in what trying feels like.** The body that has been gripping around "I have to make this work" relaxes when the next attempt is something enjoyable. The shift is detectable in the first item the person actually runs.

## When This Practice Is Used

- A person has been trying to change a behavior for years and reports nothing has worked.
- The concern [[i-cant-be-disciplined]] is presenting and the person needs a concrete first move that does not require willpower.
- [[im-a-failure]] is in play and the iteration-frame is needed in a small enough form to be completed.
- A person responds well to short, structured practices and would not sit down for the 50-experiments version.
- The audience is a YouTube-short or workshop audience where the 50-experiments practice would exceed the available time, but the iteration principle is what needs to land operationally.

## When the 50-Experiments Practice Is Better

The 15-fun-list is not always the right form. Use [[write-50-experiments-before-starting]] when:

- The goal is large enough that 15 iterations would all be obvious and the weird ones are needed.
- The person is willing to do the longer-form work and would benefit from the larger surface area.
- The domain is one where the enjoyment-gate would be a step too far up front and the structural iteration-frame needs to land first.
- The work is team-generated or organizational.

The two practices share the same mechanism. The 15-fun-list is the introductory form, designed for short attention and for the moment when the principle needs to land in three minutes.

## What to Watch For

- **The person who skips the fun-column.** The fun-column is not optional. Without it, the practice collapses back into a smaller-than-50 version of the experiments list. The point is the enjoyment-gate, not the number.
- **The person who writes "I would make it fun by powering through with motivational music" or other variants that are still the willpower frame.** The fun has to be actual fun, not effort-with-a-soundtrack. The diagnostic question: would you do this if it were not for the goal? If no, the fun-column has not produced an actual fun iteration.
- **The person who cannot generate 15.** This is itself information. Often it means the goal has been so loaded with should-energy that the imagination has shut down around enjoyable approaches. The next move is to ask what they enjoy in general, then ask which of those enjoyments could be redirected at the goal.
- **The person who runs iteration #1 with everything riding on it.** Same failure mode as the 50-experiments version. The list is the protection. Each iteration is iteration #N of a sequence, not the verdict.
