# Say Goodbye Like It's the Last Time

## The Practice

When parting from someone you care about, say goodbye in the way you would say it if you knew it were the last time you would ever see them.

Not as a performance of grief. Not as a dramatized awareness of mortality. As a small, honest act of letting the felt-acknowledgement of impermanence be in the goodbye -- so that whatever needs to be said gets said, and so that the goodbye is something that could stand as the final goodbye if it turned out to be one.

The practice came out of years of Brett's experience in extreme sports, where the friends he made would, predictably and unpredictably, die without warning:

"Having that happen so many times in my life has gotten me into the practice of always saying goodbye like it's the last time, because it might be." -- Brett Kistler, How Death Brings Life Closer

The practice is small. The shift is large.

## What This Looks Like

The practice does not require long speeches or formal closure. It is more often a difference of presence than a difference of words.

- Saying "I love you" out loud when you would otherwise have left it implicit
- Naming the thing you have been meaning to say and have been postponing for a more convenient time
- Pausing for the goodbye instead of doing it while putting on a coat
- Looking at the person while you say goodbye, instead of looking at the door
- Letting the goodbye be a small punctuation rather than a transition out of one task and into the next
- Saying the appreciation that lives in you about the person, rather than assuming there will be time later
- Letting the goodbye take 30 seconds when you usually give it 3

The practice is not about every goodbye being equally weighted. It is about every goodbye carrying the felt-acknowledgement that it could be the last, so that the goodbye is fitting for what it might be.

## What the Practice Produces

For Brett, the practice came from necessity -- so many friends had died unexpectedly that the standard goodbye had repeatedly turned out to be the last one. The practice ensured that the last one, when it came, had something in it that was worth being the last.

Beyond the protective function, the practice produces a different quality of relating throughout the relationship, not only at the end. The standard goodbye, treated as a routine transition, lets the relating accumulate small unspoken things across time. The mortality-acknowledged goodbye flushes the unspoken things. What was going to be saved for a better moment gets said. The relationship runs cleaner, because each parting is a small invitation to complete whatever was left incomplete.

The cost of the standard goodbye becomes visible from the other side of the practice. The standard goodbye depends on the assumption of indefinite future contact. When the assumption is structurally unreliable -- as it always is, biologically -- the cost of the assumption is the accumulating unsaid. The practice does not require giving up the assumption. It requires not letting the assumption do all the work.

## When This Practice Comes Up

The practice is most useful when:

- A person has experienced multiple losses without closure and is asking what to do differently
- A person has just lost someone and is processing the goodbyes that were and were not said
- A person is approaching the end of a long-running relationship, friendship, or chapter and is unclear how to be in the final stretch
- A person is in the early stages of grief and is realizing that the standard goodbyes they have been using do not feel sufficient
- A person is approaching the death of a loved one and is asking what to bring to each visit
- The teaching of [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] needs an operational practice that fits into ordinary daily life

The practice is not specific to people facing extreme circumstances. It is a baseline practice that any person can pick up and that compounds over time.

## The Slow-Death and Fast-Death Distinction

Brett's practice came from the fast-death side -- accidents, sudden losses, friends who "went around the corner and never came back." The practice is one of the moves that the slow-death side (illness, aging, anticipated decline) provides automatically. When someone is dying slowly, the goodbyes are explicit by structural necessity. When someone is dying fast, the goodbyes have to have been built into the standard pattern, because there will be no warning when the standard goodbye turns out to be the final one.

The practice closes the gap between the two. By saying every goodbye as if it might be the last, the fast-death scenario gets the kind of closure the slow-death scenario provides automatically. The closure does not require advance knowledge of which goodbye is the last. The practice extends the closure across all goodbyes by default.

See [[bretts-brother-scott]] for Brett's account of the contrast: his brother's slow-death cancer giving him the explicit goodbye he never had with the many BASE-jumping friends who died fast.

## What This Practice Is Not

The practice is not:

- A dramatic performance of mortality at every parting (which collapses the practice into theater)
- A neurotic insistence on saying everything before every goodbye (which loads the relationship with anxiety)
- A way to extract reassurances from the other person ("tell me you love me before you go") that put obligation on them
- A way to avoid the unsaid thing by saying everything in a single goodbye and then never circling back
- A discipline that makes goodbyes heavy rather than honest

The lightness of the practice is what makes it usable. A goodbye said with the acknowledgement that it might be the last can still be quick. It can still be playful. It can still be ordinary. The acknowledgement is in the presence, not in the volume.

## The Relationship to the Acknowledgement-of-Death Principle

The practice is one of the daily-life applications of [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]]. The principle says that the visceral acknowledgement of fleetingness compresses life and makes the moments sweeter. The practice puts this principle into the specific ritual of the goodbye, which is one of the moments where the felt-acknowledgement most naturally fits.

The compression of life happens in the goodbye. The goodbye that includes the felt-acknowledgement is the compressed goodbye. The goodbye that proceeds on the indefinite-future assumption is the diffuse goodbye. The first lands in the body of both people; the second passes through unnoticed.

This is also the practice that operationalizes [[death-is-not-the-end-of-the-relationship]] in the living relationship. If death does not end the relating, then each goodbye is part of a relating that continues regardless. The goodbye said well becomes part of the structure of the continuing relationship, even if the in-person form of the relationship ends shortly after.

## Boundary: The Practice Does Not Require Crisis

A common mis-application: the person waits for a crisis (a friend's illness, a sense of impending separation) to begin practicing this. The practice then becomes specific to high-stakes moments and is dropped in ordinary life. This defeats the structure. The practice is for ordinary goodbyes, not crisis goodbyes. The crisis goodbye will be high-presence by structural necessity; the ordinary goodbye is the one the practice is for.

The discipline is to bring to the ordinary goodbye the presence the crisis goodbye gets automatically. This is what makes the practice generative across years rather than only across the few weeks when a crisis is active.
