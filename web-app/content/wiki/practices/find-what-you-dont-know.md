# Find What You Don't Know (Before Asking Questions)

## Definition

When someone tells you their story, the conventional move is to take in what they said and respond with a good question. This practice inverts the move: before responding, find what you do not know about what they just told you. The right question often emerges from the unknown, not from what is already on the table.

"The first thing I'm supposed to do is take all this information and then spit out a good question. What if the thing you're supposed to do is first find out all the things you don't know? Because often, if someone tells you the story, they're telling you all the things that they know, so there's no new information." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder

## How It Works

A story contains what the person knows about their situation. Responding to that information keeps the conversation inside the same field of what is already seen. The transformation happens when something new enters: information neither of you currently has.

The practice: after hearing the story, do not respond immediately. Scan internally for the unknowns. What is missing from this account? What did they not include? What might be true that they have not considered? What might be true that you have not considered? The question that arises from this scan often opens a door neither of you knew was there.

Often the things they have not seen are also the things you have not seen. The unknown is shared. The question that surfaces it serves both of you.

## When To Use It

- In coaching, after someone has finished presenting their problem.
- In conversation with a friend who is in a loop, retelling the same story.
- In meetings, before responding to a proposal.
- Whenever you notice yourself ready to give a good answer or ask a good question. That readiness is a signal to slow down and find the unknowns first.

## How To Practice

1. Listen fully to the story.
2. Before speaking, ask yourself: what don't I know here? Let the unknowns surface without forcing.
3. Notice what the story did not include: missing context, unexamined assumptions, untouched feelings, untested alternatives.
4. Ask a question that points to one of those unknowns, in [[wonder]] rather than as a probe.

## What It Produces

The conversation moves out of the loop. The person discovers something they did not see, not because you pointed it out but because the question opened a field they had not entered. You also tend to discover something. Both parties leave the conversation with more information than they brought.

This is the structural reason [[wonder]] in coaching works. The coach is not the source of insight. The unknowns are the source. The coach simply finds them.

## Tone

Slow. Spacious. The pause before the question matters more than the question itself. The person on the other end can feel whether you are searching for what is missing or selecting a question from a stockpile.
