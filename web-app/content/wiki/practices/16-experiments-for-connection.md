# Write 16 Experiments for Connection (8 for Others, 8 for Self... or 16 of Each)

## The Practice

Write down 16 ways you think might bring you closer to connection with other people. Write down 16 ways you think might bring you closer to connection with yourself. Run them one at a time, one per week.

"This would be the exercise I would give you: write down 16 ways that you think might bring you closer to connection with other people and yourself. 16 for other people, 16 for yourself, and just run those experiments. Just try one each one out — one for a week, the next one for a week, the next one for a week. What are the iterations that I need to do? What are the experiments I get to run that will make me feel more connected?" -- Joe Hudson, Dear Lonely Person

The practice is the loneliness-specific application of the more general [[write-50-experiments-before-starting]] protocol. The number is lower (16 instead of 50) because the iteration cycles are slower — a week per experiment — and because the practice is designed to be lived inside the texture of an ordinary life, not as a focused project.

## Instructions

1. **Make two lists.** One column for "ways I might feel more connected with other people." One column for "ways I might feel more connected with myself." Write 16 in each column. Some people may only need one of these; if so, do 16 of the relevant one.

2. **Include the obvious and the weird.** Some items will be obvious: "have a real conversation with X." "Call my sister." "Sign up for the men's/women's group." Some will be weird: "change the way I meditate." "Write a letter to my younger self." "Spend an hour in nature without my phone." "Ask my barista their name." The weird ones are often the ones that produce the most movement.

3. **Order them however you want.** The list is the structural support; the order does not have to be optimal. Start with one that feels accessible. Or start with one that scares you. The order matters less than the running.

4. **Run one per week.** A week is long enough to inhabit the experiment, notice what comes up, feel the responses in the body, and learn something. A day is too short; a month is too long. Calibrate by what produces the most learning, but a week is the default.

5. **Run them with wonder.** "Run the experiments and do it with wonder." -- Joe Hudson. The point of an experiment is to find out what happens. If you are running the experiments to confirm a verdict you already hold ("nothing will work for me; I'll prove it again"), the practice will not produce anything. The wonder is what makes the iteration generative. See [[wonder]] and [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]].

6. **Notice the felt sense.** After each experiment, note: did this produce any felt sense of connection? More? Less? Neutral? The body is the diagnostic, not the success of the activity. A "failed" conversation that produced a real felt moment is a successful experiment. A "successful" social event that produced no felt sense is data too.

7. **At the end of the cycle, make another list.** The first 16 are not the only 16. The work of generating the list is itself part of the practice; running them is part of the practice; the next list is part of the practice.

## Example Items

To make the list-writing concrete, the kinds of items the practice tends to surface:

**Connection with others:**
- Sign up for one of the AOA workshops or the Connection Course.
- Go to a 12-step meeting (any flavor).
- Tell one person one thing you've been hiding this week.
- Ask one person about something they care about and stay with the answer.
- Join a local club (knitting, pickleball, book, hiking) and commit to attending three times before judging it.
- Call an old friend and tell them you missed them.
- Apologize for something you've been carrying.
- Initiate a conversation with a stranger about something real.
- Sit with someone in silence for ten minutes.
- Ask for help with something you would normally do alone.

**Connection with self:**
- Change the way you meditate (sit instead of walking, walking instead of sitting, eyes open instead of closed).
- Spend a full day off all screens.
- Cook a meal slowly and pay attention to each step.
- Express one scary thing — to yourself in writing, before to anyone else.
- Take a walk with no destination.
- Notice every time you criticize yourself for one day and ask each time "what am I trying to do for myself by saying this?"
- Sit with one strong feeling for fifteen minutes without trying to change it.
- Do one thing you used to love that you've stopped doing.
- Write a letter to your future self about what matters now.
- Spend an hour in nature without your phone.

These are starters. The personalized list will include items specific to the person's life. The exercise of generating the personal list is itself part of the work.

## Why This Practice Works

Three coordinated effects:

**Pre-empts the binary.** Without the list, every attempt to connect carries the binary weight: "this worked / this didn't, I'm okay / I'm broken." With the list, each attempt is iteration N of a sequence. The verdict frame cannot land because the next iteration is already on the page. See [[habenula]] and [[failure-is-an-idea]].

**Defuses the shoulds.** "I should be less lonely" is a shame statement and produces resistance. "I'm running experiment #4 this week" is a curiosity statement and produces engagement. The list converts shoulds into experiments. See [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]] and [[should-is-shame]].

**Distributes the risk.** The terror of "I need to fix my loneliness" is too big to act on. The willingness to run "one small experiment this week" is actionable. The list distributes the impossible-feeling weight across 16 manageable steps.

The fourth, less obvious effect: the list-making itself is connection-with-self. Sitting down to write 16 ways you might feel more connected requires you to actually think about what would feel like connection to you. Many lonely people have not asked themselves that question. The asking is already a form of the answer.

## What to Watch For

- **The person who writes 3 items and stops.** The number matters. 16 is uncomfortable. If only 3 come, sit with the discomfort and write more. The constriction that prevents the list is the same constriction that has been preventing the connection.
- **The person running experiments to prove they don't work.** "If you're running experiments to prove subconsciously the same thing, watch out for that." -- Joe Hudson. The wonder has to be real. If the orientation is "I'll prove this is hopeless," every experiment will succeed at proving it. The reframe to wonder has to come first.
- **The person who treats the list as a curriculum to complete rather than an exploration.** The point is not to finish the list. The point is to be doing the experiments while feeling what happens. If you're rushing through to check items off, the practice has become another performance.
- **The person who refuses to put the weird ones on the list.** The weird ones are often the unlocks. The list of "safe" experiments will produce mostly safe data. Include items that scare you.

## The 16 / 50 Calibration

Why 16 here when [[write-50-experiments-before-starting]] uses 50?

The 50-experiments form is for a single focused goal (lose weight, find a partner, start a business) where the iterations happen relatively quickly. The 16 form is for an ongoing way-of-life shift (be more connected) where the iterations are slower and the practice is meant to inhabit a life.

The numbers are not magic. The principle is: enough items to break the verdict frame, few enough that the list can plausibly be worked through in a meaningful timeframe. For weight loss with weekly iterations, 50 is roughly a year; for connection with weekly iterations, 16 is four months. Either is a real timeline that allows the iterative orientation to take root.

## Tone

Joe delivers the practice with the warmth of a practical handoff. Not didactic. The energy is "here's a thing you can actually do this week." The instruction to "do it with wonder" is the only piece of teaching layered on; the rest is operational.
