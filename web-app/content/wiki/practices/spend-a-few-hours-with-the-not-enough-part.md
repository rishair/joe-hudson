# Spend a Few Hours With the Not-Enough Part

## The Practice

Set aside time — Joe suggests 2 or 3 hours, 2 or 3 times — to deliberately be alone with the part of you that feels "I'm not enough" or "I'm alone." No agenda. No fix. No bypassing through phone, work, or any of the routine occupations. Just the deliberate decision to be in direct contact with the feeling that the busy-ness has been holding off.

For a person willing to go further, Joe also suggests the extended version: spend three days alone, deliberately, to really feel the aloneness.

"When they turn towards it and say, oh, I'm going to go and spend three days alone and really feel that aloneness, is that that feeling is actually the quickest way to their freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The shorter, more accessible version is the 2-or-3-hours, 2-or-3-times version:

"If you do it two or three times for two or three hours, you'll notice it gets easier right away. And it's a whole lot easier than avoiding it, because that takes an entire life of energy to avoid it." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The practice is described with the Luke Skywalker cave image. The cave is the avoided territory. Going in deliberately is the practice. Coming out with the knowledge of how to handle what was in there is the result.

"It's a bit like, you know, the classic tale of Luke Skywalker going into the dark cave and facing the thing and coming out with it, with the knowledge of how to handle it. It's the same thing. That place that we consider to be a void is often the most direct route to our freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

## What Happens On Contact

The thing the avoidance has built up as catastrophic turns out, on contact, to be something else entirely. The "not enough" part of the self is, on direct contact, the part that has always wanted to be loved. The wanting-to-be-loved is itself love. The avoided thing is love that has been kept un-met.

"What we find out is that part of ourselves that feels like they're not enough is actually our longing to be loved. And the only thing that longs to be loved is love." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

This is the principle that justifies the practice. See [[what-were-avoiding-is-usually-love]] for the developed principle and [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]] for the principle specific to this practice.

The contact also produces a different relationship to the part of self that was being avoided. The practitioner discovers that the "not enough" voice has been speaking about a 3-year-old, a 5-year-old, a 25-year-old — and the absurdity of the claim becomes visible. A 5-year-old is not "not enough." A 25-year-old is not "not enough." The claim falls apart on direct examination.

"If you actually spend time with that thing, if you spend time and just be in presence with that part of you that is, oh my gosh, I'm, I'm not good enough... you're actually doing is spending time with that young part of you that always wanted just that attention and validation that you weren't given as a kid... And you stop and you lean into that and you — oh, look at that. Like, how could a 3-year-old be enough, or a 5-year-old be enough, or a 25-year-old be enough? Or how could they be not enough? How can you say like a 25-year-old is not enough, or a 5-year-old is not enough?" -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The mechanism: the "not enough" claim survives only at a distance from the actual part of self it is about. Direct contact collapses the claim. The part of self being claimed not-enough is just a person who wanted love. The wanting was never a deficiency. The wanting is the love.

## What This Practice Replaces

The practice replaces the lifelong work of avoidance. The avoidance has been a full-time job — keeping busy, achieving, performing, scrolling, working, distracting, anything that prevents the contact. The energy required to maintain the avoidance is enormous and chronic.

The practice's claim, which is verifiable in two or three sessions: meeting the avoided thing for two or three hours is dramatically less effortful than avoiding it for a lifetime.

"It's a whole lot easier than avoiding it, because that takes an entire life of energy to avoid it." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The practice does not eliminate the work. It changes which work is being done. Instead of the chronic background work of avoidance, the practitioner does the occasional foreground work of contact. The occasional foreground work is much less expensive.

## What Happens After the Contact

The contact opens the possibility of self-loving the part of self that was being avoided. The practitioner becomes the source of the love the part has been waiting for. The external chase for love can quiet because the internal source has come online.

"Eventually, if you spend enough time with it, you go, oh, well then I'll give you the love. I'll show up for you. I don't have to reject this part of myself." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The practice also changes what life feels like outside the practice. The avoided thing was running every interaction. With the avoidance released, interactions become possible in ways they were not before. The chase for external approval (see [[chase-your-own-approval]]) quiets. The inability to receive support (see [[daily-thank-you-notes-to-3-to-5-people]]) loosens. The dynamics that produced the alone-in-room state lose their fuel.

## Why the "2-or-3 Hours, 2-or-3 Times" Specifics

Joe's specific prescription is not generic "spend more time with yourself." It is specific: 2-3 hours, 2-3 times. The specifics do work.

- **2-3 hours is long enough for the busy-ness defenses to exhaust.** A 15-minute meditation session does not produce contact because the busy-ness wins. The defenses have at least an hour to deploy before they start to fatigue.
- **2-3 hours is short enough to actually do.** A weekend retreat is theoretically better but most people cannot or will not arrange it. The 2-3 hour version fits in a weekend morning or an evening.
- **2-3 times is enough to verify the practice's claim.** One session might be exceptional. By the third session, the practitioner can verify that the contact gets easier, the catastrophic image is not catastrophic, and the avoided thing is consistently something other than what the avoidance has been preparing for.
- **2-3 times is also the minimum dose for the practice to start changing the daily baseline.** Single contact rarely shifts the configuration. Three contacts begin to shift it.

The pacing also matters. The sessions should not be back-to-back. The integration between sessions is part of the practice. The practitioner needs time between sessions to notice how the daily baseline is shifting, which becomes the motivation for the next session.

## What This Practice Is Not

- **Not a meditation practice.** The point is not stillness or mindfulness training. The point is contact with the avoided felt material. If the practitioner is using meditation technique to maintain distance from the felt material, the practice has been hijacked. See [[breath-as-fertile-soil-vs-breath-as-control]].
- **Not a thinking practice.** Reflection about the feeling is not the same as feeling the feeling. The practitioner has to be in their body, not in their head, for the contact to do its work.
- **Not a journaling-only practice.** Writing about it can be part of it but cannot replace the direct contact. Writing tends to convert the felt material into narrative, which is itself a form of distancing.
- **Not a one-shot practice.** The shift compounds with repetition. A single session may feel small. The cumulative shift across 3-5 sessions is where the practice does its real work.
- **Not heroic.** The instruction is not to white-knuckle through unbearable feeling. If the contact becomes overwhelming, the practitioner can pause, ground, return. Titration matters. See [[loneliness-titrates-like-exercise-pain]] for the principle.
- **Not a substitute for professional support if needed.** Some people have trauma that makes deliberate contact with the not-enough feeling re-traumatizing. For those people, the practice should be done with professional support. The general practice is for the general case where the avoidance is structural but not trauma-bound.

## When to Use This Practice

The practice is the dynamic-3 corrective in the [[three-dynamics-of-feeling-alone-in-the-room]] framework. It is most useful when:

- The person presents [[i-feel-alone-in-rooms-despite-success]] and the avoidance dynamic has been identified as live.
- The person has done some work on dynamics 1 and 2 (approval-chasing and pushing-support-away) and has a base of recognized support that the deeper work can come back to.
- The person reports busy-ness as their primary mode and recognizes that the busy-ness is doing work for them they have not been able to name.
- The person has begun [[daily-thank-you-notes-to-3-to-5-people]] and is ready for the next layer.
- The person is in [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] and the self-reliance has been recognized as a configuration that has its limits visible from inside it.

It is less useful — and may be premature — when:

- The person is in active crisis or has trauma that the not-enough feeling is bound up in. Those situations need different work first.
- The person has no base of recognized support. The contact may produce collapse into "I really am alone" rather than "the aloneness opens into love."
- The person is using "spend time with myself" as a way to extend their isolation rather than face it. The practice is contact, not retreat.

## Tone

Joe presents the practice with a mix of practical confidence and acknowledgment of the resistance. He knows it sounds unappealing. He knows the instinct says it is unwise. He names this directly:

"And all I can say is, if you do it two or three times for two or three hours, you'll notice it gets easier right away." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The tonal move: acknowledge the resistance, name the cost of avoidance, point at the verifiability of the practice. The practitioner is being asked to test rather than to believe. The test takes 2-3 sessions of 2-3 hours. The cost of the test is small relative to what is on offer.

When teaching the practice, match this tone. Do not promise transformation. Promise that the practitioner can verify the claim in two or three sessions. Hold the door open to the practitioner choosing not to do it. The practice cannot be done from coercion. The decision to do it has to be the practitioner's.
