# Perfectionism as Critical Parent

## The Pattern

Perfectionism is not high standards. It is the internalized voice of a critical parent (or teacher, or authority figure) that could never be pleased. The person tries to satisfy a standard that is imaginary and impossible, not because the standard serves them but because failing to meet it triggers the shame of childhood.

"On some level, perfectionism is just trying to make the critical parent pleased. And since the critical parent could never really be pleased, it wasn't about you." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

## What It Looks Like

Observable signals: rigidity in the musculature, precision that feels forced, second-guessing, stunted speech, hesitation before action. The body holds the perfectionism physically. It shows up as tightness, control, and a visible effort to get things right.

Part of being "perfect" is to not be seen as a perfectionist, which creates a double bind. Naming perfectionism in a perfectionist triggers them because it exposes the thing they are trying to hide.

## The Cycle

1. An impulse arises naturally (to write, to create, to connect)
2. The critical voice converts the impulse into a "should" ("I should do this perfectly")
3. The "should" kills the motivation because it adds shame to the task
4. Avoidance or paralysis follows. See [[i-keep-procrastinating]]
5. Failure to act produces more shame, which strengthens the critical voice
6. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]]: shame does not fix the behavior, it locks it in place

## When Someone Experiences This

When nothing you do feels good enough. When you cannot enjoy accomplishments because you immediately see what could be better. When starting is harder than doing because "starting wrong" feels dangerous. When you hold yourself to standards you would never impose on someone else.
