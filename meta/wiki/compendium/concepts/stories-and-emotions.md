# Stories and Emotions

## Definition

Stories and emotions are deeply intertwined but not the same thing. Every story carries an emotional component. Emotions are always present. Stories are not. Emotions move most fluidly when there is no story. The stronger the story, the more stuck the emotion tends to be.

A story is any idea structure put around an experience. "Nature is beautiful." "My parents screwed me up." "I'm lazy." "Humanity is destroying the planet." None is 100% true or 100% false. All are partial. All carry an emotional charge.

"I can't find any thought that I have that's 100% true. And I can't find any thought that I have that's 100% false." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

## The Two Paths

### Deconstructing Stories to Free Emotions

If stories are deconstructed until they cannot be believed, emotional fluidity follows. This is the intellectual path. Every story can be questioned. Every perspective is partial. When the mind stops insisting a particular story is the complete truth, the emotions underneath are freed to move.

### Moving Emotions to Change Stories

If a stuck emotion is fully felt, the story around it changes automatically. This is the emotional path. A person cycling through "what did I do? how do I fix it?" has a stuck emotion underneath the thought loop. Moving the emotion produces clarity. The story resolves.

"If you allow the emotions to move, your stories change, period. Every time." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

Both paths work. Both are useful at different times. In coaching, using both simultaneously is most effective.

## The Practical Example

Driving past a house where he had been kicked out by neighbors, Joe felt a kick in the stomach every time. For months, he drove faster and pushed it down. Then he decided to stop avoiding it. Every day he stopped the car, felt the kick, invited it. After 2-3 weeks of daily practice, the pain was gone. The story shifted from "these people are assholes who screwed us" to seeing their pain, their anger, the full humanity of the situation. Not through intellectual understanding. Through feeling the emotion completely.

## Stories Can Help or Hinder

Naming an emotion ("I'm angry") can help someone feel it, especially early in development when emotions have been cut off. Later, the same naming becomes a way to avoid the feeling. "I'm really sad" can be either an entry point into the sadness or a report filed to avoid actually being sad.

The test: is the story bringing you deeper into the experience, or is it creating distance from it?

## The Stronger the Story, the More Stuck the Emotion

When someone is in a thought loop (cycling, ruminating, unable to stop thinking about something), there is stuck emotion underneath. The intensity of the thought cycling is proportional to the intensity of the unfelt emotion. See [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]].

## Conflict and Stories

In interpersonal conflict, both people feel unseen and misunderstood. Each holds a story tightly because the emotion underneath cannot be felt safely. If both people feel seen and understood, their stories soften and their emotions move. Trying to fight someone's story directly makes them hold it tighter. Seeing them makes it easier to release.
