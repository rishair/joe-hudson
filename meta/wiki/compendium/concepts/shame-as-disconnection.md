# Shame as Disconnection

## Definition

Shame is the feeling that arises when connection is severed. It is not a moral judgment. It is a signal: you are disconnected right now.

A child farts. If the aunties recoil ("I can't believe you did that!"), the child feels shame because the connection broke. If the aunties laugh ("Oh, you little tooter!"), no shame. Same action, different response. The shame comes from the disconnection, not from the behavior.

## The Shame Loop

Avoiding shame creates more shame. The loop works in two directions:

**Direction 1:** Suppressing the self to avoid offense. If you say something untrue to prevent someone from getting upset, you are disconnected from yourself. The disconnection produces shame. The shame you avoided by lying is replaced by shame from self-abandonment.

**Direction 2:** Scanning the room for potential offense. The vigilance of constantly monitoring what might cause disconnection is itself a form of disconnection. You are not present. You are managing. The managing creates the shame you were trying to avoid.

"If you're avoiding shame, if you're avoiding being yourself, then there's always a reason to be shameful." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

"You're either looking around the room trying to figure out how not to feel shame, which is a disconnection which creates shame, or you're saying something that's not true so that you don't get somebody upset so you can avoid shame. So that creates shame." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

## How to Heal Shame

Shame heals by being felt, not by being avoided. If shame is welcomed as a signal ("I'm disconnected right now"), it becomes information rather than a threat. The coaching question: "How else are you going to heal your shame?" -- the answer is always: by feeling it.

"If you love the shame, invite the shame, can't wait for the shame because that's the chance to get to know the shame better and to integrate it... if you see shame that way, as just a signal that you're disconnected, then it allows you to reconnect. It's an amazing feature." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

Shame is short-lived when allowed. Minutes, not hours. The fear of shame lasts far longer than the shame itself.

## Shame and Authenticity

If shame is fully embraced, nothing stops authenticity. The reason people suppress themselves is not because they are afraid of consequences. It is because they are afraid of the shame that disconnection produces. Remove the fear of shame and the person is free to be themselves.

"If you're happy to embrace shame, then what would stop you from being yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves
