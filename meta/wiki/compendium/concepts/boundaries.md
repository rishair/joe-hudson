# Boundaries

## Definition

A well-drawn boundary does two things:

1. **Increases your capacity to love the other person**, no matter their response. Even if they react with anger, the act of stating the boundary opens more love.
2. **States what you will do, not what they should do.** It never asks the other person to change. It is about your own choices.

"A boundary, when it's used optimally, increases your capacity to love somebody and it empowers you so that you see that it's your own choice." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

Boundaries are not the opposite of love. They are a requirement for love. Every paragon of love (a great mother, Gandhi, Martin Luther King) was fiercely boundaried. It is difficult to love something that dominates you.

## The Template

"If you yell at me, I'll ask you to stop. If you continue, I'll leave. In 30 minutes I'll call you. If you're ready to talk without yelling, I'm happy to re-engage."

This boundary: does not ask the other person to change. States what the boundary-setter will do. Leaves a door open for reconnection (no abandonment). Increases the capacity to love by removing the dynamic of domination.

## Boundary vs Ultimatum

When a boundary sounds like an ultimatum, it means there is fear driving it. The person is trying to control the other rather than taking care of themselves. The somatic test: a genuine boundary produces a feeling of expansion and love. A fear-based boundary produces constriction and control.

The difference is not in the words but in the emotional signature beneath them. "I'm going to leave if this continues" can be either, depending on whether the speaker is defending themselves or loving themselves.

## Boundaries Evolve

Boundaries are not fixed rules. They move as healing happens. One example from Joe's own life with his father's drinking:

1. First: "I won't be around you at all."
2. Later: "I won't be around you when you're drinking."
3. Eventually: "I won't be around you when drinking makes you behave this way."

Each level allowed deeper love. The first boundary had to be extreme because the person needed to convince themselves they deserved not to be in that situation. As that became clear, the boundary could soften.

## Why Boundaries Are Scary

Drawing a boundary tests whether the world works the way projections say it will. If the boundary is accepted, the person's entire model of the world must change. That means grieving the fact that the cage was never there.

"It's scary if your boundary is accepted and the person loves you in your boundary, because then that means the way that you have modeled the world in the past has to now change. And that means you have to change." -- Alexa Anderson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The fear of consequences prevents most boundary-drawing. When someone shows up authentically (since they were going to quit anyway), 80% of the time the relationship improves, they get promoted, things transform. 20% it ends, and the person is clear about what they want.

"Whatever the consequence is, it is a direct path to the life where we are accepted and loved for who we are." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## Weaponized Boundaries

Boundaries can be used as a control mechanism. This happens when:
- "That's just my boundary" is used to force others to change their behavior
- "I don't feel safe" becomes a way to control rather than a statement of internal experience
- The boundary asks others to be different rather than stating what the person will do

This is [[fear-triangle]] behavior: the abused becoming the abuser, using victimhood as dominance ("topping from the bottom"). See [[dont-use-vulnerability-to-control]].

## Resentment as Pointer

Resentment indicates unexpressed wants, not necessarily a boundary that needs drawing. The boundary is the "bazooka" of tools. Often just stating the want is sufficient: "I want us to enjoy doing dishes together" rather than "If you don't do the dishes, I won't either."

## Practice

Mess up. Do bad boundaries. Then apologize: "I noticed I was trying to control you. That's not how I want to be with you." Non-shame-based apology modifies behavior naturally. Over time, bad boundaries become rarer. Perfection is not required; honesty about imperfection builds trust.
