# Inherent Goodness

## Definition

The foundational concept underneath the entire methodology. There are roughly 7 main limiting beliefs, but they all rest on one: the belief that you are not inherently good. That you have to put effort to make yourself good enough, of value, better. That your natural state is insufficient.

This belief is the root. [[shame-locks-bad-habits]], [[perfectionism-as-critical-parent]], [[shoulds-vs-wants]], and nearly every concern in the compendium trace back to it.

## The oak tree question

When is an oak tree good enough? As an acorn? At 2 years old? At 150 years old? When it is collapsing into dirt? The question is absurd because the oak tree is inherently good at every stage. It does not need to arrive somewhere to be valid. The same applies to people.

A baby does not think it is bad because it is crawling. It does not need to think poorly of itself to learn to walk. Walking happens naturally. The belief "I need to be better" does not cause growth. It causes friction that slows growth down.

## How it operates

Every time you defend something about yourself, there is some way you believe you are not inherently good. If someone told you the sky is purple at noon, you would not need to defend the fact that it is blue. Defensiveness reveals the underlying belief that there is something wrong that needs protecting.

"Every time you defend something in yourself, there's some way that you're believing that you're not inherently good, that you have to defend something about yourself." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## What is actually preventing good behavior

The only thing preventing inherently good behavior is the belief that you are not inherently good. Not willpower. Not discipline. Not self-improvement. The belief itself is the obstruction.

"What's actually preventing us from acting in an inherently good way is only the idea that we're not. It's only the idea that we've done something wrong." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## When Someone Experiences This

When you feel you have to earn the right to be happy. When you believe you need to fix yourself before you can be loved. When every accomplishment feels insufficient. When rest feels like laziness. When self-improvement is driven by self-rejection rather than curiosity.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[shame-locks-bad-habits]]: shame is the expression of the not-good-enough belief
- When [[perfectionism-as-critical-parent]]: trying to be perfect is trying to be good enough
- When [[i-cant-stop-criticizing-myself]]: the inner critic enforces the not-good-enough belief
- When [[shoulds-vs-wants]]: "I should" implies "I am not good enough as I am"
- Underneath nearly every concern in the compendium

## Downstream (Where the Coaching Path Goes)

- [[learning-to-love-all-parts]]: every part is inherently good, including the parts that feel shameful
- [[connection-over-perfection]]: connection flows from accepting things as they are; perfection flows from the belief they are not good enough
- [[enjoy-what-youre-doing]]: enjoyment becomes natural when the belief that you must earn it dissolves
- Transformation is undoing the not-good-enough belief, not adding new capacities

## Tone

Warm, matter-of-fact. This is stated as obvious, not as inspirational. The tone is "of course you are good, look at how absurd the alternative is" rather than "believe in yourself!"
