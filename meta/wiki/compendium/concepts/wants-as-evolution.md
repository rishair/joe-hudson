# Wants as Evolution

## Definition

Wants are the mechanism of human evolution. They function like a tree bending toward light. Each want teaches the next step in the learning process, not the ultimate best step. Following wants without guilt leads to progressively deeper understanding of what actually produces happiness. Blocking wants with shame or moral judgment halts the evolutionary process.

"All self-interest, it seems, leads, if you allow it to, it leads to a more refined understanding of what makes us happy." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## How Wants Refine

Wants stair-step from shallow to deep. Each level must be explored before the next becomes visible:

1. A person wants a partner with the perfect body (surface want, possibly learned from culture)
2. They pursue that, discover it is not satisfying, and realize they want someone sensual who can accept pleasure (deeper want)
3. They pursue that, and eventually discover they want real love and connection (deepest want)

Skipping steps does not work. Jumping from the body want to the connection want, out of moralistic obligation, means the body want is still active underneath. It will pull. The only way through is through.

"The wants are just strategies. We're going and learning our strategies to get to the place where we can be happy and healthy and thrive." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## Selfishness Reframed

Selfishness is what your parents called you when they wanted you to do something else. A child's job is to learn how to assert their will and get their wants and needs met. Calling a child selfish is projecting: the parent is acting from their own self-interest while labeling the child's self-interest as wrong.

The evolutionary path of self-interest leads to altruism. Altruism itself has self-interest in it: helping others feels good. There is no real boundary between self-interest and care for others at the deepest level. The mechanism for teaching compassion is not shaming selfishness but supporting someone's wants so they can discover the deeper want underneath.

"If you're doing it out of a moralistic sense, if you're doing it because you're trying not to be selfish, then it's worthless and you don't actually get the benefit of it." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## Craving vs Healthy Want

When a want becomes obsessive or addictive (the "hungry ghost" state), it is no longer a healthy want. This is an absolute indicator that a deeper want exists underneath. The craving is a strategy that has stopped refining. See [[the-want-behind-the-want]] for the practice of finding the deeper want.

## Wants in Organizations

When people do not own their wants, organizations become political and gossipy. Resentfulness is the indicator: it means someone is not following what they want, not holding their boundaries, not saying the things they need to say.

A CEO who wants things done their way but pretends to want collaboration creates dysfunction. A CEO who wants collaboration but secretly wants control on 20% of decisions and will not say so creates dysfunction. Clarity of want, even when the want feels selfish, produces healthier systems.

"When people start to realize that their wants are the company's wants, that their needs are the company's needs, like the needs of the individual parts of the organism is the need of the organism." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?
