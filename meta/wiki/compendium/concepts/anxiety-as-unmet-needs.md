# Anxiety as Unmet Needs

## Definition

Anxiety is distinct from fear. It operates on three levels simultaneously:

**Head (prefrontal cortex):** Constantly preparing for attack. Scanning for what might go wrong. Trying to modulate performance and environment to prevent anything bad.

**Heart (emotional/mammalian):** A signal of unmet needs. Mammals get anxious when their needs are not being met. Because humans have a sense of self far beyond physical survival, the range of "needs" is vast.

**Nervous system (gut):** A constriction of life force. The energy that animates and propels is being squeezed. When attention is brought to the constriction without trying to change it, the constriction loosens.

"The more I paid attention to it, the more it transformed inside of me, the more it just showed that it was life force." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

## The Unmet Needs Insight

The most actionable dimension: anxiety is telling you a need is not being met. The typical response is to loop in thought, trying to find 100% certainty before acting. The better response is to identify the actual need and address it.

Example: anxiety about being fired. The typical response is to work harder, second-guess, try to keep the boss happy. This makes the firing more likely (see [[fear-invites-what-we-fear]]). The need-based response: go to the boss and ask for clear expectations. "I notice I'm operating out of anxiety about my job. Can we agree on what success looks like so I can operate from effectiveness instead of fear?"

"Most people's fears over having to feel some way about themselves or have to feel some way, that's the consequence that we're actually avoiding." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

## Love It, Don't Manage It

Being with anxiety as a management technique stops working when the brain recognizes it as a strategy to make anxiety go away. The mind says: "If I be with this, I can get rid of it." Then it is no longer being with it; it is managing it.

The real practice: love the anxiety. Invite it. Want it.

"I can't wait to be anxious again. When you're at that place, that's the real freedom, where you're not trying to manage yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

## Life Requires Friction

Trying to eliminate anxiety is trying to eliminate friction from life. A cell cannot survive without resistance. A sun cannot exist without it. Life has anxiety as an aspect. The practice is not to eliminate it but to hold the dis-ease as lightly as possible without letting it go.

## Sustained Anxiety

Anxiety evolved for short bursts (physical threats). Modern anxiety is sustained because the sense of self can be under threat continuously. A CEO's identity during an 8-month hostile takeover. A teenager's social standing while away from Wi-Fi. The sustained nature makes it harder to process, but the mechanism is the same: feel it, find the need, address it.

Epigenetically, anxiety transfers across generations. Mouse studies show 3 generations of safety required to resolve inherited anxiety patterns. The nervous system carries what the conscious mind does not.

## Shame About Anxiety

Many people have shame about being anxious, which creates more anxiety. Noticing anxiety in someone's body within 30 seconds of meeting them, and hearing "How did you know? Did somebody tell you?" reveals the shame. The shame layer must be addressed alongside the anxiety itself.

## Integration Question

"How is my anxiety right now?" -- designed to bypass story and go to the felt quality of the anxiety. Not what the anxiety is about. How it is right now.
