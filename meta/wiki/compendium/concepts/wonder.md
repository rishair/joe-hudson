# Wonder

## Definition

Wonder is the W in [[view]]. Joe defines it as curiosity without needing the answer. It combines curiosity and awe. Joe distinguishes it from curiosity: curiosity can become focused on finding the answer, which constricts. Wonder stays in the openness of the question.

"Wonder is a lot like curiosity, except for you're not looking for the answer. There's a lot more awe and wonder than there is in curiosity, but you're definitely not looking for the answer." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

## How Joe Explains It

Joe describes a physical shift: asking an open question produces expansiveness. Trying to figure out the answer produces constriction. Wonder is staying in the expansive state.

Being with someone in wonder produces relaxation in the other person. Their system senses they are not being analyzed or pushed toward a solution.

"If you just stop and think about, like, what would it be like to be with a person who's just in the question with me, who's just, like, has wonder about what's happening with me, and they don't need me to solve anything, like, you can feel your whole system relax." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

## Wonder dissolves fear

Joe uses a visualization exercise. Imagine running from a lion. It is catching up. You are about to die. Now ask: how much does it weigh? The fear disappears.

"If you stop and you go, how much does it weigh? All the fear goes away. Which is why wonder is such an important thing to point to, because if you actually stop and say, what am I curious about? What do I have actual deep wonder for? Then the fear that you have dissipates." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course #1)

Joe connects this to interpersonal dynamics: "If you're trying to push somebody into something, that's because you're in fear." Fear drives controlling behavior. Wonder dissolves the fear, which dissolves the control.

## Under attack, no learning

Stress and perceived attack shut down curiosity and learning. This ranges from students performing worse on tests under stress to the impossibility of two people in a fight suddenly agreeing.

"If you are under attack, you can't be curious, or if you're under attack, you can't learn because you're not curious." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course #1)

Even subtle forms of pressure ("just do this task," "be happy," "stop crying") reduce the other person's capacity to learn. This is why [[impartiality]] matters: agenda is a subtle form of attack.

## Wonder is an undoing, not a doing

Joe is emphatic that effort makes wonder harder. Trying to "do" wonder takes you away from it. Wonder is accessed by removing what blocks it, not by adding a new behavior.

"It's far more of an undoing than it is a doing. Effort is one of the things that makes wonder more difficult. If I say, okay, now I have to go and do wonder and I have to be in a wonder state of mind, that immediately makes it harder to be in a wonder state of mind." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

The practice is simple: ask "what am I in wonder about right now?" ten times a day. Children demonstrate that wonder is natural. It gets removed by parents and teachers, not added by practice.

## Wonder directed inward

Joe describes turning wonder on your own internal experience as "a little slice of heaven." Wonder applied to the inner critic dissolves its authority. See [[i-cant-stop-criticizing-myself]].

"All the things that you tell yourself, that inner critic, just like it starts to lose all of its grip if you're able to focus on the wonder." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

Joe also connects wonder to depression: "The depression is being created mostly because of a critical voice in the head that there's no curiosity about." Directing wonder at the critic itself creates movement where stagnation existed.

## When Someone Experiences This

When conversations feel like interrogations. When you notice you already know what you think about someone and have stopped being curious about them. When relationships feel dull because everyone has been categorized. When fear is driving your decisions or conversations. When your inner critic is loud and relentless. Wonder reopens what has been closed.
