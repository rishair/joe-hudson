# Empathy

## Definition

Empathy is the E in [[view]]. Joe defines it as being with somebody's experience without losing yourself in it.

"It's not watching somebody's experience, and it's not wanting to change somebody's experience. It's being with them in the experience without losing yourself in it." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy (Connection Course #4)

## What empathy is NOT

Joe defines empathy as much by exclusion as inclusion:
- Not avoiding their emotional state
- Not trying to change it
- Not trying to make them happy
- Not trying to get them to an epiphany
- Not buying into their story ("oh yeah, fuck that guy")
- Not losing yourself in them
- Not watching them like an ant colony (analyzing without feeling)
- Not putting yourself above them (that is sympathy, not empathy)

"True empathy is being with somebody, it's being next to them, it's like holding their hand, it's not losing yourself in them." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course #1)

## Empathy is a felt sense, not an intellectual understanding

Joe is emphatic that empathy cannot be taught through logic. It is like proprioception: you know where your left foot is without measuring it. You know when you are done going to the bathroom without timing it. Empathy works the same way.

"Empathy is so much of a feeling more than it is an intellectual understanding." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy (Connection Course #4)

"If you want a logical conversation about empathy, go and feel people, go and be empathetic and stay in yourself while you do it. And that's a far better way. Just experiment." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy (Connection Course #4)

## How to stay in yourself while empathizing

Joe's specific practice: put some attention in your own body while being with the other person. Feel the bottom of your feet. Notice how the sound feels in your ear. This prevents losing yourself. See [[body-attention-as-anchor]].

For people who tend to lose themselves in others (children of alcoholics, children of abuse who learned to read the room to survive), Joe teaches the question [[is-this-mine]].

## Fear shuts down empathy

Fear responses (fight, flight, freeze) all shut down the capacity for empathy. Fight turns the other person's emotion into anger. Flight turns it into managing. Freeze turns it into dissociation. To restore empathy, feel the fear. See [[feeling-unfelt-emotions]].

"It makes sense that fear-- when we're in fear, it's really hard to have any empathy at all." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy (Connection Course #4)

## What empathy reveals

Joe describes empathy as a data source. In meetings, empathy reveals whether people are actually aligned or just saying they agree. In sales, empathy reveals when a customer has disconnected. In relationships, empathy reveals the emotion underneath the words.

"So many logical arguments are really not at all about the logic. It's not really about the tactics or the facts. It's about the emotional state of people and their fear and what they need and what they want." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy (Connection Course #4)

## Empathy prevents manipulation

Counter-intuitive: empathic people are LESS likely to be manipulated, not more. People who cannot feel are more susceptible because they are avoiding emotions and can be exploited through those avoidance patterns. A person who can feel everything has no button to push.

"Just look at the most non-empathetic people on our planet, and they are the most likely to be manipulated by politicians or authorities or advertising." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy (Connection Course #4)

## When Someone Experiences This

When you try to help people but end up drained. When you lose yourself in others' emotions. When conversations feel disconnected because you are analyzing. When you carry emotional residue from interactions for days. When your children's tantrums overwhelm you.
