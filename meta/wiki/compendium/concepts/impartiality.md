# Impartiality

## Definition

Impartiality is the I in [[view]]. Joe defines it as not trying to achieve an outcome for yourself or others. In a conversation, it means not trying to get the other person anywhere.

Joe offers a simpler version, from explaining it to his 11-year-old daughter: "It's saying, I trust you. You know what's best. I'm going to follow your lead. I think you know what you need better than anybody else could know what you need, and I want to explore that with you." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality (Connection Course #3)

## How Joe Explains It

**What partiality looks like.** Joe lists the common forms: "I know what you need, let me give you advice. I want you to be better, I want you to be healed, I want you to be different. I want you to feel like I'm valuable in this conversation."

**The subtle message of partiality.** "The subtle message behind partiality is that you think that you're smarter than the other person. I know what's best for you." This reinforces the listener's myth that they are not good enough. If you speak to someone as wise, the wise part shows up. If you speak to them as helpless, the helpless part shows up.

**The river.** In business, there is a river made of customer wants, employee wants, and market forces. Impartiality means reading the river and going with it. Partiality means bending the river to your will.

**Goals are fine. Attachment to the path is not.** Impartiality does not mean giving up your goals. It means letting go of how you get there. Joe's director analogy: "If you tell the actor, 'This is exactly how I want it,' you're not going to get it. But if you can give them direction and then recognize something beautiful when it comes, you can get that easily and all day."

## When Someone Experiences This

When you feel yourself pushing an outcome on someone and sensing their resistance grow. When you have an agenda for a conversation and it keeps going sideways. When you keep having the same conversation with the same person and nothing changes. When your adult children do not want to be around you. "If they don't want to be around you, I guarantee you have an agenda for them."

- When someone is controlling and frustrated by resistance from others
- When a leader is micromanaging

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Joe moves to [[owning-wants-vs-being-partial]]: own your wants outright rather than hiding them as agenda
- [[vulnerability]] is required: stating your wants directly is scary
- Drawing boundaries: impartiality requires MORE boundaries, not fewer, because you stop trying to manage people into giving you what you need
- [[wonder]] pairs with impartiality: both involve releasing the need for a specific outcome
- See [[dont-try-to-fix-people]] for the explicit guardrails

## Questions Joe Asks

- "What do you want?" (to the other person, trusting their wisdom)
- "I noticed my entire body is getting tense right now" (owning what is happening in his body, not directing the other person)

## Boundary Conditions

Impartiality is asymptotic. Every sentence has some want underneath it. Complete impartiality is impossible. The practice is about reducing agenda, not eliminating it.

If you deny your wants entirely, "it'll sap all the joy out of your life." Impartiality requires owning wants, not suppressing them. See [[owning-wants-vs-being-partial]].
