# I feel exhausted from helping everyone

## What Someone Says

- "I give and give and give and I have nothing left"
- "Everyone depends on me"
- "I can't say no"
- "If I stop taking care of everyone, everything will fall apart"
- "I'm exhausted but I can't stop"
- "I resent the people I'm helping but I feel guilty about that"

## What's Underneath

This is [[caring-vs-caretaking]] operating. The person is managing other people's emotions to avoid feeling their own. The exhaustion is not from caring. Genuine caring produces softening, not depletion. The exhaustion is from the effort of emotional management: monitoring, anticipating, preventing, tiptoeing, rehearsing.

The hidden emotion being avoided is usually one of: fear of abandonment (if I stop helping, they will leave), fear of loss (if I stop enabling, they will get worse), helplessness (I cannot actually fix this but I cannot stop trying), or grief (the relationship is not what I pretend it is).

## What to Look For (Reads)

- Is resentment building? Resentment is the indicator of caretaking. If the person resents the people they are "helping," the help is caretaking
- Is the person managing how they communicate? Rehearsing phrasings, positioning truth so it will be "heard"? This is caretaking disguised as care
- Does the person believe the other person cannot handle the truth? "They're too fragile." "They'll fall apart." This belief IS the caretaking
- Is the person getting softer or more neurotic over time? Softening = care. Spinning = caretaking

## Coaching Path

1. Name the pattern: this is caretaking, not caring. The distinction is not about how much you give but about the motive: are you managing their emotions to avoid feeling your own?
2. Ask [[what-do-you-want]]: "Underneath all the helping, what do you actually want?" The want has been buried under obligation
3. Identify the avoided emotion: [[what-feeling-am-i-resisting]] -- what would you have to feel if you stopped caretaking? Usually grief, fear of loss, or helplessness
4. [[feel-before-acting]]: feel the grief or fear first, then decide how to show up from clarity
5. The compassion paradox: the most compassionate thing for yourself IS the most compassionate thing for them. How is your authentic want NOT what is best for them too?
6. [[the-three-to-five-rule]]: when you stop caretaking, expect pushback 3-5 times. The system will test the new boundary
