# I can't stop criticizing myself

## What Someone Says

- "I have this voice in my head that won't stop telling me I'm not good enough"
- "I'm so hard on myself"
- "I know I shouldn't beat myself up but I can't stop"
- "My inner critic is relentless"
- "I keep telling myself I need to be better / do more / try harder"

## What Joe Hears

Joe hears a critical voice that has been running unchallenged because no one has ever turned [[wonder]] on it. The critic operates by being taken at face value. Its authority dissolves under examination. Joe does not hear a problem to be solved. He hears a part to be wondered about.

The inner critic is also connected to [[learning-to-love-all-parts]]: the critic itself is a part that needs love, not elimination. And the critic often maintains patterns from [[trauma-mapping]]: it recreates the criticism the person received in childhood.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- Is the person fighting the critic (trying to make it stop)? If so, Joe may point out that fighting is the critic's own game
- Is the person collapsed under the critic (believing it)? If so, Joe may introduce [[wonder]] as a way to create distance
- Has the person identified with being broken? If so, see [[learning-to-love-all-parts]]

## Coaching Path

Joe's approach to the inner critic centers on [[wonder]]. Instead of trying to silence the critic, Joe turns wonder on it.

"You can just turn that curiosity right to the critical voice in the head and things will change pretty quickly if you can consistently have that state of wonder about the voice in your head, not trying to solve it, but just to be in awe of like, whoa, what is this thing constantly managing me? What gives it the right? What makes it think it's right? What makes it think it's good at its job when it's created complete stagnation in my system? What makes it think it's good at its job if it's still having to manage me over the same shit 10 years later? Like, what is happening here?" -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

Joe's approach is NOT to:
- Try to replace negative thoughts with positive ones
- Fight or silence the critic
- Analyze where the critic came from (at least not as the first move)

Joe's approach IS to:
- Turn [[wonder]] on the critic itself
- Question the critic's competence (not its intent)
- Let the [[wonder]] create movement where stagnation existed
