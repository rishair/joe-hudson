# I can't be myself without offending people

## What Someone Says

- "How do I say what I want without it feeling like I'm attacking them?"
- "People can't handle me"
- "I know I'm good at what I do, but people get offended"
- "She was in the middle of a sentence and I couldn't interrupt"
- "I have to deliver quality" (where quality = not offending)

Often the person frames this as a social skill problem ("how do I interrupt politely?") when the actual issue is emotional: they are avoiding the feeling that arises when someone is upset with them.

## What the Coaching Hears

Underneath "I can't be myself" is shame avoidance. The person does not want to feel the disconnection that comes when someone reacts negatively. The conflict avoidance is not about the other person's feelings. It is about the shame that arises in the person when connection is severed. See [[shame-as-disconnection]].

This concern often comes with a lose-lose framing: "If I'm myself and you get angry, I feel disconnected. If I suppress myself to avoid your anger, I'm disconnected from myself." Both paths lead to disconnection, but the person only sees two options.

## What to Look For (Reads)

- If the person is intellectualizing it as a skills problem: the coaching path goes to the emotion underneath. "What's the feeling you're trying to avoid in that moment?"
- If the person presents it alongside "everything is going well, I can't find anything to work on": this IS the issue. The inability to say "everything is fine" is the same pattern as the inability to say "I disagree."
- If the person shows the pattern in the session itself (laughing off discomfort, apologizing preemptively): name it live.

## Coaching Path

1. **Reveal the impossibility of never offending.** Ask: "Who has delivered quality who hasn't offended people?" (Trick question: nobody.) Or: "Say something to me that absolutely won't offend me." Even "you have a blue sweater" can offend someone. The goal of never offending is not achievable, so the real question is: what feeling are you avoiding?

2. **Find the emotion underneath.** Usually shame. "What's the feeling that comes when someone is upset with you?" The answer is disconnection, being shut out, being alone. That is shame.

3. **Make the case for conflict.** "If you never offend anybody, you can never really build trust." Trust is built by having conflict and overcoming it together. The most trustworthy people are not conflict avoidant. Conflict avoidance guarantees stagnation.

4. **Experience the shame live.** Role-play a moment of offense. Let the shame arise. Discover it is survivable and short-lived. See [[role-playing-the-dynamic]].

5. **Reframe shame as signal.** Shame is a signal of disconnection, not a moral failing. If shame is welcomed rather than avoided, there is nothing stopping authenticity. See [[shame-as-disconnection]].
