# I freeze around authority figures

## What Someone Says

- "I freeze around people who remind me of my father"
- "I can't be myself around my boss"
- "I either fight or shut down when someone has power over me"
- "I get triggered by people telling me what to do"
- "Authority figures make me feel unseen"

## What's Underneath

The person is relating to authority figures through the lens of their relationship with a parent (usually the father). The current authority figure is standing in for the parent. Whatever dynamic was present in childhood (not being seen, being controlled, love being conditional on performance) is being replayed in the current relationship. See [[trauma-mapping]].

The false dichotomy of fight or submit is often present: "I either want to fight or I want to roll over and stay safe." Both positions give power to the authority figure. Rebellion against someone and submission to them are both forms of being controlled by them. See [[empower-over-power]].

## What to Look For (Reads)

- Is the person in a fight/submit binary? If they can only see two options (fight or give in), they are in a power-over frame. The third option (empowered, upright, undefended love) has not been found yet
- Is the person projecting? The judgment of the authority figure ("they don't see me") often mirrors what the person is doing themselves ("I'm not seeing them"). See [[reflecting-the-judgment-back]]
- Is the person undermining the authority figure as a form of rebellion? Reduced productivity, passive resistance, checking out. This is rebellion wearing the mask of helplessness
- Is the person dissociating when the emotion gets close? Watch for: breaking eye contact, voice going flat, "I lose connection with myself when I look at you"

## Coaching Path

1. Name what the person is describing: "How do you see them as like your father?" Connect present to childhood
2. Challenge the assumption: "What if they did see you and you can't recognize it?"
3. Reflect the judgment back: [[how-is-that-not-true-about-you]] -- the judgment of the other is often the person's own pattern
4. Identify what the person is not seeing about the authority figure: "What are you not seeing about them? How are you not allowing them in?"
5. Name the false dichotomy: fight and submission are both positions of disempowerment
6. Move toward the body: "What is this experience of non-fight, of non-rolling-over? What is it in the body?"
7. Find the third option: empowered, undefended, upright love. Neither fighting nor submitting but being present with boundaries
8. Practice it live: [[role-playing-the-dynamic]] -- become the authority figure and let the person practice the new stance

The arc follows [[the-developmental-arc]]: concern → childhood pattern → find the unfelt emotion → feel it → shift.
