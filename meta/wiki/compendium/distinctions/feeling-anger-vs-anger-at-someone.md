# Feeling Anger vs Anger At Someone

## The Distinction

**Feeling anger:** The emotion moves through the system. It is experienced, expressed, and resolves into clarity. "I'm angry, I'm angry, I'm angry!" -- jumping up and down in the kitchen. The anger is felt. Nobody is targeted.

**Anger at someone:** The emotion is deployed as a weapon to control, punish, or change another person's behavior. "God damn it, kids, stop talking to me while I'm cooking!" -- using anger to scare them into compliance.

The first produces connection and clarity. The second produces shame, blame, and more stuck anger.

## Why People Confuse Them

Society treats all anger as the second kind. Because most people's experience of anger involves being hurt by someone's anger or hurting someone with their own, the emotion itself gets tabooed. The sword gets hated because it was used to hack, and then it cannot be used constructively.

"Our brains don't even notice in this moment that there's another option. It's like, if I can't get angry because I might destroy my friendship, but then it never thinks, oh, well, just go get angry somewhere else." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part I

The binary is: anger at the person, or repress the anger. The third option -- feel the anger separately, then address the situation from clarity -- is invisible until it is practiced.

## The Test

Anger at someone aims to change their behavior. Feeling anger aims at nothing. One is directed outward to produce an effect. The other moves through and resolves.

Resisted anger always ends up directed at someone eventually. The pressure builds until it explodes. Allowed anger dissipates naturally and leaves determination in its place. See [[resistance-is-what-you-fear]].

## Passive Aggression as Hidden Anger

When anger is fully repressed, it does not disappear. It emerges as passive aggression: safety language used as a weapon, subtle undermining, dismissal. This is still anger at someone, just with plausible deniability.

The response: invite directness. "I hear that you're upset. Can you just be really direct and angry with me about it? Just as an experiment." When people try direct anger instead of passive aggression, they often smile afterward. They feel the freedom of actually touching their anger.
