# Past Shame vs Current Shame

## The Distinction

Joe distinguishes between past shame (shame about things you have done) and current shame (shame about what you are doing right now). Past shame is useless and actively harmful. Current shame is useful.

## How Joe Explains It

Current shame is a signal: "what we're doing right now isn't what we want to be doing. It's not in alignment with who we are." This signal is useful. It tells you something needs to change in the present.

Past shame is self-brutalization: "this idea that if I brutalize myself, then it'll somehow change the way that I act in the future, which tons of studies show that that's just not true." Past shame is a form of resistance, and resistance keeps patterns in place. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]].

"It's not really useful. And it's what really condemns us to repeat that pattern." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability (Connection Course #5)

## When Someone Experiences This

When you beat yourself up about past behavior and it does not change your future behavior. When shame about what you did 5 years ago still controls you today. When the inner critic uses past failures as evidence that you are broken. The distinction helps: past shame can be released through vulnerability. Current shame can be listened to as information.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[vulnerability]] practice surfaces old shame
- When [[i-cant-stop-criticizing-myself]]: the inner critic often runs on past shame
- When [[shame-locks-bad-habits]]: the shame is maintaining the very behavior it condemns

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- For past shame: Joe moves toward [[vulnerability]], specifically sharing the shame with others to see that you are still lovable. Confession, making amends, being seen
- For current shame: Joe treats it as useful data about misalignment. The question becomes "what would be in alignment?"
