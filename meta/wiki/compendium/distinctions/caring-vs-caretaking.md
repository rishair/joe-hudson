# Caring vs Caretaking

## The Distinction

Caring is enjoying the experience of supporting someone without needing them to change. Caretaking is managing someone else's emotions so you do not have to feel your own. Caring empowers. Caretaking creates dependency.

The difference is motive: caring is about the other person's wellbeing. Caretaking is about avoiding your own discomfort. The wife who manages the household so her husband does not get angry is not caring for him. She is managing her own fear of his anger through him.

## How to tell which one you are in

**Resentment.** Caretaking always leads to resentment. Eventually the caretaker resents the person ("I've sacrificed everything for you and this is what I get") and the person resents the caretaker ("stop treating me like I'm incapable"). If resentment is building, caretaking is operating.

**Softening vs spinning.** Over time, genuine caring softens a person. Their heart gets bigger. They become less rigid, more open. Caretaking produces the opposite: increasing neuroticism, isolation, rigidity, and spinning.

**Managing how to say it.** If you are rehearsing multiple phrasings in your head, trying to find the one that will produce the "right" reaction, that is caretaking. "If you're worried about how you're gonna say it so that the person across from you can hear it and accept it, and you've run over multiple solutions in your head, then you're definitely in it."

**Thinking they can't handle it.** "If it ever crosses your mind that the person can't handle what you're going to say to them, if you ever think that they're too weak or incapable, those absolutely are key indicators that you're in the caretaking side of things."

## The compassion paradox

The most compassionate thing for yourself is also the most compassionate thing for the other person. This is not a choice between self-love and love of another. If the system requires you to sacrifice yourself to care for someone else, the design is masochistic.

When someone breaks through from caretaking to authenticity ("this is what I actually want"), the question becomes: "How is that NOT the most compassionate thing for the person you've been caretaking?" The answer is almost always: it is.

## Caretaking as control

Caretaking is a form of control. The caretaker controls the other person's emotional state so they do not have to feel their own. This is the same dynamic as a bully controlling through anger: different strategy, same structure. Both avoid feeling something by controlling the other person.

In addiction: enabling prevents the person from hitting bottom. The caretaker is not protecting the addict. They are protecting themselves from the grief of potentially losing the addict. See [[feel-before-acting]]: feel the loss first, then decide from clarity.

## When Someone Experiences This

When you feel responsible for other people's emotions. When you cannot relax unless everyone around you is okay. When you give advice compulsively. When people around you seem dependent but also resentful. When you feel drained after helping. When you keep having the same conversation with someone about their problem and nothing changes. See [[i-keep-ending-up-in-the-same-pattern]].
