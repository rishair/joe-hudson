# Caring vs Caretaking

## The Distinction

Caring is being with someone in their experience, supporting them without needing them to change. Caretaking is trying to make them feel better, manage their emotional state, or fix their problem. Caring leaves the person empowered. Caretaking leaves the person dependent.

## How Joe Explains It

Joe describes a CEO who "tended towards caretaking." Because he felt responsible for his employees' emotions, the company developed a victim mentality. When the CEO shifted from caretaking to caring, he stopped saying "here's how we're going to fix the world" and started asking "how do you want to fix the world? Clearly you're unhappy. How are you going to fix it?" This empowered people to solve their own problems.

Joe references an Aboriginal or indigenous saying: "If you're here to help me, no thank you, but if you're here to work together on our mutual freedom, let's get to work."

## The caretaker's hidden motive

Joe identifies that caretaking often comes from the caretaker's need to manage their own emotional state through managing others: "If you feel happy, I'll be happy. If you're not angry, I'll be happy. You know, if you're in a good mood, I'm in a good mood." This is not care. It is using the other person as emotional regulation.

## When Someone Experiences This

When you feel responsible for other people's emotions. When you cannot relax unless everyone around you is okay. When you give advice compulsively. When people around you seem dependent on you but also resentful. When you feel drained after helping. See [[dont-try-to-fix-people]].

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[empathy]] is discussed and someone asks how to be with someone without fixing them
- When a leader or parent reports that the people around them seem helpless or dependent
- When someone is exhausted from "helping" everyone

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- [[impartiality]]: caretaking is a form of partiality (wanting them to be different so you feel okay)
- [[empathy]]: caring is empathy in action, being with rather than fixing
- [[owning-wants-vs-being-partial]]: the caretaker needs to own their want (to feel safe, to feel valuable) rather than hiding it behind "helping"
