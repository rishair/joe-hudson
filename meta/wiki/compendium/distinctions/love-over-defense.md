# Love over Defense

## The Distinction

Love is a deep welcoming. Not niceness. Not fondness. Not fear of losing. A deep welcoming of whatever is, including the parts that are uncomfortable, dangerous, or unknown. Defense is any form of separation: mental walls, somatic shutdown, subtle fear.

The confusion: defending and loving cannot coexist. They can. Boundaries are love. Fighting for what matters is love. Conflict held with an open heart is love. What love cannot coexist with is separation: deciding someone is an other, dropping their humanness, making them an obstacle.

"Just because I have to draw a boundary doesn't mean that I can't love you. Just because I am in a fight with you doesn't mean that I can't welcome you." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## Love as deep welcoming

Of the four faces of love (peace, enjoyment, care, deep welcoming), deep welcoming is the greatest leverage point. It activates the others.

"If you close your eyes right now and you deeply welcome yourself here and love yourself just as you are right now, that's it. That's all there is to it." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

It is a somatic experience. Not a thought. Not a decision. A felt sense of allowing yourself or another to be exactly as they are in this moment.

## Love requires empowerment

Love without empowerment is not love. It is fondness, fear of losing, or desire. Only from empowerment (being yourself despite consequences; see [[empower-over-power]]) is it possible to deeply welcome without needing the other to be different.

"You can't really love if you're not empowered. You can be fond of, you can be scared of losing, but to actually love in a way that is beyond you, that is a deep welcoming, the only way you can deeply welcome is to feel deeply empowered, to not be worried of the results." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## The mechanism of love

Love is the most powerful change agent because it allows things to move and evolve. Judgment contains and holds in place. A part of the self that is judged ("that's bad, I need to fix it") cannot move. A part that is loved ("I welcome this") can express, evolve, and transform on its own.

"If I love an aspect of myself that so far I haven't been able to love, it gets to move, it gets to express, and it gets to evolve. If I'm saying that that part is bad, I'm containing it, I'm holding it so it can't move and so it can't evolve." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

This is WHY [[learning-to-love-all-parts]] works. Not because love is nice. Because love is the condition for movement. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]]: shame holds things in place; love allows them to move.

## Misconceptions about love

Whatever love looked like in childhood becomes the misconception. Critical parent: love is criticism. Absent parent: love is absence. Violent parent: love is painful.

Common cultural misconceptions:
- **Love is nice.** Not true. Love is often a hard truth spoken with an open heart. The most loving act may be "we all know you're lying and we'd like you so much more if you didn't"
- **Love doesn't hold boundaries.** Not true. Great mothers hold boundaries constantly. Love without boundaries is codependency. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]]
- **Love and conflict can't coexist.** Not true. See the Bhagavad Gita: the instruction is to fight, but to fight with love
- **Love means wanting someone to change.** Not true. If you want them to change, you are loving them conditionally. "I don't think it's really possible to love somebody fully and want them to change."

## When Someone Experiences This

When you are afraid to love because you might get hurt. When your relationships are managed rather than welcomed. When you protect yourself from connection to avoid pain. When you equate vulnerability with weakness rather than with love. When you cannot hold a boundary AND stay open-hearted at the same time.
