# Owning Wants vs Being Partial

## The Distinction

Joe draws a sharp line between owning your wants outright and being partial. Both involve wanting something. The difference is ownership.

Owned wants are stated directly. They are vulnerable. They allow the other person to see what you need. Hidden wants (partiality) are implied through behavior, manipulation, or agenda. They are not stated. They feel political and self-serving to the other person.

## How Joe Explains It

"Wants are owned and partiality is not owned. To own your wants outright in a conversation is quite vulnerable. It allows you to feel exposed, and you're telling people your actual truth. Being partial, you can hide all that stuff and not take a look at it." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality (Connection Course #3)

Joe adds that ownership can actually reduce the intensity of the want: "When you own your want completely, ownership can actually make the want less intense and allow you to see what the world is like, especially if you add an apology with it, which is like, oh, I want for you to be different than you are, and I apologize for that."

## When Someone Experiences This

When you notice yourself subtly steering a conversation rather than saying what you want directly. When your partner accuses you of being passive-aggressive. When you are trying to get someone to do something without having to ask. When you are afraid that stating your want will be rejected. Joe's frame: it is more vulnerable (and more effective) to state the want than to manipulate toward it.

- [[vulnerability]]: owning wants requires being in your truth even when it is scary
- Drawing boundaries: "I don't want to hear this story anymore" is an owned want
- Joe models this mid-conversation: "I noticed that I really want to be valuable to you, and that's not the best way I can respect you or this conversation"
