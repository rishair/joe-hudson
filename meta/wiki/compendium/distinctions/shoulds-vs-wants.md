# Shoulds vs Wants

## The Distinction

A "should" says you need to be managed. A "want" trusts who you are. Following wants is the more effective path to change. Following shoulds is the least effective.

Shoulds are the voice of control: "I should work out. I should eat better. I should be more patient." These convert natural impulses into obligations, which kills motivation. Wants are the natural impulse itself: "I want to move my body. I want to feel healthy. I want to be present."

## Why wants work better

An infant from 0 to 7 has no shoulds. They follow their wants exclusively. This is the period of greatest development in human life. There may be cultures where shoulds never appear at all.

"Following your wants is really the most effective way to transform. And following your shoulds is the least effective way. And to make it a should is really just a doubt and doubt of trust and of who you are." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

## Should as mechanism of shame

"Should is a mechanism of shame. Shame is the locks that keep the chains of bad habits in place." Energetically, a should is oppressive. Intellectually, it is control-based. Emotionally, it is rigidity. Neurologically, it is a threat. The body detects it as danger. -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Rebellion or submission

A should produces exactly two responses, both ineffective:

1. **Rebellion.** Something innate says "no." This is why the things you have been telling yourself you should do for decades remain undone. The internal rebellion is stronger than the should.
2. **Submission.** The person collapses into compliance: "you're right, I should." This is disempowerment, not change. It produces victim mentality and dependency on the critical voice.

Neither rebellion nor submission produces transformation. Wants produce transformation.

## Why shoulds fail

Shoulds create the same dynamic as [[perfectionism-as-critical-parent]]: a critical voice imposes a standard, the natural impulse is replaced by obligation, the obligation produces shame when unmet, and the shame locks the behavior in place. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]].

The list experiment: at 26, Joe wrote down everything about himself he did not want to admit. He found it a year later. Most items had changed through awareness alone. The ones that had not changed all had heavy shoulds attached. Awareness changes things. Shoulds prevent change.

## When Someone Experiences This

When "I should" appears in every sentence about change. When self-improvement feels like punishment. When motivation evaporates the moment something becomes an obligation. When the same resolution has been made and broken repeatedly. See [[i-keep-procrastinating]].

The coaching question: what do you actually want? Not what should you do. What do you want?
