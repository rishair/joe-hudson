# Knowing vs Wonder

## The Distinction

Joe identifies "knowing" (or wanting to be seen as knowing) as the direct opposite of [[wonder]]. When a person is in knowing, they have stopped learning, stopped discovering, and begun defending a position. Wonder keeps the search open. Knowing closes it.

"The opposite of wonder is knowing, or wanting to be maybe seen as knowing." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

## The trap of knowing

Joe observes that the person in any group who "knows everything" is reliably the problem person. They are not on a learning journey with anyone.

"I don't know how many companies I've been in where I see somebody and they're like, that's the problem person. And then you can meet the problem person. The problem person thinks they know everything." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

## Certainty without knowing

Joe draws a crucial sub-distinction: you can be certain of your next step and still be in wonder. Certainty about action is compatible with wonder. Certainty about truth is not.

"You can be certain and still be in wonder. I know in my business what the next step is. I don't know that it's going to be the right step. I don't know that a better next step isn't going to show up a second from now." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

"It stops me from thinking that I know. I just know what the right next step is for me." Knowing your next step is practical. Thinking you know the answer is a trap.

## Being right

Joe tells a personal story about being expelled from college for refusing to apologize when he believed he was right. He had a 3.95 GPA and 300 supporters but lost because he was in knowing rather than wonder. He traces this pattern to his family of origin: "It was really important in my family of origin to be right. If you weren't right, you were going to get it worse."

"Being right doesn't mean anything. All the people who are right the most, like, what has it gotten them?" -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

## When Someone Experiences This

When you find yourself defending a position and the other person is pulling away. When you feel certain you are right and cannot understand why others resist. When being right has become more important than being connected. When you have categorized someone and stopped being curious about them.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is defending a position and creating resistance
- When a conversation has become adversarial or stuck
- When [[impartiality]] is failing because the person is attached to being right
- When someone asks "why does this person always cause problems?"
