# Connection over Perfection

## The Distinction

Perfection is trying to meet an imaginary standard that does not exist. It produces rigidity, hesitation, and stagnation. Connection is accepting things as they are in the moment. It produces iteration, flow, and better results.

Every system humans perceive as "perfect" (a flower, an ecosystem, a great product, an inspiring person) is actually an expression of deep connection, not perfection. Perfection does not exist. Connection does.

## Perfection

Perfection is the critical parent's voice. It is fear-based: the belief that there is a right answer, a right way to do it, a standard you must meet. Since the standard is imaginary and shifts with every observer, it can never be satisfied.

"The idea of perfection is really a fear-based idea. The idea that you have to be perfect, that you have a right answer, that there's the right way to do it, that's all fear-based, and fear does not make great decisions." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

Perfectionism shows up as rigidity in the body, hesitation, second-guessing, and stunted expression. See [[perfectionism-as-critical-parent]].

## Connection

Connection is a measure of capacity to meet and accept things as they are in the moment. The more someone is accepted as they are (not asked to be different), the deeper the connection.

"If I'm connecting with you, I'm not asking you to be any different right now. And the more I ask you to be different, the less connected we're gonna feel." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

Connection produces better outcomes than perfection in every domain: business, relationships, product development, sales, self-development. Neurochemically, it is oxytocin and serotonin. Genetically, it is what humans are programmed for.

## The impulse trap

Natural impulses (to exercise, to call a friend, to write) are expressions of connection. Perfectionism converts these impulses into "shoulds," which kills the motivation.

"We have all these impulses inside of us that are just popping up like, oh, I wanna work out. And then that impulse, which is the deep connection, immediately gets turned into a perfection of I should work out. And then it's completely unmotivating." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

## When Someone Experiences This

When you cannot start because it will not be good enough. When you have high standards that paralyze rather than motivate. When "should" has replaced "want." When you are iterating less than you know you should because you are waiting to get it right. See [[i-keep-procrastinating]] and [[i-cant-stop-criticizing-myself]].
