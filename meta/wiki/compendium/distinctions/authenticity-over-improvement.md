# Authenticity over Improvement

## The Distinction

Improvement is if/then thinking applied to the self: if I fix X, then I will be Y (loved, secure, good enough). Authenticity is expressing what is true regardless of consequences. Growth happens naturally through authenticity. Improvement attempts slow it down.

"They call it self-discovery. They don't call it self-building." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The musk deer

A parable from the Upanishads (or similar traditions). A musk deer catches a scent and spends its entire life searching for the origin. Near death, it falls off a cliff, punctures its stomach, and discovers that the scent it was searching for comes from itself.

The search for the scent IS self-improvement. The discovery that the scent comes from within IS self-realization. All the improving (lose weight, meditate more, make money, stop negative thoughts) is searching outside for something that is already inside.

"The thing that we're looking for in all the self-improvement, what we're actually looking for is ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## Why improvement fails

Improvement comes from the belief that there is something wrong with you that must be fixed before you can be loved, secure, or happy. See [[inherent-goodness]]. Every aspect of the self that "needs to improve" is a way of not loving yourself as you are. And the parts that are not loved do not change; they persist. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]].

"Any point where you can't unconditionally love yourself, whether that is because you yell, because you don't work hard enough, because you're lazy, because you're a pessimist, whatever it is that you are telling yourself that you have to change, those are just ways that you're not loving yourself. And they don't typically change." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## Why authenticity works

Growth is the nature of life. It cannot be stopped. An oak tree does not try to grow. A child from 0 to 6 does not try to learn. Smiling most at sideways faces (play) rather than feeding faces shows the biological drive to learn. This drive persists unless someone kicks the love of learning out.

Trying to improve actually brakes the natural process. Two weeks of vacation (not trying to improve) produces better performance than two weeks of effortful improvement. The instruction to "try not to improve for 2 weeks" produces floods of realizations because the trying stopped.

Authenticity means being what you are right now, including the parts that are incompetent, helpless, or imperfect. Owning incompetence produces competence. Owning helplessness reduces helplessness. Fighting these produces more of them. See [[love-the-resistance]].

## Owning what you don't want to be seen as

The part of you that needs to be "annihilated" (in Pema Chödrön's language) is the part you are defending. Whatever you don't want to be seen as (helpless, greedy, weak, hypocritical) is pointing to where the authentic self is being hidden. The coaching path moves toward owning that part, not fixing it.

"You can tell them because you're defending them. It's like when someone's like, well, you didn't do that very well, and you go, uh, then you are defining yourself as somebody who's competent, and you're not able to love the incompetent part of yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## When Someone Experiences This

When self-improvement feels exhausting and circular. When you keep trying to change the same thing and nothing shifts. When you have achieved what you set out to achieve and it does not feel like enough. When the goal of "becoming better" has become its own burden. See [[i-cant-stop-criticizing-myself]].

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[shoulds-vs-wants]]: improvement is the language of should; authenticity is the language of want
- When [[inherent-goodness]] has not been grasped: the belief "I am not good enough" drives the improvement cycle
- When someone is exhausted by self-improvement but cannot stop

## Downstream (Where the Coaching Path Goes)

- [[learning-to-love-all-parts]]: love the parts you are trying to improve, and they shift on their own
- [[vulnerability]]: authenticity requires being seen as you are, including the parts you judge
- [[what-do-you-want]]: shift from "what should I improve?" to "what do I actually want?"
- Fear of stagnation invites stagnation ([[fear-invites-what-we-fear]]). Stopping the effort to improve does not produce stagnation; it produces natural growth
