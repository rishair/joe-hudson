# Don't use wonder as a strategy

## The Anti-Pattern

Using questions as a strategy to achieve an outcome: to avoid intimacy, to create intimacy, to manipulate, or to probe. The questions may be open-ended and technically correct, but the intent behind them is strategic rather than genuine. The other person feels "creepy and probing" energy.

Joe identifies two specific versions:
1. Asking questions to **avoid** intimacy: using curiosity about the other person to keep the focus off yourself
2. Asking questions to **create** intimacy: "if you're trying to create intimacy, it doesn't -- it's not intimacy"

## Why It Fails

"If it becomes a strategy, it starts to feel creepy." The other person detects the strategic intent through the same channels that detect any misaligned state of mind (see [[technique-vs-state-of-mind]]). They feel probed or manipulated rather than met.

## What Joe Says Instead

"Have wonder for the sake of wonder, for the gifts that it brings, for the feelings and sensations in your body that it creates, and be there. It's far more enjoyable than to be strategic about it or to try to avoid intimacy." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

If someone tells you they feel probed, Joe suggests being in wonder about that: "You can be like, oh, what's making you feel creeped out right now?" Or be vulnerable: apologize and name your ulterior motive. "I was kind of asking you questions as a way to avoid myself."
