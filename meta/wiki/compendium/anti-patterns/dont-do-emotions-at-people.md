# Don't do emotions at people

## The Anti-Pattern

Using emotions (anger, sadness, fear) to get someone else to change their behavior. Anger deployed at someone to make them comply. Sadness performed at someone to produce guilt. Fear expressed at someone to make them feel responsible for your safety.

This is distinct from having emotions in front of someone. Feeling angry and saying "I'm angry" is honest. Yelling at someone to make them stop doing something is using the emotion as a weapon.

The distinction is clearest with anger: jumping up and down saying "I'm angry, I'm angry, I'm angry!" is feeling the emotion. Saying "God damn it, stop talking to me while I'm cooking!" is doing the emotion at someone to control them. See [[feeling-anger-vs-anger-at-someone]].

## Why It Fails

"To put your emotion at somebody, to try to get them to change with your emotions subconsciously or consciously, it's emotional abuse." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

Emotions deployed at people produce compliance through fear, not genuine change. The other person learns to manage the emotional deployer, not to be themselves. The relationship deteriorates because authenticity has been replaced by emotional management.

Doing anger at someone also locks the anger in place. It becomes part of the shame-blame cycle. The person feels shame for having gotten angry, which produces more stuck anger, which eventually explodes again.

"We're talking about loving your anger. We're talking about letting it move, letting it get out. We're talking about learning to have anger unresisted instead of being lost in your anger." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part I

## What to Do Instead

Feel the emotion privately, then have the conversation. See [[feel-before-acting]]. The emotion gets its full expression without being aimed at anyone. After it has moved, the conversation can happen from clarity.

"The reason that somebody gets angry at somebody else is because they haven't gotten angry by themselves. They haven't-- it takes them a while to build that kind of anger. So go release your anger and then talk to the person." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

For anger specifically, see [[expressing-anger-safely]]: the car commute, the beach, the woods, writing and burning. The anger moves, clarity arrives, and the conversation can happen without weaponization.

If someone is doing emotions at you, draw a boundary: "I'm happy to listen to your emotions if I give you permission, but otherwise I don't want the emotion at me."
