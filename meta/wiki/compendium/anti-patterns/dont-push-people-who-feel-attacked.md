# Don't push people who feel attacked

## The Anti-Pattern

Trying to get someone to learn, change, or see something differently while they feel under pressure, judged, or attacked. This includes subtle forms of pressure: having an agenda for them, wanting them to be different, pushing them toward a particular conclusion. Even well-intentioned pushing ("be happy," "stop crying," "just do this task") shuts down the other person's capacity for curiosity and learning.

## Why It Fails

The nervous system cannot be curious while under perceived threat. Joe presents this as neurological fact, not preference. Two people in a fight will never spontaneously agree. A stressed child performs worse on tests. The more pressure you apply, the less the other person can learn.

"If you are under attack, you can't be curious, or if you're under attack, you can't learn because you're not curious." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course #1)

Joe extends this to subtle pressure: "The most subtle forms of attack are you just trying to push somebody into doing what you want them to do. Be happy. Stop crying. Just do this task. And the more that attack is there, the more you're trying to push somebody into something and be different than who they are, the less able they are to learn."

## What Joe Says Instead

Create the conditions for the person to feel safe. When they feel safe, curiosity returns naturally. This is the function of [[view]]: creating a state of mind in which no one feels attacked, which allows everyone to learn. [[impartiality]] is the specific antidote: releasing agenda removes the pressure that the other person's system detects and resists.
