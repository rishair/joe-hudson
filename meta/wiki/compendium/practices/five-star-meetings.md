# Five-Star Meetings

## The Practice

Rate every meeting you attend on a 1-to-5-star scale. The question: did this meeting leave you feeling invigorated and energized, or depleted?

The goal: two consecutive weeks where every meeting is a 5-star meeting.

Do whatever it takes to get there. Tell the chairman of the board you are not attending. Walk out of meetings that are not working. Say out loud: "This meeting isn't working for me. How do we fix it?" Cancel meetings that do not need to happen. Restructure meetings that consistently drain.

## Why It Works

This practice forces every form of dysfunction to surface because none of them can coexist with 5-star meetings:

- Political behavior cannot produce invigorating meetings
- Blame cannot produce invigorating meetings
- Conflict avoidance cannot produce invigorating meetings (the avoided conflict will land on the leader's lap within 2 weeks)
- Indecision cannot produce invigorating meetings
- Defensiveness cannot produce invigorating meetings

The avoidance of difficult topics is the last thing to get caught, but 2 weeks is enough for the avoided issue to show up. Once it does, the leader discovers that the conflict around the issue is what they were avoiding, not the issue itself. And they now have the tools (from the previous 2 weeks of practice) to make even that meeting a 5-star meeting.

## What It Produces

When a CEO commits to this, the organization transforms within a month. The practice is a single lever that addresses all team dysfunction because it puts the leader in a position where they must stop tolerating every form of dysfunction.

## The Measurement

The simplest diagnostic for team dysfunction: at the end of a meeting, do you feel invigorated or depleted?

"The best way to measure if a team is dysfunctional is at the end of a meeting, do you feel invigorated or do you feel depleted?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team
