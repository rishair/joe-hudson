# The Upright Apology

## Definition

An apology delivered with the back upright, not hunched in shame. Taking responsibility while staying in your power. Not making yourself small. Not cowering. Not extracting punishment from yourself to prevent punishment from others.

"When you make an apology that's upright, that's empowered, it feels freaking fantastic. It's an awesome feeling. You feel strength in it, you feel responsible, you feel empowered." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## Types of Apology

**Habitual "I'm sorry":** A strategy to prevent others' anger. Saying sorry 10-20 times a day. Increases rather than decreases the likelihood of others getting angry because the fear of anger invites anger.

**Shame extraction:** "Say you're sorry." Forces the person to admit they are wrong, to feel small. This is why many adults resist apologizing: someone extracted apologies from them as children to exert control.

**Guilt relief:** Apologizing to ease the feeling of guilt without any intention to change behavior. The apology relieves the discomfort temporarily, but the behavior recurs.

**Empowered apology:** Taking responsibility in one's power. "This is how I was, and it's not how I want to be. I'm responsible for this, and I apologize." No shame. No making oneself small. The distinction: there is no shame in making mistakes. We all make mistakes multiple times a day. That makes us human.

**Empathic apology:** "I'm sorry you're having a hard day." Not taking responsibility for someone else's experience. Communicating: I see you, I'm here with you, I don't want this for you.

## Why Empowered Apologies Transform

Shame-based apologies recur because the behavior recurs. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]]. When shame is attached to an apology, the emotional stuckness that produced the behavior in the first place is reinforced rather than released.

Empowered apologies produce emotional fluidity. Grief may move, fear may move. It feels like a cleansing, an emptying out of the holding. The person is empowered AND grieving, not collapsed and grieving.

## The Somatic Test

Feel the body. If the back is hunched, eyes down, shoulders collapsed, the apology is shame-based. If the back is upright, eyes forward, posture strong, the apology is empowered. The body knows the difference before the mind does.

## The Hack

Never use "you" in an apology. Make it about yourself, not about them. "I'm sorry for raising my voice. That's not how I want to be with you." Not: "I'm sorry you felt hurt by what I said."

## Apology as Fight-Breaker

When in a fight, look for anything to apologize for that is in integrity and empowered. "I'm sorry for being in a power struggle with you. That's not how I want to be with you." This exits the [[fear-triangle]] instantly. The apology is not a concession. It is authenticity.

## Modeling for Children

Joe never asked his children to say sorry. They say it naturally, in an empowered way, because they have seen it modeled. When apologies are extracted ("Say you're sorry!"), the child learns that apologizing means being controlled. When apologies are modeled in power, the child learns that apologizing means taking responsibility while staying whole.
