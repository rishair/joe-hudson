# Letting Judgment Break Your Heart

## Definition

A practice Joe describes for dissolving reactivity to judgment. Instead of letting judgment roll off your back (defense) or collapsing under it (shame), you let it in and feel the pain of it. The heartbreak, fully felt, eventually dissolves the reactivity.

## How Joe Explains It

Joe contrasts three responses to judgment:
1. **Letting it roll off your back.** This looks strong but is actually defended. You are blocking the feeling.
2. **Collapsing.** This is groveling, taking on shame, losing yourself.
3. **Letting it break your heart.** This is the vulnerable path. You feel the pain of being judged without defending or collapsing.

"It's letting it break your heart. It doesn't mean that you have to grovel at the floor for the person. Because one of the greatest strengths that most people don't have is the ability to actually just feel their emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability (Connection Course #5)

## How to Practice

Joe describes doing this for approximately 6 weeks when he noticed that judgment triggered defensiveness in him. He intentionally let judgment break his heart rather than defending against it. "I'd be crying in odd places and all that crazy stuff."

The result: "Now when people judge me, there's just like no, very seldom a reaction. It doesn't even cross my consciousness anymore."

## What it produces

The practice does not make you weak. It makes you free. "That's what the heartbreak provides. It provides you the courage to love more deeply."

## When Someone Experiences This

When judgment from others triggers defensiveness or anxiety. When you spend energy managing how others see you. When you cannot tolerate criticism without either fighting or freezing.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[vulnerability]] practice reveals reactivity to judgment
- When someone asks "how do I stop caring what people think?"

- After the practice, judgment stops triggering reactivity
- Greater capacity for [[vulnerability]] because there is less to defend
- Freedom to love more openly because the fear of judgment has been felt through
