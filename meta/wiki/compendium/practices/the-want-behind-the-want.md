# The Want Behind the Want

## The Practice

1. Write down the want you feel most guilty about. The one you do not want to admit to yourself. Often something about sex, money, fame, or power.
2. Ask: what is the need behind that want?
3. Whatever comes up, ask again: what is the need behind that need?
4. Keep going until you arrive at something essential.

Every single want, followed to its root, traces back to something essential that humans need to thrive: love, connection, safety, belonging, autonomy, meaning.

"The wants are just strategies. We're going and learning our strategies to get to the place where we can be happy and healthy and thrive. And when you see that inside of yourself, all of a sudden it's really clear with everybody else." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## What It Produces

The exercise dissolves the guilt around the surface want. Once you see that wanting money traces to wanting safety which traces to wanting love, the want is no longer shameful. It is human. This removes the self-judgment that keeps the want stuck at the surface level. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]].

It also produces compassion for others. When you have traced your own "selfish" wants to their roots, you can see the same process in other people. Someone acting from a short-term, self-destructive want is running a strategy. The want underneath is legitimate. Judging the strategy keeps the guilt in place. Being with them in the deeper want without judgment helps it evolve.

## When to Use It

- When someone feels guilty about a want and is suppressing it
- When a want has become obsessive or addictive (the want is stuck, not refining)
- When [[i-dont-know-what-i-want]]: the deeper wants are often more accessible than the surface ones, which have been shamed into hiding
- As a personal practice anytime self-judgment about desires arises

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[what-do-you-want]] produces shame or guilt instead of clarity
