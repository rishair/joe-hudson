# Gratitude Rounds

## The Practice

Before a meeting, each person shares 3 things they are grateful for, going around the circle. Three rounds total, so everyone shares three times.

The key requirement: actually feel the gratitude. Not a list. Not a 5-year-old who just wants to eat. The gratitude must be felt in the body.

"I'm really grateful that I get a paycheck" is not the practice. "I'm really grateful that this team works together in such a way that allows us all to be paid and allows me to take care of my children" is the practice.

## Two Ways to Solve Problems

Most people default to seeing what is wrong and trying to fix it. This is useful but limited. The other way: see what is right and build on it. Gratitude enables the second approach, which produces solutions invisible to problem-focused thinking.

When applied to the problem itself, this becomes especially useful. If a product is not selling, gratitude around the product not selling reveals: customers are telling us what they do not like. Salespeople are telling us their concerns. These are opportunities, not just failures.

## What It Produces

- Shifts the team from criticism to creative problem-solving
- Builds team cohesion
- Counteracts the negativity bias (the intelligent person's tendency toward self-criticism)
- Makes both the positive and the negative data visible, producing better decisions

## Why People Resist It

The resistance to gratitude is the resistance to joy. If you are happy and content, you will not be prepared for the inevitable doom around the corner. Joy becomes the signal that the other shoe is about to drop. See [[unfelt-joy]].

"The real core as to why people resist gratitude, besides it's strange and awkward at the beginning, is the fact that they're scared that they won't be prepared for the inevitable doom that is always around the corner if they are happy." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

## When to Use It

- At the start of team meetings, especially recurring ones
- When a team is stuck in blame or problem-fixation mode
- When morale is low and the team can only see what is wrong
- With families: the same practice works at the dinner table
