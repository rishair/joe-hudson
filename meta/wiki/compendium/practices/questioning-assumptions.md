# Questioning Assumptions and Context

## Definition

A specific practice Joe teaches for accessing [[wonder]] and [[impartiality]] quickly. When someone makes a statement, Joe looks for the hidden assumptions and the assumed context, then questions them. This immediately opens wonder because it reveals how much is taken for granted.

## How Joe Explains It

Joe gives a concrete example. When someone says "Life is challenging because I don't have enough work," he identifies multiple hidden assumptions: that it is challenging not to have enough work, that they do not have enough work, that they should have more. Each can be questioned.

"Questioning the assumption is a great way to also get out of partiality and to become impartial as well." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

Joe also questions context. "I don't have enough work in America is very different than I don't have enough work in Africa, which is very different than I don't have enough work in Iceland." When someone states a truth, Joe asks: is this true everywhere? Is it true for you right now?

## How to Practice

1. When you hear a statement (from yourself or another), identify the assumptions embedded in it
2. Question each assumption: is this necessarily true?
3. Question the context: in what situation would the opposite be true?
4. Notice what opens up when the assumption is loosened

## When Someone Experiences This

When you are stuck in a belief that feels obviously true but produces suffering. When a conversation is cycling around a fixed position. When someone cannot access [[wonder]] because they are certain about their situation.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is stuck in [[knowing-vs-wonder]] and cannot access wonder
- When a conversation is cycling around a fixed belief
- When [[impartiality]] is needed but the person is attached to their framing

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Once assumptions are questioned, [[wonder]] opens naturally
- The person discovers alternative framings they had not considered
- Often reveals that the "problem" is an assumption, not a fact

## Questions Joe Asks

- "What are you assuming there?"
- "Is that true everywhere?"
- "In what context would the opposite be true?"
- "What if that assumption isn't right?"
