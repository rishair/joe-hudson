# Visualize the Feared Outcome

## Definition

A practice Joe teaches for releasing fear of specific outcomes. Visualize the feared event in full detail and feel all the emotions that would come with it. Once the emotions have been fully felt in advance, the fear loses its grip and impartiality becomes possible.

## How Joe Explains It

Joe connects this to the Stoic tradition, Tibetan Buddhism, and samurai practices of visualizing death repeatedly.

"If you're scared of getting fired, go visualize yourself getting fired over and over again, every step of it -- before, during, after. Feel everything that you would have to feel if you got fired." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality (Connection Course #3)

Joe locates the fear not in the event but in the emotions the event would produce: "Our fear really isn't about the thing happening. The fear is what we will have to feel if the thing happens." If those emotions can be felt in advance and welcomed, the fear dissolves.

"All of the emotions can be enjoyed, can be welcomed, can be loved." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality (Connection Course #3)

## How to Practice

1. Choose a specific feared outcome (getting fired, relationship ending, failure)
2. Visualize it in full detail: before, during, and after
3. Feel every emotion that arises: grief, fear, shame, helplessness
4. Do not resist the emotions. Welcome them
5. Repeat until the visualization no longer produces fear

## When Someone Experiences This

When fear of a specific outcome is preventing you from acting. When you are being partial (controlling) because you are afraid of what will happen if you let go. When you cannot practice [[impartiality]] because the stakes feel too high.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is stuck in [[impartiality]] because they are afraid of an outcome
- When fear is driving controlling behavior
- Connects to [[feeling-unfelt-emotions]]: the feared emotions need to be felt for the fear to dissolve

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Once the fear dissolves, [[impartiality]] becomes accessible
- Joe also suggests "just grieve the loss" as a simpler version of this practice
- Joe connects to [[fear-invites-what-we-fear]]: the things you do to avoid what you fear often bring it to you
