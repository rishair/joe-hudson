# Expressing Anger Safely

## The Practice

Find a place where anger can move without being directed at anyone. Common options:
- **The car commute.** Get angry at everything that happened during the day. Every night on the drive home.
- **The ocean or the woods.** Walk to the water and yell. Nobody can hear.
- **Alone in a room.** Jump up and down. Say "I'm angry" out loud.
- **Writing and burning.** Write something furious. Then burn it.

The form does not matter. What matters: the anger moves through the body, is expressed out loud or physically, and is not aimed at any person.

## What It Produces

An executive who felt like a victim in a large bureaucracy was given this homework: get angry every night on the commute. Within 2-3 weeks, her empowerment changed significantly. She stopped feeling trapped. A low-level depression shifted into determination and agency.

This result is consistent. When repressed anger is given a safe outlet, depression lifts, clarity arrives, and the sense of being stuck dissolves. The anger was already there. The practice gives it a way out.

## When to Use It

- When there is chronic low-level frustration or depression (often repressed anger)
- Before a difficult conversation, to separate the emotion from the communication (see [[feel-before-acting]])
- When anger at a person is building and the impulse is to either explode or repress
- As a regular daily practice, not just in crisis

## What If It Is Not Enough

If the commute does not release all the anger and the person walks in the door still angry: keep driving. Or walk in and say: "I'm really angry right now. I don't want to take it out on any of you. I'm going to go for 20 minutes and I'll be back."

The key difference between this and emotional withdrawal: a timeline. "I'll be back in 20 minutes" signals care. "I just can't be around you" without a timeline signals punishment.

## The Brain's Resistance

The brain will find every reason not to do this. If someone was told as a child that anger was bad, or was punished for anger, or experienced abuse through anger, the system will shut anger down. It will produce reasons why there is no safe place, no time, no point.

"There's always a way to do it. And there's always a reason not to. Because our brain will find any way that it can to not be angry." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part I
