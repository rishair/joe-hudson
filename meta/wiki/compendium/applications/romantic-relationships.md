# Romantic Relationships

## Why We Choose Who We Choose

We instinctually find the person who can trigger us best. Childhood teaches what love looks like: love associated with shame, authoritarianism, money, food, or abandonment. Whatever the pattern, the romantic partner holds the other side of it.

"One of the main reasons I think that we find ourselves attracted to certain people and that we find ourselves married to certain people is that we have an instinctual way of finding the person who can trigger us the best." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

This is why people say "my husband is so much like my father" or "my wife is so much like my mother." The patterns interlock. This is a feature, not a bug. The opportunity is to heal the pattern if both people are willing.

## The Relationship Agreement

The agreement that "we're here to make each other happy" kills relationships. When the oxytocin fades after 3-5 months and the partner stops "making you happy," the trauma shows up. Trying to get each other to change begins.

The functional agreement: each person is here for their own freedom, their own transformation. They are kind and supportive to each other, but neither is responsible for the other's happiness or growth.

"If you're actually in a relationship where both people are saying, okay, we're here primarily to use a relationship as a way to create our own freedom, to make ourselves happy ourselves, and to be kind and supportive to one another, then that's going to be a relationship that's successful." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

## Don't Fix Your Partner

Taking responsibility for a partner's growth is "hell." It is full of arrogance, hubris, and distraction from one's own unfinished work. The authority on what someone needs is always the person themselves. See [[dont-try-to-fix-people]], [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

The sexual relationship illustrates this: if sex is all about pleasing the other, it is bad sex. If all about yourself, also bad sex. The only person who can be the authority on your pleasure is you. The same principle applies to healing and growth within a relationship.

## Relationships as Healing Containers

When the relationship is understood as a container for mutual healing rather than a source of happiness, conflict becomes productive. Each trigger is information about what has not been healed. Each pattern that arises is an invitation to feel what could not be felt in childhood. See [[trauma-mapping]], [[feeling-unfelt-emotions]].
