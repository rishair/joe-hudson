# Functional Teams

## Definition

A functional team is one where people want to be on it. Not where everyone agrees, not where goals are always hit, but where the people who stay enjoy performing together and want to remain.

"The best way I know to measure is that people want to be a part of it. People like it. And I don't mean everybody, but the people who are staying, who are consistently a part of that team, enjoy being a part of the team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

Functionality is context-dependent. A cryptocurrency fund runs differently than a Navy SEAL unit or a family. But all functional teams share one quality: trust.

## Trust Is Multifaceted

Trust is not a single thing. It includes:
- Trust to talk and listen to each other
- Financial trust (not screwing each other)
- Trust that conflict can be resolved and produce strength
- Trust that commitments will be kept
- Trust in each other's capacity to perform

Trust is harder to weaponize than "safety." Telling someone they need to be more trustworthy usually points to something real. Telling someone you "don't feel safe" can be used as control. See [[dont-use-vulnerability-to-control]].

## How Trust Is Built

Trust is built by having difficult conversations, not by avoiding them. Doing what you say you will do. Bringing up the dirty laundry instead of letting it fester. Dealing with conflict directly.

"Any conflict-avoidant manager, if you are listening to this and you are a conflict-avoidant manager, your team does not trust you. I guarantee it." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

A leader who does not share what is happening for them (fears, concerns, frustrations) becomes unpredictable. The team watches for moods and reactions instead of focusing on work. Not sharing what is going on makes the leader untrustworthy.

A diagnostic: if people keep bringing conflict to you, you are conflict avoidant. The conflict is not being resolved, so it keeps arriving.

## Safety: The Right Amount

Safety works like a guitar string. Too tight (too much safety, no challenge) produces a flat, cautious team. Too loose (not enough safety, constant threat) produces anxiety and politics.

The challenge itself should remain unsafe. Climbing Everest is unsafe. Building a startup is unsafe. That cannot be removed. What should be safe: expressing opinions, saying "I need help," saying "this isn't working," having an unpopular view.

## Politics = Unclear Success Criteria

When people do not know what will make them succeed or fail, politics arise. Success becomes about pleasing the boss rather than performing. The team optimizes for relationship management rather than results.

"If you're sitting in a company right now and it's a political company, it means that the success is based on relationship, not on performance." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The fix: make success criteria explicit. Professional sports are high-pressure but low-politics because performance criteria are visible. When people know what keeps them and what removes them, their anxiety directs toward performance instead of politics.

## Culture as Experiment

Most CEOs either project their personality into culture (unconsciously replicating their patterns) or import culture from a book. Neither is experimental.

The functional approach: treat culture like a product. State a thesis (principles and goals). Run it. Measure what happens. Refine. The culture should serve the mission, not the preferences of management.

## Owning Wants Creates Alignment

When someone asks each team member what they want from a team, they almost always agree. Everyone wants support, trust, directness, safety to express, accountability. Nobody wants politics, backstabbing, or unclear expectations.

The path to alignment is not imposing a culture but owning wants. "I want a team where I hear from everyone. I want a team where the difficult things get said." See [[wants-as-evolution]].
