# The Fear Triangle

## Definition

All power dynamics are two people who are scared. Each person thinks they are defending themselves and the other is attacking. The fear presents in one of three roles: Bully (fight), Victim (freeze), or Savior (flight). What psychology calls the "drama triangle" is more accurately the fear triangle, because all three roles are driven by fear.

"Both people walk around thinking what they just said was a defense and the other person is hearing that as an attack." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

## The Three Roles

### Bully (Fight)

**Emotional signature:** Alone. No one is helping. Must be self-reliant.

**Behavior:** Aggression, dominance, intimidation, taking charge through force. The bully does not feel like a bully. They feel like the only person willing to do what needs to be done.

**Self-reinforcing cycle:** The feeling of being alone drives aggressive behavior. The aggression pushes people away. The loneliness increases. The aggression increases.

### Victim (Freeze)

**Emotional signature:** Stuck. Cannot move. Trapped.

**Behavior:** Passive aggression, guilting, lateness, undone chores, subtle undermining, blaming others for their emotional experience. The victim does not feel aggressive. They feel oppressed.

**Self-reinforcing cycle:** The feeling of stuckness produces passive aggression. The passive aggression agitates everyone around them. The agitation increases their feeling of stuckness.

### Savior (Flight)

**Emotional signature:** Obligation. Responsibility. Must fix it.

**Behavior:** Caretaking, managing others' emotions, trying to make everyone okay, leaving themselves to control the environment. The savior does not feel controlling. They feel responsible.

**Self-reinforcing cycle:** The feeling of obligation drives caretaking. Caretaking tells others they are not capable. Resentment builds. The environment becomes less safe. The obligation increases. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

## How the Roles Interact

A fighter who is met with withdrawal (victim response) experiences the withdrawal as an attack: "They're punishing me by leaving." A freezer who is met with aggression (bully response) experiences the aggression as control: "They're trying to control me even more." Each person's defensive response looks like an attack to the other.

## Internal Fear Triangle

The three roles also operate as voices in the head, cycling within a single person:
- **Bully voice:** "You gotta stop talking over people."
- **Victim voice:** "I've tried for 20 years, I can't stop."
- **Savior voice:** "Okay, this time we'll set a phone reminder and..."

All three are operating from fear. None produces change. The external drama triangle is a projection of the internal one.

## People Play Different Roles in Different Contexts

Someone can be the bully at home and the victim at work. The dominant role is circumstantial, not a fixed identity. All three roles are present in everyone, with one typically foregrounded in a given relationship.

## What Dissolves the Triangle

The triangle dissolves when the fear is acknowledged without shame:
- **Bully says:** "I'm scared."
- **Victim says:** "I have choice here. I can do this."
- **Savior says:** "The only person I can save is myself."

Each of these statements is the opposite of what the role wants to say. Each lands in the fear that the role is defending against. There is nothing to figure out. Feeling the underlying emotion (fear, sadness, hurt, helplessness) collapses the dynamic instantly.

"There's nothing to defend. It doesn't take anything to drop a hot frying pan." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

## Shame Keeps the Triangle in Place

All three roles carry shame. The bully is ashamed of being a bully. The victim is ashamed of being a victim. The savior is ashamed of being a savior. This shame prevents acknowledgment, which prevents dissolution.

## Connection to Empowerment

Power can be taken away. Empowerment cannot. "If you're worried about losing it, it's power." The fear triangle runs on power: protecting reputation, position, love, identity. When authenticity replaces accumulation of power, there is less to lose, less fear, and less reason to enter the triangle. See [[empower-over-power]].

## Downstream (Where the Coaching Path Goes)

- Identify which role the person is playing and name the emotional signature
- Ask: "What are you defending?" to reveal that there is nothing under the shell
- Move toward feeling the underlying emotion: [[helplessness]], [[fear-as-path-to-freedom]]
- [[empower-over-power]]: shift from protecting power to living from authenticity
