# Stages of Emotional Development

## Definition

A 7-stage developmental framework describing how the relationship to emotions evolves. The beginning and end are similar (fluid, non-personal emotions) but the end includes awareness and choice that the beginning does not. The path is not linear: it spirals, pendulates, and moves through each stage multiple times at different levels of subtlety. See [[development-as-corkscrew]].

## The Stages

### 1. Fluid and non-personal

Infancy. Emotions move freely through the body without constriction. A baby with colic cries for 3 hours without wearing out their throat because there is no contraction around the crying. No "I shouldn't be crying." Emotions are impersonal processes, not stories about the self.

### 2. Repression

Childhood programming. Emotions are taught to be wrong: don't cry, don't be angry, don't be scared, calm down, man up. The teaching happens through punishment, bribery, or withdrawal of love. Muscular constriction develops to hold the emotions back. The body contorts to suppress what is not allowed. See [[trauma-mapping]], [[projection]].

### 3. Emotions control us

The suppressed emotions leak out in distorted form. "I can't believe I said that when I was angry." Loss of control. This is where most people's understanding of emotions stops: emotions are disruptive forces that need to be managed.

### 4. Management

The intellectual attempt to control emotions. Running, meditating to suppress, positive thinking, ignoring. More sophisticated than raw repression but the same structure: emotions are bad and must be contained. Even meditation can be management rather than allowing. "Most people, meditation is management, and that's torture." See [[enjoy-over-manage]].

Management can be maintained for a lifetime but produces a specific cost: all the joy leaves. The capacity for play disappears. See [[resistance-is-what-you-fear]]: managing anger produces controlled explosions, managing sadness produces depression, managing fear produces anxiety.

### 5. Allowing and feeling

The turning point. Emotions are allowed to move through the body rather than being held back. Not just observed (meditation-style watching) but physically expressed: the muscles that held the emotion in place release, the body moves, tears flow, anger sounds.

The negative emotions (by cultural definition: anger, sadness, fear) need to move first. Then the positive ones (joy, bliss, love, excitement) surface. The positive emotions often have as much or more resistance than the negative ones.

This is where the bulk of the coaching work happens. See [[feeling-unfelt-emotions]], [[feel-before-acting]], [[dont-do-emotions-at-people]], [[resistance-is-what-you-fear]].

### 6. Emotional fluidity

Emotions become fluid again, as in infancy, but now with awareness. They are non-personal: the stories behind emotions are seen as stories, not truth. The emotion itself is not believed to be the truth either. It is a movement that passes through.

"Now all my sadness is wrapped in love." The distinction between emotions begins to fade. Joy and sadness become hard to distinguish. Excitement and fear merge.

### 7. Emotions wrapped in love

The endpoint (which is also the beginning at a new level). All emotions are experienced as expressions of love. The enjoy-yourself-10%-more practice applied to crying. The matriarch principle operating in full: joy is present because all her children (anger, sadness, fear) are welcome.

## The matriarch principle

"Joy is the matriarch of a family of emotions. She won't come into your house unless her children are welcome." -- Joe Hudson

Joy cannot coexist with repression. If anger is not welcome, joy will not come. If sadness is not welcome, joy will not come. Welcoming all emotions is the condition for joy.

## When Someone Experiences This

When joy is absent despite "having it all." When emotions feel uncontrollable and frightening. When meditation feels like torture. When the question "what stage am I at?" arises, the answer determines the coaching path. See [[the-developmental-arc]].

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[resistance-is-what-you-fear]]: understanding why resisted emotions produce the feared outcome
- When someone says "I've lost my joy" or "I can't feel anything"
