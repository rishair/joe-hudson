# Is this mine?

## The Question

- "Is this mine?"
- "Is this yours?"

## When Joe Uses It

When a highly empathetic person is overwhelmed by emotion that may not be theirs. Joe describes people who "go beyond empathy" and cannot distinguish their own emotional state from another person's. This is common in children of alcoholics or children of abuse who developed hyper-awareness of others' emotional states to survive.

Joe uses this with clients who are carrying anxiety, anger, or sadness that belongs to someone else in their environment: "Recently there was some COVID anxiety that one of my clients was feeling, and I was like, is this yours? And they'd just immediately drop. They're like, oh no, it's not mine."

## What It Produces

The question creates instant clarity. The person recognizes the emotion belongs to someone else (a partner, a parent, a coworker, the news, the culture). The emotion often drops immediately once it is identified as not theirs.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person says "no, it's not mine," Joe does not pursue it further. The question itself was the intervention. If the person says "yes, it is mine," that is also useful: now they know what they are working with.

## Tone

Light, curious. Not diagnostic. Joe asks this the way you would ask someone if a jacket on the chair is theirs.
