# If you take the shame out of it, what's the problem?

## The Question

- "If you take the shame out of it, what's the problem with all this?"
- "Without the shame, what actually needs to change?"

## When to Use It

When a person is describing a behavior or pattern and the distress seems disproportionate to the actual consequences. The shame is doing most of the damage, not the behavior itself. This question separates them.

In the coaching session, Brett described a pattern of hyper-focusing on things and losing balance. The pattern produced learning, growth, and income. The suffering came from the shame about the pattern, not from the pattern itself.

## What It Produces

Often a pause, then surprise. The person realizes that without the shame, the "problem" is either not a problem or is a much smaller problem than it appeared. The shame was generating most of the suffering. Common responses:

- "Actually, I kind of like this about myself" -- the behavior is not the issue, the judgment of the behavior is
- "The real problem is much smaller than I thought" -- the shame was amplifying a minor issue into a major one
- "I guess the problem is... other people's reactions?" -- which leads to [[what-feeling-am-i-resisting]] (usually fear of rejection or abandonment)

## What to Do With the Response

If the problem shrinks or disappears without shame: the coaching path shifts from fixing the behavior to addressing the shame itself. See [[shame-locks-bad-habits]], [[past-shame-vs-current-shame]], [[learning-to-love-all-parts]].

If a real problem remains after removing the shame: now it can be addressed clearly, without the distortion of shame.

## Tone

Light, curious. Not challenging. The question gives permission to consider that shame might not be a useful lens.
