# How is that not true about you?

## The Question

- "How is that not true about you?"
- "That's a scary question for me to ask, but how is that not true about you?"

## When to Use It

When someone is judging another person. The judgment of the other almost always reflects something the person is doing themselves but cannot see. This question redirects the attention from the other person back to the self.

In the coaching session, the person judged her father/boss as "wrapped up in himself so much that he couldn't see anyone else." The question "How is that not true about you?" revealed that she was doing the same thing: wrapped up in wanting to be seen, unable to see the other person.

This connects to [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]]: judgment of others is one of the three signals that an emotion is not being felt.

## What It Produces

A pause. Often surprise. The person has been focused outward (the other person is wrong) and this question pivots inward. Common responses:

- Immediate recognition: "Oh. I think that is true." The insight lands
- Defensiveness: "When they do it, it's way worse than when I do it." This IS the defense, and naming it with humor can dissolve it
- New emotion: the recognition that you are doing what you are judging produces shame, sadness, or grief. This is the door opening

## What to Do With the Response

If recognition: stay with it. "So you're judging them for what you're doing. What's that like to see?"

If defensiveness: meet it with warmth and humor. Do not push. Let the recognition land when it is ready.

If emotion surfaces: stay with the emotion. This is the pathway to [[feeling-unfelt-emotions]]. The judgment was covering the emotion; now it is exposed.

## Tone

Direct but warm. Joe prefaced this with: "That's a scary question for me to ask." Modeling [[vulnerability]] by naming that the question itself is risky. This gives the person permission to receive it.
