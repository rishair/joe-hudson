# Reflecting the Judgment Back

## Definition

A coaching move where the person's judgment of someone else is turned back as a question about themselves. The judgment of the other is almost always a mirror of something the person is doing, cannot see, and is avoiding feeling.

## How It Works

1. The person describes what the other person is doing wrong ("they don't see me," "they're controlling," "they're wrapped up in themselves")
2. The coaching question: [[how-is-that-not-true-about-you]]
3. The person sees the mirror: they are doing the same thing
4. The emotion underneath the judgment surfaces

In the coaching session, the person judged authority figures for being "wrapped up in themselves" and not seeing her. Reflecting the judgment back revealed: she was wrapped up in wanting to be seen, and not seeing them either.

## What Read Triggers This

- [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]]: judgment of others is one of the three signals
- When someone is focused entirely on what the other person is doing wrong
- When the complaint about the other person has a persistent, charged quality (not neutral observation but heated judgment)

## What It Produces

The move collapses the projection. The person can no longer maintain the frame "they are wrong and I am right." This creates space for a more nuanced understanding of the dynamic: both people are contributing. The emotion that the judgment was protecting against (usually helplessness, shame, or grief) becomes accessible.

## Tone

Direct but preceded by vulnerability. Joe modeled this by saying "That's a scary question for me to ask" before asking it. The question is challenging. The vulnerability makes it receivable.

## What NOT to do

Do not use this to invalidate the person's experience. The other person may genuinely be doing something harmful. The point is not "you're wrong about them." The point is "you are also doing this, and seeing that changes the dynamic."
