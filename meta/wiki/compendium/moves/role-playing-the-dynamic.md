# Role-Playing the Dynamic

## Definition

A coaching move where the coach takes on the role of the person the client is in conflict with (boss, parent, partner) and lets the client practice a new way of relating in real time. Not a discussion about what to do. An embodied practice of doing it.

## How It Works

1. The client has identified a new stance they want to take (in this case: empowered, undefended love instead of fight or submit)
2. The coach says: "I'll be the authority figure. You see what happens. Speak from that place and see what happens."
3. The client practices. The coach responds as the other person might respond (including the triggering response)
4. The client navigates the trigger in real time from the new stance

In the coaching session: after the client found the stance of "empowered, upright, undefended love," Joe role-played as the patronizing boss. The client said something vulnerable ("I have been treating you like a person in my head instead of the person who's here"). Joe, as the boss, responded with the triggering pat-on-the-head: "Thank you for that. That really lets me know that you're ready for leadership soon." The client felt the "ouch" and had to navigate it from the new stance rather than retreating to fight or submit.

## What Read Triggers This

- When the client has intellectually understood a new way of being but has not practiced it in the body
- When the dynamic is relational (it involves another person) and the shift needs to be tested against a realistic response
- When the client says "I understand but I don't know how to do it"

## What It Produces

Embodied experience of the new stance. Not theory. Practice. The body learns the new pattern by doing it, even in a role-play context.

## Tone

Playful but committed. The coach fully inhabits the role, including delivering the triggering response. Without the trigger, the practice has no value.
