# Following Wonder

## Definition

A coaching move where Joe follows whatever he is genuinely curious about in the conversation rather than pursuing a predetermined path. He treats wonder as a trail to follow.

## How Joe Uses It

"Follow your wonder, like it's a trail, and just follow it in the conversation about the other person, about yourself in the conversation. Just follow it." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

Joe goes further: "If you're going to have to hold an agenda, if you're going to be partial about anything, be partial about staying in wonder." This makes wonder the one thing worth being intentional about.

## Seek what you don't know

A specific variant: when someone tells Joe their story, instead of processing the information and generating a question, Joe first asks himself what he does not know.

"If someone tells you the story, they're telling you all the things that they know. So there's no new information. But if you start focusing on what you don't know, it might also be stuff that they don't know." -- Joe Hudson, Wonder (Connection Course #2)

## Tone

Joe reports being surprised by what comes out of his own mouth. He does not prepare. He follows wonder in real time. "People will tell me, they'll be like, oh, this thing happened on your podcast, that was so great. And I'm like, really? I said that? I have no recollection of it."

The tone is light, curious, unhurried. Not strategic.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- Joe is in VIEW state and notices genuine pull of attention
- [[impartiality]] allows him to release attachment to where the conversation "should" go
- When a client has presented their story and Joe needs to find the thread that matters

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Often leads to deeper insight than the original question
- Following wonder often arrives at what the person does not know about themselves
- If the thread reveals hidden beliefs: [[questioning-assumptions]]
