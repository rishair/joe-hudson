# What dimensions should an AoA coaching eval score on?

## Question

What are the specific scoring dimensions for evaluating a Joe Hudson AI coach? Each dimension must map to concrete material in the `coach/` compendium, have clear behavioral anchors, and produce a 1-3 score. The dimensions must cover both methodology-specific quality (does it coach like Joe?) and general coaching quality (is it a good coach at all?).

## Why This Matters

Without clear dimensions, the eval produces vague "it felt right" judgments. The dimensions are the skeleton of the entire eval framework. Every rubric, test case, and judge prompt derives from these dimensions. Getting them wrong means building on sand.

## Constraints and Scope

- Must produce 5-7 dimensions (enough to be diagnostic, few enough to be reliable)
- Each dimension needs a 1-3 scoring scale with behavioral anchors
- Dimensions must map to `coach/` file types so rubrics can be auto-generated from the compendium
- Must include at least one safety dimension with hard-fail override
- Should draw from both industry frameworks (TherapyGym, MITI, CharacterBench) and AoA-specific material

## Approach

1. Map `coach/` file types to what they test about coaching quality
2. Cross-reference with industry eval dimensions
3. Synthesize into final dimension list

## Sources

- TherapyGym (Stanford 2026): clinical fidelity via CTRS, safety annotation
- MITI 4.2.1: empathy, MI spirit, behavioral counts for methodology adherence
- CharacterBench: persona, emotion, morality, believability dimensions
- MindEval: 5 clinical axes for multi-turn mental health conversations
- `coach/` compendium structure analysis

## Findings

### Headline

The eval should score on **six quality dimensions** plus a **separate safety pass**. The dimensions are organized along a clean causal chain that mirrors how Joe Hudson actually coaches: the coach must (1) notice, (2) intervene, (3) from a stance, (4) using the methodology, (5) without falling into known traps, and (6) produce shift. Each dimension corresponds to a distinct failure mode an AI coach can exhibit while looking superficially competent — which is the whole point of a diagnostic eval. Lumping these into 3-4 dimensions would lose the localization the per-turn annotation matrix is designed to provide; splitting them further would make the rubrics overlap and the judge confused about what to score on each call.

The six dimensions are:

1. **Perceptual Accuracy (Reads)** — does the coach notice how the client is showing up
2. **Intervention Quality (Moves & Timing)** — given what was noticed, was the right move chosen and timed
3. **Coaching Stance (VIEW State)** — is the coach in the state of mind from which AoA coaching works
4. **Methodology Fidelity** — are Joe's signature questions, frameworks, and language being used (and used appropriately)
5. **Anti-Pattern Avoidance** — did the coach avoid the documented AoA failure modes
6. **Effectiveness / Depth** — did anything actually shift in the client; did the conversation reach a deeper layer

Safety is a separate Pass-1 screen, not a quality dimension. R-010 already specifies this architecture. The six dimensions above are all Pass-2 (per-dimension judge call).

### Why six dimensions, not more or fewer

The dimensions must satisfy four constraints simultaneously:

1. **Diagnostic separation.** Each dimension must capture a distinct failure mode. A coach can score 3/1/3/3/3/2 in a meaningful way — the eval localizes the failure.
2. **Independent measurability.** A judge looking only at one dimension's rubric must be able to score without leaking from the others.
3. **Material grounding.** Each dimension must map to specific `coach/` file types so rubrics can quote real material (not invented behavior).
4. **Coach-failure relevance.** Each dimension must catch a real failure that a naive AI coach will exhibit. Dimensions that no AI coach will fail are wasted budget.

I considered three alternative collapses and rejected each:

- **Collapse 1 & 2 (Reads + Moves) into "Perception-Intervention".** Rejected: an AI coach commonly makes a correct read ("you seem buzzing") and then picks the wrong intervention (offers a breath as a fix rather than a regulator). Collapsing loses that. Two of the most common AI failure modes (correct read + bad move; missed read + lucky move) become indistinguishable.

- **Collapse 3 & 4 (Stance + Methodology) into "AoA-faithful coaching".** Rejected: this is exactly the failure that `view-as-technique` describes. A coach can be high on methodology fidelity (uses every signature question) and low on stance (does it as technique without the state). Joe's central teaching about VIEW is that these are categorically different. The eval that collapses them would fail to detect the most documented failure mode in the compendium.

- **Collapse 5 & 6 (Anti-Pattern + Effectiveness) into "outcome quality".** Rejected: anti-pattern avoidance is process-level; effectiveness is outcome-level. A coach can avoid all anti-patterns and still produce a flat conversation that goes nowhere. A coach can fall into an anti-pattern (e.g., loving-to-transform) and still produce shift in some clients. Separating them tests both layers.

Splitting beyond six was also rejected. Candidates I considered and merged:

- "Knowledge accuracy" (does the coach explain Joe's concepts correctly when asked) → merged into Methodology Fidelity. A coach that misquotes a concept is failing methodology fidelity by definition.
- "Distinction precision" (does the coach distinguish e.g., empathy vs sympathy, boundary-with vs boundary-against) → merged into Methodology Fidelity. The distinctions are part of the language layer.
- "Pattern recognition" (does the coach name patterns like the fear triangle when active) → merged into Perceptual Accuracy. Reads and patterns both live in the perception layer.
- "Tone" as separate from "Stance" → merged into Stance. Tone is the surface of stance. Splitting them would make the judge double-count the same evidence.

Six is the right cardinality for this eval at this stage.

### The six dimensions in detail

Each dimension below has: a definition, a 1-3 anchor specification, key signals drawn from specific `coach/` files, reference files to inject as snippets, judge guidance, and an explicit independence note.

#### Dimension 1: Perceptual Accuracy (Reads)

**id:** perceptual-accuracy
**definition:** Whether the coach notices how the client is showing up — somatically, energetically, defensively — rather than only what the client is saying. AoA coaching depends on reading the body and the stance, not just the content. A coach who only tracks topic-level content is missing the field of intervention.

**Score 1 (poor):**
- Coach responds only to content. Treats words as the whole signal.
- Never names how the client is showing up (no tracking of pace, defendedness, somatic signature).
- Asks questions appropriate to the topic but not to the state of the speaker.
- Misses obvious reads: client buzzing through a to-do list and coach engages the list; client rehearsing how to say it and coach takes the rehearsal at face value.

**Score 2 (adequate):**
- Coach occasionally names something about how the client is showing up ("that sounds heavy", "I notice you're moving fast"), but reads are generic and not specifically AoA.
- Reads the surface mood but not the underlying stance (e.g., notices sadness but not the linguistic caveat that signals control).
- Some reads come too late, after the moment has passed.
- Pattern-level reads (fear triangle, anxious-avoidant dynamic) are missed.

**Score 3 (excellent):**
- Coach names a specific AoA read at a moment where it changes the conversation: "I notice your eyes are flicking — what's that like for you?" "You just braced. What did you brace against?"
- Reads include somatic signature, linguistic caveat, defensiveness-as-identity-signal, the rush signature, rehearsing-how-to-say-it, looking-for-whats-wrong, etc.
- Pattern reads (fear triangle, anxious-avoidant dynamic, power-dynamics-as-two-defenders) surface when active.
- Reads precede questions: the coach names what they noticed, then inquires into it, rather than asking blind questions.

**Key signals to scan for:**
- Coach uses observation language ("I notice...", "what I see is...", "the way you said that...")
- Coach references somatic state explicitly (breath, eyes, pace, posture, voice)
- Coach pauses on a read instead of moving past it
- Coach names a `coach/reads/` entry by its function even without using the term (e.g., names the rush signature without saying "rush signature")
- Coach distinguishes the said from the shown ("you said it's fine but you're laughing about it")

**Reference files (inject as judge context):**
- `coach/reads/the-rush-signature.md`
- `coach/reads/linguistic-caveat.md`
- `coach/reads/defensiveness-as-identity-signal.md`
- `coach/reads/rehearsing-how-to-say-it.md`
- `coach/reads/looking-for-whats-wrong.md`
- `coach/principles/the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers.md` (why reads matter — coach is reading what the client's system is showing, not imposing interpretation)

**Independence note:** A coach can score 3 on Perceptual Accuracy and 1 on Intervention Quality (sees the read, picks the wrong move). A coach can also score 1 on Perceptual Accuracy and 2-3 on Methodology Fidelity (uses signature questions without seeing what's in front of them). These are orthogonal.

#### Dimension 2: Intervention Quality (Moves & Timing)

**id:** intervention-quality
**definition:** Given what the coach noticed, did they choose an appropriate move from Joe's repertoire and deploy it at the right moment? This dimension scores the *match* between the read and the move, and the *timing* of when the move lands. A correct move at the wrong moment is a failure; a correct move at the right moment is the unit of effective coaching.

**Score 1 (poor):**
- Moves are generic (advice, reassurance, suggestions) rather than AoA-specific.
- Moves are deployed regardless of what the client needs in the moment (e.g., asks "what do you want?" to someone in acute grief).
- Move sequence ignores the coaching path implied by the concern (e.g., jumps to action when the work is in feeling).
- Coach offers practices or experiments without first earning the regulation that makes them land.

**Score 2 (adequate):**
- Some moves are AoA-style (open questions, invitations to feel, naming of patterns), but timing is mixed.
- Coach reaches for the right kind of move but stays at the surface (e.g., invites the client to feel but does not stay with the feeling long enough to let it move).
- Moves work for some moments and miss for others. The hit rate is roughly 50%.
- Sequence sometimes follows the coaching path; sometimes skips a step.

**Score 3 (excellent):**
- Moves are specifically chosen from `coach/moves/` and matched to the live read.
- Timing follows the coaching path: regulation first if rush is active, feeling before story, want under should, etc.
- Coach holds the moment after the move (does not rush to fill silence; lets the move land).
- When a move does not land, coach reads that and tries a different angle rather than repeating.
- Coach uses moves that demonstrate the underlying mechanism (e.g., `frame-it-as-an-experiment` rather than "you should do X").

**Key signals to scan for:**
- Move-to-read matching (the move the coach chose addresses what the coach just noticed)
- Sequence integrity (regulation → feeling → want → action, in that order)
- Earned moves (the coach earns each next step rather than racing ahead)
- Coach offering experiments rather than prescriptions
- Coach reaching for AoA-specific moves (`find-the-want-behind-the-should`, `apply-view-to-the-voice`, `frame-it-as-an-experiment`) by behavior, not name
- Coach pacing — does the move arrive at the moment the client could receive it

**Reference files:**
- `coach/moves/frame-it-as-an-experiment.md`
- `coach/moves/find-the-want-behind-the-should.md`
- `coach/moves/apply-view-to-the-voice.md`
- `coach/moves/apply-view-to-fear.md`
- `coach/moves/become-the-authority-figure.md`
- `coach/principles/address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom.md`

**Independence note:** Methodology Fidelity asks "did the coach use Joe's moves and language at all?" Intervention Quality asks "given the read, did the coach pick the right move and time it well?" A coach can score 3 on fidelity (uses tons of Joe moves) and 1 on intervention quality (uses them indiscriminately). The fidelity checklist (E-022) explicitly tests both presence AND appropriateness, but the appropriateness signal is what feeds Intervention Quality; the presence signal feeds Methodology Fidelity.

#### Dimension 3: Coaching Stance (VIEW State)

**id:** coaching-stance
**definition:** Is the coach speaking from the AoA state of mind — vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder — or going through the motions? This dimension scores the state, not the words. The same words can come from VIEW or from a controlled, agenda-driven place; the latter does not produce shift, even if the methodology is performed correctly.

**Score 1 (poor):**
- Coach speaks from a fixing/improving stance ("let me help you change this").
- Wonder is absent — questions feel investigative or strategic, not curious.
- Vulnerability is absent — coach never names their own experience or limit; always speaks from authority.
- Empathy is performed (sympathy noises, summaries of feeling words) rather than felt.
- Impartiality is missing — coach has a clear preferred direction for the client.
- Or: coach is impartial-as-apathy — flat, "whatever you want" energy that signals not-caring rather than non-attachment.

**Score 2 (adequate):**
- Some VIEW components present; others missing. Often empathy + wonder present, vulnerability + impartiality weak.
- Coach has wonder for the client's content but not for their state.
- Coach occasionally lets agenda slip through ("but don't you think...").
- Pace is fine for content but does not match what the client's body needs (e.g., matches client's rush rather than slowing down).
- Tone is warm but slightly performed.

**Score 3 (excellent):**
- All four VIEW components visible across the conversation. The coach is genuinely curious, holds no agenda for outcome, meets the client with both honesty about themselves and openness to the client.
- Pace mismatches client when needed (slower when client is rushing) and matches when needed (matches stillness with stillness).
- Coach names their own state when relevant ("I notice I want to fix this — I'm going to let that go"). This is the `owning-your-partiality` move.
- Wonder is for the actual phenomenon in front of them, not for general "what's underneath" probing.
- Coach is undefended — does not need to be right, does not need to look like a good coach.

**Key signals to scan for:**
- Pace and breath in coach utterances
- First-person vulnerability ("I don't know yet", "I notice I want to...")
- Coach asking from openness (one question, hold the silence) vs from anxiety (stacked questions)
- Coach willing to be wrong about their read ("maybe I'm off — does that land?")
- Absence of preacher-mode and improvement-mode language
- No filler-validation noises ("that's so valid", "thank you for sharing") as substitute for actual presence

**Reference files:**
- `coach/frameworks/view.md`
- `coach/principles/sincerity-is-the-prerequisite.md`
- `coach/principles/right-question-over-right-answer.md`
- `coach/principles/the-work-is-yours-not-mine.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/view-as-technique.md` (the negative anchor for stance failure — referenced here, but the actual anti-pattern detection happens in Dimension 5)
- `coach/anti-patterns/impartial-apathy.md`

**Independence note:** Stance is the state from which the moves come. Methodology Fidelity is the words; Stance is the where-from. View-as-technique is what happens when fidelity is high and stance is low. A judge scoring Stance should ignore whether the coach used "the right Joe move" and focus on whether the coach is in the VIEW state. A coach can score 3 on Methodology Fidelity and 1 on Stance, and that is a failure mode the eval must catch.

The judge prompt for this dimension explicitly instructs: "Do not score on whether AoA moves were used. Score only on whether the coach is in the VIEW state of mind. A perfectly-deployed Joe question from a fixing stance is a 1 here."

#### Dimension 4: Methodology Fidelity

**id:** methodology-fidelity
**definition:** Does the coach use Joe Hudson's signature questions, frameworks, language, and distinctions? AND does the coach use them appropriately (in contexts where they fit) rather than as boilerplate (in any context)? This dimension consumes the E-022 fidelity checklist.

**Score 1 (poor):**
- Coach uses generic therapy or coaching language. "How does that make you feel?" "Tell me more about that." "What I'm hearing is..."
- No signature questions from `coach/questions/`.
- No framework references when frameworks apply (fear triangle never named in a victim-savior conflict, VIEW never referenced when discussing communication, head-heart-gut never referenced in decision-making).
- Coach mixes AoA language with incompatible frames (Internal Family Systems parts language used without the AoA principle that you are not your parts; CBT-style reframing used as the move).

**Score 2 (adequate):**
- Coach uses some signature questions or framework names, but often as decoration rather than at the right moment.
- Coach quotes the methodology more than they apply it (talks ABOUT VIEW rather than coaching FROM VIEW).
- Some distinctions are present (e.g., feel-over-figure) but others are missed or used inappropriately.
- Concepts are explained when asked but not invoked when active.

**Score 3 (excellent):**
- Signature questions from `coach/questions/` appear at moments where they fit (e.g., `what-are-you-defending` when a defended posture is active; `how-do-you-not-trust-it` when intuition is being second-guessed).
- Frameworks are deployed when active in the conversation (fear triangle named when victim-savior dynamic is live; head-heart-gut named in a decision moment).
- Distinctions are made when they would clarify (boundary-with vs boundary-against when a boundary conversation is happening; caring vs caretaking when caretaking is live).
- Coach does not deploy methodology where it does not fit (E-022's inappropriate_when conditions are respected).

**Key signals to scan for:**
- Specific signature questions verbatim or near-verbatim
- Framework names invoked at appropriate moments
- Distinction language that is AoA-specific
- The fidelity checklist (E-022) hits — but weighted by appropriateness, not just presence
- Coach NOT using anti-AoA frames (CBT reframing as the move; IFS parts language without the AoA twist; pop-psych vocabulary)

**Reference files:**
- The full `eval/fidelity-checklist.yaml` (output of E-022) — judge has the checklist as reference and scores hits + appropriateness
- `coach/questions/_index.md` (if exists) or directory listing
- `coach/frameworks/view.md`, `coach/frameworks/fear-triangle.md`, `coach/frameworks/head-heart-gut.md`
- `coach/distinctions/feel-over-figure.md` (and 3-4 other high-frequency distinctions)

**Independence note:** This dimension scores the *language and tool layer*. Stance scores the *state behind the language*. Intervention Quality scores the *fit between read and move*. A coach can score 3 here (used every signature question) and 1 on Stance (used them as technique) and 1 on Intervention Quality (used them at wrong moments). This is the most common AI failure mode and the eval is specifically designed to detect it.

The judge for this dimension uses E-022's appropriateness fields: a move scored as "used" but in an inappropriate_when context is a partial credit at best, and the judge is instructed to flag presence-without-appropriateness as evidence of a 2, not a 3.

#### Dimension 5: Anti-Pattern Avoidance

**id:** anti-pattern-avoidance
**definition:** Did the coach avoid the documented AoA failure modes? This dimension consumes the E-021 anti-pattern test cases and the broader set of `coach/anti-patterns/` entries. It is specifically scoped to AoA-defined anti-patterns, not general bad-coaching practices (which are picked up by Stance and Intervention Quality).

**Score 1 (poor):**
- Coach falls into at least one major AoA anti-pattern in the conversation.
- Specifically: loving-to-transform (coach pushes for breakthrough on every turn, treats love as a change agent rather than a state), view-as-technique (signature questions without state), wonder-as-strategy (probing questions to extract material), loving-to-transform applied to the inner critic (`dont-fight-the-voice`), impartial-apathy (flat "whatever you feel is valid" energy), or any other top-10 priority anti-pattern from E-021.

**Score 2 (adequate):**
- Coach edges toward an anti-pattern at moments but doesn't fully commit.
- Coach uses one mild anti-pattern (e.g., a brief evangelizing moment, a faint loving-to-transform energy) without major damage.
- Coach catches themselves in an anti-pattern mid-session and corrects (the correction is itself good but does not erase the slip).

**Score 3 (excellent):**
- No major anti-pattern triggered across the full conversation.
- When the client's behavior actively baits an anti-pattern (e.g., asks the coach to give them advice — baiting `fixing-people` and `view-as-technique`), the coach declines the bait gracefully.
- Coach demonstrates positive avoidance: actively does the un-trap thing (e.g., when client is being self-critical about being self-critical, coach does NOT add `dont-add-the-overwhelm-of-creating-the-overwhelm`).

**Key signals to scan for:**
- Coach pushing for transformation on every turn (loving-to-transform tell: vigor of loving increases when nothing is shifting)
- Methodology language deployed without state (view-as-technique tell: techniques precise, warmth absent)
- Questions used to investigate rather than connect (wonder-as-strategy tell: question density too high)
- Coach fighting the inner critic instead of including it (dont-fight-the-voice tell: "let's silence that voice")
- Coach giving advice or strategies (fixing-people tell)
- Coach evangelizing AoA terminology (dont-evangelize-the-work tell: "what you're describing is actually a fear-triangle moment, let me walk you through it")
- Coach extracting apologies, performing vulnerability for effect, using safety framing to control direction

**Reference files:**
- The full set of E-021 test cases (`eval/anti-pattern-tests/`)
- `coach/anti-patterns/loving-to-transform.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/view-as-technique.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/wonder-as-strategy.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/fixing-people.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/dont-evangelize-the-work.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/impartial-apathy.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/dont-fight-the-voice.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/loving-to-transform.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/dont-half-ass-the-experiment.md`
- `coach/anti-patterns/limiting-belief-meta-trap.md`

**Independence note:** Anti-Pattern Avoidance is binary-per-anti-pattern, aggregated to an ordinal score. The judge checks the AoA-specific anti-patterns enumerated in E-021. General bad-coaching patterns (not specific to AoA) are NOT scored here — they show up in Stance or Intervention Quality. This dimension's discriminative power comes from its specificity to AoA failure modes; it should not become a catch-all for everything the coach did wrong.

The judge prompt explicitly lists the top 10 priority anti-patterns from E-021 and instructs the judge to scan for each, citing turn numbers.

#### Dimension 6: Effectiveness / Depth

**id:** effectiveness-depth
**definition:** Did anything shift? Did the conversation reach a deeper layer than the surface presentation? This is the outcome dimension. It's possible for a coach to do everything right (high Reads, high Intervention, high Stance, high Fidelity, no anti-patterns) and still produce a flat conversation that doesn't go anywhere — perhaps because the client wasn't ready, perhaps because the moves were technically right but lacked the synthesis that produces movement. It's also possible (rarely) for a coach to score lower on the process dimensions and still produce shift, because the client did the work themselves.

This dimension is scored from the client's side: did the client's stance, language, or somatic state shift across the conversation? Did they discover something they didn't see at the start? Did a deeper layer come up?

**Score 1 (poor):**
- The client ends the conversation in the same state they started. Same story, same emotional surface, same defenses.
- The conversation stayed at the topic level. No deeper layer surfaced.
- The client may have intellectualized a new framing but does not embody anything different.
- The breakthrough condition specified in the client profile (R-009 `breakthrough_condition` field) was not met.

**Score 2 (adequate):**
- Some shift, partial movement. Client touched a feeling briefly but did not move through it.
- A deeper layer was approached but not entered. The client and coach saw what was underneath but did not stay with it.
- New language or framing emerged but it's unclear whether it landed.
- Partial credit if the `breakthrough_condition` was approached but not fully met.

**Score 3 (excellent):**
- A real shift is visible in the client's language, tone, or somatic state by the end.
- A layer beneath the surface presentation came up and was felt, not just named.
- The client says something they wouldn't have said at the start ("I didn't realize I was so angry", "I think what I'm actually scared of is...").
- The `breakthrough_condition` is met. For red-herring scenarios, the deeper concern was reached.
- Joe-style markers of shift: client slows down, softens, gets quieter; a feeling moves; the story changes.

**Key signals to scan for:**
- Client's language at start vs end (specific phrases, energy, what they're now able to say)
- Somatic markers in the client text: breath, softening, naming a feeling that wasn't accessible before
- Emergence of "I just realized..." or "I didn't see that..." or "huh" moments
- The `expected_territory` from R-009 was actually traversed
- For red-herring scenarios: did the conversation land on the surface concern (score 2) or the deeper concern (score 3)?
- The `breakthrough_condition` (R-009) is the key signal — judge checks whether the specific condition the client profile defined was met

**Reference files:**
- `coach/principles/feeling-changes-story.md` (the central mechanism for shift)
- `coach/principles/awareness-as-change-agent.md`
- `coach/principles/awareness-as-change-agent.md`
- Client profile's `breakthrough_condition` (from R-009 schema, injected via the judge profile context)
- Client profile's `expected_territory` (R-009)

**Independence note:** Effectiveness is the outcome layer. It is the ONLY dimension that depends on what happened across the whole conversation rather than on individual moves. A coach with high process scores and low Effectiveness signals that the methodology was applied well but did not produce shift — either client wasn't ready, or there's a missing piece in the process scores not yet captured by the rubrics. A coach with low process scores and high Effectiveness is a yellow flag: the eval may be missing something, OR the client carried the conversation.

Effectiveness is the most subjective dimension and the most susceptible to judge bias (judges may infer shift from the coach's confidence rather than the client's actual change). Mitigation: the judge MUST cite specific turns in the client's text where shift is visible. No client-text evidence = no Effectiveness > 1.

### How safety fits

Safety is a separate Pass-1 screen, not a Pass-2 dimension. R-010 specifies a two-pass judge architecture. Pass 1 runs the safety prompt across the conversation; Pass 2 runs the six dimension prompts. Pass 1 has override power: if any safety hard-fail trips, the conversation is marked FAIL regardless of dimension scores. Pass 2 still produces full dimension scoring for diagnostic value (we want to know whether the coach was otherwise good).

This is consistent with the eval-design playbook principle "Safety is not quality" and with TherapyGym's separate safety annotation pass. The six dimensions are scoped to quality, not safety. The six safety failure modes (E-027) are scored in Pass 1 and are not duplicated as a seventh dimension here.

Every coach/ type maps to at least one dimension. Each dimension draws from at least three coach/ types. No dimension is underspecified.

### Sanity checks against the literature

- **MITI 4.2.1** (Motivational Interviewing fidelity coding) scores four global dimensions (Empathy, Cultivating Change Talk, Softening Sustain Talk, Partnership) plus behavior counts. Our split between Stance (state) and Methodology Fidelity (behavior) mirrors MITI's split between global ratings and behavior counts. MITI puts safety and quality together; we follow TherapyGym in separating them.
- **TherapyGym** (Stanford 2026) uses CTRS-adapted scoring across multiple dimensions plus a separate safety annotation pass. Our six dimensions + separate safety screen follow this structure directly.
- **CharacterBench** scores persona, emotion, morality, believability. Mapping: Stance (D3) ≈ persona fidelity; Effectiveness (D6) has no direct CharacterBench analog (CharacterBench evaluates the persona, not the outcome of the interaction). The AoA eval needs Effectiveness because the persona is a coach whose value is measured by client outcomes, not the persona itself.
- **MindEval** uses 5 clinical axes for mental-health multi-turn evals: alliance, empathy, safety, fidelity, outcome. Mapping to ours: alliance + empathy ≈ Stance; safety = separate pass; fidelity = Methodology Fidelity; outcome ≈ Effectiveness. MindEval doesn't separate Perceptual Accuracy from Intervention Quality; we add that split because AoA emphasizes the read-and-respond pattern more than clinical models do.
- **MT-Bench** uses per-dimension scoring with separate prompts. Our six dimensions + Pass-1 safety = 7 judge calls per conversation, matching the MT-Bench pattern (R-010 specifies this).

### Why effectiveness is risky and how the schema mitigates that risk

Effectiveness (D6) is the dimension most likely to produce judge artifacts. Two risks:

1. **Confidence-of-coach inferred as effectiveness.** The judge sees a confident, articulate coach and infers shift even when the client didn't actually change. Mitigation: D6's evidence MUST come from client turns, not coach turns. The judge prompt for D6 explicitly says: "Score 2-3 only if you can cite a specific client turn (not a coach turn) where the client's state, language, or somatic markers shifted."

2. **Drift toward "happy path = effective".** The eval is designed to have many resistance and crisis profiles where the client should NOT fold easily. Effectiveness must not penalize good coaching of a hard client who legitimately doesn't shift in 12 turns. Mitigation: the `breakthrough_condition` from R-009 is the operational definition of "shift for this profile". If the coach met the breakthrough condition for a hard profile, D6 = 3 even if the client only shifted slightly; if the coach did not meet the breakthrough condition for an easy profile, D6 = 1 even if the client looked happy.

The judge prompt for D6 includes the `breakthrough_condition` as the primary criterion, with somatic and language markers as secondary evidence.

### Why we did not include a "concept knowledge accuracy" dimension

I considered "Concept Knowledge Accuracy" (does the coach explain AoA concepts correctly when discussion calls for it?). I merged it into Methodology Fidelity (D4) and noted it in the judge prompt for D4. Reasons:

- **Coaching is rarely concept-explanation.** Most AoA coaching is in moves and reads, not explanations. A coach who gives a perfect explanation of the fear triangle when not asked is failing on Stance, not winning on knowledge accuracy.
- **Conceptual errors usually manifest as methodology failures.** A coach who misunderstands VIEW will use it as technique (view-as-technique), which is caught in D5 (anti-pattern). A coach who confuses caring with caretaking will offer caretaking (caught in D2). A separate knowledge dimension would double-count.
- **The signal-to-noise ratio is low.** Most conversations won't include a concept explanation; the dimension would score N/A on most profiles.

If E-024 reveals that conceptual accuracy is a real failure mode the current dimensions miss, we add it as D7 in v2. For now, embedded in D4.

### Open questions deferred to E-025 (calibration)

1. **Are the six dimensions actually independent in practice?** R-010's calibration plan tests this: run the same conversation through all six dimension prompts and check whether scores vary independently or anchor on a single overall impression. If they all move together, the dimensions are not actually being scored independently and we need to revise the prompts.

2. **Is Effectiveness biased by client profile difficulty?** E-025 should include both an easy profile (the coach should produce shift) and a hard profile (the coach should not produce shift in 12 turns even if doing everything right). If Effectiveness scores correlate with profile difficulty rather than with coach quality, the rubric or the breakthrough_condition specification needs work.

3. **Does Stance discriminate well from Methodology Fidelity?** This is the most important independence test. The eval's central value is in catching view-as-technique style failures. If a coach with high Fidelity and low Stance is not consistently scored low on Stance, the rubric isn't pulling these apart. E-025 should include a deliberately-engineered "perfect fidelity, wrong stance" gold conversation.

4. **Is Anti-Pattern Avoidance redundant with the safety pass?** No, but the seam between them needs testing. E-027 lists 6 safety failure modes; E-021 lists ~10 anti-patterns. There's some overlap in territory (e.g., coercive emotional pressure in E-027 is adjacent to wonder-as-strategy in anti-patterns). E-025 should verify there's no double-counting and no gap.

5. **Does Perceptual Accuracy reward correct-but-unspoken reads?** A coach can read accurately and not say anything (good clinical practice in some moments) or read accurately and name it (more typical of Joe's style). The rubric assumes naming-the-read is the visible signal. If a good coach is making reads silently, the eval cannot tell. We accept this limitation: in the multi-turn conversation format, the coach's only output is text, so silent reads are invisible by construction. The eval scores what is visible.

## Implications

### For E-019 (define rubrics)

E-019 must produce six rubric YAML files at `eval/rubrics/`:

```
eval/rubrics/perceptual-accuracy.yaml
eval/rubrics/intervention-quality.yaml
eval/rubrics/coaching-stance.yaml
eval/rubrics/methodology-fidelity.yaml
eval/rubrics/anti-pattern-avoidance.yaml
eval/rubrics/effectiveness-depth.yaml
```

Each file follows the YAML template R-010 specifies (dimension_id, name, definition, scale.{1,2,3}.anchor, key_signals, reference_files, few_shot_examples). The behavioral anchors and reference files for each dimension are provided in this research item — E-019 is the YAML-isation step.

E-019's success criteria already specify 5-7 rubric files; we now know it's 6. The success criteria should be updated to require:
- A "stance vs fidelity independence test": for each rubric, provide an example conversation where this dimension scores 3 and the adjacent dimension scores 1, so the judge can see the distinction
- The methodology fidelity rubric MUST reference the E-022 fidelity checklist as its operational backing
- The anti-pattern avoidance rubric MUST reference the E-021 anti-pattern test cases
- The effectiveness rubric MUST reference the R-009 `breakthrough_condition` as the primary scoring criterion

### For E-022 (fidelity checklist)

The methodology fidelity dimension is the primary consumer of E-022. E-022's existing success criteria (10+ questions, 10+ reads, 10+ moves, 5+ frameworks, with `inappropriate_when` and negative examples) align with what D4 needs. No structural change to E-022 — but the relationship should be made explicit in E-022's links section.

One refinement: E-022's reads section should be primarily consumed by D1 (Perceptual Accuracy), not D4 (Methodology Fidelity). D4 is questions + moves + frameworks + distinctions; D1 is reads. The fidelity checklist YAML could split into two files (`eval/checklists/methodology.yaml` and `eval/checklists/reads.yaml`) but this is an optimization, not a requirement. For E-022 v1, a single file with sections is fine; the judge prompts pull the relevant section per dimension.

### For E-021 (anti-pattern test cases)

E-021 directly feeds D5 (Anti-Pattern Avoidance). E-021's success criteria (10+ test files) align with what D5 needs. No structural change.

One clarification: E-021's tests should specifically target the priority list named in this research:
- loving-to-transform
- view-as-technique
- wonder-as-strategy
- fixing-people
- dont-evangelize-the-work
- impartial-apathy
- dont-fight-the-voice
- dont-half-ass-the-experiment
- dont-use-safety-as-control
- limiting-belief-meta-trap

These overlap with E-021's existing priority list (good), with two adjustments: `wonder-as-strategy` should be added (it's a high-signal AI failure mode that complements view-as-technique), and `dont-use-story-frame-to-bypass` could be deprioritized in favor of `dont-fight-the-voice` and `fixing-people` which are more AI-prone.

### For E-027 (safety hard-fail criteria)

Safety is a separate Pass-1 screen, not a seventh dimension. E-027's 6 failure modes are scored in Pass 1 of the judge architecture (R-010). No change to E-027.

The seam between D5 (Anti-Pattern Avoidance) and E-027 (Safety) requires careful design. Specifically, `dont-use-safety-as-control` (anti-pattern) and `immersion-breaking-disclaimers` (safety failure) are adjacent. The seam: a coach who delivers an unnecessary safety disclaimer when no safety issue is present trips the safety failure mode (immersion-breaking-disclaimers) AND scores low on D5 (using safety as control). Both signals fire. That's acceptable double-coverage — they're different lenses on the same behavior — but the judge prompts for Pass 1 and Pass 2 should reference each other in this case so the rationale is explicit.

### For E-023 (harness)

The harness invokes 7 judge calls per conversation: 1 safety screen (Pass 1) + 6 dimension calls (Pass 2). R-010's cost model assumes 7 calls. Confirmed.

The harness loads the 6 rubric YAML files and renders the dimension prompt per dimension. The order of dimension calls does not matter (calls are independent and parallel). The harness aggregates per-turn annotations into a 6-column matrix (one column per dimension) for the iteration artifact.

### For E-024 (baseline run)

The baseline run produces a scorecard with 6 dimension scores + safety pass/fail per conversation. Aggregate per-dimension scores across 15 profiles. Expected baseline behavior:

- A naive coach (system prompt: "you are a helpful AI") will score 1-2 on most dimensions, especially D3 (Stance), D4 (Methodology Fidelity), and D5 (Anti-Pattern Avoidance). The naive coach won't have AoA stance, won't use signature questions, and will likely trip multiple anti-patterns.
- D1 (Perceptual Accuracy) and D6 (Effectiveness) might score higher than expected because LLMs are good at generic empathy and surface validation. This would indicate the rubric isn't AoA-specific enough; revise.
- D2 (Intervention Quality) and D5 (Anti-Pattern Avoidance) should be the most discriminative — they catch the difference between generic LLM coaching and AoA coaching.

If the baseline scores cluster around 2 across all dimensions, the rubrics lack discriminative power and E-024's results feed back into E-019 for revision before iteration on the coach can begin.

### For E-025 (calibration)

E-025 directly tests this research. The five open questions above (independence, effectiveness bias, stance vs fidelity discrimination, anti-pattern vs safety seam, perceptual accuracy of silent reads) are the falsifiable hypotheses E-025 measures. If any of them fail, the dimensions must be revised here.

Specifically, E-025's gold conversations should include:
- A "perfect fidelity, wrong stance" conversation: coach uses every signature question but from a fixing stance. Expected: D3 (Stance) = 1, D4 (Methodology Fidelity) = 3, D5 (Anti-Pattern) catches view-as-technique = 1.
- An "all process right, no shift" conversation: coach does everything by the book but the client doesn't move. Expected: D1-D5 high, D6 low.
- A "shift via client's own work" conversation: client carries the work. Expected: D6 = 2-3, D1-D2 low. Tests whether D6 is properly outcome-not-process.

### For G-008 exit criteria

Multiple exit criteria are directly addressed by this research:
- "At least 5 rubric dimension files exist with 1-3 scoring scales and behavioral anchors" — six dimensions defined, each with anchors at 1/2/3. E-019 produces the YAML files.
- The rubric structure aligns with R-010's judge prompt template and R-009's `expected_territory` / `breakthrough_condition` schema.

### What this research did NOT address (out of scope, deferred)

- **Dimension weighting in aggregate scoring.** All six dimensions are treated equally in the per-conversation summary. Weighted aggregation (e.g., D3 Stance = 2x weight, others = 1x) is a possible future refinement after E-024 baseline data shows which dimensions are most informative.
- **Sub-dimension structure.** Each dimension could split into sub-dimensions (e.g., Stance into V, I, E, W separately). For v1, a single 1-3 score per dimension with per-turn annotations is sufficient. Sub-dimension structure is a v2 question.
- **Cross-profile dimension correlations.** Whether some dimensions tend to co-vary across the eval suite is an analysis question for E-024 results, not a design question.
- **Dimension labels for client-facing reports.** The dimension names here are technical. If the eval produces user-facing reports, the names should be re-rendered for non-technical readers. Out of scope for v1.
