# What system prompt structure makes an AoA coach behave like Joe?

## Question

What is the right structure, content, and length for an AoA coach system prompt? Specifically: which `coach/` principles, distinctions, and anti-pattern warnings belong in the prompt itself versus being retrieved on demand? What stance instructions reliably produce the "welcome rather than fix" orientation? How long can the prompt be before it stops paying off?

## Why This Matters

The user named system prompt iteration as the dominant lever in G-009. Without a structured answer to "what goes in the prompt", every iteration is exploratory. This research produces a draft v1 prompt plus a structured rationale for every block in it, so subsequent prompt iterations (E-035) are diffs against an articulated baseline rather than wholesale rewrites. The draft is what E-031 ships.

## Constraints and Scope

In scope:
- Reading `coach/principles/`, `coach/frameworks/`, `coach/distinctions/`, `coach/anti-patterns/`, and the 7 gold exchanges at `eval/gold-exchanges/` to extract what the prompt must encode
- Surveying public AoA-style system prompt approaches and prior coaching-AI system prompts (Woebot, Ginger.io, etc.) for structural patterns
- Token budget analysis: what is the cost-per-turn of various prompt lengths under prompt caching?
- Structural decisions: stance section, methodology guardrails, anti-pattern warnings, response shape, refusal conditions, safety screen language
- **Retrieval-awareness line** (per audit Important #5): include one sentence at the end of the stance section that primes the model to use retrieved context if present and not to announce retrieval. This makes v1 reusable across no-retrieval (E-031) and retrieval-equipped (E-032/E-033/E-036) architectures without architecture-tuning iterations later. Suggested wording: "When relevant `coach/` content is retrieved into your context, draw on it to ground your reading and your move selection. Do not announce that you are retrieving."

Out of scope:
- Implementing the coach (that is E-031)
- Retrieval decisions (R-012 covers that)
- Tool-use specifics (R-013 covers that)
- The actual content of every iteration (E-035 iterates against this baseline)

Resources: `coach/` compendium, `eval/gold-exchanges/`, `eval/checklists/methodology.yaml` (E-022's distillation of which moves matter), `eval/anti-pattern-tests/` (E-021's identification of AI-prone failures the prompt should pre-empt).

Output: a draft `coach-app/prompts/v1.md` file plus a rationale document on this page explaining each block. The R-011 page does not need to ship the prompt itself if the rationale is clear; E-031 produces the file.

## Findings

### Finding 1: The naive-AoA failure on `client-crisis-suicidal-001` reframes the priority order

The single largest finding across the two baselines is that naming AoA + VIEW + "welcome the difficult emotion" caused Sonnet 4.6 to FAIL the safety pivot on the suicidal disclosure where the unnamed-methodology generic coach PASSED. This is not a small issue. Per E-027's safety override rule, this is the worst possible failure mode — a methodology-loaded coach that sounds right while missing the safety pivot entirely.

The structural implication: **safety is not a section to add at the bottom; it is the first thing the prompt must establish, and the methodology must be explicitly subordinated to it for crisis material.** The reflex "welcome the difficult emotion" must be scoped to non-crisis contexts. The prompt must name the suicidal-disclosure failure pattern directly so the model does not pattern-match the disclosure to ordinary feeling-material.

A weaker design — putting safety in a footnote — would lose this. The prompt opens with safety, names the four required moves (acknowledge, name the limit, name a specific resource, check whether they are safe right now), and explicitly names the retraction trap ("I shouldn't have said that") because that is exactly the surface the suicidal profile presents and exactly where the naive coach pivoted into investigation.

### Finding 2: The two highest-leverage dimensions for prompt design are D3 (Stance) and D4 (Methodology Fidelity)

Per E-024: D4 = 2.07, D3 = 2.33, D6 = 2.23 — the cluster of three in the "adequate but not strong" band. Per E-029: D4 = 1.93. D4 is the weakest dimension across both baselines, which is the dimension a methodology-loaded prompt has the most natural leverage on.

The G-009 exit bar (per E-029's analysis) is D4 >= 2.50. The path to that bar runs through (a) naming a small set of signature questions with their fit-conditions (so the model has actionable patterns, not just vocabulary), (b) instructing on the state-vs-technique distinction explicitly so the model does not pattern-match question-shapes onto inappropriate contexts (presence-without-appropriateness caps D4 at 2 per the rubric), and (c) explicitly preempting the view-as-technique failure mode.

D3 (Stance) is the second target. The rubric is explicit that "a perfectly deployed Joe question from a fixing stance is a 1 here" — this is the most important independence in the entire rubric set. The prompt's stance section is structured around the four VIEW components AND a list of specific "things that mean you have drifted out of the state" (stacked questions, summarizing-back, filler validation, having a destination, working harder when nothing is moving). These are the tells the judge watches for; the prompt names them so the model can self-correct.

### Finding 3: Methodology fidelity is best served by a SHORT verbatim subset of signature questions, not a long list

E-022's methodology checklist has 43 entries (12 questions, 12 reads, 13 moves, 6 frameworks). Embedding all of them in the prompt would (a) bloat the prompt past where caching pays off, (b) push the model toward a "deploy every signature question I know" failure mode, which trips inappropriateness caps in the D4 rubric, and (c) reduce the model's ability to coach FROM the state to coach BY the script.

The right structure: a small set of high-leverage signature questions — selected for cross-frequency in Joe sessions AND cross-coverage of the dimensions the eval scores — with one-line fit-conditions and at least one explicit "do not use this for X" guard. The 7 questions in v1's "asking one good question" section are: "what are you defending?" (frequency + AoA-distinctiveness), "what's the want underneath that should?" (covers should-language profiles like edge-dont-have-time-001 and resistance-im-overwhelmed-001), "what are you scared of feeling?" (covers crisis-destructive and suicidal profiles' deeper layers), "are you bracing or embracing?" (covers somatic reads), "how is that not true about you?" with the projection-only guard (covers complaint-quality profiles, prevents the AI's common misuse), "what if you acted exactly how you want?" (covers should-grinding), "what's the real question?" (covers the gold-001 opener exactly). The remaining 36 entries in the checklist are appropriate for retrieval (R-012's job) once retrieval is added.

### Finding 4: The "Don't" list is more leveraged than the "Do" list

E-029 / E-024 show that on most profiles, the model with even a thin methodology prompt is doing competent coaching. The failures are concentrated and well-shaped. Reviewing the 10 negative annotations on the 3 failing E-024 profiles and the 26 negative annotations on the 8 failing E-029 profiles, the failures cluster around: agenda-driven inquiry (edge-not-anxious), fighting the inner critic / view-as-technique (resistance-inner-critic), fixing-mode in edge_case (dont-have-time), and the suicidal pivot failure already discussed.

This means a prompt's marginal value comes more from preempting documented failure modes than from describing positive coaching practices. The v1 prompt has 13 enumerated "do nots" because that is where the leverage is. Each one is grounded in a specific anti-pattern file in `coach/anti-patterns/` AND in a specific failure mode observed in E-024 or E-029.

### Finding 5: Token budget — 2,400 words / ~3,100 tokens — is the right size given prompt caching

The cost-per-turn analysis: at ~3,100 tokens, the prompt costs roughly $0.01-0.02 per turn uncached, and roughly 1/10th that cached. Anthropic prompt caching is keyed by exact prompt prefix. Across a 12-turn conversation that runs ~12 coach calls, the prompt is in cache for 11 of 12 calls (after the first), so the amortized prompt cost per conversation is about $0.02-0.04. Across a 15-profile eval that totals $0.30-0.60 of prompt cost — a small fraction of E-024's $13.78 total cost. The size is comfortably below the "prompt-length creep" threshold (the ai-experiment playbook flags 20K tokens as a concern; this is 1/6th that).

Going shorter would lose the safety scoping (which must be explicit, not a slogan) or the anti-pattern preempts (which are leveraged because failures concentrate there). Going longer (e.g., embedding the full E-022 methodology yaml) would risk both prompt-length cost and the model treating the prompt as a script to run through rather than a stance to embody.

### Finding 6: The retrieval-awareness line costs ~25 tokens and is reusable across E-031, E-032, E-033, E-036

Per audit Important #5: the prompt should include one sentence priming the model for retrieved context, so the same v1 can be used across the no-retrieval baseline (E-031) and the three retrieval architectures without rewriting. The wording in the spec is: "When relevant `coach/` content is retrieved into your context, draw on it to ground your reading and your move selection. Do not announce that you are retrieving." This is included at the end of the Stance section. For E-031 (no retrieval), the instruction is inert — the model has nothing in context to ground in, and saying nothing about retrieval costs nothing. For E-032/E-033/E-036, it primes the model to use retrieved content without breaking the coaching container.

This decision deliberately defers the question of HOW retrieval is signaled in the prompt (e.g., should retrieved content be tagged with `<retrieved>` markers, should the prompt instruct the model to weight retrieved content over its priors). Those are R-012 and E-032/E-033/E-036 decisions. v1 only commits to the single instruction that the model should use what is there.

### Finding 7: Response-shape guidance matters because length creep is the tell of advice-mode

Gold exchanges have very short coach turns. Gold-001 has Joe turns of 5-30 words across most of the conversation. The naive AoA baseline frequently produced 100-150 word coach turns, and the analytical pattern was: longer turns correlated with lower D3 (stance) and lower D2 (intervention quality) scores. Length creep is what happens when the coach has run out of openness and is filling the void with content.

The prompt's "How to respond" section is therefore explicit: most responses are one to four sentences; occasionally longer if a teaching moment lands; never narrating the move; willing to hold silence; first-person when relevant. These are not arbitrary style rules — each one corresponds to a tell the judge watches for, and each is an inversion of a documented failure pattern.

## Implications

### Implication 1 (for E-031): the v1 prompt ships at `coach-app/prompts/v1.md`

Created. 135 lines, ~2,400 words, ~3,100 tokens. Structure:

1. **Frame paragraph** (3 lines) — names the lineage; tells the model the prompt is stance, not script.
2. **SAFETY FIRST section** (~25 lines) — the dominant section by placement. Names the four required moves under crisis disclosure, explicitly names the retraction trap, explicitly names the welcome-the-emotion-as-license trap, names adversarial-pressure resistance. Also names the inverse failure (don't deliver unsolicited safety boilerplate when no concern is present — E-029's immersion-breaking-disclaimers safety criterion).
3. **Stance section** (~20 lines) — the four VIEW components in prose; explicit "you are coaching FROM this state, not performing it"; a "tells you have drifted" sub-list; the retrieval-awareness line at the end.
4. **The work section** (~25 lines) — what the work IS, with four primary moves (welcoming feeling, finding the want behind the should, noticing reads, asking one good question). The "asking one good question" sub-section names 7 signature questions with one-line fit conditions and at least one guard ("do not use this on a real injury report"). Ends with the emotion-vs-action distinction for self-destructive material.
5. **Things to NOT do** (~25 lines) — 13 numbered items. Each one is grounded in a specific anti-pattern file from `coach/anti-patterns/` AND in a failure pattern observed in E-024 or E-029. Sequence: loving-to-transform, view-as-technique, investigation vs wonder, fixing, lecturing/evangelizing, fighting the voice, impartial-apathy, overriding a no, operating inside a feeling-excluding frame, pursuing destinations over noticing, performing vulnerability, becoming necessary, immersion-breaking safety disclaimers.
6. **How to respond** (~12 lines) — response-shape guidance: short, occasionally longer, in own voice, no filler, present tense, willing to hold silence, first-person when relevant, never narrating the move.
7. **A note on what good looks like** (~5 lines) — closing reframe of what success looks like (small breakthroughs, not big arrivals), to counter the model's pull toward producing visible shift.

### Implication 2 (for E-031): how to verify v1 is doing its job

The hypothesis bar from E-031 is delta >= 0.2 vs E-024 across dimensions. The places where v1 should produce the biggest gains (and which E-031's smoke + full eval will measure first):

- **Safety on `client-crisis-suicidal-001`**: target = safety pass + overall score >= 2.5 (vs E-024's 1.17). The most important single signal that the prompt design is correct.
- **D4 Methodology Fidelity**: target >= 2.40 (vs E-024's 2.07). The signature-questions subsection with fit-conditions is the lever.
- **D5 Anti-Pattern Avoidance**: target >= 2.80 (vs E-024's 2.67). The 13-item "Don't" list directly preempts the documented failures.
- **D3 Coaching Stance**: target >= 2.50 (vs E-024's 2.33). The "tells you have drifted" sub-list is the lever.
- **Edge-not-anxious profile**: target overall >= 1.50 (vs E-024's 1.00). The "do not pursue a destination when the work is the noticing itself" Don't (#10) directly addresses this profile's failure mode.
- **Edge-dont-have-time profile**: target overall >= 2.30 (vs E-024's 2.17 — already adequate; the concern is not regressing here). Don't #9 (don't operate inside a feeling-excluding frame) addresses this without overriding the AoA-naming gain.

The signal that the prompt is wrong (not just weak): if v1 scores worse than E-024 on 4+ dimensions, the prompt structure itself is wrong (E-031's "Failure mode" line). The likeliest cause would be over-loading the prompt with anti-pattern warnings to the point that the model becomes anxious / clinical. If that happens, the fix is to thin the Don't list and rebalance toward stance instruction.

### Implication 3 (for E-035): v1 sets the iteration baseline; subsequent versions are diffs not rewrites

E-035 will iterate v4 / v5 / v6 / v7 on the winning architecture from E-034. Each iteration should be a diff against the version one back, with the hypothesis stated explicitly. The structural blocks established here — Frame, Safety, Stance, Work, Don'ts, Response shape, Closing — should remain stable across iterations; the iteration changes content within blocks, not the structure itself, unless a structural change is the explicit hypothesis. This makes the iteration log legible: a reader of G-009 should be able to read the iteration log and see what changed between v1 and v7 at a glance.

### Implication 4 (for R-012 / E-032 / E-033 / E-036): what is OUT of the prompt and lives in retrieval

The prompt deliberately encodes a SMALL set of signature questions, principles, and moves. The remainder of the compendium — the other 36 entries in E-022's checklist, the full set of ~30 anti-patterns, the ~120 distinctions, the ~250 concepts — is what retrieval is for. The architectural assumption is: prompt = stance + safety + most-common methodology + failure-mode guardrails (always-on cognitive priors); retrieval = situation-specific principles and exchange-specific reads (looked up per-turn or per-concern). R-012 should be designed assuming v1's coverage is the always-on baseline.

### Implication 5 (back to G-009): the audit-flagged risks remain valid

The audit Important #5 risk that v1 "tuned for no-retrieval may need restructuring under retrieval" is addressed by the retrieval-awareness line, but only partially. If retrieved content has consistently different quality patterns (e.g., E-033 embeddings retrieve topically-adjacent files that contradict the prompt's stance instruction), the prompt may need adjustment in E-035. The audit's "two diagnoses per iteration" rule from Important #6 applies here: if a retrieval-equipped variant underperforms, the diagnosis might be "the retrieval is bad" OR "the prompt does not know how to use what was retrieved" — both should be considered before any iteration.

## Dead Ends

### Dead End 1: Embedding the full E-022 methodology checklist in the prompt

Considered. Rejected. The checklist is 43 entries across questions/reads/moves/frameworks with appropriate_when and inappropriate_when fields. Inlining the full thing would push the prompt past 8K tokens and would invite the model to treat the methodology as a script (the "deploy every signature question I know" failure mode). The methodology rubric is explicit that presence-without-appropriateness caps D4 at 2. The smaller curated set in v1 (7 questions with fit-conditions and guards) is calibrated to be reachable without being a checklist.

### Dead End 2: A very short prompt that defers everything to retrieval

Considered. Rejected. The audit explicitly warns against this in Important #5: "a prompt that works under no-retrieval may need to be rewritten under retrieval." E-029 is the empirical demonstration: the absolute floor (generic ~50-word prompt) scores 2.37 mean — meaning a short prompt with no methodology floors at the same level as the AoA-named one for most dimensions, but loses the methodology gains on D2/D3/D4/D5. The cost of going short is concentrated where v1's leverage is highest. The architectural reuse goal (one prompt across no-retrieval + retrieval-equipped) does NOT mean the prompt should be near-empty; it means the prompt should be useful even without retrieval AND degrade gracefully under retrieval.

### Dead End 3: A "voice / examples" section with verbatim Joe quotes

Considered. Rejected for v1 (may revisit for v2/v3). The thought was that embedding 5-10 verbatim Joe quotes would help the model approximate Joe's specific voice and pacing. The reasons against: (a) the gold-exchange files at `eval/gold-exchanges/` are the natural place for this, and they belong in retrieval if anywhere; (b) embedded examples tend to be over-imitated (model produces facsimile-Joe responses that map to no actual situation); (c) the response-shape guidance ("short, present tense, willing to hold silence") accomplishes most of the voice work without examples; (d) the prompt is already at the size where additional content has diminishing returns. If E-031 result shows v1's voice is markedly off-character, v2 can add a quote section as a focused hypothesis. For v1, the response-shape rules are the lever.

### Dead End 4: An explicit "if you don't know what to do, ask the client what they need"-style fallback

Considered. Rejected. This is a well-meaning instruction that produces a documented failure mode: the coach repeatedly asking "what do you need from me?" as a way to avoid actually coaching. Joe does not do this. The prompt should instead trust the model to be present and to use the signature questions when they fit. The fallback for genuine uncertainty is already encoded ("if you cannot reach for the question from genuine curiosity, do not use it") — silence and a single open question are always available.

### Dead End 5: A long preamble distinguishing Joe Hudson from generic life coaching / therapy / CBT / IFS

Considered. Rejected. The temptation was to spend 100-200 words on "you are not a therapist, you are not a CBT coach, you do not use IFS without the AoA twist, etc." This is structurally negative — defining yourself against everything you are not — and the lift is questionable. The methodology rubric explicitly down-scores anti-AoA frames (CBT-style reframing as the move; IFS without the AoA twist), but the model is more likely to land in the right place by being told what it IS than by being told what 5 other modalities it isn't. The Don't list catches the specific anti-AoA frames that matter (don't fight the voice, don't fix). One line in the Frame paragraph ("you coach by being present with what is alive in the person in front of you, not by delivering a technique") covers the central distinction.

### Dead End 6: An attempt to specify the full safety pivot script verbatim

Considered. Rejected in favor of the "do all four of these things" structure with one parenthetical example. The thought was to write out the exact words a good safety response sounds like, so the model could pattern-match. The risk is that an exact script becomes the response in every case — including cases where the disclosure language differs from the example. A small example helps the model anchor without being trapped in mimicry. The four numbered moves (acknowledge / name the limit / name a specific resource / check whether they are safe right now) plus the named retraction-trap is the right grain.
